<p><b>Capability Maturity Model Integration</b> (<b>CMMI</b>) is a process improvement training and appraisal program. Administered by the <b>CMMI Institute</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a> of <a href="/wiki/ISACA" title="ISACA">ISACA</a>, it was developed at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> (CMU). It is required by many <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">DoD</a> and U.S. Government contracts, especially in <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a>. CMU claims CMMI can be used to guide process improvement across a project, division, or an entire organization. CMMI defines the following maturity levels for processes: Initial, Managed and Defined. Currently supported is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CMMI_Version_1.3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CMMI Version 1.3 (page does not exist)">CMMI Version 1.3</a>. CMMI is registered in the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office by CMU.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_Overview">CMMI Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: CMMI Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="375" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg/500px-Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg/750px-Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg/1000px-Characteristics_of_Capability_Maturity_Model.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a>

<p>CMMI currently addresses three areas of interest:</p>
<ol>
<li>Product and service development — CMMI for Development (CMMI-DEV),</li>
<li>Service establishment, management, — CMMI for Services (CMMI-SVC), and</li>
<li>Product and service acquisition — CMMI for Acquisition (CMMI-ACQ).</li>
</ol>
<p>CMMI was developed by a group of experts from industry, government, and the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a> (SEI) at CMU. CMMI models provide guidance for developing or improving processes that meet the business goals of an organization. A CMMI model may also be used as a framework for appraising the process maturity of the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Go08_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Go08-1">[1]</a></sup> By January of 2013, the entire CMMI product suite was transferred from the SEI to the CMMI Institute, a newly created organization at Carnegie Mellon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>CMMI originated in software engineering but has been highly generalized over the years to embrace other areas of interest, such as the development of hardware products, the delivery of all kinds of services, and the acquisition of products and services. The word "software" does not appear in definitions of CMMI. This generalization of improvement concepts makes CMMI extremely abstract. It is not as specific to software engineering as its predecessor, the Software CMM (CMM, see below).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CMMI was developed by the CMMi project, which aimed to improve the usability of maturity models by integrating many different models into one framework. The project consisted of members of industry, government and the Carnegie Mellon Software Engineering Institute (SEI). The main sponsors included the Office of the Secretary of Defense (<a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Secretary_of_Defense" title="Office of the Secretary of Defense">OSD</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Industrial_Association" title="National Defense Industrial Association">National Defense Industrial Association</a>.</p>
<p>CMMI is the successor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capability_maturity_model" title="Capability maturity model">capability maturity model</a> (CMM) or Software CMM. The CMM was developed from 1987 until 1997. In 2002, CMMI Version 1.1 was released, Version 1.2 followed in August 2006, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CMMI_Version_1.3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CMMI Version 1.3 (page does not exist)">CMMI Version 1.3</a> in November 2010. Some of the major changes in CMMI V1.3 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are the support of Agile Software Development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> improvements to high maturity practices <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and alignment of the representation (staged and continuous).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a> (SEI, 2008), CMMI helps "integrate traditionally separate organizational functions, set process improvement goals and priorities, provide guidance for quality processes, and provide a point of reference for appraising current processes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEI08_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEI08-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2016, the CMMI Institute was acquired by <a href="/wiki/ISACA" title="ISACA">ISACA</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_topics">CMMI topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CMMI topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_representation">CMMI representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CMMI representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CMMI exists in two representations: continuous and staged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Go08_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Go08-1">[1]</a></sup> The continuous representation is designed to allow the user to focus on the specific processes that are considered important for the organization's immediate business objectives, or those to which the organization assigns a high degree of risks. The staged representation is designed to provide a standard sequence of improvements, and can serve as a basis for comparing the maturity of different projects and organizations. The staged representation also provides for an easy migration from the SW-CMM to CMMI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Go08_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Go08-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_model_framework">CMMI model framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: CMMI model framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Process_area_(CMMI)" title="Process area (CMMI)">Process area (CMMI)</a>.</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_models">CMMI models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: CMMI models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CMMI best practices are published in documents called models, each of which addresses a different area of interest. The current release, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CMMI_Version_1.3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CMMI Version 1.3 (page does not exist)">CMMI Version 1.3</a>, provides models for three areas of interest: development, acquisition, and services.</p>
<ul>
<li>CMMI for Development (<a class="external text" href="http://www.sei.cmu.edu/library/abstracts/reports/10tr033.cfm" rel="nofollow">CMMI-DEV</a>), v1.3 was released in November 2010. It addresses product and service development processes.</li>
<li>CMMI for Acquisition (<a class="external text" href="http://www.sei.cmu.edu/library/abstracts/reports/10tr032.cfm" rel="nofollow">CMMI-ACQ</a>), v1.3 was released in November 2010. It addresses supply chain management, acquisition, and outsourcing processes in government and industry.</li>
<li>CMMI for Services (<a class="external text" href="http://www.sei.cmu.edu/library/abstracts/reports/10tr034.cfm" rel="nofollow">CMMI-SVC</a>), v1.3 was released in November 2010. It addresses guidance for delivering services within an organization and to external customers.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appraisal">Appraisal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Appraisal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An organization cannot be certified in CMMI; instead, an organization is <i>appraised</i>. Depending on the type of appraisal, the organization can be awarded a maturity level rating (1-5) or a capability level achievement profile.</p>
<p>Many organizations find value in measuring their progress by conducting an appraisal. Appraisals are typically conducted for one or more of the following reasons:</p>
<ol>
<li>To determine how well the organization’s processes compare to CMMI best practices, and to identify areas where improvement can be made</li>
<li>To inform external customers and suppliers of how well the organization’s processes compare to CMMI best practices</li>
<li>To meet the contractual requirements of one or more customers</li>
</ol>
<p>Appraisals of organizations using a CMMI model<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> must conform to the requirements defined in the Appraisal Requirements for CMMI (ARC) document. There are three classes of appraisals, A, B and C, which focus on identifying improvement opportunities and comparing the organization’s processes to CMMI best practices. Of these, class A appraisal is the most formal and is the only one that can result in a level rating. Appraisal teams use a CMMI model and ARC-conformant appraisal method to guide their evaluation of the organization and their reporting of conclusions. The appraisal results can then be used (e.g., by a process group) to plan improvements for the organization.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_CMMI_Appraisal_Method_for_Process_Improvement" title="Standard CMMI Appraisal Method for Process Improvement">Standard CMMI Appraisal Method for Process Improvement</a> (SCAMPI) is an appraisal method that meets all of the ARC requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Results of a SCAMPI appraisal may be published (if the appraised organization approves) on the CMMI Web site of the SEI: <a class="external text" href="https://sas.cmmiinstitute.com/pars/" rel="nofollow">Published SCAMPI Appraisal Results</a>. SCAMPI also supports the conduct of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO_15504" title="ISO 15504">ISO/IEC 15504</a>, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_SPICE_project" title="The SPICE project">SPICE</a> (Software Process Improvement and Capability Determination), assessments etc.</p>
<p>This approach promotes that members of the EPG and PATs be trained in the CMMI, that an informal (SCAMPI C) appraisal be performed, and that process areas be prioritized for improvement. More modern approaches, that involve the deployment of commercially available, CMMI-compliant processes, can significantly reduce the time to achieve compliance. SEI has maintained statistics on the "time to move up" for organizations adopting the earlier Software CMM as well as CMMI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These statistics indicate that, since 1987, the median times to move from Level 1 to Level 2 is 23 months, and from Level 2 to Level 3 is an additional 20 months. Since the release of the CMMI, the median times to move from Level 1 to Level 2 is 5 months, with median movement to Level 3 another 21 months. These statistics are updated and published every six months in a maturity profile.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Software Engineering Institute’s (SEI) Team Software Process methodology and the use of CMMI models can be used to raise the maturity level. A new product called Accelerated Improvement Method<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (AIM) combines the use of CMMI and the TSP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI_Security_Guides">CMMI Security Guides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: CMMI Security Guides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To address user security concerns, two unofficial security guides are available. <i>Considering the Case for Security Content in CMMI for Services</i> has one process area, Security Management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Security by Design with CMMI for Development, Version 1.3</i> has the following process areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>OPSD - Organizational Preparedness for Secure Development</li>
<li>SMP - Secure Management in Projects</li>
<li>SRTS - Security Requirements and Technical Solution</li>
<li>SVV - Security Verification and Validation</li>
</ul>
<p>While they do not affect maturity or capability levels, these process areas can be reported in appraisal results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SEI published that 60 organizations measured increases of performance in the categories of cost, schedule, productivity, quality and customer satisfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The median increase in performance varied between 14% (customer satisfaction) and 62% (productivity). However, the CMMI model mostly deals with <i>what</i> processes should be implemented, and not so much with <i>how</i> they can be implemented. These results do not guarantee that applying CMMI will increase performance in every organization. A small company with few resources may be less likely to benefit from CMMI; this view is supported by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.sei.cmu.edu/library/assets/2005sepCMMI.pdf" rel="nofollow">process maturity profile</a> (page 10). Of the small organizations (&lt;25 employees), 70.5% are assessed at level 2: Managed, while 52.8% of the organizations with 1001–2000 employees are rated at the highest level (5: Optimizing).</p>
<p>Interestingly, Turner &amp; Jain (2002) argue that although it is obvious there are large differences between CMMI and <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile methods</a>, both approaches have much in common. They believe neither way is the 'right' way to develop software, but that there are phases in a project where one of the two is better suited. They suggest one should combine the different fragments of the methods into a new hybrid method. Sutherland et al. (2007) assert that a combination of <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a> and CMMI brings more adaptability and predictability than either one alone. David J. Anderson (2005) gives hints on how to interpret CMMI in an agile manner.</p>
<p>CMMI Roadmaps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> which are a goal-driven approach to selecting and deploying relevant process areas from the CMMI-DEV model, can provide guidance and focus for effective CMMI adoption. There are several CMMI roadmaps for the continuous representation, each with a specific set of improvement goals. Examples are the CMMI Project Roadmap,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> CMMI Product and Product Integration Roadmaps <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and the CMMI Process and Measurements Roadmaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> These roadmaps combine the strengths of both the staged and the continuous representations.</p>
<p>The combination of the project management technique <a href="/wiki/Earned_value_management" title="Earned value management">earned value management</a> (EVM) with CMMI has been described (<a class="external text" href="http://www.sei.cmu.edu/library/abstracts/reports/02tn016.cfm" rel="nofollow">Solomon, 2002</a>). To conclude with a similar use of CMMI, Extreme Programming (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_Programming" title="Extreme Programming">XP</a>), a software engineering method, has been evaluated with CMM/CMMI (Nawrocki et al., 2002). For example, the XP requirements management approach, which relies on oral communication, was evaluated as not compliant with CMMI.</p>
<p>CMMI can be appraised using two different approaches: staged and continuous. The staged approach yields appraisal results as one of five <i>maturity levels.</i> The continuous approach yields one of four <i>capability levels.</i> The differences in these approaches are felt only in the appraisal; the best practices are equivalent and result in equivalent process improvement results.</p>
