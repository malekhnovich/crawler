<p><b>Niklaus Emil Wirth</b> (born 15 February 1934) is a <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer scientist</a>, best known for designing several <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>, and for pioneering several classic topics in <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>. In 1984 he won the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a>, generally recognized as the highest distinction in computer science,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> for developing a sequence of innovative computer languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niklaus_Wirth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wirth was born in <a href="/wiki/Winterthur" title="Winterthur">Winterthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, in 1934. In 1959 he earned a degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronics_Engineering" title="Electronics Engineering">Electronics Engineering</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Institute_of_Technology_Z%C3%BCrich" title="Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Zürich">Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Zürich</a> (ETH Zürich). In 1960 he earned an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.Sc." title="M.Sc.">M.Sc.</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Laval" title="Université Laval">Université Laval</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Then in 1963 he was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_Engineering_and_Computer_Science" title="Electrical Engineering and Computer Science">Electrical Engineering and Computer Science</a> (EECS) from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, supervised by the computer designer pioneer <a href="/wiki/Harry_Huskey" title="Harry Huskey">Harry Huskey</a>.</p>
<p>From 1963 to 1967 he served as assistant professor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_Science" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a> at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> and again at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zurich" title="University of Zurich">University of Zurich</a>. Then in 1968 he became Professor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informatics_(academic_field)" title="Informatics (academic field)">Informatics</a> at ETH Zürich, taking two one-year sabbaticals at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> (1976–1977 and 1984–1985). Wirth retired in 1999.</p>
<p>In 2004, he was made a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a> "for seminal work in programming languages and algorithms, including Euler, Algol-W, Pascal, Modula, and Oberon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niklaus_Wirth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="649" data-file-width="474" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg/220px-Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg/330px-Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg/440px-Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niklaus_Wirth_large.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Niklaus Wirth, 1969, sitting on a spaghetti chair</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wirth was the chief designer of the <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> <a href="/wiki/Euler_(programming_language)" title="Euler (programming language)">Euler</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algol_W" title="Algol W">Algol W</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modula" title="Modula">Modula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modula-2" title="Modula-2">Modula-2</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oberon_programming_language" title="Oberon programming language">Oberon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oberon-2" title="Oberon-2">Oberon-2</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(programming_language)#Oberon-07" title="Oberon (programming language)">Oberon-07</a>. He was also a major part of the design and implementation team for the <a href="/wiki/Lilith_(computer)" title="Lilith (computer)">Lilith</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oberon_operating_system" title="Oberon operating system">Oberon</a> operating systems, and for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lola_(hardware_description_language)" title="Lola (hardware description language)">Lola</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_hardware" title="Digital hardware">digital hardware</a> design and simulation system. He received the <i><a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a></i> for the development of these languages in 1984 and in 1994 he was inducted as a Fellow of the ACM. He designed the simple programming language <a href="/wiki/PL/0" title="PL/0">PL/0</a> to illustrate <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a> design. It has formed the basis for many university compiler design classes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_publications">Notable publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niklaus_Wirth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His book, written jointly with Kathleen Jensen, <a class="external text" href="http://www.amazon.com/Pascal-User-Manual-Report-Standard/dp/0387976493" rel="nofollow"><i>The Pascal User Manual And Report</i></a>, served as the basis of many language implementation efforts in the 1970s and 1980s in the United States and across Europe.</p>
<p>His article <a class="external text" href="http://sunnyday.mit.edu/16.355/wirth-refinement.html" rel="nofollow"><i>Program Development by Stepwise Refinement</i></a>, about the teaching of programming, is considered to be a classic text in <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>. In 1975 he wrote the book <i><a href="/wiki/Algorithms_%2B_Data_Structures_%3D_Programs" title="Algorithms + Data Structures = Programs">Algorithms + Data Structures = Programs</a></i>, which gained wide recognition. Major revisions of this book with the new title <i>Algorithms + Data Structures</i> were published in 1985 and 2004. The examples in the first edition were written in Pascal. These were replaced in the later editions with examples written in Modula-2 and Oberon respectively.</p>
<p>His textbook <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Systematic_Programming:_An_Introduction&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Systematic Programming: An Introduction (page does not exist)">Systematic Programming: An Introduction</a></i> was considered a good source for students who wanted to do more than "just coding." Regarded as a challenging text to work through, it was sought as imperative reading for those interested in numerical mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wirth.27s_law">Wirth's law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niklaus_Wirth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wirth's law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wirth%27s_law" title="Wirth's law">Wirth's law</a></div>
<p>In 1995, he popularized the adage now known as <a href="/wiki/Wirth%27s_law" title="Wirth's law">Wirth's law</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">Software</a> is getting slower more rapidly than <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">hardware</a> becomes faster." In his 1995 paper <i>A Plea for Lean Software</i> he attributes it to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Reiser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Martin Reiser (page does not exist)">Martin Reiser</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humor">Humor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niklaus_Wirth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Humor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wirth has reportedly told the joke that, because Europeans pronounce his name properly, while Americans pronounce it as "nickels worth", he is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Call_by_name" title="Call by name">called by name</a> in Europe and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Called_by_value" title="Called by value">called by value</a> in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
