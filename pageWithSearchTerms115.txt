<p><b>Tektronix, Inc.</b> "Tek" is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> company best known for manufacturing test and measurement devices such as <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscopes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logic_analyzers" title="Logic analyzers">logic analyzers</a>, and video and mobile test protocol equipment. Originally an independent company, it is now a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Fortive" title="Fortive">Fortive</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spinoff</a> from <a href="/wiki/Danaher_Corporation" title="Danaher Corporation">Danaher Corporation</a>. Several charities are or were associated with Tektronix, including the Tektronix Foundation and the M.J. Murdock Charitable Trust in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver,_Washington" title="Vancouver, Washington">Vancouver, Washington</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1946.E2.80.931954">1946–1954</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1946–1954">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1016" data-file-width="832" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg/220px-Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg/330px-Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg/440px-Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tektronix_511_The_Oscilloscope_made_Tektronix_Famous.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The company traces its roots to the electronics revolution that immediately followed <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The company’s founders <a href="/wiki/Howard_Vollum" title="Howard Vollum">C. Howard Vollum</a> and Melvin J. "Jack" Murdock invented the world’s first triggered <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a> in 1946, a significant technological breakthrough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ee_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ee-1">[1]</a></sup> This oscilloscope touted by Tektronix was the model 511. The model 511 was a triggering with sweep oscilloscope. The first oscilloscope with a true time-base was the Tektronix Model 513. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The leading oscilloscope manufacturer at the time was <a href="/wiki/DuMont_Laboratories" title="DuMont Laboratories">DuMont Laboratories</a>. DuMont pioneered the frequency-synch trigger and sweep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_DuMont" title="Allen DuMont">Allen DuMont</a> personally tried the 511 at an electronics show and was impressed, but when he saw the price of $795, which was about twice as much as his equivalent model, he told Howard Vollum at the show that they would have a hard time to sell many.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Tektronix was incorporated in 1946 with its headquarters at SE Foster Road and SE 59th Avenue in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1947 there were 12 employees, and 250 in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1950 the company began building a manufacturing facility in <a href="/wiki/Washington_County,_Oregon" title="Washington County, Oregon">Washington County, Oregon</a>, at Barnes Road and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunset_Highway_(Oregon)" title="Sunset Highway (Oregon)">Sunset Highway</a>, and expanded the facility by 1956 to 80,000 square feet (7,000 m<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> The company then moved its headquarters to this site, following an employee vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A detailed story of Howard Vollum and Jack Murdock along with the products that made Tektronix a leading maker of oscilloscopes can be found at the Museum of Vintage Tektronix Equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1955.E2.80.931969">1955–1969</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1955–1969">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1956, a large piece of property in nearby <a href="/wiki/Beaverton,_Oregon" title="Beaverton, Oregon">Beaverton</a> became available, and the company’s employee retirement trust purchased the land and leased it back to the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> Construction began in 1957 and on May 1, 1959 Tektronix moved into its new Beaverton headquarters campus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> on a 313-acre (1.27 km<sup>2</sup>) site which came to be called the Tektronix Industrial Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oreg199408_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oreg199408-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg/220px-Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg/330px-Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg/440px-Tektronix_321_Portable_Transistor_Oscilloscope_1961.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the late 1950s (1957–58), Tektronix set a new trend in oscilloscope applications that would continue into the 1980s. This was the introduction of the plug-in oscilloscope. Started with the 530 and 540 series oscilloscopes, the operator could switch in different horizontal sweep or vertical input plug-ins. This allowed the oscilloscope to be a flexible or adaptable test instrument. Later Tektronix would add in plug-ins to have the scope operate as a spectrum analyzer, waveform sampler, cable tester and transistor curve tracer. The 530 and 540 series also ushered in the delayed trigger, allowing to trigger between a sweep rather than at the beginning. This allows more stable triggering and better waveform reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, Tektronix sold its first (possibly the world's first practical) completely portable oscilloscope, the model 321. This oscilloscope could run on AC line (power mains) or on rechargeable batteries. It also brought the oscilloscope into the transistor age (only a Nuvistor ceramic tube was used for the vertical amp input). A year and a half later the model 321A came out and that was all transistors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 560 series introduced the rectangular CRT to oscilloscopes. In 1964 Tektronix made an oscilloscope breakthrough, the world's first mass-produced analog storage oscilloscope the model 564. <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a> is credited with the first working storage oscilloscope (the model 104D) but it was made in very small numbers and is extremely rare today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1966, Tektronix brought out a line of high frequency full function oscilloscopes called the 400 series. The oscilloscopes were packed with features for field work applications. These scopes were outstanding performers often preferred over their laboratory bench models. The first models were the 422, a 16 MHz bandwidth and the 453, a 50 MHz bandwidth model. The following year the 454, a 150 MHz portable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These models put Tektronix well ahead of their competitors for years. The US Military contracted with Tektronix for a model 453 "ruggedized" for field servicing. The 400 series models would continue to be popular choices in the 1970s and 80's. In addition the styling of the 400 series would be copied by Tektronix's competitors. 400 series oscilloscopes are still being used currently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970.E2.80.931985">1970–1985</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970–1985">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oscilloscope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="590" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Oscilloscope.jpg/220px-Oscilloscope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Oscilloscope.jpg/330px-Oscilloscope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Oscilloscope.jpg/440px-Oscilloscope.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oscilloscope.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Tektronix model 475A portable analog oscilloscope, a very typical instrument of the late 1970s.</div>

<p>The company's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initial_Public_Offering" title="Initial Public Offering">IPO</a>, when it publicly sold its first shares of <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a>, was on September 11, 1963.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1974, the company acquired 256 acres (1.0 km<sup>2</sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Wilsonville,_Oregon" title="Wilsonville, Oregon">Wilsonville, Oregon</a> where it built a facility for its imaging group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1976, the company employed nearly 10,000, and was the state’s largest employer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argus_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-argus-5">[5]</a></sup> Tektronix's 1956 expansion and, in 1962, <a href="/wiki/Electro_Scientific_Industries" title="Electro Scientific Industries">Electro Scientific Industries</a>' similar move to Washington County and expansion are credited with fostering the development of a large high-tech industry in Washington County, a number of firms which collectively are often referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Silicon_Forest" title="Silicon Forest">Silicon Forest</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oreg199408_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oreg199408-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>For many years, Tektronix was the major electronics manufacturer in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, and in 1981, its U.S. payroll peaked at over 24,000 employees. Tektronix also had operations in Europe, South America and Asia. European factories were located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Peter%27s,_Guernsey" title="Saint Peter's, Guernsey">Saint Peter's, Guernsey</a> (then in the <a href="/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association" title="European Free Trade Association">European Free Trade Association</a>) until 1990,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Hoddesdon (North London, UK) and <a href="/wiki/Heerenveen" title="Heerenveen">Heerenveen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> (then in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Community" title="European Community">European Common Market</a>). Some oscilloscopes marketed in Europe and the UK were sold under the brand name Telequipment but many in the UK used the Tektronix brand name in the 1960s and 70s.</p>
<p>For many years, Tektronix operated in Japan as Sony-Tektronix, a 50-50 joint venture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Corporation" title="Sony Corporation">Sony Corporation</a> and Tektronix, Inc; this was due to Japanese trade restrictions at the time. Since then, Tektronix has bought out Sony's share and is now the sole owner of the Japanese operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Under the Sony-Tektronix name, the 300 series oscilloscopes were light weight and totally portable. They replaced the model 321/321A oscilloscopes. Examples of the Sony/Tektronix models were 314, 323, 335 and 370.</p>
<p>During the early 1970s, Tektronix made a major design change to their oscilloscopes. The introduction of the 5000 and 7000 series oscilloscopes. These oscilloscopes maintained the plug-in capabilities that originally started with the 530 and 540 series however the choice of plug-ins was even greater. These scopes used custom designed integrated circuits fabricated by Tektronix. The CRT's were all rectangular and were all fabricated by Tektronix. These oscilloscopes provided on screen controls setting. The 5000 series was the general purpose line while the 7000 series were capable of a wide variety of applications and could accept as many as 4 plug-ins. One model the 7104 (introduced 1978) was a true 1 GHz bandwidth oscilloscope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning with the firm's first cathode ray <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscopes</a>, Tektronix has enjoyed a leading position in the test and measurement market. Although its equipment was expensive, it had performance, quality, and stability. Most test equipment manufacturers built their oscilloscopes with off-the-shelf, generally available components. But Tektronix, in order to gain an extra measure of performance, used many custom-designed or specially-selected components. They even had their own factory for making ultra-bright and sharp CRT tubes. Later on, they built their own <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> manufacturing facility in order to make custom ICs for their equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Tektronix instruments contributed significantly to the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computers" title="Computers">computers</a>, test, and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">communications</a> equipment and to the advancement of <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-technology" title="High-technology">high-technology</a> <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> industry generally.</p>
<p>Tektronix as time went on fabricated more and more of their electronic parts. This led to very specialized skills and talents which in time led to employees forming new businesses. Some former Tektronix employees left to create other successful "Silicon Forest" companies. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_out" title="Spin out">Spin-offs</a> include <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planar_Systems" title="Planar Systems">Planar Systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floating_Point_Systems" title="Floating Point Systems">Floating Point Systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Microtech" title="Cascade Microtech">Cascade Microtech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merix_Corporation" title="Merix Corporation">Merix Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthro_Corporation" title="Anthro Corporation">Anthro Corporation</a> and Northwest Instrument Systems (NWIS) - later renamed to MicroCase. Even some of the spin-offs have created spin-offs, such as <a href="/wiki/InFocus" title="InFocus">InFocus</a>. As Tektronix fabricated more specialized parts, they spread out their product base to include logic analyzers, digital multimeters and signal generators. The TM500 and TM5000 rack mount series was born featuring custom designed test instruments chosen by the buyer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1986.E2.80.932006">1986–2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1986–2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Tektronix faces big challenges to its business structure. In the 1980s, Tektronix found itself distracted with too many divisions in too many markets. This led to decreasing earnings in almost every quarter. A period of <a href="/wiki/Layoff" title="Layoff">layoffs</a>, top management changes and sell-offs followed. In 1994, Tektronix spun off its <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit board</a> manufacturing operation as a separate company, Merix Corp., headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Forest_Grove,_Oregon" title="Forest Grove, Oregon">Forest Grove, Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Eventually, Tektronix was left with its original test and measurement equipment. Upon his promotion in 2000, the current CEO, Richard H. "Rick" Wills, carefully limited corporate spending in the face of the collapsing <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">high-tech bubble</a>. This led the way for Tektronix to emerge as one of the largest companies in its product niche, with a <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> of $3 billion as of April 2006<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>. However, this failed to prevent it from becoming an acquisition target, and Tektronix was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Danaher_Corporation" title="Danaher Corporation">Danaher Corporation</a> in 2007.</p>
<p>Major Product Changes—Digital Sampling Scopes. The advancement in signal sampling techniques and digital processing, oscilloscope manufacturers found a new horizon in the market. The ability to sample the signal and digitize it for real time viewing or digitally store it for future use and maintain the integrity of the waveform. In addition a computer can be integrated with the scope to store many waveforms or instruct the scope to do further analysis. Color enhanced waveforms can be produced for ease in identification.</p>
<p>Tektronix was heavily involved with designing digital sampling oscilloscopes. In the mid-1980s, they quickly replaced their analog oscilloscopes. Their 400, 5000 and 7000 series oscilloscopes were replaced with a new generation of digital oscilloscopes with storage capability, the 11000 and TDS series. The 11000 series were large rack mount laboratory models with large a flat CRT face and had touch screen, multiple color, and multiwaveform display capability. They were still plug-in units and could accept the older 7000 series 7- plug-ins and the new 11000 series 11A- plug-ins. The TDS series replaced the 300 and 400 series portable line. They had the same panel layout but with enhanced storage and measuring capabilities. During this period Tektronix would also expand its test equipment line to logic analyzers, signal generators etc.</p>
<p>By the mid 1990s the use of the CRT was dropped and Tektronix started using LCD panels for display. The 11000 series would be replaced by the MSO (Mixed Signal Oscilloscope) which featured a color active matrix LCD. The TDS continued but with LCD panels starting with the TDS-210. In the TDS models, the lower priced models replaced the last of the 2000 series analog scopes and featured monochrome display while the higher end models were color LCD models which were more like the older 400 series scopes in performance. Spinoffs of the TDS was the TBS storage scope series. Later Tektronix would replace the 200 mini oscilloscopes with the TH series hand held digital oscilloscopes. All TDS and spinoff series with LCD display are totally portable (light weight and can run AC or on batteries).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007_to_present">2007 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tektronix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2007 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 21, 2007, Tektronix was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Danaher_Corporation" title="Danaher Corporation">Danaher Corporation</a> for $2.85 billion. Prior to the acquisition, Tektronix traded on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> under the symbol <i>TEK</i>, the nickname by which Tektronix is known to its employees, customers, and neighbors. On October 15, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Danaher_Corporation" title="Danaher Corporation">Danaher Corporation</a> tendered an offer to acquire Tektronix for $38 cash a share, which equated to a valuation of approximately $2.8 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The deal closed five and a half weeks later, with 90 percent of TEK shares being sold in the tender offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Also, as part of its acquisition by Danaher, the Communications Business division of Tektronix was spun off into a separate business entity under Danaher, Tektronix Communications.</p>
<p>The digital oscilloscope line that was introduced in the 1990s (MSO, TDS, TH series) are still being manufactured in some form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 1, 2016, Tektronix introduced a new logo design, replacing a logo that had been in use since 1992, and indicated a shift in strategy to offer measurement products tailored for specific fields such as computing, communications and automotive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Danaher spun-off several subsidiaries, including Tektronix, in 2016 to create Fortive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
