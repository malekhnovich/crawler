<p><b>Agile software development</b> describes a set of principles for <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> under which requirements and solutions evolve through the collaborative effort of self-organizing <a href="/wiki/Cross-functional_team" title="Cross-functional team">cross-functional teams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collier_2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collier_2011-1">[1]</a></sup> It advocates adaptive planning, evolutionary development, early delivery, and continuous improvement, and it encourages rapid and flexible response to change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These principles support the definition and continuing evolution of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_development_methodologies" title="Software development methodologies">software development methods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rajeshkumar_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rajeshkumar-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <i>agile</i> was first coined for this in 2001, in the <i>Manifesto for Agile Software Development (</i>Agile Manifesto<i>),</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agile_Manifesto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agile_Manifesto-4">[4]</a></sup> and although originally abbreviated as <i>Agile</i> (with a capital <i>A</i>) this is progressively becoming deprecated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Incremental software development methods can be traced back to 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craig2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-craig2003-6">[6]</a></sup> Evolutionary project management<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EvolutionaryProjectManagement_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EvolutionaryProjectManagement-7">[7]</a></sup> and adaptive software development<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edmonds1974_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-edmonds1974-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> emerged in the early 1970s. During the 1990s, a number of <i>lightweight</i> software development methods evolved in reaction to the prevailing <i>heavyweight</i> methods that critics described as heavily regulated, regimented, and <a href="/wiki/Micromanagement" title="Micromanagement">micro-managed</a>. These included: from 1991, <a href="/wiki/Rapid_application_development" title="Rapid application development">rapid application development</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> from 1994, <a href="/wiki/Unified_Process" title="Unified Process">unified process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_systems_development_method" title="Dynamic systems development method">dynamic systems development method</a> (DSDM); from 1995, <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a>; from 1996, <a href="/wiki/Crystal_Clear_(software_development)" title="Crystal Clear (software development)">Crystal Clear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a> (XP); and from 1997, <a href="/wiki/Feature-driven_development" title="Feature-driven development">feature-driven development</a>. Although these originated before the publication of the Agile Manifesto in 2001, they are now collectively referred to as agile methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rajeshkumar_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-rajeshkumar-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Agile_Manifesto">The Agile Manifesto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Agile Manifesto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2001, 17 software developers met at the <a href="/wiki/Snowbird,_Utah" title="Snowbird, Utah">Snowbird</a> resort in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> to discuss lightweight development methods. They published the <i>Manifesto for Agile Software Development</i>, in which they said that by "uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it," they had come to value "<i>individuals and interactions</i> over processes and tools, <i>working software</i> over comprehensive documentation, <i>customer collaboration</i> over contract negotiation, and <i>responding to change</i> over following a plan"; while the secondary concerns were important the primary concerns were more critical to success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agile_Manifesto_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Agile_Manifesto-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By these terms, they meant:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abmmw_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-abmmw-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Individuals and interactions</b></i>: self-organization and motivation are important, as are interactions like <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">co-location</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pair_programming" title="Pair programming">pair programming</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Working software</b></i>: working software is more useful and welcome than just presenting documents to clients in meetings.</li>
<li><i><b>Customer collaboration</b></i>: requirements cannot be fully collected at the beginning of the software development cycle, therefore continuous customer or stakeholder involvement is very important.</li>
<li><i><b>Responding to change</b></i>: agile methods are focused on quick responses to change and continuous development.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some of the authors formed the Agile Alliance, a non-profit organization that promotes software development according to the manifesto's values and principles. Introducing the manifesto on behalf of the Agile Alliance, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Highsmith" title="Jim Highsmith">Jim Highsmith</a> said,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Agile movement is not anti-methodology, in fact many of us want to restore credibility to the word methodology. We want to restore a balance. We embrace modeling, but not in order to file some diagram in a dusty corporate repository. We embrace documentation, but not hundreds of pages of never-maintained and rarely-used tomes. We plan, but recognize the limits of planning in a turbulent environment. Those who would brand proponents of XP or SCRUM or any of the other Agile Methodologies as "hackers" are ignorant of both the methodologies and the original definition of the term hacker.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Jim Highsmith, History: The Agile Manifesto<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agile_principles">Agile principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agile principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Agile Manifesto is based on twelve principles:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manifestoprinciples_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-manifestoprinciples-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Customer satisfaction by early and continuous delivery of valuable software</li>
<li>Welcome changing requirements, even in late development</li>
<li>Working software is delivered frequently (weeks rather than months)</li>
<li>Close, daily cooperation between business people and developers</li>
<li>Projects are built around motivated individuals, who should be trusted</li>
<li>Face-to-face conversation is the best form of communication (co-location)</li>
<li>Working software is the principal measure of progress</li>
<li>Sustainable development, able to maintain a constant pace</li>
<li>Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design</li>
<li>Simplicity—the art of maximizing the amount of work not done—is essential</li>
<li>Best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams</li>
<li>Regularly, the team reflects on how to become more effective, and adjusts accordingly</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutions">Evolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Later, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Schwaber" title="Ken Schwaber">Ken Schwaber</a> with others founded the Scrum Alliance and created the Certified Scrum Master programs and its derivatives. Schwaber left the Scrum Alliance in the fall of 2009, and founded Scrum.org.</p>
<p>In 2005, a group headed by <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Cockburn" title="Alistair Cockburn">Alistair Cockburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Highsmith" title="Jim Highsmith">Jim Highsmith</a> wrote an addendum of <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a> principles, the <a href="/wiki/PM_Declaration_of_Interdependence" title="PM Declaration of Interdependence">Declaration of Interdependence</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> to guide software project management according to agile software development methods.</p>
<p>In 2009, a movement by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cecil_Martin" title="Robert Cecil Martin">Robert C Martin</a> wrote an extension of <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> principles, the <a href="/wiki/Software_craftsmanship" title="Software craftsmanship">Software Craftsmanship Manifesto</a>, to guide agile software development according to <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professional</a> conduct and mastery.</p>
<p>In 2011 the original Agile Alliance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> created the Guide to Agile Practices, an evolving open-source compendium of the working definitions of agile practices, terms, and elements, along with interpretations and experience guidelines from the world-wide community of agile practitioners.</p>
<p>Related disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a> (<a href="/wiki/PRINCE2" title="PRINCE2">PRINCE2</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Institute" title="Project Management Institute">PMI</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Business_analysis" title="Business analysis">business analysis</a> (<a href="/wiki/International_Institute_of_Business_Analysis" title="International Institute of Business Analysis">IIBA</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>) have extended or updated their <a href="/wiki/Body_of_knowledge" title="Body of knowledge">bodies of knowledge</a> and certifications to embrace working with agile methods.</p>

<p>There are many specific agile development methods. Most promote teamwork, collaboration, and process adaptability throughout the product development life-cycle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iterative.2C_incremental_and_evolutionary">Iterative, incremental and evolutionary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Iterative, incremental and evolutionary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most agile development methods break product development work into small increments that minimize the amount of up-front planning and design. Iterations are short time frames (<a href="/wiki/Timeboxing" title="Timeboxing">timeboxes</a>) that typically last from one to four weeks. Each iteration involves a <a href="/wiki/Cross-functional_team" title="Cross-functional team">cross-functional team</a> working in all functions: <a href="/wiki/Project_planning" title="Project planning">planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Requirements_analysis" title="Requirements analysis">analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">coding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unit_testing" title="Unit testing">unit testing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acceptance_testing" title="Acceptance testing">acceptance testing</a>. At the end of the iteration a working product is demonstrated to stakeholders. This minimizes overall risk and allows the product to adapt to changes quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-20">[20]</a></sup> An iteration might not add enough functionality to warrant a market release, but the goal is to have an available release (with minimal <a href="/wiki/Software_bug" title="Software bug">bugs</a>) at the end of each iteration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-embracing_change_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-embracing_change-21">[21]</a></sup> Multiple iterations might be required to release a product or new features.</p>
<p>Working software is the primary measure of progress.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficient_and_face-to-face_communication">Efficient and face-to-face communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Efficient and face-to-face communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No matter which development method is followed, every team should include a <a href="/wiki/Customer_representative" title="Customer representative">customer representative</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)#Product_Owner" title="Scrum (software development)">product owner</a></i> in Scrum). This person is agreed by stakeholders to act on their behalf<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and makes a personal commitment to being available for developers to answer questions throughout the iteration. At the end of each iteration, stakeholders and the customer representative review progress and re-evaluate priorities with a view to optimizing the <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_return" title="Rate of return">return on investment</a> (ROI) and ensuring alignment with customer needs and company goals.</p>
<p>In agile software development, an <i>information radiator</i> is a (normally large) physical display located prominently near the development team, where passers-by can see it. It presents an up-to-date summary of the product development status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cockburn.2C_Information_radiator_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cockburn.2C_Information_radiator-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ambler_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ambler-24">[24]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Build_light_indicator" title="Build light indicator">build light indicator</a> may also be used to inform a team about the current status of their product development.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very_short_feedback_loop_and_adaptation_cycle">Very short feedback loop and adaptation cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Very short feedback loop and adaptation cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common characteristic in agile development is the <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_meeting" title="Stand-up meeting">daily stand-up</a> (also known as the <i>daily scrum)</i>. In a brief session, team members report to each other what they did the previous day toward their team's iteration goal, what they intend to do today toward the goal, and any roadblocks or impediments they can see to the goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_focus">Quality focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Quality focus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Specific tools and techniques, such as <a href="/wiki/Continuous_integration" title="Continuous integration">continuous integration</a>, automated <a href="/wiki/Unit_testing" title="Unit testing">unit testing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pair_programming" title="Pair programming">pair programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Test-driven_development" title="Test-driven development">test-driven development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_design_pattern" title="Software design pattern">design patterns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domain-driven_design" title="Domain-driven design">domain-driven design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Code_refactoring" title="Code refactoring">code refactoring</a> and other techniques are often used to improve quality and enhance product development agility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compared to traditional software engineering, agile software development mainly targets complex systems and product development with dynamic, non-deterministic and non-linear characteristics, where accurate estimates, stable plans, and predictions are often hard to get in early stages—and big up-front designs and arrangements would probably cause a lot of waste, i.e., are not economically sound. These basic arguments and previous industry experiences, learned from years of successes and failures, have helped shape agile development's favor of adaptive, iterative and evolutionary development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larman2004_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larman2004-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptive_vs._predictive">Adaptive vs. predictive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Adaptive vs. predictive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Development methods exist on a continuum from <i>adaptive</i> to <i>predictive</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boehm2004App_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-boehm2004App-28">[28]</a></sup> Agile methods lie on the <i>adaptive</i> side of this continuum. One key of adaptive development methods is a "<a href="/wiki/Rolling_Wave_planning" title="Rolling Wave planning">Rolling Wave</a>" approach to schedule planning, which identifies milestones but leaves flexibility in the path to reach them, and also allows for the milestones themselves to change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Adaptive methods focus on adapting quickly to changing realities. When the needs of a project change, an adaptive team changes as well. An adaptive team has difficulty describing exactly what will happen in the future. The further away a date is, the more vague an adaptive method is about what will happen on that date. An adaptive team cannot report exactly what tasks they will do next week, but only which features they plan for next month. When asked about a release six months from now, an adaptive team might be able to report only the mission statement for the release, or a statement of expected value vs. cost.</p>
<p><i>Predictive</i> methods, in contrast, focus on analysing and planning the future in detail and cater for known risks. In the extremes, a predictive team can report exactly what features and tasks are planned for the entire length of the development process. Predictive methods rely on effective early phase analysis and if this goes very wrong, the project may have difficulty changing direction. Predictive teams often institute a <a href="/wiki/Change_control_board" title="Change control board">change control board</a> to ensure they consider only the most valuable changes.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Risk_analysis_(engineering)" title="Risk analysis (engineering)">Risk analysis</a> can be used to choose between adaptive (<i>agile</i> or <i>value-driven</i>) and predictive (<i>plan-driven</i>) methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMBridgeToAgility_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMBridgeToAgility-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Barry_Boehm" title="Barry Boehm">Barry Boehm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Turner_(software)" title="Richard Turner (software)">Richard Turner</a> suggest that each side of the continuum has its own <i>home ground</i>, as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boehm2004_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-boehm2004-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iterative_vs._waterfall">Iterative vs. waterfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iterative vs. waterfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the differences between agile and waterfall is the approach to quality and testing. In the <a href="/wiki/Waterfall_model" title="Waterfall model">waterfall model</a>, there is always a separate <i>testing phase</i> after a <i>build phase</i>; however, in agile development testing is completed in the same iteration as programming.</p>
<p>Because testing is done in every iteration—which develops a small piece of the software—users can frequently use those new pieces of software and validate the value.</p>
<p>After the users know the real value of the updated piece of software, they can make better decisions about the software's future. Having a value retrospective and software re-planning session in each iteration—<a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a> typically has iterations of just two weeks—helps the team continuously adapt its plans so as to maximize the value it delivers.</p>
<p>This iterative approach supports a <i>product</i> rather than a <i>project</i> mindset. This provides greater flexibility throughout the development process; whereas on projects the requirements are defined and locked down from the very beginning, making it difficult to change them later. Iterative product development allows the software to evolve in response to changes in business environment or market requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the short iteration style of agile software development, it also has strong connections with the <a href="/wiki/Lean_startup" title="Lean startup">lean startup</a> concept.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Code_vs._documentation">Code vs. documentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Code vs. documentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a letter to <i><a href="/wiki/Computer_(magazine)" title="Computer (magazine)">IEEE Computer</a></i>, Steven Rakitin expressed cynicism about agile development, calling it "yet another attempt to undermine the discipline of software engineering" and translating "Working software over comprehensive documentation" as "We want to spend all our time coding. Remember, real programmers don't write documentation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rakitin2001_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-rakitin2001-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>This is disputed by proponents of agile software development, who state that developers should write documentation if that's the best way to achieve the relevant goals, but that there are often better ways to achieve those goals than writing static documentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agiledoc_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-agiledoc-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Scott_Ambler" title="Scott Ambler">Scott Ambler</a> states that documentation should be "Just Barely Good Enough" (JBGE),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> that too much or comprehensive documentation would usually cause waste, and developers rarely trust detailed documentation because it's usually out of sync with code,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agiledoc_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-agiledoc-34">[34]</a></sup> while too little documentation may also cause problems for maintenance, communication, learning and knowledge sharing. <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Cockburn" title="Alistair Cockburn">Alistair Cockburn</a> wrote of the <i>Crystal Clear</i> method:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Crystal considers development a series of co-operative games, and intends that the documentation is enough to help the next win at the next game. The work products for Crystal include use cases, risk list, iteration plan, core domain models, and design notes to inform on choices...however there are no templates for these documents and descriptions are necessarily vague, but the objective is clear, <b>just enough documentation</b> for the next game. I always tend to characterize this to my team as: what would you want to know if you joined the team tomorrow.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Alistair Cockburn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agile_methods">Agile methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Agile methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Agile methods support a broad range of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_development_life_cycle" title="Software development life cycle">software development life cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abrahamsson2002_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abrahamsson2002-37">[37]</a></sup> Some focus on the practices (e.g., XP, pragmatic programming, agile modeling), while some focus on managing the flow of work (e.g., Scrum, Kanban). Some support activities for requirements specification and development (e.g., FDD), while some seek to cover the full development life cycle (e.g., DSDM, <a href="/wiki/Rational_Unified_Process" title="Rational Unified Process">RUP</a>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_tailoring">Method tailoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Method tailoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the literature, different terms refer to the notion of method adaptation, including 'method tailoring', 'method fragment adaptation' and 'situational method engineering'. Method tailoring is defined as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A process or capability in which human agents determine a system development approach for a specific project situation through responsive changes in, and dynamic interplays between contexts, intentions, and method fragments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aydin2004_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aydin2004-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Potentially, almost all agile methods are suitable for method tailoring. Even the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_Systems_Development_Method" title="Dynamic Systems Development Method">DSDM</a> method is being used for this purpose and has been successfully tailored in a <a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model" title="Capability Maturity Model">CMM</a> context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abrahamsson2003_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abrahamsson2003-41">[41]</a></sup> Situation-appropriateness can be considered as a distinguishing characteristic between agile methods and traditional software development methods, with the latter being relatively much more rigid and prescriptive. The practical implication is that agile methods allow product development teams to adapt working practices according to the needs of individual products. Practices are concrete activities and products that are part of a method framework. At a more extreme level, the philosophy behind the method, consisting of a number of principles, could be adapted (Aydin, 2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aydin2004_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aydin2004-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some approaches, such as Scrum and extreme programming, make the need for method adaptation explicit. With these less-prescriptive frameworks, one of the principles is that no single process fits every product development, but rather that practices should be tailored to the needs of the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abrahamsson2002_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Abrahamsson2002-37">[37]</a></sup> Mehdi Mirakhorli<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> proposes a tailoring practice that provides a sufficient road-map and guidelines for adapting all the practices. <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">RDP Practice</a> is designed for customizing XP. This practice, first proposed as a long research paper in the APSO workshop at the ICSE 2008 conference, is currently the only proposed and applicable method for customizing XP. Although it is specifically a solution for XP, this practice has the capability of extending to other methodologies. At first glance, this practice seems to be in the category of static method adaptation but experiences with RDP Practice says that it can be treated like dynamic method adaptation. The distinction between static method adaptation and dynamic method adaptation is subtle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aydin2005_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aydin2005-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scrum isn't designed for method tailoring. Schwaber notes that "Scrum is not a methodology that needs enhancing. That is how we got into trouble in the first place, thinking that the problem was not having a perfect methodology. Effort centers on the changes in the enterprise that is needed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Bas Vodde reinforces this statement, suggesting that Scrum isn't like traditional, large methodologies that require you to "pick and choose" elements. It is the basics on top of which you add additional elements to localise and contextualise its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_methods">Comparison with other methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Comparison with other methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="RAD">RAD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: RAD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Agile methods have much in common with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_Application_Development" title="Rapid Application Development">Rapid Application Development</a> techniques from the 1980/90s as espoused by <a href="/wiki/James_Martin_(author)" title="James Martin (author)">James Martin</a> and others. In addition to technology-focused methods, customer-and-design-centered methods, such as Visualization-Driven Rapid Prototyping developed by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Willison" title="Brian Willison">Brian Willison</a>, work to engage customers and end users to facilitate agile software development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further, James M. Kerr and Richard Hunter wrote a book on the subject that presented a day-by-day diary of a real RAD development. It covered work right from inception through to production and contains many of the techniques that forge the backbone of, and are very much present in, today's agile product development approaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="CMMI">CMMI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: CMMI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a> (SEI) published the technical report "CMMI or Agile: Why Not Embrace Both"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> to make clear that the <a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model_Integration" title="Capability Maturity Model Integration">Capability Maturity Model Integration</a> and agile methods can co-exist. Modern CMMI-compatible development processes are also iterative. The CMMI Version 1.3 includes tips for implementing CMMI and agile process improvement together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DevOps">DevOps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: DevOps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Organizations that adopt agile development see more frequent releases; which led to the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Continuous_delivery" title="Continuous delivery">continuous delivery</a> and DevOps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/DevOps" title="DevOps">DevOps</a> and agile software development both embody many <a href="/wiki/Lean_software_development" title="Lean software development">Lean</a> philosophies, such as collaboration and communication, they are distinct concepts. While agile principles represent a change in thinking, DevOps seeks to implement actual organizational cultural change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agile_Now_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Agile_Now-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Large-scale.2C_offshore_and_distributed">Large-scale, offshore and distributed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Large-scale, offshore and distributed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Agile development has been widely seen as highly suited to certain types of environments, including small teams of experts working on <a href="/wiki/Greenfield_project" title="Greenfield project">greenfield projects</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boehm2004_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-boehm2004-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck1999_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-beck1999-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:157</sup> and the challenges and limitations encountered in the adoption of agile methods in a large organization with <a href="/wiki/Legacy_system" title="Legacy system">legacy infrastructure</a> are well-documented and understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response, a range of strategies and patterns has evolved for overcoming challenges with large-scale development efforts (&gt;20 developers)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ambler2006_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-ambler2006-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sstc2007_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-sstc2007-56">[56]</a></sup> or distributed (non-colocated) development teams,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BridgingTheDistance_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-BridgingTheDistance-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AgileOffshore_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-AgileOffshore-58">[58]</a></sup> amongst other challenges; and there are now several recognised frameworks that seek to mitigate or avoid these challenges, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scaled_Agile_Framework" title="Scaled Agile Framework">Scaled Agile Framework</a> (SAFe),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Dean Leffingwell <i>inter alia</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Disciplined_agile_delivery" title="Disciplined agile delivery">Disciplined agile delivery</a> (DAD), <a href="/wiki/Scott_Ambler" title="Scott Ambler">Scott Ambler</a> <i>inter alia</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Large-Scale_Scrum_(LeSS)" title="Large-Scale Scrum (LeSS)">Large-scale scrum (LeSS)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craig_Larman" title="Craig Larman">Craig Larman</a> and Bas Vodde</li>
<li>Nexus (scaled professional Scrum),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Schwaber" title="Ken Schwaber">Ken Schwaber</a></li>
<li>Scrum at Scale,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Sutherland" title="Jeff Sutherland">Jeff Sutherland</a>, Alex Brown</li>
<li>Enterprise Scrum,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Mike Beedle</li>
<li>Setchu (Scrum-based lightweight framework),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Michael Ebbage</li>
<li>Xscale<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></li>
<li>Agile path<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>Holistic Software Development <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There are many conflicting viewpoints on whether all of these are effective or indeed fit the definition of agile development, and this remains an active and ongoing area of research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ambler2006_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-ambler2006-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oopsla2002_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-oopsla2002-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>When agile software development is applied in a distributed setting (with teams dispersed across multiple business locations), it is commonly referred to as distributed agile development. The goal is to leverage the unique benefits offered by each approach. Distributed development allow organizations to build software by strategically setting up teams in different parts of the globe, virtually building software round-the-clock (more commonly referred to as follow-the-sun model). On the other hand, agile development provides increased transparency, continuous feedback and more flexibility when responding to changes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulated_domains">Regulated domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Regulated domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Agile methods were initially seen as best suitable for non-critical product developments, thereby excluded from use in regulated domains such as medical devices, pharmaceutical, financial, nuclear systems, automotive, and avionics sectors, etc. However, in the last several years, there have been several initiatives for the adaptation of agile methods for these domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitzgerald2013_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fitzgerald2013-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cawley2010_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cawley2010-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McHugh2014_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-McHugh2014-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are numerous standards that may apply in regulated domains, including <a href="/wiki/ISO_26262" title="ISO 26262">ISO 26262</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_9000" title="ISO 9000">ISO 9000</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO_9001" title="ISO 9001">ISO 9001</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_15504" title="ISO/IEC 15504">ISO/IEC 15504</a>. A number of key concerns are of particular importance in regulated domains which may conflict with the use of agile methods:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitzgerald2013_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fitzgerald2013-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quality_Assurance" title="Quality Assurance">Quality Assurance</a> (QA): Systematic and inherent quality management underpinning a controlled professional process and reliability and correctness of product.</li>
<li>Safety and Security: Formal planning and risk management to mitigate safety risks for users and securely protecting users from unintentional and malicious misuse.</li>
<li>Traceability: Documentation providing auditable evidence of regulatory compliance and facilitating traceability and investigation of problems.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verification_and_Validation" title="Verification and Validation">Verification and Validation</a> (V&amp;V): Embedded throughout the software development process (e.g. user requirements specification, functional specification, design specification, code review, unit tests, integration tests, system tests).</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrum_(development)" title="Scrum (development)">Scrum</a> framework in particular has received considerable attention. Two derived methods have been defined: R-Scrum (Regulated Scrum)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitzgerald2013_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fitzgerald2013-68">[68]</a></sup> and SafeScrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SafeScrum_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-SafeScrum-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agile_coaching">Agile coaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Agile coaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Teams and organizations choosing to adopt more agile ways of working typically undertake an <i>agile transformation</i> with the support of one or more agile coaches. The agile coach guides teams through the transformation. There are typically two styles of agile coaching: push based and <a href="/wiki/Pull-based_agile_coaching" title="Pull-based agile coaching">pull-based agile coaching</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experience_and_adoption">Experience and adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Experience and adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agile methods were first used by technology early adopters such as <a href="/wiki/Tektronix" title="Tektronix">Tektronix</a>. Although agile methods can be used with any programming paradigm or language in practice, they were originally closely associated with object-oriented environments such as Smalltalk and Lisp and later Java. The initial adopters of agile methods were usually small to medium-sized teams working on unprecedented systems with requirements that were difficult to finalize and likely to change as the system was being developed. This section describes common problems that organizations encounter when they try to adopt agile methods as well as various techniques to measure the quality and performance of agile teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_agile_pitfalls">Common agile pitfalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Common agile pitfalls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Organizations and teams implementing agile development often face difficulties transitioning from more traditional methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterfall_development" title="Waterfall development">waterfall development</a>, such as teams having an agile process forced on them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Art_of_Agile_Development_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Art_of_Agile_Development-74">[74]</a></sup> These are often termed <i>agile anti-patterns</i> or more commonly <i>agile smells</i>. Below are some common examples:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_overall_product_design">Lack of overall product design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Lack of overall product design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A goal of agile software development is to focus more on producing working software and less on documentation. This is in contrast to waterfall models where the process is often highly controlled and minor changes to the system require significant revision of supporting documentation. However, this does not justify completely doing without any analysis or design at all. Failure to pay attention to design can cause a team to proceed rapidly at first but then to have significant rework required as they attempt to scale up the system. One of the key features of agile software development is that it is iterative. When done correctly design emerges as the system is developed and commonalities and opportunities for re-use are discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adding_stories_to_an_iteration_in_progress">Adding stories to an iteration in progress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Adding stories to an iteration in progress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In agile software development, <i>stories (</i>similar to <a href="/wiki/Use_case" title="Use case">use case</a> descriptions) are typically used to define requirements and an <i>iteration</i> is a short period of time during which the team commits to specific goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Adding stories to an iteration in progress is detrimental to a good flow of work. These should be added to the product backlog and prioritized for a subsequent iteration or in rare cases the iteration could be cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-axisagile.com.au_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-axisagile.com.au-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>This does not mean that a story cannot expand. Teams must deal with new information, which may produce additional tasks for a story. If the new information prevents the story from being completed during the iteration, then it should be carried over to a subsequent iteration. However, it should be prioritized against all remaining stories, as the new information may have changed the story's original priority.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_sponsor_support">Lack of sponsor support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Lack of sponsor support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Agile software development is often implemented as a grassroots effort in organizations by software development teams trying to optimize their development processes and ensure consistency in the software development life cycle. By not having sponsor support, teams may face difficulties and resistance from business partners, other development teams and management. Additionally, they may suffer without appropriate funding and resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> This increases the likelihood of failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Insufficient_training">Insufficient training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Insufficient training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A survey performed by Version One found respondents cited insufficient training as the most significant cause for failed agile implementations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Teams have fallen into the trap of assuming the reduced processes of agile development compared to other methodologies such as waterfall means that there are no actual rules for agile development. Agile development is a set of prescribed methodologies, and training/practice is a requirement.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_owner_role_is_not_properly_filled">Product owner role is not properly filled</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Product owner role is not properly filled">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_owner" title="Product owner">product owner</a> is responsible for representing the business in the development activity and is often the most demanding role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common mistake is to have the product owner role filled by someone from the development team. This requires the team to make its own decisions on prioritization without real feedback from the business. They try to solve business issues internally or delay work as they reach outside the team for direction. This often leads to distraction and a breakdown in collaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams_are_not_focused">Teams are not focused</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Teams are not focused">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The agile process requires teams to meet product commitments, which means they should focus only on work for that product. However, team members who are seen as have spare capacity are often expected to take on other work, which means it is then difficult for them to help complete the work to which their team had commmited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Excessive_preparation.2Fplanning">Excessive preparation/planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Excessive preparation/planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Teams may fall into the trap of spending too much time preparing or planning. This is a common trap for teams less familiar with the agile process where the teams feel obligated to have a complete understanding and specification of all stories. Teams should be prepared to move forward only with those stories in which they have confidence, then during the iteration continue discovery (sometimes referred to as refinement or grooming) to help inform work for subsequent iterations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Problem-solving_in_the_daily_standup">Problem-solving in the daily standup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Problem-solving in the daily standup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A daily standup should be a focused, timely meeting where all team members disseminate information. If problem-solving occurs, it often can only involve certain team members and potentially is not the best use of the entire team's time. If during the daily standup the team starts diving into problem-solving, it should be tabled until a sub-team can discuss, usually immediately after the standup completes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Assigning_tasks">Assigning tasks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Assigning tasks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the intended benefits of agile development is to empower the team to make choices, as they are closest to the problem. Additionally, they should make choices as close to implementation as possible, to use more timely information in the decision. If team members are assigned tasks by others or too early in the process, the benefits of localized and timely decision making can be lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Effective_Sprint_Planning_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Effective_Sprint_Planning-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>Being assigned work also constrains team members into certain roles (for example, team member A must always do the database work), which limits opportunities for cross-training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Effective_Sprint_Planning_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-Effective_Sprint_Planning-85">[85]</a></sup> Team members themselves can choose to take on tasks that stretch their abilities and provide cross-training opportunities.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scrum_master_as_a_contributor">Scrum master as a contributor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Scrum master as a contributor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another common pitfall is for a scrum master to act as a contributor. While not prohibited by the Scrum methodology, the scrum master needs to ensure they have the capacity to act in the role of scrum master first and not working on development tasks. A scrum master's role is to facilitate the process rather than create the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agileconnection.com_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-agileconnection.com-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having the scrum master also multitasking may result in too many context switches to be productive. Additionally, as a scrum master is responsible for ensuring roadblocks are removed so that the team can make forward progress, the benefit gained by individual tasks moving forward may not outweigh roadblocks that are deferred due to lack of capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agileconnection.com_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-agileconnection.com-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_test_automation">Lack of test automation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Lack of test automation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Due to the iterative nature of agile development, multiple rounds of testing are often needed. Automated testing helps reduce the impact of repeated unit, integration, and regression tests and frees developers and testers to focus on higher value work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>Test automation also supports continued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refactoring" title="Refactoring">refactoring</a> required by iterative software development. Allowing a developer to quickly run tests to confirm refactoring has not modified the functionality of the application may reduce the workload and increase confidence that cleanup efforts have not introduced new defects.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Allowing_technical_debt_to_build_up">Allowing technical debt to build up</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Allowing technical debt to build up">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Focusing on delivering new functionality may result in increased <a href="/wiki/Technical_debt" title="Technical debt">technical debt</a>. The team must allow themselves time for defect remediation and refactoring. Technical debt hinders planning abilities by increasing the amount of unscheduled work as production defects distract the team from further progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Technical_Debt_.2B_Red_October_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Technical_Debt_.2B_Red_October-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the system evolves it is important to <a href="/wiki/Code_refactoring" title="Code refactoring">refactor</a> as entropy of the system naturally increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Over time the lack of constant maintenance causes increasing defects and development costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Technical_Debt_.2B_Red_October_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Technical_Debt_.2B_Red_October-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempting_to_take_on_too_much_in_an_iteration">Attempting to take on too much in an iteration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Attempting to take on too much in an iteration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A common misconception is that agile development allows continuous change, however an iteration backlog is an agreement of what work can be completed during an iteration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Having too much <a href="/wiki/Work_in_process" title="Work in process">work-in-progress (WIP)</a> results in inefficiencies such as context-switching and queueing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The team must avoid feeling pressured into taking on additional work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_time.2C_resources.2C_scope.2C_and_quality">Fixed time, resources, scope, and quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Fixed time, resources, scope, and quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Agile development fixes time (iteration duration), quality, and ideally resources in advance (though maintaining fixed resources may be difficult if developers are often pulled away from tasks to handle production incidents), while the scope remains variable. The customer or product owner often pushes for a fixed scope for an iteration. However, teams should be reluctant to commit to locked time, resources and scope (commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Project_management_triangle" title="Project management triangle">project management triangle</a>). Efforts to add scope to the fixed time and resources of agile development may result in decreased quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adeptechllc.com_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-adeptechllc.com-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_agility">Measuring agility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Measuring agility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The best agile practitioners have always emphasized sound engineering principles. As a result, there are a number of agile best practices and tools for measuring the performance of product development and teams.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_assessments">Internal assessments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Internal assessments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>Agility Index Measurements</i> (AIM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> scores product development against a number of agility factors to achieve a total. The similarly named <i>Agility Measurement Index</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> scores developments against five dimensions of product development (duration, risk, novelty, effort, and interaction).</p>
<p>Other techniques are based on measurable goals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> and one study suggests that <a href="/wiki/Velocity_(software_development)" title="Velocity (software development)">velocity</a> can be used as a metric of agility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurian_2006_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurian_2006-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also agile self-assessments to determine whether a team is using agile practices (Nokia test,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Karlskrona test,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> 42 points test<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_surveys">Public surveys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Public surveys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the early studies reporting gains in quality, productivity, and business satisfaction by using agile methods was a survey conducted by Shine Technologies from November 2002 to January 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<p>A similar survey, the State of Agile, is conducted every year starting in 2006 with thousands of participants from around the software development community. This tracks trends on the benefits of agile, lessons learned, and good practices. Each survey has reported increasing numbers saying that agile development helps them deliver software faster; improves their ability to manage changing customer priorities; and increases their productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Surveys have also consistently shown better results with agile product development methods compared to classical project management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> In balance, there are reports that some feel that agile development methods are still too young to enable extensive academic research of their success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agile methodologies can be inefficient in large organizations and certain types of developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Agile methods seem best for early-stage non-sequential product development. Many organizations believe that agile methodologies are too extreme and adopt a hybrid approach that mixes elements of agile and plan-driven approaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Some methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_systems_development_method" title="Dynamic systems development method">DSDM</a>, have combined elements of agile and plan-driven approaches in a disciplined way, without sacrificing the fundamental principles that make agile work.</p>
<p>The term "agile" has also been criticized as being a <a href="/wiki/Management_fad" title="Management fad">management fad</a> that simply describes existing good practices under new jargon, promotes a "one size fits all" mindset towards development strategies, and wrongly emphasizes method over results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alistair_Cockburn" title="Alistair Cockburn">Alistair Cockburn</a> organized a celebration of the 10th anniversary of the <a href="#The_Agile_Manifesto">Agile Manifesto</a> in Snowbird, Utah on February 12, 2011, gathering some 30+ people who had been involved at the original meeting and since. A list of about 20 <a href="/wiki/Elephant_in_the_room" title="Elephant in the room">elephants in the room</a> ("undiscussable" agile topics/issues) were collected, including aspects: the alliances, failures and limitations of agile practices and context (possible causes: commercial interests, decontextualization, no obvious way to make progress based on failure, limited objective evidence, cognitive biases and reasoning fallacies), politics and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Kruchten" title="Philippe Kruchten">Philippe Kruchten</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>The agile movement is in some ways a bit like a teenager: very self-conscious, checking constantly its appearance in a mirror, accepting few criticisms, only interested in being with its peers, rejecting en bloc all wisdom from the past, just because it is from the past, adopting fads and new jargon, at times cocky and arrogant. But I have no doubts that it will mature further, become more open to the outside world, more reflective, and also therefore more effective.</i></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_outside_software_development">Applications outside software development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agile_software_development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Applications outside software development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg/220px-Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg/330px-Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg/440px-Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Agile_Brazil_2014_conference_entrance.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Agile Brazil 2014 conference</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Agile methods have been extensively used for development of software products and some of them use certain characteristics of software, such as object technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2007_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2007-110">[110]</a></sup> However, these techniques can be applied to the development of non-software products, such as computers, motor vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> medical devices, food, clothing, and music;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> see <a href="/wiki/Flexible_product_development" title="Flexible product development">Flexible product development</a>. Agile methods have been used in non-development IT infrastructure deployments and migrations. Some of the wider principles of agile have also found application in general management<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> (e.g., strategy, governance, risk, finance) under the terms <a href="/wiki/Business_agility" title="Business agility">business agility</a> or agile business management. Agile principles are also used in marketing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under an agile business management model, agile techniques, practices, principles and values are expressed across five domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Integrated customer engagement – to embed customers within any delivery process to share accountability for product/service delivery.</li>
<li>Facilitation-based management – adopting agile management models, like the role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrum_Master" title="Scrum Master">Scrum Master</a>, to facilitate the day-to-day operation of teams.</li>
<li>Agile work practices – adopting specific iterative and incremental work practices such as <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanban_(development)" title="Kanban (development)">Kanban</a>, <a href="/wiki/Test-driven_development" title="Test-driven development">test-driven development</a> or <a href="/wiki/Feature-driven_development" title="Feature-driven development">feature-driven development</a> across all business functions (from sales, <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a>, finance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">Marketing</a>).</li>
<li>An enabling organisational structure – with a focus on staff <a href="/wiki/Engagement_marketing" title="Engagement marketing">engagement</a>, personal autonomy and outcomes based governance.</li>
<li>An <a class="external text" href="http://agilelearningcenters.org" rel="nofollow">education model</a> that blends agile practices and philosophies to create micro-schools that emphasize collaborative culture creation and self-directed learning.</li>
</ol>
<p>Agile development paradigms can be used in other areas of life such as raising children. Its success in child development might be founded on some basic management principles; communication, adaptation and awareness. Bruce Feiler has claimed that the basic agile development paradigms can be applied to household management and raising children. In his TED Talk "Agile programming – for your family",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> these paradigms brought significant changes to his household environment, such as the kids doing dishes, taking out the trash, and decreasing his children's emotional outbreaks, which inadvertently increased their emotional stability.</p>
