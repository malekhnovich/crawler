<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Photography</h1>

<p><b>Photography</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, application and practice of creating durable <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a> by recording <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, either electronically by means of an <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensor</a>, or chemically by means of a light-sensitive material such as <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typically, a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a> is used to <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focus</a> the light reflected or emitted from objects into a <a href="/wiki/Real_image" title="Real image">real image</a> on the light-sensitive surface inside a <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a> during a timed <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">exposure</a>. With an electronic image sensor, this produces an <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">electrical charge</a> at each <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a>, which is <a href="/wiki/Image_processing" title="Image processing">electronically processed</a> and stored in a <a href="/wiki/Image_file_formats" title="Image file formats">digital image file</a> for subsequent display or processing. The result with <a href="/wiki/Photographic_emulsion" title="Photographic emulsion">photographic emulsion</a> is an invisible <a href="/wiki/Latent_image" title="Latent image">latent image</a>, which is later chemically <a href="/wiki/Photographic_developer" title="Photographic developer">"developed"</a> into a visible image, either <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">negative</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positive_(photography)" title="Positive (photography)">positive</a> depending on the purpose of the photographic material and the method of <a href="/wiki/Photographic_processing" title="Photographic processing">processing</a>. A negative image on film is traditionally used to photographically create a positive image on a paper base, known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_print" title="Photographic print">print</a>, either by using an <a href="/wiki/Enlarger" title="Enlarger">enlarger</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Contact_print" title="Contact print">contact printing</a>.</p>
<p>Photography is employed in many fields of science, manufacturing (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Photolithography" title="Photolithography">photolithography</a>) and business, as well as its more direct uses for art, film and video production, recreational purposes, hobby, and mass communication.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "photography" was created from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> roots φωτός (<i>phōtos</i>), genitive of φῶς (<i>phōs</i>), "light"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and γραφή (<i>graphé</i>) "representation by means of lines" or "drawing",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> together meaning "drawing with light".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several people may have coined the same new term from these roots independently. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hercules_Florence" title="Hercules Florence">Hercules Florence</a>, a French painter and inventor living in Campinas, Brazil, used the French form of the word, <i>photographie</i>, in private notes which a Brazilian historian believes were written in 1834.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_von_Maedler" title="Johann von Maedler">Johann von Maedler</a>, a Berlin astronomer, is credited in a 1932 German history of photography as having used it in an article published on 25 February 1839 in the German newspaper <i>Vossische Zeitung</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Both of these claims are now widely reported but apparently neither has ever been independently confirmed as beyond reasonable doubt. Credit has traditionally been given to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Herschel" title="Sir John Herschel">Sir John Herschel</a> both for coining the word and for introducing it to the public. His uses of it in private correspondence prior to 25 February 1839 and at his <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> lecture on the subject in London on 14 March 1839 have long been amply documented and accepted as settled facts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursor_technologies">Precursor technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precursor technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Camera_obscura_box.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Camera_obscura_box.jpg/220px-Camera_obscura_box.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Camera_obscura_box.jpg/330px-Camera_obscura_box.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Camera_obscura_box.jpg/440px-Camera_obscura_box.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Photography is the result of combining several technical discoveries. Long before the first photographs were made, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Science_and_Technology_in_China" title="History of Science and Technology in China">ancient Han Chinese</a> philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mo_Di" title="Mo Di">Mo Di</a> from the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Names" title="School of Names">Mohist School of Logic</a> was the first to discover and develop the scientific principles of <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">camera obscura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinhole_camera" title="Pinhole camera">pinhole camera</a>. Later Greek mathematicians <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> also independently described a <a href="/wiki/Pinhole_camera" title="Pinhole camera">pinhole camera</a> in the 5th and 4th centuries BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krebs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-krebs-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 6th century CE, Byzantine mathematician <a href="/wiki/Anthemius_of_Tralles" title="Anthemius of Tralles">Anthemius of Tralles</a> used a type of <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">camera obscura</a> in his experiments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Science_and_Technology_in_China" title="History of Science and Technology in China">Han Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a> <a href="/wiki/Shen_Kuo" title="Shen Kuo">Shen Kuo</a> (1031–95) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Ibn al-Haytham</a> (Alhazen) (965–1040) independently invented the <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">camera obscura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinhole_camera" title="Pinhole camera">pinhole camera</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krebs_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-krebs-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wade2001_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wade2001-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> (1193–1280) discovered <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Fabricius" title="Georg Fabricius">Georg Fabricius</a> (1516–71) discovered <a href="/wiki/Silver_chloride" title="Silver chloride">silver chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shen_Kuo" title="Shen Kuo">Shen Kuo</a> explains the science of camera obscura and optical physics in his scientific work <a href="/wiki/Dream_Pool_Essays" title="Dream Pool Essays">Dream Pool Essays</a> while the techniques described in <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Ibn al-Haytham</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a> are capable of producing primitive photographs using medieval materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daniele_Barbaro" title="Daniele Barbaro">Daniele Barbaro</a> described a <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(optics)" title="Diaphragm (optics)">diaphragm</a> in 1566.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Homberg" title="Wilhelm Homberg">Wilhelm Homberg</a> described how light darkened some chemicals (photochemical effect) in 1694.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The fiction book <i><a href="/wiki/Giphantie" title="Giphantie">Giphantie</a></i>, published in 1760, by French author <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiphaigne_de_la_Roche" title="Tiphaigne de la Roche">Tiphaigne de la Roche</a>, described what can be interpreted as photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The discovery of the camera obscura that provides an image of a scene dates back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Science_and_Technology_in_China" title="History of Science and Technology in China">ancient China</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> mentions natural camerae obscurae that are formed by dark caves on the edge of a sunlit valley. A hole in the cave wall will act as a pinhole camera and project a laterally reversed, upside down image on a piece of paper. So the birth of photography was primarily concerned with inventing means to capture and keep the image produced by the camera obscura.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> painters used the camera obscura which, in fact, gives the optical rendering in color that dominates Western Art. The camera obscura literally means "dark chamber" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. It is a box with a hole in it which allows light to go through and create an image onto the piece of paper.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention_of_photography">Invention of photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Invention of photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="330" data-file-width="495" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg/240px-Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg/360px-Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg/480px-Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce_Oldest_Photograph_1825.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>

This was a step towards the first permanent photograph taken with a camera.</div>

<p>Around the year 1800, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British</a> inventor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wedgwood_(photographer)" title="Thomas Wedgwood (photographer)">Thomas Wedgwood</a> made the first known attempt to capture the image in a camera obscura by means of a light-sensitive substance. He used paper or white leather treated with <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a>. Although he succeeded in capturing the shadows of objects placed on the surface in direct sunlight, and even made shadow-copies of paintings on glass, it was reported in 1802 that "the images formed by means of a camera obscura have been found too faint to produce, in any moderate time, an effect upon the nitrate of silver." The shadow images eventually darkened all over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoetching" title="Photoetching">photoetching</a> was an image produced in 1822 by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> inventor <a href="/wiki/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce" title="Nicéphore Niépce">Nicéphore Niépce</a>, but it was destroyed in a later attempt to make prints from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utexas_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-utexas-18">[18]</a></sup> Niépce was successful again in 1825. In 1826 or 1827, he made the <i><a href="/wiki/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras" title="View from the Window at Le Gras">View from the Window at Le Gras</a></i>, the earliest surviving photograph from nature (i.e., of the image of a real-world scene, as formed in a <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">camera obscura</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras,_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1805" data-file-width="2597" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg/220px-View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg/330px-View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg/440px-View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras%2C_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras,_Joseph_Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/View_from_the_Window_at_Le_Gras" title="View from the Window at Le Gras">View from the Window at Le Gras</a></i>, 1826 or 1827, the earliest surviving camera photograph</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because Niépce's camera photographs required an extremely long <a href="/wiki/Exposure_(photography)" title="Exposure (photography)">exposure</a> (at least eight hours and probably several days), he sought to greatly improve his <a href="/wiki/Bitumen_of_Judea" title="Bitumen of Judea">bitumen</a> process or replace it with one that was more practical. In partnership with <a href="/wiki/Louis_Daguerre" title="Louis Daguerre">Louis Daguerre</a>, he worked out post-exposure processing methods that produced visually superior results and replaced the bitumen with a more light-sensitive resin, but hours of exposure in the camera were still required. With an eye to eventual commercial exploitation, the partners opted for total secrecy.</p>
<p>Niépce died in 1833 and Daguerre then redirected the experiments toward the light-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Silver_halide" title="Silver halide">silver halides</a>, which Niépce had abandoned many years earlier because of his inability to make the images he captured with them light-fast and permanent. Daguerre's efforts culminated in what would later be named the <a href="/wiki/Daguerreotype" title="Daguerreotype">daguerreotype</a> process. The essential elements—a silver-plated surface sensitized by <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> vapor, developed by <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> vapor, and "fixed" with hot saturated <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">salt</a> water—were in place in 1837. The required exposure time was measured in minutes instead of hours. Daguerre took the earliest confirmed photograph of a person in 1838 while capturing a view of a Paris street: unlike the other pedestrian and horse-drawn traffic on the busy boulevard, which appears deserted, one man having his boots polished stood sufficiently still throughout the several-minutes-long exposure to be visible. The existence of Daguerre's process was publicly announced, without details, on 7 January 1839. The news created an international sensation. France soon agreed to pay Daguerre a pension in exchange for the right to present his invention to the world as the gift of France, which occurred when complete working instructions were unveiled on 19 August 1839.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="638" data-file-width="489" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg/170px-Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg/255px-Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg/340px-Latticed_window_at_lacock_abbey_1835.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A latticed window in <a href="/wiki/Lacock_Abbey" title="Lacock Abbey">Lacock Abbey</a>, England, photographed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Fox_Talbot" title="William Fox Talbot">William Fox Talbot</a> in 1835. Shown here in positive form, this may be the oldest extant photographic negative made in a camera.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hercules_Florence" title="Hercules Florence">Hercules Florence</a> had apparently started working out a silver-salt-based paper process in 1832, later naming it <i>Photographie</i>.</p>
<p>Meanwhile a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British</a> inventor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Fox_Talbot" title="William Fox Talbot">William Fox Talbot</a>, had succeeded in making crude but reasonably light-fast silver images on paper as early as 1834 but had kept his work secret. After reading about Daguerre's invention in January 1839, Talbot published his hitherto secret method and set about improving on it. At first, like other pre-daguerreotype processes, Talbot's paper-based photography typically required hours-long exposures in the camera, but in 1840 he created the <a href="/wiki/Calotype" title="Calotype">calotype</a> process, which used the <a href="/wiki/Photographic_processing" title="Photographic processing">chemical development</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Latent_image" title="Latent image">latent image</a> to greatly reduce the exposure needed and compete with the daguerreotype. In both its original and calotype forms, Talbot's process, unlike Daguerre's, created a translucent <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">negative</a> which could be used to print multiple positive copies, this the basis of most modern chemical photography up to the present day, as Daguerreotypes could only be replicated by rephotographing them with a camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Talbot's famous tiny paper negative of the Oriel window in <a href="/wiki/Lacock_Abbey" title="Lacock Abbey">Lacock Abbey</a>, one of a number of camera photographs he made in the summer of 1835, may be the oldest camera negative in existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>British chemist <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a> made many contributions to the new field. He invented the <a href="/wiki/Cyanotype" title="Cyanotype">cyanotype</a> process, later familiar as the "blueprint". He was the first to use the terms "photography", "negative" and "positive". He had discovered in 1819 that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_thiosulphate" title="Sodium thiosulphate">sodium thiosulphate</a> was a solvent of silver halides, and in 1839 he informed Talbot (and, indirectly, Daguerre) that it could be used to "fix" silver-halide-based photographs and make them completely light-fast. He made the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_negative" title="Glass negative">glass negative</a> in late 1839.</p>
<p>In the March 1851 issue of <i>The Chemist</i>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Scott_Archer" title="Frederick Scott Archer">Frederick Scott Archer</a> published his wet plate <a href="/wiki/Collodion_process" title="Collodion process">collodion process</a>. It became the most widely used photographic medium until the gelatin dry plate, introduced in the 1870s, eventually replaced it. There are three subsets to the collodion process; the <a href="/wiki/Ambrotype" title="Ambrotype">Ambrotype</a> (a positive image on glass), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrotype" title="Ferrotype">Ferrotype</a> or Tintype (a positive image on metal) and the glass negative, which was used to make positive prints on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albumen" title="Albumen">albumen</a> or salted paper.</p>
<p>Many advances in <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic glass plates</a> and printing were made during the rest of the 19th century. In 1891, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Lippmann" title="Gabriel Lippmann">Gabriel Lippmann</a> introduced a process for making natural-color photographs based on the optical phenomenon of the <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">interference</a> of light waves. His scientifically elegant and important but ultimately impractical invention earned him the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1908.</p>
<p>Glass plates were the medium for most original camera photography from the late 1850s until the general introduction of flexible plastic films during the 1890s. Although the convenience of film greatly popularized amateur photography, early films were somewhat more expensive and of markedly lower optical quality than their glass plate equivalents, and until the late 1910s they were not available in the large formats preferred by most professional photographers, so the new medium did not immediately or completely replace the old. Because of the superior dimensional stability of glass, the use of plates for some scientific applications, such as <a href="/wiki/Astrophotography" title="Astrophotography">astrophotography</a>, continued into the 1990s, and in the niche field of laser <a href="/wiki/Holography" title="Holography">holography</a> it has persisted into the 2010s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_photography">Film photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">Photographic film</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Undeveloped_film.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="494" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Undeveloped_film.png/350px-Undeveloped_film.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Undeveloped_film.png 1.5x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Undeveloped_film.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Undeveloped Arista black-and-white film, <a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">ISO</a> 125/22°</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurter_and_Driffield" title="Hurter and Driffield">Hurter and Driffield</a> began pioneering work on the <a href="/wiki/Sensitometry" title="Sensitometry">light sensitivity</a> of photographic emulsions in 1876. Their work enabled the first quantitative measure of film speed to be devised.</p>
<p>The first flexible photographic roll film was marketed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">George Eastman</a> in 1885, but this original "film" was actually a coating on a paper base. As part of the processing, the image-bearing layer was stripped from the paper and transferred to a hardened gelatin support. The first transparent plastic roll film followed in 1889. It was made from highly flammable <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a>"), now usually called "<a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose#Nitrate_film" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrate film</a>".</p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate" title="Cellulose acetate">cellulose acetate</a> or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_film" title="Safety film">safety film</a>" had been introduced by Kodak in 1908,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> at first it found only a few special applications as an alternative to the hazardous nitrate film, which had the advantages of being considerably tougher, slightly more transparent, and cheaper. The changeover was not completed for <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> films until 1933, and although safety film was always used for 16 mm and 8 mm home movies, nitrate film remained standard for theatrical 35 mm motion pictures until it was finally discontinued in 1951.</p>
<p>Films remained the dominant form of photography until the early 21st century, when advances in digital photography drew consumers to digital formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConciseFocal_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConciseFocal-25">[25]</a></sup> Although modern photography is dominated by digital users, film continues to be used by enthusiasts and professional photographers. The distinctive "look" of film based photographs compared to digital images is likely due to a combination of factors, including: (1) differences in spectral and tonal sensitivity (S-shaped density-to-exposure (H&amp;D curve) with film vs. linear response curve for digital CCD sensors) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> (2) resolution and (3) continuity of tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manual-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black-and-white">Black-and-white</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Black-and-white">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monochrome_photography" title="Monochrome photography">Monochrome photography</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dark_room.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2933" data-file-width="4414" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dark_room.jpg/220px-Dark_room.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dark_room.jpg/330px-Dark_room.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Dark_room.jpg/440px-Dark_room.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dark_room.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A photographic <a href="/wiki/Darkroom" title="Darkroom">darkroom</a> with <a href="/wiki/Safelight" title="Safelight">safelight</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Originally, all photography was monochrome, or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a></i>. Even after color film was readily available, black-and-white photography continued to dominate for decades, due to its lower cost and its "classic" photographic look. The tones and contrast between light and dark areas define black-and-white photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It is important to note that monochromatic pictures are not necessarily composed of pure blacks, whites, and intermediate shades of gray, but can involve shades of one particular <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hue</a> depending on the process. The <a href="/wiki/Cyanotype" title="Cyanotype">cyanotype</a> process, for example, produces an image composed of blue tones. The <a href="/wiki/Albumen_print" title="Albumen print">albumen print</a> process, first used more than 160 years ago, produces brownish tones.</p>
<p>Many photographers continue to produce some monochrome images, sometimes because of the established archival permanence of well-processed silver-halide-based materials. Some full-color digital images are processed using a variety of techniques to create black-and-white results, and some manufacturers produce digital cameras that exclusively shoot monochrome. Monochrome printing or electronic display can be used to salvage certain photographs taken in color which are unsatisfactory in their original form; sometimes when presented as black-and-white or single-color-toned images they are found to be more effective. Although color photography has long predominated, monochrome images are still produced, mostly for artistic reasons. Almost all digital cameras have an option to shoot in monochrome, and almost all image editing software can combine or selectively discard <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">RGB</a> color channels to produce a monochrome image from one shot in color.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Color_photography" title="Color photography">Color photography</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colonel_William_Willoughby_Verner,_Sanger_Shepherd_process,_by_Sarah_Acland_1903.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="301" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Colonel_William_Willoughby_Verner%2C_Sanger_Shepherd_process%2C_by_Sarah_Acland_1903.png/220px-Colonel_William_Willoughby_Verner%2C_Sanger_Shepherd_process%2C_by_Sarah_Acland_1903.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Colonel_William_Willoughby_Verner%2C_Sanger_Shepherd_process%2C_by_Sarah_Acland_1903.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colonel_William_Willoughby_Verner,_Sanger_Shepherd_process,_by_Sarah_Acland_1903.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Color photography was possible long before <a href="/wiki/Kodachrome" title="Kodachrome">Kodachrome</a>, as this 1903 portrait by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Angelina_Acland" title="Sarah Angelina Acland">Sarah Angelina Acland</a> demonstrates, but in its earliest years the need for special equipment, long exposures and complicated printing processes made it extremely rare.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Color_photography" title="Color photography">Color photography</a> was explored beginning in the 1840s. Early experiments in color required extremely long exposures (hours or days for camera images) and could not "fix" the photograph to prevent the color from quickly fading when exposed to white light.</p>
<p>The first permanent color photograph was taken in 1861 using the three-color-separation principle first published by physicist <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> in 1855. Maxwell's idea was to take three separate black-and-white photographs through red, green and blue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(photography)" title="Filter (photography)">filters</a>. This provides the <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">photographer</a> with the three basic channels required to recreate a color image. Transparent prints of the images could be projected through similar color filters and superimposed on the projection screen, an <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">additive method</a> of color reproduction. A color print on paper could be produced by superimposing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_print" title="Carbon print">carbon prints</a> of the three images made in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_color" title="Complementary color">complementary colors</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_color" title="Subtractive color">subtractive method</a> of color reproduction pioneered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Ducos_du_Hauron" title="Louis Ducos du Hauron">Louis Ducos du Hauron</a> in the late 1860s.</p>
<p>Russian photographer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Mikhailovich_Prokudin-Gorskii" title="Sergei Mikhailovich Prokudin-Gorskii">Sergei Mikhailovich Prokudin-Gorskii</a> made extensive use of this color separation technique, employing a special camera which successively exposed the three color-filtered images on different parts of an oblong <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">plate</a>. Because his exposures were not simultaneous, unsteady subjects exhibited color "fringes" or, if rapidly moving through the scene, appeared as brightly colored ghosts in the resulting projected or printed images.</p>
<p>Implementation of color photography was hindered by the limited sensitivity of early photographic materials, which were mostly sensitive to blue, only slightly sensitive to green, and virtually insensitive to red. The discovery of dye sensitization by photochemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_W._Vogel" title="Hermann W. Vogel">Hermann Vogel</a> in 1873 suddenly made it possible to add sensitivity to green, yellow and even red. Improved color sensitizers and ongoing improvements in the overall sensitivity of <a href="/wiki/Photographic_emulsion" title="Photographic emulsion">emulsions</a> steadily reduced the once-prohibitive long exposure times required for color, bringing it ever closer to commercial viability.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autochrome" title="Autochrome">Autochrome</a>, the first commercially successful color process, was introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Auguste_and_Louis_Lumi%C3%A8re" title="Auguste and Louis Lumière">Lumière brothers</a> in 1907. Autochrome <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">plates</a> incorporated a <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> color filter layer made of dyed grains of <a href="/wiki/Potato_starch" title="Potato starch">potato starch</a>, which allowed the three color components to be recorded as adjacent microscopic image fragments. After an Autochrome plate was <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">reversal processed</a> to produce a positive <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">transparency</a>, the starch grains served to illuminate each fragment with the correct color and the tiny colored points blended together in the eye, synthesizing the color of the subject by the <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">additive method</a>. Autochrome plates were one of several varieties of additive color screen plates and films marketed between the 1890s and the 1950s.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kodachrome" title="Kodachrome">Kodachrome</a>, the first modern "integral tripack" (or "monopack") color film, was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Kodak" title="Kodak">Kodak</a> in 1935. It captured the three color components in a multi-layer <a href="/wiki/Photographic_emulsion" title="Photographic emulsion">emulsion</a>. One layer was sensitized to record the red-dominated part of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">spectrum</a>, another layer recorded only the green part and a third recorded only the blue. Without special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_processing" title="Film processing">film processing</a>, the result would simply be three superimposed black-and-white images, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_color" title="Complementary color">complementary</a> cyan, magenta, and yellow dye images were created in those layers by adding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_coupler" title="Color coupler">color couplers</a> during a complex processing procedure.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agfa-Gevaert" title="Agfa-Gevaert">Agfa's</a> similarly structured <a href="/wiki/Agfacolor" title="Agfacolor">Agfacolor Neu</a> was introduced in 1936. Unlike Kodachrome, the color couplers in Agfacolor Neu were incorporated into the emulsion layers during manufacture, which greatly simplified the processing. Currently available color films still employ a multi-layer emulsion and the same principles, most closely resembling Agfa's product.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Instant_film" title="Instant film">Instant color film</a>, used in a special camera which yielded a unique finished color print only a minute or two after the exposure, was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Polaroid_Corporation" title="Polaroid Corporation">Polaroid</a> in 1963.</p>
<p>Color photography may form images as positive transparencies, which can be used in a <a href="/wiki/Slide_projector" title="Slide projector">slide projector</a>, or as color negatives intended for use in creating positive color enlargements on specially coated paper. The latter is now the most common form of film (non-digital) color photography owing to the introduction of automated photo printing equipment. After a transition period centered around 1995–2005, color film was relegated to a niche market by inexpensive multi-megapixel digital cameras. Film continues to be the preference of some photographers because of its distinctive "look".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_photography">Digital photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Digital photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Digital_photography" title="Digital photography">Digital photography</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">Digital camera</a></div>
<p>In 1981, Sony unveiled the first consumer camera to use a <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled device</a> for imaging, eliminating the need for film: the <a href="/wiki/Sony_Mavica" title="Sony Mavica">Sony Mavica</a>. While the Mavica saved images to disk, the images were displayed on television, and the camera was not fully digital. In 1991, Kodak unveiled the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DCS_100" title="DCS 100">DCS 100</a>, the first commercially available digital single lens reflex camera. Although its high cost precluded uses other than <a href="/wiki/Photojournalism" title="Photojournalism">photojournalism</a> and professional photography, commercial <a href="/wiki/Digital_photography" title="Digital photography">digital photography</a> was born.</p>
<p>Digital imaging uses an electronic <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensor</a> to record the image as a set of electronic data rather than as chemical changes on film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> An important difference between digital and chemical photography is that chemical photography resists <a href="/wiki/Photo_manipulation" title="Photo manipulation">photo manipulation</a> because it involves <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">photographic paper</a>, while digital imaging is a highly manipulative medium. This difference allows for a degree of image post-processing that is comparatively difficult in film-based photography and permits different communicative potentials and applications.</p>
<p>Digital photography dominates the 21st century. More than 99% of photographs taken around the world are through digital cameras, increasingly through smartphones.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_photography">Synthesis photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Synthesis photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Synthesis photography is part of <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated imagery</a> (CGI) where the shooting process is modeled on real photography. The CGI, creating digital copies of real universe, requires a visual representation process of these universes. Synthesis photography is the application of <a href="/wiki/Analog_photography" title="Analog photography">analog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_photography" title="Digital photography">digital photography</a> in digital space. With the characteristics of the real photography but not being constrained by the physical limits of real world, synthesis photography allows to get away from real photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_aspects">Technical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Technical aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">Camera</a></div>
<p>The camera is the image-forming device, and a <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> electronic <a href="/wiki/Image_sensor" title="Image sensor">image sensor</a> is the capture medium. The respective recording medium can be the plate or film itself, or a digital magnetic or electronic memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Photographers control the camera and lens to "expose" the light-recording material to the required amount of light to form a "<a href="/wiki/Latent_image" title="Latent image">latent image</a>" (on plate or film) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAW_file" title="RAW file">RAW file</a> (in digital cameras) which, after appropriate processing, is converted to a usable image. <a href="/wiki/Digital_photography" title="Digital photography">Digital cameras</a> use an electronic image sensor based on light-sensitive electronics such as <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled device</a> (CCD) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_metal-oxide-semiconductor" title="Complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor">complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor</a> (CMOS) technology. The resulting digital image is stored electronically, but can be reproduced on a paper.</p>
<p>The camera (or '<a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">camera obscura</a>') is a dark room or chamber from which, as far as possible, all light is excluded except the light that forms the image. The subject being photographed, however, must be illuminated. Cameras can range from small to very large, a whole room that is kept dark while the object to be photographed is in another room where it is properly illuminated. This was common for reproduction photography of flat copy when large film negatives were used (see <a href="/wiki/Process_camera" title="Process camera">Process camera</a>).</p>
<p>As soon as photographic materials became "fast" (sensitive) enough for taking <a href="/wiki/Candid_photography" title="Candid photography">candid</a> or surreptitious pictures, small "detective" cameras were made, some actually disguised as a book or handbag or pocket watch (the <i>Ticka</i> camera) or even worn hidden behind an <a href="/wiki/Ascot_tie" title="Ascot tie">Ascot</a> necktie with a tie pin that was really the lens.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Movie_camera" title="Movie camera">movie camera</a> is a type of photographic camera which takes a rapid sequence of photographs on recording medium. In contrast to a still camera, which captures a single snapshot at a time, the movie camera takes a series of images, each called a "frame". This is accomplished through an intermittent mechanism. The frames are later played back in a movie projector at a specific speed, called the "frame rate" (number of frames per second). While viewing, a person's eyes and brain merge the separate pictures together to create the illusion of motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camera_controls">Camera controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Camera controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In all but certain specialized cameras, the process of obtaining a usable exposure must involve the use, manually or automatically, of a few controls to ensure the photograph is clear, sharp and well illuminated. The controls usually include but are not limited to the following:</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Control</th>
<th>Description</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">Focus</a></td>
<td>The position of a viewed object or the adjustment of an optical device necessary to produce a clear image: in focus; out of focus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary.com.2C_LLC_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.com.2C_LLC-33">[33]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">Aperture</a></td>
<td>Adjustment of the <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(optics)" title="Diaphragm (optics)">lens opening</a>, measured as <a href="/wiki/F-number" title="F-number">f-number</a>, which controls the amount of light passing through the lens. Aperture also has an effect on <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">depth of field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> – the higher the f-number, the smaller the opening, the less light, the greater the depth of field, and the more the diffraction blur. The focal length divided by the f-number gives the effective aperture diameter.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Shutter_speed" title="Shutter speed">Shutter speed</a></td>
<td>Adjustment of the speed (often expressed either as fractions of seconds or as an angle, with mechanical shutters) of the shutter to control the amount of time during which the imaging medium is exposed to light for each exposure. Shutter speed may be used to control the amount of light striking the image plane; 'faster' shutter speeds (that is, those of shorter duration) decrease both the amount of light and the amount of image blurring from motion of the subject and/or camera. The slower shutter speeds allow for long exposure shots that are done used to photograph images in very low light, including the images of the night sky.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Color_balance" title="Color balance">White balance</a></td>
<td>On digital cameras, electronic compensation for the <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">color temperature</a> associated with a given set of lighting conditions, ensuring that white light is registered as such on the imaging chip and therefore that the colors in the frame will appear natural. On mechanical, film-based cameras, this function is served by the operator's choice of <a href="/wiki/Film_stock" title="Film stock">film stock</a> or with color correction filters. In addition to using white balance to register natural coloration of the image, photographers may employ white balance to aesthetic end, for example white balancing to a blue object in order to obtain a warm <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">color temperature</a>.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Metering_mode" title="Metering mode">Metering</a></td>
<td>Measurement of exposure so that highlights and shadows are exposed according to the photographer's wishes. Many modern cameras meter and set exposure automatically. Before automatic exposure, correct exposure was accomplished with the use of a separate <a href="/wiki/Light_meter" title="Light meter">light metering device</a> or by the photographer's knowledge and experience of gauging correct settings. To translate the amount of light into a usable aperture and shutter speed, the meter needs to adjust for the sensitivity of the film or sensor to light. This is done by setting the "film speed" or ISO sensitivity into the meter.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">Film speed</a></td>
<td>Traditionally used to "tell the camera" the <a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">film speed</a> of the selected film on film cameras, film speed numbers are employed on modern digital cameras as an indication of the system's <i><a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a></i> from light to numerical output and to control the automatic exposure system. Film speed is usually measured via the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> system. The higher the film speed number the greater the film sensitivity to light, whereas with a lower number, the film is less sensitive to light. A correct combination of film speed, aperture, and shutter speed leads to an image that is neither too dark nor too light, hence it is 'correctly exposed', indicated by a centered meter.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Autofocus" title="Autofocus">Autofocus</a> point</td>
<td>On some cameras, the selection of a point in the imaging frame upon which the auto-focus system will attempt to focus. Many <a href="/wiki/Single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Single-lens reflex camera">Single-lens reflex cameras</a> (SLR) feature multiple auto-focus points in the viewfinder.</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Many other elements of the imaging device itself may have a pronounced effect on the quality and/or aesthetic effect of a given photograph; among them are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">Focal length</a></b> and <b>type of lens</b> (<a href="/wiki/Normal_lens" title="Normal lens">normal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_focus_lens" title="Long focus lens">long focus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wide-angle_lens" title="Wide-angle lens">wide angle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephoto_lens" title="Telephoto lens">telephoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macro_photography" title="Macro photography">macro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheye_lens" title="Fisheye lens">fisheye</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Zoom_lens" title="Zoom lens">zoom</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Photographic_filter" title="Photographic filter">Filters</a></b> placed between the subject and the light recording material, either in front of or behind the lens</li>
<li>Inherent <b>sensitivity</b> of the medium to light intensity and color/wavelengths.</li>
<li>The nature of the light <b>recording material</b>, for example its resolution as measured in pixels or grains of <a href="/wiki/Silver_halide" title="Silver halide">silver halide</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_and_rendering">Exposure and rendering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Exposure and rendering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Camera controls are interrelated. The total amount of light reaching the film plane (the 'exposure') changes with the duration of exposure, aperture of the lens, and on the effective focal length of the lens (which in variable focal length lenses, can force a change in aperture as the lens is zoomed). Changing any of these controls can alter the exposure. Many cameras may be set to adjust most or all of these controls automatically. This automatic functionality is useful for occasional photographers in many situations.</p>
<p>The duration of an exposure is referred to as shutter speed, often even in cameras that do not have a physical shutter, and is typically measured in fractions of a second. It is quite possible to have exposures from one up to several seconds, usually for still-life subjects, and for night scenes exposure times can be several hours. However, for a subject that is in motion use a fast shutter speed. This will prevent the photograph from coming out blurry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effective aperture is expressed by an <a href="/wiki/F-number" title="F-number">f-number</a> or f-stop (derived from <a href="/wiki/F-number#Focal_ratio" title="F-number"><i>focal ratio</i></a>), which is proportional to the ratio of the focal length to the diameter of the aperture. Longer focal length lenses will pass less light through the same aperture diameter due to the greater distance the light has to travel; shorter focal length lenses will transmit more light through the same diameter of aperture.</p>
<p>The smaller the f/number, the larger the effective aperture. The present system of f/numbers to give the effective aperture of a lens was standardized by an international convention in 1963, and is referred to as the British Standard (BS-1013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Other aperture measurement scales had been used through the early 20th century, including the European Scale, Intermediate settings, and the 1881 Uniform System proposed by the Royal Photographic Society, which are all now largely obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:30</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/F-number#T-stop" title="F-number">T-stops</a></i> have been used for color motion picture lenses, to account for differences in <i><a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">light transmission</a></i> through compound lenses, are calculated as T-number = f/number x <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">transmittance</span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:615</sup></p>
<p>If the f-number is decreased by a factor of <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">2</span></span>, the aperture diameter is increased by the same factor, and its area is increased by a factor of 2. The f-stops that might be found on a typical lens include 2.8, 4, 5.6, 8, 11, 16, 22, 32, where going up "one stop" (using lower f-stop numbers) doubles the amount of light reaching the film, and <a href="/wiki/Stopping_down" title="Stopping down">stopping down</a> one stop halves the amount of light.</p>
<p>Image capture can be achieved through various combinations of shutter speed, aperture, and film or sensor speed. Different (but related) settings of aperture and shutter speed enable photographs to be taken under various conditions of film or sensor speed, lighting and motion of subjects and/or camera, and desired depth of field. A slower speed film will exhibit less "grain", and a slower speed setting on an electronic sensor will exhibit less "noise", while higher film and sensor speeds allow for a faster shutter speed, which reduces motion blur or allows the use of a smaller aperture to increase the depth of field.</p>
<p>For example, a wider aperture is used for lower light and a lower aperture for more light. If a subject is in motion, then a high shutter speed may be needed. A <a href="/wiki/Tripod_(photography)" title="Tripod (photography)">tripod</a> can also be helpful in that it enables a slower shutter speed to be used.</p>
<p>For example, f/8 at 8 ms (1/125 of a second) and f/5.6 at 4 ms (1/250 of a second) yield the same amount of light. The chosen combination affects the final result. The aperture and focal length of the lens determine the <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">depth of field</a>, which refers to the range of distances from the lens that will be in focus. A longer lens or a wider aperture will result in "shallow" depth of field (i.e., only a small plane of the image will be in sharp focus). This is often useful for isolating subjects from backgrounds as in individual portraits or macro photography.</p>
<p>Conversely, a shorter lens, or a smaller aperture, will result in more of the image being in focus. This is generally more desirable when photographing landscapes or groups of people. With very small apertures, such as <a href="/wiki/Pinhole_camera" title="Pinhole camera">pinholes</a>, a wide range of distance can be brought into focus, but sharpness is severely degraded by <a href="/wiki/Diffraction-limited_system" title="Diffraction-limited system">diffraction</a> with such small apertures. Generally, the highest degree of "sharpness" is achieved at an aperture near the middle of a lens's range (for example, f/8 for a lens with available apertures of f/2.8 to f/16). However, as lens technology improves, lenses are becoming capable of making increasingly sharp images at wider apertures.</p>
<p>Image capture is only part of the image forming process. Regardless of material, some process must be employed to render the latent image captured by the camera into a viewable image. With slide film, the developed film is just mounted for <a href="/wiki/Slide_projector" title="Slide projector">projection</a>. Print film requires the developed film negative to be printed onto <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">photographic paper</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(photography)" title="Transparency (photography)">transparency</a>. Prior to the advent of laser jet and inkjet printers, celluloid photographic negative images had to be mounted in an <a href="/wiki/Enlarger" title="Enlarger">enlarger</a> which projected the image onto a sheet of light-sensitive paper for a certain length of time (usually measured in seconds or fractions of a second). This sheet then was soaked in a chemical bath of <a href="/wiki/Photographic_developer" title="Photographic developer">developer</a> (to bring out the image) followed immediately by a <a href="/wiki/Stop_bath" title="Stop bath">stop bath</a> (to neutralize the progression of development and prevent the image from changing further once exposed to normal light). After this, the paper was hung until dry enough to safely handle. This post-production process allowed the photographer to further manipulate the final image beyond what had already been captured on the negative, adjusting the length of time the image was projected by the enlarger and the duration of both chemical baths to change the image's intensity, darkness, clarity, etc. This process is still employed by both amateur and professional photographers, but the advent of digital imagery means that the vast majority of modern photographic work is captured digitally and rendered via printing processes that are no longer dependent on chemical reactions to light. Such digital images may be uploaded to an image server (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Photo_sharing" title="Photo sharing">photo-sharing</a> web site), viewed on a <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, or transferred to a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Digital_photo_frame" title="Digital photo frame">digital photo frame</a>. Every type can then be produced as a hard copy on regular paper or photographic paper via a printer.</p>

<p>Prior to the rendering of a viewable image, modifications can be made using several controls. Many of these controls are similar to controls during image capture, while some are exclusive to the rendering process. Most printing controls have equivalent digital concepts, but some create different effects. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dodging_and_burning" title="Dodging and burning">dodging and burning</a> controls are different between digital and film processes. Other printing modifications include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Chemicals and process used during <a href="/wiki/Photographic_developer" title="Photographic developer">film development</a>.</li>
<li>Duration of print exposure – equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Shutter_speed" title="Shutter speed">shutter speed</a></li>
<li>Printing aperture – equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>, but has no effect on depth of field</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Contrast_(vision)" title="Contrast (vision)">Contrast</a> – changing the visual properties of objects in an image to make them distinguishable from other objects and the background</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dodging_and_burning" title="Dodging and burning">Dodging</a> – reduces exposure of certain print areas, resulting in lighter areas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dodging_and_burning" title="Dodging and burning">Burning in</a> – increases exposure of certain areas, resulting in darker areas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">Paper texture</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloss_(material_appearance)" title="Gloss (material appearance)">glossy</a>, matte, etc.</li>
<li>Paper type – resin-coated (RC) or fiber-based (FB)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paper_size" title="Paper size">Paper size</a></li>
<li>Exposure Shape — resulting prints in shapes such as circular, oval, loupe, etc.</li>
<li>Toners – used to add warm or cold tones to black-and-white prints</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_photographic_techniques">Other photographic techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other photographic techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stereoscopic">Stereoscopic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Stereoscopic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">Stereoscopy</a></div>
<p>Photographs, both monochrome and color, can be captured and displayed through two side-by-side images that emulate human stereoscopic vision. Stereoscopic photography was the first that captured figures in motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> While known colloquially as "3-D" photography, the more accurate term is stereoscopy. Such cameras have long been realized by using film, and more recently in digital electronic methods (including cellphone cameras).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-spectrum.2C_ultraviolet_and_infrared">Full-spectrum, ultraviolet and infrared</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Full-spectrum, ultraviolet and infrared">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_spectrum_photography" title="Full spectrum photography">Full spectrum photography</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_photography" title="Ultraviolet photography">Ultraviolet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared_photography" title="Infrared photography">infrared</a> films have been available for many decades and employed in a variety of photographic avenues since the 1960s. New technological trends in digital photography have opened a new direction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_spectrum_photography" title="Full spectrum photography">full spectrum photography</a>, where careful filtering choices across the ultraviolet, visible and infrared lead to new artistic visions.</p>
<p>Modified digital cameras can detect some ultraviolet, all of the visible and much of the near infrared spectrum, as most digital imaging sensors are sensitive from about 350 nm to 1000 nm. An off-the-shelf digital camera contains an infrared <a href="/wiki/Hot_mirror" title="Hot mirror">hot mirror</a> filter that blocks most of the infrared and a bit of the ultraviolet that would otherwise be detected by the sensor, narrowing the accepted range from about 400 nm to 700 nm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Replacing a hot mirror or infrared blocking filter with an infrared pass or a wide spectrally transmitting filter allows the camera to detect the wider spectrum light at greater sensitivity. Without the hot-mirror, the red, green and blue (or cyan, yellow and magenta) colored micro-filters placed over the sensor elements pass varying amounts of ultraviolet (blue window) and infrared (primarily red and somewhat lesser the green and blue micro-filters).</p>
<p>Uses of full spectrum photography are for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_art_photography" title="Fine art photography">fine art photography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing#Geodetic" title="Remote sensing">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_forensic_photography" title="History of forensic photography">forensics</a> and law enforcement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_field_photography">Light field photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Light field photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Light-field_camera" title="Light-field camera">Light-field camera</a></div>
<p>Digital methods of image capture and display processing have enabled the new technology of "light field photography" (also known as synthetic aperture photography). This process allows focusing at various depths of field to be selected <i>after</i> the photograph has been captured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> As explained by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> in 1846, the "<a href="/wiki/Light_field" title="Light field">light field</a>" is understood as 5-dimensional, with each point in 3-D space having attributes of two more angles that define the direction of each ray passing through that point.</p>
<p>These additional vector attributes can be captured optically through the use of microlenses at each pixel-point within the 2-dimensional image sensor. Every pixel of the final image is actually a selection from each sub-array located under each microlens, as identified by a post-image capture focus algorithm.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_imaging_techniques">Other imaging techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other imaging techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Besides the camera, other methods of forming images with light are available. For instance, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photocopy" title="Photocopy">photocopy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xerography" title="Xerography">xerography</a> machine forms permanent images but uses the transfer of static <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electrical charges</a> rather than photographic medium, hence the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrophotography" title="Electrophotography">electrophotography</a>. <a href="/wiki/Photogram" title="Photogram">Photograms</a> are images produced by the shadows of objects cast on the photographic paper, without the use of a camera. Objects can also be placed directly on the glass of an <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">image scanner</a> to produce digital pictures.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modes_of_production">Modes of production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Modes of production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur">Amateur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Amateur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An amateur photographer is one who practices photography as a <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobby</a> / <a href="/wiki/Passion_(emotion)" title="Passion (emotion)">passion</a> and not for profit. The quality of some amateur work is comparable to that of many <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professionals</a> and may be highly specialized or <a href="/wiki/Eclecticism_in_art" title="Eclecticism in art">eclectic</a> in choice of subjects. Amateur photography is often pre-eminent in photographic subjects which have little prospect of commercial use or reward. Amateur photography grew during the late 19th century due to the popularization of the hand-held camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial">Commercial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Commercial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commercial photography is probably best defined as any photography for which the photographer is paid for <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a> rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Works_of_art" title="Works of art">works of art</a>. In this light, money could be paid for the subject of the photograph or the photograph itself. Wholesale, retail, and professional uses of photography would fall under this definition. The commercial photographic world could include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Advertising photography: photographs made to illustrate and usually sell a service or product. These images, such as <a href="/wiki/Packshot" title="Packshot">packshots</a>, are generally done with an <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">advertising agency</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_firm" title="Design firm">design firm</a> or with an in-house corporate design team.</li>
<li>Fashion and glamour photography usually incorporates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_model" title="Photographic model">models</a> and is a form of advertising photography. <a href="/wiki/Fashion_photography" title="Fashion photography">Fashion photography</a>, like the work featured in <a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Bazaar" title="Harper's Bazaar">Harper's Bazaar</a>, emphasizes clothes and other products; glamour emphasizes the model and body form. Glamour photography is popular in advertising and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men%27s_magazine" title="Men's magazine">men's magazines</a>. Models in <a href="/wiki/Glamour_photography" title="Glamour photography">glamour photography</a> sometimes work <a href="/wiki/Nude_photography" title="Nude photography">nude</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crime_scene_photography" title="Crime scene photography">Crime scene photography</a> consists of photographing scenes of crime such as robberies and murders. A black and white camera or an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_camera" title="Infrared camera">infrared camera</a> may be used to capture specific details.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Still_life_photography" title="Still life photography">Still life photography</a> usually depicts inanimate subject matter, typically commonplace objects which may be either natural or man-made. Still life is a broader category for food and some natural photography and can be used for advertising purposes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Food_photography" title="Food photography">Food photography</a> can be used for editorial, packaging or advertising use. Food photography is similar to still life photography, but requires some special skills.</li>
<li>Editorial photography illustrates a story or idea within the context of a magazine. These are usually assigned by the magazine and encompass fashion and glamour photography features.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Photojournalism" title="Photojournalism">Photojournalism</a> can be considered a subset of editorial photography. Photographs made in this context are accepted as a documentation of a news story.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Portrait_photography" title="Portrait photography">Portrait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wedding_photography" title="Wedding photography">wedding photography</a>: photographs made and sold directly to the end user of the images.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Landscape_photography" title="Landscape photography">Landscape photography</a> depicts locations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wildlife_photography" title="Wildlife photography">Wildlife photography</a> demonstrates the life of animals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paparazzi" title="Paparazzi">Paparazzi</a> is a form of photojournalism in which the photographer captures candid images of athletes, celebrities, politicians, and other prominent people.</li>
<li>Pet photography involves several aspects that are similar to traditional studio portraits. It can also be done in natural lighting, outside of a studio, such as in a client's home.</li>
</ul>

<p>The market for photographic services demonstrates the <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">aphorism</a> "<a href="/wiki/A_picture_is_worth_a_thousand_words" title="A picture is worth a thousand words">A picture is worth a thousand words</a>", which has an interesting basis in the <a href="#History_of_photography">history of photography</a>. Magazines and newspapers, companies putting up Web sites, advertising agencies and other groups pay for photography.</p>
<p>Many people take photographs for commercial purposes. Organizations with a budget and a need for photography have several options: they can employ a photographer directly, organize a public competition, or obtain rights to <a href="/wiki/Stock_photography" title="Stock photography">stock photographs</a>. Photo stock can be procured through traditional stock giants, such as <a href="/wiki/Getty_Images" title="Getty Images">Getty Images</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corbis" title="Corbis">Corbis</a>; smaller <a href="/wiki/Microstock_photography" title="Microstock photography">microstock</a> agencies, such as <a href="/wiki/Fotolia" title="Fotolia">Fotolia</a>; or web marketplaces, such as Cutcaster.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Stieglitz_(American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4197" data-file-width="3333" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Alfred_Stieglitz_%28American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Stieglitz_(American_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Classic <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">Alfred Stieglitz</a> photograph, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Steerage" title="The Steerage">The Steerage</a></i> shows unique aesthetic of black-and-white photos.</div>

<p>During the 20th century, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_art_photography" title="Fine art photography">fine art photography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Documentary_photography" title="Documentary photography">documentary photography</a> became accepted by the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-speaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> world and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_gallery" title="Art gallery">gallery</a> system. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, a handful of photographers, including <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">Alfred Stieglitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Steichen" title="Edward Steichen">Edward Steichen</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Szarkowski" title="John Szarkowski">John Szarkowski</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._Holland_Day" title="F. Holland Day">F. Holland Day</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a>, spent their lives advocating for photography as a fine art. At first, fine art photographers tried to imitate painting styles. This movement is called <a href="/wiki/Pictorialism" title="Pictorialism">Pictorialism</a>, often using <a href="/wiki/Soft_focus" title="Soft focus">soft focus</a> for a dreamy, 'romantic' look. In reaction to that, Weston, <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams" title="Ansel Adams">Ansel Adams</a>, and others formed the <a href="/wiki/Group_f/64" title="Group f/64">Group f/64</a> to advocate '<a href="/wiki/Straight_photography" title="Straight photography">straight photography</a>', the photograph as a (sharply focused) thing in itself and not an imitation of something else.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> of photography is a matter that continues to be discussed regularly, especially in artistic circles. Many artists argued that photography was the mechanical reproduction of an image. If photography is authentically art, then photography in the context of art would need redefinition, such as determining what component of a photograph makes it <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">beautiful</a> to the viewer. The controversy began with the earliest images "written with light"; <a href="/wiki/Nic%C3%A9phore_Ni%C3%A9pce" title="Nicéphore Niépce">Nicéphore Niépce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Daguerre" title="Louis Daguerre">Louis Daguerre</a>, and others among the very earliest photographers were met with acclaim, but some questioned if their work met the definitions and purposes of art.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clive_Bell" title="Clive Bell">Clive Bell</a> in his classic essay <i>Art</i> states that only "significant form" can distinguish art from what is not art.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There must be some one quality without which a work of art cannot exist; possessing which, in the least degree, no work is altogether worthless. What is this quality? What quality is shared by all objects that provoke our aesthetic emotions? What quality is common to Sta. Sophia and the windows at Chartres, Mexican sculpture, a Persian bowl, Chinese carpets, Giotto's frescoes at Padua, and the masterpieces of Poussin, Piero della Francesca, and Cezanne? Only one answer seems possible — significant form. In each, lines and colors combined in a particular way, certain forms and relations of forms, stir our aesthetic emotions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>On 14 February 2004, Sotheby's London sold the 2001 photograph <i><a href="/wiki/99_Cent_II_Diptychon" title="99 Cent II Diptychon">99 Cent II Diptychon</a></i> for an unprecedented $3,346,456 to an anonymous bidder, making it the most expensive at the time.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conceptual_photography" title="Conceptual photography">Conceptual photography</a> turns a concept or idea into a photograph. Even though what is depicted in the photographs are real objects, the subject is strictly abstract.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_forensics">Science and forensics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Science and forensics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wootton_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1012" data-file-width="1500" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wootton_bridge.jpg/220px-Wootton_bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wootton_bridge.jpg/330px-Wootton_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Wootton_bridge.jpg/440px-Wootton_bridge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wootton_bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Wootton_bridge_collapse" title="Wootton bridge collapse">Wootton bridge collapse</a> in 1861</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The camera has a long and distinguished history as a means of recording scientific phenomena from the first use by Daguerre and Fox-Talbot, such as astronomical events (<a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Photography" title="Solar eclipse">eclipses</a> for example), small creatures and plants when the camera was attached to the eyepiece of microscopes (in <a href="/wiki/Micrograph" title="Micrograph">photomicroscopy</a>) and for <a href="/wiki/Macro_photography" title="Macro photography">macro photography</a> of larger specimens. The camera also proved useful in recording <a href="/wiki/Crime_scene" title="Crime scene">crime scenes</a> and the scenes of accidents, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wootton_bridge_collapse" title="Wootton bridge collapse">Wootton bridge collapse</a> in 1861. The methods used in analysing photographs for use in legal cases are collectively known as <a href="/wiki/Forensic_photography" title="Forensic photography">forensic photography</a>. Crime scene photos are taken from three vantage point. The vantage points are overview, mid-range, and close up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1845 <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds" title="Francis Ronalds">Francis Ronalds</a>, the Honorary Director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kew_Observatory" title="Kew Observatory">Kew Observatory</a>, invented the first successful camera to make continuous recordings of meteorological and geomagnetic parameters. Different machines produced 12- or 24- hour photographic traces of the minute-by-minute variations of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_electricity" title="Atmospheric electricity">atmospheric electricity</a>, and the three components of <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">geomagnetic forces</a>. The cameras were supplied to numerous observatories around the world and some remained in use until well into the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Brooke_(surgeon)" title="Charles Brooke (surgeon)">Charles Brooke</a> a little later developed similar instruments for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">Greenwich Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Science uses image technology that has derived from the design of the Pin Hole camera. X-Ray machine are similar in design to Pin Hole cameras with high grade filters and laser radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Photography has become ubiquitous in recording events and data in science and engineering, and at <a href="/wiki/Crime_scene" title="Crime scene">crime scenes</a> or accident scenes. The method has been much extended by using other wavelengths, such as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_photography" title="Infrared photography">infrared photography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_photography" title="Ultraviolet photography">ultraviolet photography</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>. Those methods were first used in the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> and improved much further since that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_cultural_implications">Social and cultural implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Social and cultural implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>There are many ongoing questions about different aspects of photography. In her writing "<a href="/wiki/On_Photography" title="On Photography">On Photography</a>" (1977), <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a> discusses concerns about the objectivity of photography. This is a highly debated subject within the photographic community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Sontag argues, "To photograph is to appropriate the thing photographed. It means putting one's self into a certain relation to the world that feels like knowledge, and therefore like power."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sontag_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-sontag-51">[51]</a></sup> Photographers decide what to take a photo of, what elements to exclude and what angle to frame the photo, and these factors may reflect a particular socio-historical context. Along these lines it can be argued that photography is a subjective form of representation.</p>
<p>Modern photography has raised a number of concerns on its effect on society. In <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Rear_Window" title="Rear Window">Rear Window</a></i> (1954), the camera is presented as promoting voyeurism. 'Although the camera is an observation station, the act of photographing is more than passive observing'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sontag_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-sontag-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The camera doesn't rape or even possess, though it may presume, intrude, trespass, distort, exploit, and, at the farthest reach of metaphor, assassinate – all activities that, unlike the sexual push and shove, can be conducted from a distance, and with some detachment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sontag_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-sontag-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Digital imaging has raised ethical concerns because of the ease of manipulating digital photographs in post-processing. Many photojournalists have declared they will not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cropping" title="Cropping">crop</a> their pictures, or are forbidden from combining elements of multiple photos to make "<a href="/wiki/Photomontage" title="Photomontage">photomontages</a>", passing them as "real" photographs. Today's technology has made <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">image editing</a> relatively simple for even the novice photographer. However, recent changes of in-camera processing allows digital fingerprinting of photos to detect tampering for purposes of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_photography" title="Forensic photography">forensic photography</a>.</p>
<p>Photography is one of the new media forms that changes perception and changes the structure of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Further unease has been caused around cameras in regards to desensitization. Fears that disturbing or explicit images are widely accessible to children and society at large have been raised. Particularly, photos of war and pornography are causing a stir. Sontag is concerned that "to photograph is to turn people into objects that can be symbolically possessed." Desensitization discussion goes hand in hand with debates about censored images. Sontag writes of her concern that the ability to censor pictures means the photographer has the ability to construct reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sontag_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-sontag-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the practices through which photography constitutes society is <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>. Tourism and photography combine to create a "tourist gaze"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> in which local inhabitants are positioned and defined by the camera lens. However, it has also been argued that there exists a "reverse gaze"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> through which indigenous photographees can position the tourist photographer as a shallow consumer of images.</p>
<p>Additionally, photography has been the topic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songs_about_photography" title="Songs about photography">many songs</a> in popular culture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photography_and_the_law" title="Photography and the law">Photography and the law</a></div>
<p>Photography is both restricted as well as protected by the law in many jurisdictions. Protection of photographs is typically achieved through the granting of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> or moral rights to the photographer. In the United States, photography is protected as a <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment right</a> and anyone is free to photograph anything seen in public spaces as long as it is in plain view.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In the UK a recent law (Counter-Terrorism Act 2008) increases the power of the police to prevent people, even press photographers, from taking pictures in public places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
