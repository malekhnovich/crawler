<p><b>Architectural lighting design</b> is a field within <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">interior design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> that is concerned with the design of lighting systems, including natural light, electric light, or both, to serve human needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design process takes account of:</p>
<ul>
<li>The kind of human activity for which lighting is to be provided</li>
<li>The amount of light required</li>
<li>The colour of the light as it may affect the views of particular objects and the environment as a whole</li>
<li>The distribution of light within the space to be lighted, whether indoor or outdoor</li>
<li>The effect of the lightened system itself on the user</li>
</ul>
<p>It is important to appreciate that the ultimate criterion of success in lighting is the human response, that is. whether what is to be seen clearly, easily and without discomfort."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> Lighting designers are often specialists who must understand the physics of light production and distribution, the physiology and psychology of light perception by humans, the anatomy of the human eye, and the response of the rods and cones to light.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a>, the earliest form of artificial lighting used to illuminate an area were <a href="/wiki/Campfire" title="Campfire">campfires</a> or <a href="/wiki/Torch" title="Torch">torches</a>. As early as 400,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BCE" title="BCE">BCE</a>, fire was kindled in the caves of <a href="/wiki/Peking_Man" title="Peking Man">Peking Man</a>. Prehistoric people used primitive lamps to illuminate surroundings. These lamps were made from naturally occurring materials such as rocks, shells, horns and stones, were filled with <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">grease</a>, and had a <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> <a href="/wiki/Candle_wick" title="Candle wick">wick</a>. Lamps typically used animal or vegetable fats as fuel. Hundreds of these lamps (hollow worked stones) have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a> caves in modern-day <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, dating to about 15,000 years ago. Oily animals (birds and fish) were also used as lamps after being threaded with a wick. <a href="/wiki/Firefly" title="Firefly">Fireflies</a> have been used as lighting sources. <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">Candles</a> and glass and pottery lamps were also invented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chandelier" title="Chandelier">Chandeliers</a> were an early form of "<a href="/wiki/Light_fixture" title="Light fixture">light fixture</a>".</p>
<p>Major reductions in the cost of lighting occurred with the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planet_money_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-planet_money-3">[3]</a></sup> The potential of electric light as a new building material was recognized in the 1920s and became a useful design tool by the mid-century. Skillful lighting allowed for theatricality, narrative, and a new emphasis on structure and space</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">Gas lighting</a> was economical enough to power street lights in major cities starting in the early 1800s, and was also used in some commercial buildings and in the homes of wealthy people. The <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">gas mantle</a> boosted the luminosity of utility lighting and of kerosene lanterns. The next major drop in price came about with the <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulb</a> powered by <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planet_money_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-planet_money-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over time, electric lighting became ubiquitous in developed countries. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Segmented_sleep" title="Segmented sleep">Segmented sleep</a> patterns disappeared, improved nighttime lighting made people made more activities possible at night, and more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_lights" title="Street lights">street lights</a> reduced urban crime.Without light fittings there can be no Architectural Lighting Design. As these light sources change so does the practice of lighting Design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Candles">Candles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Candles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">Candle</a></div>
<p>For most of recorded history candles were <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">tallow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beeswax" title="Beeswax">beeswax</a> until the mid 1800s at which point they were made mainly from <a href="/wiki/Spermaceti" title="Spermaceti">spermaceti</a> (spurring larger demand for <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil#Applications" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a>), and purified animal fats (<a href="/wiki/Stearin" title="Stearin">stearin</a>) ox fat, rat fat and even pigeon fat when sailors were at sea. The Whaling industry was based around acquiring the whale oil to create candles and light.</p>
<p>A candle manufacturer is traditionally known as a <a href="/wiki/Chandlery" title="Chandlery">chandler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Various devices have been invented to hold candles, from simple tabletop candle holders, to elaborate <a href="/wiki/Chandelier" title="Chandelier">chandeliers</a>.</p>

<p>The Argand lamp is a home lighting <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">oil lamp</a> producing a light output of 6 to 10 <a href="/wiki/Candela" title="Candela">candela</a> which was invented and <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patented</a> in 1780 by <a href="/wiki/Aim%C3%A9_Argand" title="Aimé Argand">Aimé Argand</a>. Aside from the improvement in brightness, the more complete combustion of the wick and oil required much less frequent trimming of the wick.</p>
<p>In France, they are known as "Quinquets" after <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Quinquet" title="Antoine Quinquet">Antoine-Arnoult Quinquet</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacist</a> in Paris, who used the idea originated by Argand and popularized it in France. He is sometimes credited with the addition of the glass chimney to the lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was the lamp of choice until about 1850 when <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamps</a> were introduced.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamp</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraffin_lamp" title="Paraffin lamp">paraffin lamp</a> is a type of lighting device that uses kerosene as a fuel. Kerosene lamps have a wick or mantle as light source, protected by a glass chimney or globe; lamps may be used on a table, or hand-held lanterns may be used for portable lighting.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">Kerosene</a> was cheaper than vegetable oil, it produced a whiter <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a>, and as a liquid of low <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> it could easily travel up a wick eliminating the need for complicated mechanisms to feed the fuel to the burner.</p>
<p>The first description of a simple lamp using crude mineral oil, the predecessor of the kerosene lamp, was provided by al-Razi (Rhazes) in 9th century Baghdad, who referred to it as the "naffatah" in his Kitab al-Asrar (Book of Secrets). In 1846, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Pineo_Gesner" title="Abraham Pineo Gesner">Abraham Pineo Gesner</a> pioneered the use of kerosene distilled from coal a substitute for whale oil for lighting. Later made from petroleum, kerosene became a popular lighting fuel. Modern versions of the kerosene lamp were later constructed by the Polish inventor Ignacy Łukasiewicz in 1853 Lemberg.</p>

<p>Before electricity became sufficiently widespread and economical to allow for general public use, gas was the most popular means of outdoor and indoor lighting in cities and suburbs. Early gas lights had to be lit manually, but many later designs are self-igniting. It must be remembered that the last gas lighting workers finished in London during the 1950s. Gas lighting competed with the development of electric light for many years. In addition, some urban historical districts retain gas street lighting, and gas lighting is used indoors or outdoors to create or preserve a nostalgic effect as well as a status symbol among the elite.</p>
<p>Among the economic impacts of gas lighting, was much longer work hours in factories. This was particularly important in Great Britain during the winter months when nights are significantly longer. Factories could even work continuously over 24 hours, resulting in increased production. Following successful commercialization, gas lighting spread to other countries.</p>
<p>Early lighting fuels consisted of olive oil, beeswax, fish oil, whale oil, sesame oil, nut oil, and similar substances. These were the most commonly used fuels until the late 18th century.</p>

<p>Architectural lighting design coincides with the rapid development of cities and electric light to achieve what was coined at the turn of the century as "nocturnal modernity". The image of a New York City night scape with electric light spurned the imagination. The potential of electric light as a new building “material” was recognized in the 1920s and became a useful design tool by the mid-century. Skillful lighting allowed for theatricality, narrative, and a new emphasis on structure and space. These ideas of using at the time new light source of electricity and mass-produced glass walls for <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">daylighting</a> were first experimented with by Josef Hoffmann of the Vienna Secession and Wiener Werkstätte, Walter Gropius' art school, known as Bauhaus of 1919 and Russian state art and technical school in Moscow, known as Higher Art and Technical Studios and abbreviated simply as Vkhutemas.</p>
<p>With the exodus from Europe after the second world war the Bahaus brought its modern ideas to the United States. Mies van der Rohe and Walter Gropius ideas were brought to America and quickly taken up by architects such as Phillip Johnson in the conceptual Glass House a supreme example of the use of daylighting and natural light. They both used the lighting designer Richard Kelly who was one of the field’s most influential figure in developing Lighting design as a professional in the United States. One of the oldest architectural lighting manufacturers in the world today is run by the fourth generation of the Baldinger family, Howard Baldinger of HB Architectural Lighting( <a class="external free" href="http://www.hblightinginc.com/" rel="nofollow">http://www.hblightinginc.com/</a> ). Started in 1893 in Pennsylvania, the Baldinger family created a name for themselves by their continuous execution of high quality manufacturing of architectural lighting. They produced lighting design by some of the most famous designers of the twentieth century. Their high quality craftsmanship continues to this day with some of the most highly regarded institutions among the Hospitality Industry, among others.</p>

<p>A fluorescent lamp or a fluorescent tube is a low pressure mercury-vapor gas-discharge lamp that uses fluorescence to produce visible light. An electric current in the gas excites mercury vapor which produces short-wave ultraviolet light that then causes a phosphor coating on the inside of the bulb to glow. A fluorescent lamp converts electrical energy into useful light much more efficiently than incandescent lamps. The luminous efficacy of a fluorescent light bulb can exceed 100 lumens per watt, several times the efficacy of an incandescent bulb with comparable light output.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Extensive luminaire photometric designing calls for consideration of the amount of functional light present, the energy expended, as well as the aesthetic impact supplied by the lighting system. Some buildings, like surgical centers and sports facilities, are primarily concerned with providing the appropriate amount of light for the associated task. Some buildings, like warehouses and office buildings, are primarily concerned with saving money through the energy efficiency of the lighting system. Other buildings, like casinos and theatres, are primarily concerned with enhancing the appearance and emotional impact of architecture through lighting systems. Therefore, it is important that the sciences of light production and luminaire photometrics are balanced with the artistic application of light as a medium in our built environment. These electrical lighting systems should also consider the impacts of, and ideally be integrated with, daylighting systems. Factors involved in lighting design are essentially the same as those discussed above in energy conservation analysis.</p>
<p>Architectural lighting design focuses on three fundamental aspects of the illumination of buildings or spaces. The first is the aesthetic appeal of a building, an aspect particularly important in the illumination of <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retail</a> environments. Secondly, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomic" title="Ergonomic">ergonomic</a> aspect: the measure of how much of a function the lighting plays. Thirdly is the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy efficiency</a> issue to ensure that light is not wasted by <a href="/wiki/Over-illumination" title="Over-illumination">over-illumination</a>, either by illuminating vacant spaces unnecessarily or by providing more light than needed for the <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> or the task.</p>
<p>Each of these three aspects is looked at in considerable detail when the lighting designer is at work. In aesthetic appeal, the lighting designer attempts to raise the general attractiveness of the design, measure whether it should be subtly blended into the background or whether it should stand out, and assess what kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emotions" title="Emotions">emotions</a> the lighting should evoke. The functional aspects of the project can encompass the need for the project to be visible (by night mostly, but also by day), the impact of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> on the project and <a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">safety</a> issues (<a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">glare</a>, colour confusion etc.).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_lighting">Day lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Day lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the Sun crosses the sky, it may appear to be red, orange, yellow or white depending on its position. The changing color of the Sun over the course of the day is mainly a result of <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a> of light and is not due to changes in black-body radiation. The blue color of the sky is caused by <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> of the sunlight from the atmosphere, which tends to scatter blue light more than red light.</p>
<p>Daylight has a spectrum similar to that of a black body with a correlated color temperature of 6,500 K (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIE_Standard_Illuminant_D65" title="CIE Standard Illuminant D65">D65</a> viewing standard) or 5,500 K (daylight-balanced photographic film standard).</p>
<p>For colors based on black-body theory, blue occurs at higher temperatures, while red occurs at lower, cooler, temperatures. This is the opposite of the cultural associations attributed to colors, in which "red" is "hot", and "blue" is "cold".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixtures">Fixtures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fixtures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Lighting fixtures come in a wide variety of styles for various functions. The most important functions are as a holder for the light source, to provide directed light and to avoid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_glare" title="Visual glare">visual glare</a>. Some are very plain and functional, while some are pieces of art in themselves. Nearly any material can be used, so long as it can tolerate the excess heat and is in keeping with safety codes.</p>
<p>An important property of light fixtures is the <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">luminous efficacy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wall-plug_efficiency" title="Wall-plug efficiency">wall-plug efficiency</a>, meaning the amount of usable light emanating from the fixture per used energy, usually measured in <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumen</a> per <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>. A fixture using replaceable light sources can also have its efficiency quoted as the percentage of light passed from the "bulb" to the surroundings. The more <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">transparent</a> the lighting fixture is, the higher efficacy. <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">Shading</a> the light will normally decrease efficiency but increase the directionality and the <a href="/wiki/Visual_comfort_probability" title="Visual comfort probability">visual comfort probability</a>.</p>

<p>Photometric studies (also sometimes referred to as "layouts" or "point by points") are often used to simulate lighting designs for projects before they are built or renovated. This enables architects, lighting designers, and engineers to determine whether a proposed lighting setup will deliver the amount of light intended. They will also be able to determine the contrast ratio between light and dark areas. In many cases these studies are referenced against <a href="/wiki/Illuminating_Engineering_Society_of_North_America" title="Illuminating Engineering Society of North America">IESNA</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Institution_of_Building_Services_Engineers" title="Chartered Institution of Building Services Engineers">CIBSE</a> recommended lighting practices for the type of application. Depending on the type of area, different design aspects may be emphasized for safety or practicality (i.e. such as maintaining uniform light levels, avoiding glare or highlighting certain areas). Specialized software is often used to create these, which typically combine the use of two-dimensional digital <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">CAD</a> drawings and lighting <a href="/wiki/Simulation_software" title="Simulation software">calculation software</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/AGi32" title="AGi32">AGi32or</a> Dialux).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">Color temperature</a> for white light sources also affects their use for certain applications. The color temperature of a white light source is the temperature in <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a> of a theoretical <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> emitter that most closely matches the spectral characteristics of the lamp. An incandescent bulb has a color temperature around 2800 to 3000 Kelvin; daylight is around 6400 Kelvin. Lower color temperature lamps have relatively more energy in the yellow and red part of the visible spectrum, while high color temperatures correspond to lamps with more of a blue-white appearance. For critical inspection or color matching tasks, or for retail displays of food and clothing, the color temperature of the lamps will be selected for the best overall lighting effect.</p>
<p>The <b>PH-lamp</b>s are a series of light fixtures designed by Danish designer and writer <a href="/wiki/Poul_Henningsen" title="Poul Henningsen">Poul Henningsen</a> from 1926 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The lamp is designed with multiple concentric shades to eliminate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_glare" title="Visual glare">visual glare</a>, only emitting reflected light, obscuring the light source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The lamps are still in production today<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Correlated_color_temperature">Correlated color temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Correlated color temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>color temperature</b> of a light source is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of an ideal <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black-body radiator</a> that radiates light of comparable <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hue</a> to that of the light source. Color temperature is a characteristic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> that has important applications in <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Videography" title="Videography">videography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a>, and other fields. In practice, color temperature is only meaningful for light sources that do in fact correspond somewhat closely to the radiation of some black body, i.e., those on a line from reddish/orange via yellow and more or less white to blueish white; it does not make sense to speak of the color temperature of, e.g., a green or a purple light. Color temperature is conventionally stated in the unit of absolute temperature, the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a>, having the unit symbol K.</p>
<p>For lighting building interiors, it is often important to take into account the color temperature of illumination. For example, a warmer (i.e., lower color temperature) light is often used in public areas to promote relaxation, while a cooler (higher color temperature) light is used to enhance concentration in offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>CCT dimming for LED technology is regarded as a difficult task, since binning, age and temperature drift effects of LEDs change the actual color value output. Here feedback loop systems are used for example with color sensors, to actively monitor and control the color output of multiple color mixing LEDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The color temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> emitted from an ideal <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> is defined as its surface temperature in Kelvin, or alternatively in <i><a href="/wiki/Mired" title="Mired">mireds</a></i> (micro-reciprocal Kelvin).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This permits the definition of a standard by which light sources are compared.</p>

<p>To the extent that a hot surface emits <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> but is not an ideal black-body radiator, the color temperature of the light is not the actual temperature of the surface. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp">incandescent lamp</a>'s light is thermal radiation, and the bulb approximates an ideal black-body radiator, so its color temperature is essentially the temperature of the filament.</p>
<p>Many other light sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LEDs</a> (light emitting diodes) emit light primarily by processes other than thermal radiation. This means that the emitted radiation does not follow the form of a <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck's law">black-body spectrum</a>. These sources are assigned what is known as a <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature#Correlated_color_temperature" title="Color temperature">correlated color temperature</a> (CCT). CCT is the color temperature of a black-body radiator which to <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">human color perception</a> most closely matches the light from the lamp. Because such an approximation is not required for incandescent light, the CCT for an incandescent light is simply its unadjusted temperature, derived from the comparison to a black-body radiator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For simple installations, hand-calculations based on tabular data can be used to provide an acceptable lighting design. More critical or optimized designs now routinely use mathematical modeling on a computer.</p>
<p>Based on the positions and mounting heights of the fixtures, and their photometric characteristics, the proposed lighting layout can be checked for uniformity and quantity of illumination. For larger projects or those with irregular floor plans, lighting design software can be used. Each fixture has its location entered, and the reflectance of walls, ceiling, and floors can be entered. The computer program will then produce a set of contour charts overlaid on the project floor plan, showing the light level to be expected at the working height. More advanced programs can include the effect of light from windows or skylights, allowing further optimization of the operating cost of the lighting installation. The amount of daylight received in an internal space can typically be analyzed by undertaking a <a href="/wiki/Daylight_factor" title="Daylight factor">daylight factor</a> calculation.</p>
<p>The Zonal Cavity Method is used as a basis for both hand, tabulated, and computer calculations. This method uses the reflectance coefficients of room surfaces to model the contribution to useful illumination at the working level of the room due to light reflected from the walls and the ceiling. Simplified photometric values are usually given by fixture manufacturers for use in this method.</p>
<p>Computer modeling of outdoor flood lighting usually proceeds directly from photometric data. The total lighting power of a lamp is divided into small solid angular regions. Each region is extended to the surface which is to be lit and the area calculated, giving the light power per unit of area. Where multiple lamps are used to illuminate the same area, each one's contribution is summed. Again the tabulated light levels (in lux or foot-candles) can be presented as contour lines of constant lighting value, overlaid on the project plan drawing. Hand calculations might only be required at a few points, but computer calculations allow a better estimate of the uniformity and lighting level.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_professional_organisations">International professional organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: International professional organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Illuminating Engineering Society of Australia and New Zealand was established in 1930 during the Great Depression.</p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Association_of_Lighting_Designers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Association of Lighting Designers (page does not exist)">International Association of Lighting Designers</a> (IALD) was founded in 1969, and its current mission is "to serve the IALD worldwide membership by promoting the visible success of its members in practicing lighting design." The organization created a new attitude towards the profession and raised the profile of architectural lighting design, one its principal goals.</p>
<p>The European Lighting Designers' Association (ELDA, later ELDA+) was formed in 1993; in 2007, ELDA changed its name to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_Lighting_Designers_Association" title="Professional Lighting Designers Association">Professional Lighting Designers Association</a> (PLDA). The IALD and PLDA are the main authorities regarding lighting design in architecture.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Illuminating_Engineering_Society_of_North_America" title="Illuminating Engineering Society of North America">Illuminating Engineering Society of North America</a> (IESNA) seeks to improve the lighted environment by bringing together those with lighting knowledge and by translating that knowledge into actions that benefit the public.</p>
<p>The National Council on Qualifications for the Lighting Professions (NCQLP) is a non-profit organization founded in 1991 to serve and protect the well-being of the public through effective and efficient lighting practice. Through a peer-review process, the NCQLP establishes the education, experience and examination requirements for baseline certification across the lighting professions. The NCQLP has established a certification process by which practitioners in lighting and related fields, through testing, demonstrate their knowledge and experience across the lighting professions. Those who successfully complete the NCQLP Lighting Certification Examination are entitled to use the appellation LC (Lighting Certified) after their name for professional purposes.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Illumination" title="International Commission on Illumination">International Commission on Illumination</a> (CIE) is an organization "devoted to international cooperation and exchange of information among its member countries on all matters relating to the science and art of lighting." CIE works globally to develop and publish lighting design standardization and best-practice documents.</p>
<p>The Professional Lighting &amp; Sound Association (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PLASA" title="PLASA">PLASA</a>) represents the interests of many lighting designers and manufacturers, several of which are involved in the Architectural lighting market. PLASA is UK orientated, but does represent companies on a European and International level.</p>
<p>There are many more nationally-based organizations such as the Schweizerische Licht Gesellschaft (SLG) in Switzerland, the Association des Concepteurs Lumière et Éclairagistes (ACE) in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the Hellenic Illumination Committee (HIC) in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and the Associazione Professionisti dell'Illuminazione (APIL) in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.</p>

It produces, for all practical purposes, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> orange/yellow light, which gives a similarly monochromatic perceprtion of any illuminated scene. Low-pressure sodium lights are favoured for public lighting by astronomers, since the <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> that they generate can be easily filtered, contrary to broadband or continuous spectra.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incandescent_light_bulb">Incandescent light bulb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Incandescent light bulb">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">Incandescent light bulb</a></div>
<p>The modern incandescent lightbulb, with a coiled filament of tungsten, was commercialized in the 1920s developed from the carbon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_filament" title="Electrical filament">filament</a> lamp introduced in about 1880. As well as bulbs for normal illumination, there is a very wide range, including low voltage, low-power types often used as components in equipment, but now largely displaced by LEDs</p>
<p>There is currently interest in banning some types of filament lamp in some countries, such as Australia planning to ban standard incandescent light bulbs by 2010, because they are inefficient at converting electricity to light. Sri Lanka has already banned importing filament bulbs because of high use of electricity and less light. Less than 3% of the input energy is converted into usable light. Nearly all of the input energy ends up as heat that, in warm climates, must then be removed from the building by <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>, often resulting in more energy consumption. In colder climates where heating and lighting is required during the cold and dark winter months, the heat byproduct has at least some value.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogen_lamp">Halogen lamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Halogen lamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Halogen_lamp" title="Halogen lamp">Halogen lamp</a></div>
<p>Halogen lamps are usually much smaller than standard incandescents, because for successful operation a bulb temperature over 200 °C is generally necessary. For this reason, most have a bulb of fused silica (quartz), but sometimes aluminosilicate glass. This is often sealed inside an additional layer of glass. The outer glass is a safety precaution, reducing UV emission and because halogen bulbs can occasionally explode during operation. One reason is if the quartz bulb has oily residue from <a href="/wiki/Fingerprint" title="Fingerprint">fingerprints</a>. The risk of burns or fire is also greater with bare bulbs, leading to their prohibition in some places unless enclosed by the luminaire.</p>
<p>Those designed for 12 V or 24 V operation have compact filaments, useful for good optical control, also they have higher efficiencies (lumens per watt) and better lives than non halogen types. The light output remains almost constant throughout life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluorescent_lamp">Fluorescent lamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Fluorescent lamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">Fluorescent lamp</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting">Fluorescent</a> lamps consist of a glass tube that contains mercury vapour or argon under low pressure. Electricity flowing through the tube causes the gases to give off ultraviolet energy. The inside of the tubes are coated with <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> that give off visible light when struck by ultraviolet energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> have much higher efficiency than Incandescent lamps. For the same amount of light generated, they typically use around one-quarter to one-third the power of an incandescent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LED_lamp">LED lamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: LED lamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_lighting" title="Solid-state lighting">Solid-state lighting</a></div>
<p>Solid state <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diodes" title="Light-emitting diodes">LEDs</a> have been popular as indicator lights since the 1970s. In recent years, <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">efficacy</a> and output have risen to the point where LEDs are now being used in niche lighting applications.</p>
<p>Indicator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diodes" title="Light-emitting diodes">LEDs</a> are known for their extremely long life, up to 100,000 hours, but lighting LEDs are operated much less conservatively (due to high LED cost per watt), and consequently have much shorter lives.</p>
<p>Due to the relatively high cost per watt, LED lighting is most useful at very low powers, typically for lamp assemblies of under 10 W. LEDs are currently most useful and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-effective" title="Cost-effective">cost-effective</a> in low power applications, such as <a href="/wiki/Nightlight" title="Nightlight">nightlights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">flashlights</a>. Colored LEDs can also be used for <a href="/wiki/Accent_lighting" title="Accent lighting">accent lighting</a>, such as for glass objects, and even in fake ice cubes for drinks at parties. They are also being increasingly used as holiday lighting.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diodes" title="Light-emitting diodes">LED</a> efficiencies vary over a very wide range. Some have lower efficiency than filament lamps, and some significantly higher. LED performance in this respect is prone to being misinterpreted, as the inherent directionality of LEDs gives them a much higher light intensity in one direction per given total light output.</p>
<p>Single color <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diodes" title="Light-emitting diodes">LEDs</a> are well developed technology, but white LEDs at time of writing still have some unresolved issues:</p>
<ul>
<li>CRI is not particularly good, resulting in less than accurate color rendition.</li>
<li>The light distribution from the phosphor does not fully match the distribution of light from the LED die, so color temperature varies at differing angles.</li>
<li>Phosphor performance degrades over time, resulting in change of color temperature and falling output. With some LEDs degradation can be quite fast.</li>
<li>Limited heat tolerance means that the amount of power packable into a lamp assembly is a fraction of the power usable in a similarly sized incandescent lamp.</li>
</ul>
<p>LED technology is useful for lighting designers because of its low power consumption, low heat generation, instantaneous on-and-off control, and in the case of single color LEDs, continuity of color throughout the life of the diode and relatively low cost of manufacture.</p>
<p>In the last few years, software has been developed to merge lighting and video by enabling lighting designers to stream video content to their LED fixtures, creating low resolution video walls.</p>
<p>For general domestic lighting, total cost of ownership of LED lighting is still much higher than for other well established lighting types.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Architectural_lighting_design#Dubious" title="Talk:Architectural lighting design">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_arc_lamp">Carbon arc lamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Carbon arc lamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">Arc lamp</a></div>
<p>Carbon arc lamps consist of two carbon rod <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> in open air, supplied by a current-limiting <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">ballast</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a> is struck by touching the rods then separating them. The ensuing arc heats the carbon tips to white heat. These lamps have higher efficiency than filament lamps, but the carbon rods are short lived and require constant adjustment in use. The lamps produce significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-violet" title="Ultra-violet">ultra-violet</a> output, they require ventilation when used indoors, and due to their intensity they need protecting from direct sight.</p>
<p>Invented by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> around 1805, the carbon arc was the first practical electric light. They were used commercially beginning in the 1870s for large building and street lighting until they were superseded in the early 20th century by the incandescent light. Carbon arc lamps operate at high powers and produce high intensity white light. They also are a point source of light. They remained in use in limited applications that required these properties, such as <a href="/wiki/Movie_projector" title="Movie projector">movie projectors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stage_lighting" title="Stage lighting">stage lighting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Searchlight" title="Searchlight">searchlights</a>, until after World War 2.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discharge_lamp">Discharge lamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Architectural_lighting_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Discharge lamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>discharge lamp</b> has a glass or silica envelope containing two metal <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> separated by a gas. Gases used include, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_halide" title="Metal halide">metal halide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>.</p>
<p>The core operating principle is much the same as the carbon arc lamp, but the term 'arc lamp' is normally used to refer to carbon arc lamps, with more modern types of gas discharge lamp normally called discharge lamps.</p>
<p>With some discharge lamps, very high voltage is used to strike the arc. This requires an electrical circuit called an igniter, which is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballast_(electrical)" title="Ballast (electrical)">ballast</a> circuitry. After the arc is struck, the internal resistance of the lamp drops to a low level, and the ballast limits the current to the operating current. Without a ballast, excess current would flow, causing rapid destruction of the lamp.</p>
<p>Some lamp types contain a little neon, which permits striking at normal running voltage, with no external ignition circuitry. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_lamp" title="Sodium vapor lamp">Low pressure sodium lamps</a> operate this way.</p>
<p>The simplest ballasts are just an inductor, and are chosen where cost is the deciding factor, such as street lighting. More advanced electronic ballasts may be designed to maintain constant light output over the life of the lamp, may drive the lamp with a square wave to maintain completely flicker-free output, and shut down in the event of certain faults.</p>
