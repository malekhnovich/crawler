<p><b>Scrum</b> is an iterative and incremental <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile software development</a> framework for managing product development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumalliance_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-scrumalliance-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunther_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunther-2">[2]</a></sup> It defines "a flexible, <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> product development strategy where a development team works as a unit to reach a common goal",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-3">[3]</a></sup> challenges assumptions of the "traditional, sequential approach"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-3">[3]</a></sup> to product development, and enables teams to self-organize by encouraging physical <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">co-location</a> or close online collaboration of all team members, as well as daily face-to-face communication among all team members and disciplines involved.</p>
<p>A key principle of Scrum is its recognition that during <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">product development</a>, the customers can change their minds about what they want and need (often called requirements volatility<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>), and that unpredicted challenges cannot be easily addressed in a traditional predictive or planned manner. As such, Scrum adopts an evidence-based <a href="/wiki/Empirical_process_(process_control_model)" title="Empirical process (process control model)">empirical approach</a>—accepting that the problem cannot be fully understood or defined, focusing instead on maximizing the team's ability to deliver quickly, to respond to emerging requirements and to adapt to evolving technologies and changes in market conditions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hirotaka_Takeuchi" title="Hirotaka Takeuchi">Hirotaka Takeuchi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ikujiro_Nonaka" title="Ikujiro Nonaka">Ikujiro Nonaka</a> introduced the word "scrum" as a term in the context of product development in 1986 in their article on the <i>New New Product Development Game</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2016)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Takeuchi and Nonaka later argued in <i>The Knowledge Creating Company</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that it is a form of "organizational knowledge creation, [...] especially good at bringing about innovation continuously, incrementally and spirally".</p>
<p>The authors described a new approach to commercial <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">product-development</a> that would increase speed and flexibility, based on case studies from manufacturing firms in the automotive, photocopier and printer <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-3">[3]</a></sup> They called this the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">holistic</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a> approach</i>, as the whole process is performed by one <a href="/wiki/Cross-functional_team" title="Cross-functional team">cross-functional team</a> across multiple overlapping phases, where the team "tries to go the distance as a unit, passing the ball back and forth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-3">[3]</a></sup> (In <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby football</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(rugby)" title="Scrum (rugby)"><i>scrum</i></a> refers to a tight-packed formation of players with their heads down who attempt to gain possession of the ball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>In the early 1990s <a href="/wiki/Ken_Schwaber" title="Ken Schwaber">Ken Schwaber</a> used what would become Scrum at his company, Advanced Development Methods; while <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Sutherland" title="Jeff Sutherland">Jeff Sutherland</a>, John Scumniotales and Jeff McKenna, developed a similar approach at Easel Corporation, referring to it using the single word <i>Scrum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1995 Sutherland and Schwaber jointly presented a paper describing the <i>Scrum methodology</i> at the Business Object Design and Implementation Workshop held as part of <a href="/wiki/OOPSLA" title="OOPSLA">Object-Oriented Programming, Systems, Languages &amp; Applications '95</a> (OOPSLA '95) in Austin, Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Over the following years, Schwaber and Sutherland collaborated to combine this material—with their experience and evolving good practice—to develop what became known as Scrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 Schwaber worked with Mike Beedle to describe the method in the book <i>Agile Software Development with Scrum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Scrum's approach to planning and managing product development involves bringing <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a> authority to the level of operation properties and certainties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwaber_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-11">[11]</a></sup> Although the word "scrum" is not an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>, some companies implementing the process have been known<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to spell it with capital letters as "SCRUM". This may be due to one of Ken Schwaber's early papers, which capitalized SCRUM in the title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwaber_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 Schwaber with others founded the Scrum Alliance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and set up the Certified Scrum Master program and its derivatives. Schwaber left the Scrum Alliance in late 2009 and founded Scrum.org.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Values">Values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scrum is a feedback-driven empirical approach which is, like all empirical process control, underpinned by the three pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation. All work within the Scrum framework should be visible to those responsible for the outcome: the process, the workflow, progress, etc. In order to make these things visible, Scrum Teams need to frequently inspect the product being developed and how well the team is working. With frequent inspection, the team can spot when their work deviates outside of acceptable limits and adapt their process or the product under development.</p>
<p>These three pillars require trust and openness in the team, which the following five values of Scrum enable:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Commitment</dt>
<dd>Team members individually commit to achieving their team goals, each and every Sprint.</dd>
<dt>Courage</dt>
<dd>Team members know they have the courage to work through conflict and challenges together so that they can do the right thing.</dd>
<dt>Focus</dt>
<dd>Team members focus exclusively on their team goals and the Sprint Backlog; there should be no work done other than through their backlog.</dd>
<dt>Openness</dt>
<dd>Team members and their stakeholders agree to be transparent about their work and any challenges they face.</dd>
<dt>Respect</dt>
<dd>Team members respect each other to be technically capable and to work with good intent.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles">Roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three core roles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> in the Scrum framework. These core roles are ideally <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">colocated</a> to deliver potentially shippable Product Increments. They represent the <i>Scrum Team</i>. Although other roles involved with product development may be encountered, Scrum does not define any team roles other than those described below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_Owner">Product Owner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Product Owner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Product Owner represents the product's <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_the_customer" title="Voice of the customer">voice of the customer</a>; and is accountable for ensuring that the team delivers value to the business. The Product Owner writes customer-centric items (typically <a href="/wiki/User_story" title="User story">user stories</a>), prioritizes them based on importance and dependencies, and adds them to the <a href="#Product_backlog">Product Backlog</a>. Scrum Teams should have one Product Owner. This role should not be combined with that of the Scrum Master. The Product Owner should focus on the business side of product development and spend the majority of their time liaising with stakeholders and should not dictate how the team reaches a technical solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="an anon gave the concern that the reference gives the opinion of a single author and could hardly be considered authoritative (February 2016)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This role is equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Customer_representative" title="Customer representative">customer representative</a> role in some other agile frameworks such as <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a> (XP).</p>
<p>Communication is a core responsibility of the Product Owner. The ability to convey priorities and empathize with team members and stakeholders is vital to steer product development in the right direction. Product Owners bridge the communication gap between the team and its stakeholders. They serve as a proxy for stakeholders to the team and as a team representative to the overall stakeholder community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pichler.2C_Roman_2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pichler.2C_Roman_2010-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the face of the team to the stakeholders, the following are some of the communication tasks of the Product Owner to the stakeholders:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>demonstrates the solution to key stakeholders who were not present at a Sprint Review;</li>
<li>defines and announces releases;</li>
<li>communicates team status;</li>
<li>organizes milestone reviews;</li>
<li>educates stakeholders in the development process;</li>
<li>negotiates priorities, scope, funding, and schedule;</li>
<li>ensures that the Product Backlog is visible, transparent, and clear.</li>
</ul>
<p>Empathy is a key attribute for a Product Owner to have—the ability to put one's self in another's shoes. A Product Owner converses with different stakeholders, who have a variety of backgrounds, job roles, and objectives. A Product Owner must be able to see from these different points of view. To be effective, it is wise for a Product Owner to know the level of detail the audience needs. The Development Team needs thorough feedback and specifications so they can build a product up to expectation, while an executive sponsor may just need summaries of progress. Providing more information than necessary may lose stakeholder interest and waste time. A direct means of communication is the most preferred by seasoned agile Product Owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Product Owner’s ability to communicate effectively is also enhanced by being skilled in techniques that identify stakeholder needs, negotiate priorities between stakeholder interests, and collaborate with developers to ensure effective implementation of requirements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_Team">Development Team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development Team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Development Team is responsible for delivering potentially shippable increments (PSIs) of product at the end of each Sprint (the Sprint goal). A team is made up of 3–9 individuals who do the actual work (analyse, design, develop, test, technical communication, document, etc.). Development Teams are cross-functional, with all of the skills as a team necessary to create a Product Increment. The Development Team in Scrum is self-organizing, even though there may be some interaction with a project management office (PMOs).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scrum_Master">Scrum Master</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scrum Master">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scrum is facilitated by a Scrum Master, who is accountable for removing impediments to the ability of the team to deliver the product goals and deliverables. The Scrum Master is not a traditional <a href="/wiki/Team_leader" title="Team leader">team lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Project_manager" title="Project manager">project manager</a>, but acts as a buffer between the team and any distracting influences. The Scrum Master ensures that the Scrum framework is followed. The Scrum Master helps to ensure the team follows the agreed processes in the Scrum framework, often facilitates key sessions, and encourages the team to improve. The role has also been referred to as a <i>team facilitator<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Servant_leadership" title="Servant leadership">servant-leader</a></i> to reinforce these dual perspectives.</p>
<p>The core responsibilities of a Scrum Master include (but are not limited to):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Helping the Product Owner maintain the Product Backlog in a way that ensures the needed work is well understood so the team can continually make forward progress</li>
<li>Helping the team to determine the <i>definition of done</i> for the product, with input from key stakeholders</li>
<li>Coaching the team, within the Scrum principles, in order to deliver high-quality features for its product</li>
<li>Promoting self-organization within the team</li>
<li>Helping the Scrum Team to avoid or remove impediments to its progress, whether internal or external to the team</li>
<li>Facilitating team events to ensure regular progress</li>
<li>Educating key stakeholders in the product on Scrum principles</li>
</ul>
<p>One of the ways the Scrum Master role differs from a project manager is that the latter may have <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">people management</a> responsibilities and the Scrum Master does not. Scrum does not formally recognise the role of project manager, as traditional command and control tendencies would cause difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumprimer_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-scrumprimer-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Workflow">Workflow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Workflow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>A Sprint (or iteration) is the basic unit of development in Scrum. The Sprint is a <a href="/wiki/Timeboxing" title="Timeboxing"><i>timeboxed</i></a> effort; that is, it is restricted to a specific duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sprint_2009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sprint_2009-21">[21]</a></sup> The duration is fixed in advance for each Sprint and is normally between one week and one month, with two weeks being the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwaber_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each Sprint starts with a Sprint Planning event that aims to define a Sprint Backlog, identify the work for the Sprint, and make an estimated commitment for the Sprint goal. Each Sprint ends with a Sprint Review and Sprint Retrospective,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-7">[7]</a></sup> that reviews progress to show to stakeholders and identify lessons and improvements for the next Sprints.</p>
<p>Scrum emphasizes working product at the end of the Sprint that is really <i>done</i>. In the case of software, this likely includes that the software has been fully integrated, tested and documented, and is potentially shippable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumprimer_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-scrumprimer-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning">Planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the beginning of a Sprint, the Scrum Team holds a <i>Sprint Planning event</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sprint_2009_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sprint_2009-21">[21]</a></sup> to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Communicate the scope of work that is intended to be done during that Sprint</li>
<li>Select Product Backlog Items that can be completed in one Sprint</li>
<li>Prepare the Sprint Backlog that details the work needed to finish the selected Product Backlog Items</li>
<li>Time-boxed to a four-hour limit for a two-week Sprint (pro rata for other Sprint durations) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>During the first half, the whole Scrum Team (Development Team, Scrum Master, and Product Owner) selects the Product Backlog Items might be achievable in that Sprint</li>
<li>During the second half, the Development Team decomposes the work items (tasks) required to deliver those Product Backlog Items; resulting in a confirmed <i>Sprint Backlog</i>
<ul>
<li>Some Product Backlog Items may be split or deprioritized if the identified work is not achievable in that Sprint</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Once the Development Team prepares the <i>Sprint Backlog</i>, they commit (usually by voting) to deliver tasks within the Sprint.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daily_Scrum">Daily Scrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Daily Scrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/220px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/330px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/440px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Daily Scrum in the computing room. This centralized location helps the team start on time.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Each day during a Sprint, the team holds a <i>Daily Scrum</i> (or <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_meeting" title="Stand-up meeting"><i>stand-up</i></a>) with specific guidelines:</p>
<ul>
<li>All members of the Development Team come prepared. The Daily Scrum...
<ul>
<li>...starts precisely on time even if some Development Team members are missing</li>
<li>...should happen at the same time and place every day</li>
<li>...is limited (<a href="/wiki/Timeboxing" title="Timeboxing">timeboxed</a>) to fifteen minutes</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Anyone is welcome, though normally only Scrum Team roles contribute.</li>
<li>During the Daily Scrum, each team-member answers three questions:
<ul>
<li>What did I do yesterday that helped the Development Team meet the Sprint goal?</li>
<li>What will I do today to help the Development Team meet the Sprint goal?</li>
<li>Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the Development Team from meeting the Sprint goal?</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Any impediment (e.g., stumbling block, risk, issue, delayed dependency, assumption proved unfounded) identified in the Daily Scrum should be captured by the Scrum Master and displayed on the team's Scrum board or a shared ROAM board, with an agreed person designated to working toward a resolution (outside of the Daily Scrum). No detailed discussions should happen during the Daily Scrum.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Review_and_retrospective">Review and retrospective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Review and retrospective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of a Sprint, the team holds two events: the <i>Sprint Review</i> and the <i>Sprint Retrospective</i>.</p>
<p>At the <b>Sprint Review</b>, the team:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reviews the work that was completed and the planned work that was not completed</li>
<li>Presents the completed work to the stakeholders (a.k.a. the <i>demo</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Guidelines for Sprint Reviews:</p>
<ul>
<li>Incomplete work cannot be demonstrated</li>
<li>The recommended duration is two hours for a two-week Sprint (pro-rata for other Sprint durations)</li>
</ul>
<p>At the <b>Sprint Retrospective</b>, the team:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reflects on the past Sprint</li>
<li>Identifies and agrees on continuous process improvement actions</li>
</ul>
<p>Guidelines for Sprint Retrospectives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Two main questions are asked in the Sprint Retrospective: What went well during the Sprint? What could be improved in the next Sprint?</li>
<li>The recommended duration is one-and-a-half hours for a two-week Sprint (pro-rata for other Sprint durations)</li>
<li>This event is facilitated by the Scrum Master</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions">Extensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following activities are commonly done, although not considered by all as a core part of Scrum:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Backlog_refinement">Backlog refinement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Backlog refinement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Backlog refinement (once called <i>backlog grooming</i>) is the ongoing process of reviewing Product Backlog Items and checking that they are appropriately prioritised and prepared in a way that makes them clear and executable for teams once they enter Sprints via the Sprint Planning activity. Product Backlog Items may be broken into multiple smaller ones; acceptance criteria may be clarified; and dependencies, investigation, and preparatory work may be identified and agreed as <i>technical spikes</i>.</p>
<p>Although not originally a core Scrum practice, backlog refinement has been adopted as a way of managing the quality of Product Backlog Items entering a Sprint, with a recommended duration of up to 10% of the Sprint capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scrum_of_Scrums">Scrum of Scrums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scrum of Scrums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>Scrum of Scrums</i> is a technique to operate Scrum at scale, for multiple teams working on the same product, allowing them to discuss progress on their interdependencies, focusing on how to coordinate delivering software,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumofscrums_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-scrumofscrums-23">[23]</a></sup> especially on areas of overlap and integration. Depending on the cadence (timing) of the Scrum of Scrums, the relevant Daily Scrum for each Scrum Team ends by designating one member as an <i>ambassador</i> to participate in the Scrum of Scrums with ambassadors from other teams. Depending on the context, the ambassadors may be technical contributors or each team's Scrum Master.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumofscrums_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-scrumofscrums-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rather than simply a progress update, the Scrum of Scrums should focus on how teams are collectively working to resolve, mitigate, or accept any risks, impediments, dependencies, and assumptions (RIDAs) that have been identified. The Scrum of Scrums tracks these RIDAs via a backlog of its own, such as a ROAM board,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which typically leads to greater coordination and collaboration between teams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumofscrums_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-scrumofscrums-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>This should run similar to a Daily Scrum, with each ambassador answering the following four questions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>What risks, impediments, dependencies, and assumptions has your team moved on since we last met?</li>
<li>What risks, impediments, dependencies, and assumptions will your team move on before we meet again?</li>
<li>Are there any new risks, impediments, dependencies, and assumptions slowing your team down or getting in their way?</li>
<li>Are you about to introduce a new risk, impediment, dependency, and assumption that will get in another team's way?</li>
</ul>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Sutherland" title="Jeff Sutherland">Jeff Sutherland</a> commented,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumofscrums_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-scrumofscrums-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Since I originally defined the Scrum of Scrums (Ken Schwaber was at IDX working with me), I can definitively say the Scrum of Scrums is not a "meta Scrum". The Scrum of Scrums as I have used it is responsible for delivering the working software of all teams to the Definition of Done at the end of the Sprint, or for releases during the Sprint. PatientKeeper delivered to production four times per Sprint. Ancestry.com delivers to production 220 times per two-week Sprint. Hubspot delivers live software 100-300 times a day. The Scrum of Scrums Master is held accountable for making this work. So the Scrum of Scrums is an operational delivery mechanism.</i></p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_Backlog">Product Backlog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Product Backlog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Product Backlog</i> comprises an ordered list of <i><a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">requirements</a></i> that a Scrum Team maintains for a <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">product</a>. It consists of <a href="/wiki/Software_feature" title="Software feature">features</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">bug fixes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-functional_requirement" title="Non-functional requirement">non-functional requirements</a>, etc.—whatever must be done to successfully deliver a viable product. The Product Owner orders the <i>Product Backlog Items</i> (PBIs) based on considerations such as risk, business value, dependencies, and date needed.</p>
<p>Items added to a backlog are commonly written in story format. The Product Backlog is <i>what</i> will be delivered, ordered into the sequence in which it should be delivered. It is visible to everyone but may only be changed with the consent of the Product Owner, who is ultimately responsible for ordering Product Backlog Items for the Development Team to choose.</p>
<p>The Product Backlog contains the Product Owner's assessment of business value and the Development Team's assessment of development effort, which are often, but not always, stated in <a href="/wiki/Planning_poker" title="Planning poker">story points</a> using a rounded <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci_number" title="Fibonacci number">Fibonacci</a> sequence. These estimates help the Product Owner to gauge the timeline and may influence ordering of Product Backlog Items; for example, if the "add spellcheck" and "add table support" features have the same business value, the Product Owner may schedule earlier delivery of the one with the lower development effort (because the <a href="/wiki/Return_on_investment" title="Return on investment">return on investment</a> is higher) or the one with higher development effort (because it is more complex or riskier, and they want to retire that risk earlier).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Product Backlog and the business value of each Product Backlog Item is the responsibility of the Product Owner. The size (i.e. estimated complexity or effort) of each item is, however, determined by the Development Team, who contributes by sizing in story points or in estimated hours.</p>
<p>There is a common misunderstanding that only <a href="/wiki/User_story" title="User story">user stories</a> are allowed in a Product Backlog. By contrast, Scrum is neutral on requirement techniques. As the <i>Scrum Primer</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumprimer_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-scrumprimer-20">[20]</a></sup> states,</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Product Backlog Items are articulated in any way that is clear and sustainable. Contrary to popular misunderstanding, the Product Backlog does not contain "user stories"; it simply contains items. Those items can be expressed as user stories, <a href="/wiki/Use_case" title="Use case">use cases</a>, or any other requirements approach that the group finds useful. But whatever the approach, most items should focus on delivering value to customers.</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Scrum advocates that the role of Product Owner be assigned. The Product Owner is responsible for maximizing the value of the product. The Product Owner gathers input and takes feedback from, and is lobbied by, many people, but ultimately makes the call on what gets built.</p>
<p>The Product Backlog:</p>
<ul>
<li>Captures requests to modify a product—including new features, replacing old features, removing features, and fixing issues</li>
<li>Ensures the Development Team has work that maximizes business benefit to the Product Owner</li>
</ul>
<p>Typically, the Product Owner and the Scrum Team come together and write down everything that must be prioritized, and this becomes content for the first Sprint—which is a block of time meant for focused work on selected items that can be accommodated within a timeframe. The Product Backlog can evolve as new information surfaces about the product and about its customers, and so later Sprints may address new work.</p>
<p>The following items typically comprise a Product Backlog: features, bugs, technical work, and knowledge acquisition. A feature is "wanted", while a bug is "unintended" or "unwanted" (but may not be necessarily a defective thing). An example of technical work would be: "run virus check on all developers' workstations". An example of knowledge acquisition could be a Product Backlog Item about researching Wordpress plugin libraries and making a selection.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A Product Backlog, in its simplest form, is merely a list of items to work on. Having well-established rules about how work is added, removed and ordered helps the whole team make better decisions about how to change the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Product Owner prioritizes which Product Backlog Items are most needed. The team then chooses which items they can complete in the coming Sprint. On the Scrum board, the team moves items from the Product Backlog to the Sprint Backlog, which is the list of items they will build. Conceptually, it is ideal for the team to only select what they think they can accomplish from the top of the list, but it is not unusual to see in practice that teams are able to take lower-priority items from the list along with the top ones selected. This normally happens because there is time left within the Sprint to accommodate more work. Items at the top of the backlog, the items to work on first, should be broken down into stories that are suitable for the Development Team to work on. The further down the backlog goes, the less refined the items should be. As Schwaber and Beedle put it "The lower the priority, the less detail, until you can barely make out the backlog item."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwaber_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the team works through the backlog, it must be assumed that "changes in the world can happen"—the team can learn about new market opportunities to take advantage of, competitor threats that arise, and feedback from customers that can change the way the product was meant to work. All of these new ideas tend to trigger the team to adapt the backlog to incorporate new knowledge. This is part of the fundamental mindset of an agile team. The world changes, the backlog is never finished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pichler.2C_Roman_2010_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pichler.2C_Roman_2010-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sprint_Backlog">Sprint Backlog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sprint Backlog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scrum_task_board.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1183" data-file-width="887" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Scrum_task_board.jpg/220px-Scrum_task_board.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Scrum_task_board.jpg/330px-Scrum_task_board.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Scrum_task_board.jpg/440px-Scrum_task_board.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scrum_task_board.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Scrum task board</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Sprint Backlog</i> is the list of work the Development Team must address during the next Sprint. The list is derived by the Scrum Team selecting Product Backlog Items from the top of the Product Backlog until the Development Team feels it has enough work to fill the Sprint. This is done by the Development Team asking "Can we also do this?" and adding Product Backlog Items to the Sprint Backlog. The Development Team should keep in mind its past performance assessing its capacity for the new Sprint, and use this as a guide line of how much "effort" they can complete.</p>
<p>The Product Backlog Items may be broken down into tasks by the Development Team. Tasks on the Sprint Backlog are never assigned; rather, tasks are signed up for by the team members as needed according to the set priority and the Development Team member skills. This promotes self-organization of the Development Team, and developer buy-in.</p>
<p>The Sprint Backlog is the property of the Development Team, and all included estimates are provided by the Development Team. Often an accompanying <i>task board</i> is used to see and change the state of the tasks of the current Sprint, like "to do", "in progress" and "done".</p>
<p>Once a Sprint Backlog is committed, no additional work can be added to the Sprint Backlog except by the team. Once a Sprint has been delivered, the Product Backlog is analyzed and reprioritized if necessary, and the next set of functionality is selected for the next Sprint.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_Increment">Product Increment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Product Increment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>increment</i> (or <i>potentially shippable increment</i>, PSI) is the sum of all the Product Backlog Items completed during a Sprint and all previous Sprints. At the end of a Sprint, the increment must be complete, according to the Scrum Team's definition of done (DoD), and in a usable condition regardless of whether the Product Owner decides to actually release it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions_2">Extensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Extensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following artifacts are commonly used, although not considered by all as a core part of Scrum:</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sprint_burn-down_chart">Sprint burn-down chart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sprint burn-down chart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SampleBurndownChart.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="853" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SampleBurndownChart.png/220px-SampleBurndownChart.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SampleBurndownChart.png/330px-SampleBurndownChart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SampleBurndownChart.png/440px-SampleBurndownChart.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SampleBurndownChart.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sample burn-down chart for a completed Sprint, showing remaining effort at the end of each day.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Burn_down_chart" title="Burn down chart">Burn down chart</a></div>
<p>The Sprint burn-down chart is a public displayed chart showing remaining work in the Sprint Backlog. Updated every day, it gives a simple view of the Sprint progress. It also provides quick visualizations for reference. The horizontal axis of the Sprint burn-down chart shows the days in a Sprint, while the vertical axis shows the amount of work remaining each day (typically representing estimate of hours of work remaining).</p>
<p>During Sprint Planning the ideal burndown chart is plotted. Then, during the Sprint, each member picks up tasks from the Sprint Backlog and works on them. At the end of the day, they update the remaining hours for tasks to be completed. In such a way, the actual burndown chart is updated day by day.</p>
<p>It should not be confused with an <a href="/wiki/Earned_value_management" title="Earned value management">earned value chart</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_burn-up_chart">Release burn-up chart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Release burn-up chart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SampleBurnupChart.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="632" data-file-width="986" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/220px-SampleBurnupChart.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/330px-SampleBurnupChart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/440px-SampleBurnupChart.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SampleBurnupChart.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sample burn-up chart for a release, showing scope completed each Sprint.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The release burn-up chart is a way for the team to provide visibility and track progress toward a release. Updated at the end of each Sprint, it shows progress toward delivering a forecast scope. The horizontal axis of the release burn-up chart shows the Sprints in a release, while the vertical axis shows the amount of work completed at the end of each Sprint (typically representing cumulative story points of work completed). Progress is plotted as a line that grows up to meet a horizontal line that represents the forecast scope; often shown with a forecast, based on progress to date, that indicates how much scope might be completed by a given release date or how many sprints it will take to complete the given scope.</p>
<p>The release burn-up chart makes it easy to see how much work has been completed, how much work has been added or removed (if the horizontal scope line moves), and how much work is left to be done.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following terms are often used in product development using the Scrum framework:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Scrum Team</dt>
<dd>Product Owner, Scrum Master and Development Team</dd>
<dt>Product Owner</dt>
<dd>The person responsible for maintaining the Product Backlog by representing the interests of the stakeholders, and ensuring the value of the work the Development Team does.</dd>
<dt>Scrum Master</dt>
<dd>The person responsible for the Scrum framework, making sure it is used correctly and maximizing its benefits.</dd>
<dt>Development Team</dt>
<dd>A cross-functional group of people responsible for delivering potentially shippable increments of product at the end of every Sprint.</dd>
<dt>Sprint burn-down chart</dt>
<dd>Daily progress for a Sprint over the Sprint's length.</dd>
<dt>Release burn-down chart</dt>
<dd>Feature level progress of completed Product Backlog Items in the Product Backlog.</dd>
<dt>Product Backlog (PBL) list</dt>
<dd>A prioritized list of high-level requirements.</dd>
<dt>Sprint Backlog (SBL) list</dt>
<dd>A prioritized list of tasks to complete during the Sprint.</dd>
<dt>Sprint</dt>
<dd>A time period (typically 1–4 weeks) in which development occurs on a set of Product Backlog Items that the team has committed to—commonly referred to as a time-box or iteration</dd>
<dt>Spike</dt>
<dd>A time boxed period used to research a concept or create a simple prototype. Spikes can either be planned to take place in between Sprints or, for larger teams, a spike might be accepted as one of many Sprint delivery objectives. Spikes are often introduced before the delivery of large or complex Product Backlog Items in order to secure budget, expand knowledge, or produce a proof of concept. The duration and objective(s) of a spike is agreed between Product Owner and Development Team before the start. Unlike Sprint commitments, spikes may or may not deliver tangible, shippable, valuable functionality. For example, the objective of a spike might be to successfully reach a decision on a course of action. The spike is over when the time is up, not necessarily when the objective has been delivered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Tracer bullet</dt>
<dd>Also called a "drone spike", a tracer bullet is a spike with the current architecture, current technology set, current set of best practices that results in production quality code. It might just be a very narrow implementation of the functionality but is not throw away code. It is of production quality, and the rest of the iterations can build on this code. The name has military origins as <a href="/wiki/Tracer_ammunition" title="Tracer ammunition">ammunition</a> that makes the path of the bullet visible, allowing for corrections. Often these implementations are a 'quick shot' through all layers of an application, such as connecting a single form's input field to the back-end, to prove the layers connect as expected.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
<dt>Tasks</dt>
<dd>Work items added to the Sprint Backlog in Sprint Planning, or during the Sprint, with an estimate of hours to complete. Generally, each task should be small enough to be easily completed within a single day.</dd>
<dt>Definition of done (DoD)</dt>
<dd>The <a href="/wiki/Exit-criteria" title="Exit-criteria">exit-criteria</a> to determine whether a Product Backlog Item is complete. In many cases the DoD requires that all <a href="/wiki/Regression_testing" title="Regression testing">regression tests</a> should be successful. The definition of "done" may vary from one Scrum Team to another, but must be consistent within one team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Velocity</dt>
<dd>The total effort a team is capable of in a Sprint. The number is derived by evaluating the work (typically in <a href="/wiki/User_story" title="User story">user story</a> points) completed in the last Sprint. The collection of historical velocity data is a guideline for assisting the team in understanding how much work they can likely achieve in a future Sprint.</dd>
<dt>Impediment</dt>
<dd>Anything that prevents a team member from performing work as efficiently as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Sashimi</dt>
<dd>A term used to describe one or more user stories, indicating that they are thin slices of a product feature or capability.</dd>
<dt>Abnormal termination</dt>
<dd>The Product Owner can cancel a Sprint if necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup> The Product Owner may do so with input from the team, Scrum Master or management. For instance, management may wish to cancel a Sprint if external circumstances negate the value of the Sprint goal. If a Sprint is abnormally terminated, the next step is to conduct a new Sprint Planning, where the reason for the termination is reviewed.</dd>
<dt>ScrumBut</dt>
<dd>ScrumBut (or Scrum but) is a term to describe the approach of a team who have heavily adapted the Scrum framework to their own needs in some way contradictory to the principles of Scrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scrum works less well in the following circumstances:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Teams whose members are geographically dispersed or part-time. In Scrum, developers should have close and ongoing interaction, ideally working together in the same space most of the time.</li>
<li>Teams whose members have very specialised skills. In Scrum, developers should be able to work on any task or pick up work that another developer has started.</li>
<li>Products with many external dependencies. In Scrum, dividing product development into short Sprints requires careful planning; external dependencies, such as deliveries of software from other teams, can lead to delays and the failure of individual Sprints.</li>
<li>Products that are <a href="/wiki/Product_life-cycle_theory" title="Product life-cycle theory">mature</a> or <a href="/wiki/Legacy_system" title="Legacy system">legacy</a> or with regulated <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality control</a>. In Scrum, Product Increments should be fully developed and tested in a single Sprint; products that need large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Regression_testing" title="Regression testing">regression testing</a> or safety testing (e.g., medical devices or vehicle control) for each release are less suited to short Sprints than to longer waterfall releases.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scrumban">Scrumban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Scrumban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scrumban" title="Scrumban">Scrumban</a></div>
<p>Scrumban is a software production model based on Scrum and <a href="/wiki/Kanban_(development)" title="Kanban (development)">Kanban</a>. Scrumban is especially suited for <a href="/wiki/Software_maintenance" title="Software maintenance">product maintenance</a> with frequent and unexpected work items, such as <a href="/wiki/Software_bug" title="Software bug">production defects</a> or programming errors. In such cases the time-limited Sprints of the Scrum framework are of less benefit, although Scrum's daily events and other practices can still be applied, depending on the team and the situation at hand. Visualization of the work stages and limitations for simultaneous unfinished work and defects are familiar from the Kanban model. Using these methods, the team's <a href="/wiki/Workflow" title="Workflow">workflow</a> is directed in a way that allows for minimum completion time for each work item or programming error, and on the other hand ensures each team member is constantly employed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knibergskarin_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-knibergskarin-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>To illustrate each stage of work, teams working in the same space often use post-it notes or a large whiteboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumban_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-scrumban-36">[36]</a></sup> In the case of decentralized teams, stage-illustration software such as <a href="/wiki/Assembla" title="Assembla">Assembla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Targetprocess" title="Targetprocess">Targetprocess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eylean_Board" title="Eylean Board">Eylean Board</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jira_(software)" title="Jira (software)">JIRA</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agilo_for_Trac" title="Agilo for Trac">Agilo for Trac</a>.</p>
<p>In their simplest, the tasks are categorized into the work stages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Unstarted</li>
<li>Ongoing</li>
<li>Completed</li>
</ul>
<p>If desired, though, the teams can add more stages of work (such as "defined", "designed", "tested" or "delivered"). These additional phases can be of assistance if a certain part of the work becomes a bottleneck and the limiting values of the unfinished work cannot be raised. A more specific task division also makes it possible for employees to specialize in a certain phase of work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumban_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-scrumban-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are no set limiting values for unfinished work. Instead, each team has to define them individually by trial and error; a value too small results in workers standing idle for lack of work, whereas values too high tend to accumulate large amounts of unfinished work, which in turn hinders completion times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knibergskarin_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-knibergskarin-35">[35]</a></sup> A rule of thumb worth bearing in mind is that no team member should have more than two simultaneous selected tasks, and that on the other hand not all team members should have two tasks simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumban_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-scrumban-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The major differences between Scrum and Kanban is that in Scrum work is divided into Sprints that last a fixed amount of time, whereas in Kanban the flow of work is continuous. This is visible in work stage tables, which in Scrum are emptied after each Sprint, whereas in Kanban all tasks are marked on the same table. Scrum focuses on teams with multifaceted know-how, whereas Kanban makes specialized, functional teams possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knibergskarin_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-knibergskarin-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since Scrumban is such a new development model, there is not much reference material. Kanban, on the other hand, has been applied by Microsoft and Corbis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_for_implementation">Tools for implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Tools for implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other agile methods, Scrum can be implemented through a wide range of tools.</p>
<p>Many companies use universal tools, such as spreadsheets to build and maintain artifacts such as the Sprint Backlog. There are also open-source and proprietary software packages for Scrum—which are either dedicated to product development using the Scrum framework, or support multiple product development approaches including Scrum. Other organizations implement Scrum without software tools, and maintain their artifacts in hard-copy forms such as paper, whiteboards, and sticky notes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hybridization of Scrum with other software development methodologies is common as Scrum does not cover the whole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_lifecycle" title="Software lifecycle">product development lifecycle</a>; therefore, organizations find the need to add in additional processes to create a more comprehensive implementation. For example, at the start of product development, organizations commonly add process guidance on business case, requirements gathering and prioritization, initial high-level design, and budget and schedule forecasting.</p>
<p>Various authors and communities of people who use Scrum have also suggested more detailed techniques for how to apply or adapt Scrum to particular problems or organizations. Many refer to these methodological techniques as "patterns" - by analogy with <a href="/wiki/Design_pattern" title="Design pattern">design patterns</a> in architecture and software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Such patterns have extended Scrum outside of the software development domain into Manufacturing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Finance and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Resources" title="Human Resources">Human Resources</a>.</p>
<p>While the Scrum Guide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scrumguide_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguide-9">[9]</a></sup> prescribes the essential elements of Scrum, it seems that many companies deviate significantly from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The least variation is in the Sprints and Sprint length, events, team size and requirements engineering. The most variation can be found in the roles, effort estimation and quality assurance.</p>
