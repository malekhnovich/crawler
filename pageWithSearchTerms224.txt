<p><b>Information technology</b> (<b>IT</b>) is the application of <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> to store, retrieve, transmit, and manipulate <a href="/wiki/Data_(computing)" title="Data (computing)">data</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOP-1">[1]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, often in the context of a business or other enterprise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> IT is considered a subset of <a href="/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology" title="Information and communications technology">information and communications technology</a> (ICT). In 2012, Zuppo proposed an ICT hierarchy where each hierarchy level "contain some degree of commonality in that they are related to technologies that facilitate the transfer of information and various types of electronically mediated communications."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defining_ICT_in_a_Boundaryless_World:_The_Development_of_a_Working_Hierarchy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Defining_ICT_in_a_Boundaryless_World:_The_Development_of_a_Working_Hierarchy-3">[3]</a></sup> Business/IT was one level of the ICT hierarchy.</p>
<p>The term is commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a> for computers and computer networks, but it also encompasses other <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> distribution technologies such as television and telephones. Several <a href="/wiki/Computer_industry" title="Computer industry">industries</a> are associated with information technology, including <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">computer hardware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductors" title="Semiconductors">semiconductors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_equipment" title="Telecommunications equipment">telecom equipment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare" title="Healthcare">healthcare</a>, <a href="/wiki/E-commerce" title="E-commerce">e-commerce</a>, and computer services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[a]</a></sup></p>
<p>Humans have been storing, retrieving, manipulating, and communicating information since the <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerians</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">writing</a> in about 3000 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-7">[6]</a></sup> but the term <i>information technology</i> in its modern sense first appeared in a 1958 article published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i>; authors <a href="/wiki/Harold_Leavitt" title="Harold Leavitt">Harold J. Leavitt</a> and Thomas L. Whisler commented that "the new technology does not yet have a single established name. We shall call it information technology (IT)." Their definition consists of three categories: techniques for processing, the application of statistical and mathematical methods to decision-making, and the simulation of higher-order thinking through computer programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeavittWhisler_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeavittWhisler-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on the storage and processing technologies employed, it is possible to distinguish four distinct phases of IT development: pre-mechanical (3000 BC – 1450 AD), mechanical (1450–1840), electromechanical (1840–1940), electronic (1940–present),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butler_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Butler-7">[6]</a></sup> and moreover, <a href="/wiki/IT_as_a_service" title="IT as a service">IT as a service</a>. This article focuses on the most recent period (electronic), which began in about 1940.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_computer_technology">History of computer technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of computer technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG/300px-Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG/450px-Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG/600px-Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Z3_Deutsches_Museum.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Zuse Z3 replica on display at <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. The Zuse Z3 is the first programmable computer.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_computing_hardware" title="History of computing hardware">History of computing hardware</a></div>
<p>Devices have been used to aid computation for thousands of years, probably initially in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Tally_stick" title="Tally stick">tally stick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmandt-Besserat_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmandt-Besserat-9">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Antikythera_mechanism" title="Antikythera mechanism">Antikythera mechanism</a>, dating from about the beginning of the first century BC, is generally considered to be the earliest known mechanical <a href="/wiki/Analog_computer" title="Analog computer">analog computer</a>, and the earliest known geared mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWright2012279_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWright2012279-10">[9]</a></sup> Comparable geared devices did not emerge in Europe until the 16th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChildress200094_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChildress200094-11">[10]</a></sup> and it was not until 1645 that the first <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_calculator" title="Mechanical calculator">mechanical calculator</a> capable of performing the four basic arithmetical operations was developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChaudhuri20043_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChaudhuri20043-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Electronic computers, using either <a href="/wiki/Relay" title="Relay">relays</a> or <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valves</a>, began to appear in the early 1940s. The electromechanical <a href="/wiki/Z3_(computer)" title="Z3 (computer)">Zuse Z3</a>, completed in 1941, was the world's first <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programmable</a> computer, and by modern standards one of the first machines that could be considered a complete <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a> machine. <a href="/wiki/Colossus_computer" title="Colossus computer">Colossus</a>, developed during the Second World War to decrypt <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> messages was the first <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_data" title="Digital data">digital</a> computer. Although it was <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programmable</a>, it was not general-purpose, being designed to perform only a single task. It also lacked the ability to store its program in memory; programming was carried out using plugs and switches to alter the internal wiring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavington1980_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavington1980-13">[12]</a></sup> The first recognisably modern electronic digital <a href="/wiki/Stored-program_computer" title="Stored-program computer">stored-program computer</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Small-Scale_Experimental_Machine" title="Manchester Small-Scale Experimental Machine">Manchester Small-Scale Experimental Machine</a> (SSEM), which ran its first program on 21 June 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enticknap_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enticknap-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The development of <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> in the late 1940s at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Laboratories" title="Bell Laboratories">Bell Laboratories</a> allowed a new generation of computers to be designed with greatly reduced power consumption. The first commercially available stored-program computer, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mark_I" title="Ferranti Mark I">Ferranti Mark I</a>, contained 4050 valves and had a power consumption of 25 kilowatts. By comparison the first transistorised computer, developed at the University of Manchester and operational by November 1953, consumed only 150 watts in its final version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_storage">Data storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Data storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2323" data-file-width="3484" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg/300px-PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg/450px-PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg/600px-PaperTapes-5and8Hole.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Data_storage_device" title="Data storage device">Data storage device</a></div>
<p>Early electronic computers such as <a href="/wiki/Colossus_computer" title="Colossus computer">Colossus</a> made use of <a href="/wiki/Punched_tape" title="Punched tape">punched tape</a>, a long strip of paper on which data was represented by a series of holes, a technology now obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlavudeenVenkateshwaran2010178_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlavudeenVenkateshwaran2010178-16">[15]</a></sup> Electronic data storage, which is used in modern computers, dates from World War II, when a form of <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">delay line memory</a> was developed to remove the clutter from <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> signals, the first practical application of which was the mercury delay line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavington19981_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavington19981-17">[16]</a></sup> The first <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">random-access</a> digital storage device was the <a href="/wiki/Williams_tube" title="Williams tube">Williams tube</a>, based on a standard <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Resurrection_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Resurrection-18">[17]</a></sup> but the information stored in it and delay line memory was volatile in that it had to be continuously refreshed, and thus was lost once power was removed. The earliest form of non-volatile computer storage was the <a href="/wiki/Drum_memory" title="Drum memory">magnetic drum</a>, invented in 1932<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MagDrum_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MagDrum-19">[18]</a></sup> and used in the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mark_1" title="Ferranti Mark 1">Ferranti Mark 1</a>, the world's first commercially available general-purpose electronic computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Digital60MM1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Digital60MM1-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>IBM introduced the first <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> in 1956, as a component of their <a href="/wiki/IBM_305_RAMAC" title="IBM 305 RAMAC">305 RAMAC</a> computer system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKhurshudov20016_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKhurshudov20016-21">[20]</a></sup> Most digital data today is still stored magnetically on hard disks, or optically on media such as <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROMs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWangTaratorin19994.E2.80.935_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWangTaratorin19994.E2.80.935-22">[21]</a></sup> Until 2002 most information was stored on <a href="/wiki/Analog_device" title="Analog device">analog devices</a>, but that year digital storage capacity exceeded analog for the first time. As of 2007 almost 94% of the data stored worldwide was held digitally:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCNews_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USCNews-23">[22]</a></sup> 52% on hard disks, 28% on optical devices and 11% on digital magnetic tape. It has been estimated that the worldwide capacity to store information on electronic devices grew from less than 3 <a href="/wiki/Exabyte" title="Exabyte">exabytes</a> in 1986 to 295 exabytes in 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-24">[23]</a></sup> doubling roughly every 3 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilbertvideo2011_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hilbertvideo2011-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Databases">Databases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Databases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">Database</a></div>
<p>Database management systems emerged in the 1960s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062-26">[25]</a></sup> to address the problem of storing and retrieving large amounts of data accurately and quickly. One of the earliest such systems was <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s <a href="/wiki/IBM_Information_Management_System" title="IBM Information Management System">Information Management System</a> (IMS),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062-26">[25]</a></sup> which is still widely deployed more than 40 years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMS_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMS-27">[26]</a></sup> IMS stores data <a href="/wiki/Hierarchical_database_model" title="Hierarchical database model">hierarchically</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062-26">[25]</a></sup> but in the 1970s <a href="/wiki/Edgar_F._Codd" title="Edgar F. Codd">Ted Codd</a> proposed an alternative relational storage model based on <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">set theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predicate_logic" title="Predicate logic">predicate logic</a> and the familiar concepts of tables, rows and columns. The first commercially available <a href="/wiki/Relational_database_management_system" title="Relational database management system">relational database management system</a> (RDBMS) was available from <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a> in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20063_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20063-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>All database management systems consist of a number of components that together allow the data they store to be accessed simultaneously by many users while maintaining its integrity. A characteristic of all databases is that the structure of the data they contain is defined and stored separately from the data itself, in a <a href="/wiki/Database_schema" title="Database schema">database schema</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20062-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">extensible markup language</a> (XML) has become a popular format for data representation in recent years. Although XML data can be stored in normal <a href="/wiki/File_system" title="File system">file systems</a>, it is commonly held in <a href="/wiki/Relational_database" title="Relational database">relational databases</a> to take advantage of their "robust implementation verified by years of both theoretical and practical effort".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardede20092_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardede20092-29">[28]</a></sup> As an evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Generalized_Markup_Language" title="Standard Generalized Markup Language">Standard Generalized Markup Language</a> (SGML), XML's text-based structure offers the advantage of being both machine and human-readable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardede20094_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardede20094-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_retrieval">Data retrieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Data retrieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The relational database model introduced a programming-language independent <a href="/wiki/SQL" title="SQL">Structured Query Language</a> (SQL), based on <a href="/wiki/Relational_algebra" title="Relational algebra">relational algebra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20063_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWardDafoulas20063-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The terms "data" and "information" are not synonymous. Anything stored is data, but it only becomes information when it is organized and presented meaningfully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKedar20091.E2.80.939_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKedar20091.E2.80.939-31">[30]</a></sup> Most of the world's digital data is unstructured, and stored in a variety of different physical formats<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_der_Aalst20112_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_der_Aalst20112-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-format_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-format-33">[b]</a></sup> even within a single organization. <a href="/wiki/Data_warehouse" title="Data warehouse">Data warehouses</a> began to be developed in the 1980s to integrate these disparate stores. They typically contain data extracted from various sources, including external sources such as the Internet, organized in such a way as to facilitate <a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">decision support systems</a> (DSS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDych.C3.A920004.E2.80.936_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDych.C3.A920004.E2.80.936-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_transmission">Data transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Data transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Data_transmission" title="Data transmission">Data transmission</a> has three aspects: transmission, propagation, and reception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeik2000361_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeik2000361-35">[33]</a></sup> It can be broadly categorized as <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcasting</a>, in which information is transmitted unidirectionally downstream, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a>, with bidirectional upstream and downstream channels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>XML has been increasingly employed as a means of data interchange since the early 2000s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardede2009xiii_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardede2009xiii-36">[34]</a></sup> particularly for machine-oriented interactions such as those involved in web-oriented <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" title="Communications protocol">protocols</a> such as <a href="/wiki/SOAP" title="SOAP">SOAP</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardede20094_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardede20094-30">[29]</a></sup> describing "data-in-transit rather than ... data-at-rest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardede2009xiii_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardede2009xiii-36">[34]</a></sup> One of the challenges of such usage is converting data from relational databases into XML <a href="/wiki/Document_Object_Model" title="Document Object Model">Document Object Model</a> (DOM) structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELewis2003228.E2.80.9331_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis2003228.E2.80.9331-37">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_manipulation">Data manipulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Data manipulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hilbert and Lopez identify the exponential pace of technological change (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a>): machines' application-specific capacity to compute information per capita roughly doubled every 14 months between 1986 and 2007; the per capita capacity of the world's general-purpose computers doubled every 18 months during the same two decades; the global telecommunication capacity per capita doubled every 34 months; the world's storage capacity per capita required roughly 40 months to double (every 3 years); and per capita broadcast information has doubled every 12.3 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Massive amounts of data are stored worldwide every day, but unless it can be analysed and presented effectively it essentially resides in what have been called data tombs: "data archives that are seldom visited".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei20115_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei20115-38">[36]</a></sup> To address that issue, the field of <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a> – "the process of discovering interesting patterns and knowledge from large amounts of data"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei20118_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei20118-39">[37]</a></sup> – emerged in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei2011xxiii_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanKamberPei2011xxiii-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perspective">Perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_perspective">Academic perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Academic perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an academic context, the Association for Computing Machinery defines IT as "undergraduate degree programs that prepare students to meet the computer technology needs of business, government, healthcare, schools, and other kinds of organizations .... IT specialists assume responsibility for selecting hardware and software products appropriate for an organization, integrating those products with organizational needs and infrastructure, and installing, customizing, and maintaining those applications for the organization’s computer users."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curricula_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-curricula-41">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_and_employment_perspective">Commercial and employment perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial and employment perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a business context, the <a href="/wiki/Information_Technology_Association_of_America" title="Information Technology Association of America">Information Technology Association of America</a> has defined information technology as "the study, design, development, application, implementation, support or management of computer-based <a href="/wiki/Information_system" title="Information system">information systems</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProctor2011preface_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProctor2011preface-42">[40]</a></sup> The responsibilities of those working in the field include network administration, software development and installation, and the planning and management of an organization's technology life cycle, by which hardware and software are maintained, upgraded and replaced.</p>
<p>The business value of information technology lies in the automation of business processes, provision of information for decision making, connecting businesses with their customers, and the provision of productivity tools to increase efficiency.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_perspective">Ethical perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ethical perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Information_ethics" title="Information ethics">Information ethics</a></div>
<p>The field of information ethics was established by mathematician <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Wiener" title="Norbert Wiener">Norbert Wiener</a> in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBynum20089_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBynum20089-45">[43]</a></sup> Some of the ethical issues associated with the use of information technology include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds200920.E2.80.9321_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds200920.E2.80.9321-46">[44]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Breaches of copyright by those downloading files stored without the permission of the copyright holders</li>
<li>Employers monitoring their employees' emails and other Internet usage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">Unsolicited emails</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">Hackers</a> accessing online databases</li>
<li>Web sites installing <a href="/wiki/HTTP_cookie" title="HTTP cookie">cookies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spyware" title="Spyware">spyware</a> to monitor a user's online activities</li>
</ul>

<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">On the later more broad application of the term IT, Keary comments: "In its original application 'information technology' was appropriate to describe the convergence of technologies with application in the broad field of data storage, retrieval, processing, and dissemination. This useful conceptual term has since been converted to what purports to be concrete use, but without the reinforcement of definition ... the term IT lacks substance when applied to the name of any function, discipline, or position."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERalstonHemmendingerReilly2000869_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERalstonHemmendingerReilly2000869-5">[5]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-format-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-format_33-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Format" refers to the physical characteristics of the stored data such as its <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">encoding</a> scheme; "structure" describes the organisation of that data.</span></li>
