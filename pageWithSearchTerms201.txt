<p><b>David Lorge Parnas</b> (born February 10, 1941) is a <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> early pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>, who developed the concept of <a href="/wiki/Information_hiding" title="Information hiding">information hiding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Modular_programming" title="Modular programming">modular programming</a>, which is an important element of <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented programming</a> today. He is also noted for his advocacy of precise documentation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Parnas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parnas earned his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>. Parnas also earned a professional engineering license in Canada and was one of the first to apply traditional engineering principles to software design. He worked there as a professor for many years. He also taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a> (U.S.), the <a href="/wiki/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt" title="Technische Universität Darmstadt">Technische Universität Darmstadt</a> (Germany), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">University of Victoria</a> (<a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada), <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University" title="Queen's University">Queen's University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">Kingston, Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/McMaster_University" title="McMaster University">McMaster University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton, Ontario</a>, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Limerick" title="University of Limerick">University of Limerick</a> (Republic of Ireland).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Parnas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modular_design">Modular design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Parnas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modular design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Modular_design" title="Modular design">modular design</a>, his double dictum<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of high <a href="/wiki/Cohesion_(computer_science)" title="Cohesion (computer science)">cohesion</a> within modules and loose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coupling_(computer_science)" title="Coupling (computer science)">coupling</a> between modules is fundamental to modular design in software. However, in Parnas's seminal 1972 paper <i>On the Criteria to Be Used in Decomposing Systems into Modules</i>, this dictum is expressed in terms of <a href="/wiki/Information_hiding" title="Information hiding">information hiding</a>, and the terms <i>cohesion</i> and <i>coupling</i> are not used. He never used them. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParnas1972_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParnas1972-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_activism">Technical activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Parnas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technical activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dr Parnas took a public stand against the US <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a> (also known as "Star Wars") in the mid 1980s, arguing that it would be impossible to write an application of sufficient quality that it could be trusted to prevent a nuclear attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He has also been in the forefront of those urging the professionalization of "<a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>" (a term that he characterizes as "an unconsummated marriage"). Dr. Parnas is also a heavy promoter of ethics in the field of software engineering.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stance_on_academic_evaluation_methods">Stance on academic evaluation methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Parnas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stance on academic evaluation methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parnas has joined the group of scientists which openly criticize the number-of-publications-based approach towards ranking academic production. On his November 2007 paper <i>Stop the Numbers Game</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> he elaborates on several reasons on why the current number-based academic evaluation system used in many fields by universities all over the world (be it either oriented to the amount of publications or the amount of quotations each of those get) is flawed and, instead of generating more advance of the sciences, it leads to knowledge stagnation.</p>
