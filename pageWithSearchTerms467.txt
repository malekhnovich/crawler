<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Instructional design</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Instructional design</b>, or <b>instructional systems design</b> (<b>ISD</b>), is the practice of creating "instructional experiences which make the acquisition of knowledge and skill more efficient, effective, and appealing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The process consists broadly of determining the state and needs of the learner, defining the end goal of instruction, and creating some "intervention" to assist in the transition. Ideally, the process is informed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedagogically" title="Pedagogically">pedagogically</a> (process of teaching) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andragogically" title="Andragogically">andragogically</a> (adult learning) tested theories of learning and may take place in student-only, teacher-led or community-based settings. The outcome of this instruction may be directly observable and scientifically measured or completely hidden and assumed. There are many instructional design models but many are based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ADDIE_model" title="ADDIE model">ADDIE model</a> with the five phases: analysis, design, development, implementation, and evaluation. As a field, instructional design is historically and traditionally rooted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_psychology" title="Behavioral psychology">behavioral psychology</a>, though recently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructivism_(learning_theory)" title="Constructivism (learning theory)">constructivism</a> has influenced thinking in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During World War II, a considerable amount of training materials for the military were developed based on the principles of instruction, learning, and human behavior. Tests for assessing a learner’s abilities were used to screen candidates for the training programs. After the success of military training, psychologists began to view training as a system, and developed various analysis, design, and evaluation procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1946, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Dale" title="Edgar Dale">Edgar Dale</a> outlined a hierarchy of instructional methods, organized intuitively by their concreteness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slides_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-slides-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>B. F. Skinner's 1954 article “<i>The Science of Learning and the Art of Teaching</i>” suggested that effective instructional materials, called programmed instructional materials, should include small steps, frequent questions, and immediate feedback; and should allow self-pacing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Mager" title="Robert F. Mager">Robert F. Mager</a> popularized the use of learning objectives with his 1962 article “<i>Preparing Objectives for Programmed Instruction”</i>. The article describes how to write objectives including desired behavior, learning condition, and assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1956, a committee led by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Bloom" title="Benjamin Bloom">Benjamin Bloom</a> published an influential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomy_of_Educational_Objectives" title="Taxonomy of Educational Objectives">taxonomy</a> with three domains of learning: cognitive (what one knows or thinks), psychomotor (what one does, physically) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affective" title="Affective">affective</a> (what one feels, or what <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitudes</a> one has). These taxonomies still influence the design of instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slides_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-slides-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Glaser" title="Robert Glaser">Robert Glaser</a> introduced “criterion-referenced measures” in 1962. In contrast to norm-referenced tests in which an individual's performance is compared to group performance, a criterion-referenced test is designed to test an individual's behavior in relation to an objective standard. It can be used to assess the learners’ entry level behavior, and to what extent learners have developed mastery through an instructional program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965, Robert Gagne (see below for more information) described three domains of learning outcomes (cognitive, affective, psychomotor), five learning outcomes (Verbal Information, Intellectual Skills, Cognitive Strategy, Attitude, Motor Skills), and nine events of instruction in “<i>The conditions of Learning</i>”, which remain foundations of instructional design practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup> Gagne’s work in learning hierarchies and hierarchical analysis led to an important notion in instruction – to ensure that learners acquire prerequisite skills before attempting superordinate’s ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1967, after analyzing the failure of training material, Michael Scriven suggested the need for formative assessment – e.g., to try out instructional materials with learners (and revise accordingly) before declaring them finalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s, the number of instructional design models greatly increased and prospered in different sectors in military, academia, and industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup> Many instructional design theorists began to adopt an information-processing-based approach to the design of instruction. David Merrill for instance developed Component Display Theory (CDT), which concentrates on the means of presenting instructional materials (presentation techniques).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although interest in instructional design continued to be strong in business and the military, there was little evolution of ID in schools or higher education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReiserPDF_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReiserPDF-10">[10]</a></sup> However, educators and researchers began to consider how the personal computer could be used in a <a href="/wiki/Learning_environment" title="Learning environment">learning environment</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Learning_space" title="Learning space">learning space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slides_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-slides-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YouTube_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-YouTube-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/PLATO_(computer_system)" title="PLATO (computer system)">PLATO</a> (Programmed Logic for Automatic Teaching Operation) is one example of how computers began to be integrated into instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperHist_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-HyperHist-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of the first uses of computers in the classroom were for "drill and skill" exercises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markham_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Markham-13">[13]</a></sup> There was a growing interest in how cognitive psychology could be applied to instructional design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slides_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-slides-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The influence of <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(philosophy_of_education)" title="Constructivism (philosophy of education)">constructivist theory</a> on instructional design became more prominent in the 1990s as a counterpoint to the more traditional cognitive learning theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReiserPDF_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReiserPDF-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-History-14">[14]</a></sup> Constructivists believe that learning experiences should be "authentic" and produce real-world learning environments that allow the learner to construct their own knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReiserPDF_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReiserPDF-10">[10]</a></sup> This emphasis on the learner was a significant departure away from traditional forms of instructional design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slides_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-slides-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-History-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Performance_improvement" title="Performance improvement">Performance improvement</a> was also seen as an important outcome of learning that needed to be considered during the design process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YouTube_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-YouTube-11">[11]</a></sup> The World Wide Web emerged as an online learning tool with <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">hypertext</a> and hypermedia being recognized as good tools for learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HyperHist_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-HyperHist-12">[12]</a></sup> As technology advanced and constructivist theory gained popularity, technology’s use in the classroom began to evolve from mostly drill and skill exercises to more interactive activities that required more complex thinking on the part of the learner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markham_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Markham-13">[13]</a></sup> Rapid prototyping was first seen during the 1990s. In this process, an instructional design project is prototyped quickly and then vetted through a series of try and revise cycles. This is a big departure from traditional methods of instructional design that took far longer to complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReiserPDF_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReiserPDF-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Online learning became common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braine_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braine-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braine_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Braine-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Technology advances permitted sophisticated simulations with authentic and realistic learning experiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markham_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Markham-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Academic degrees focused on integrating technology, internet, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" title="Human-computer interaction">human-computer interaction</a> with education gained momentum with the introduction of Learning Design and Technology (LDT) majors. Universities such as <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_State_University" title="Bowling Green State University">Bowling Green State University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_State" title="Penn State">Penn State</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purdue" title="Purdue">Purdue</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_State_University" title="San Diego State University">San Diego State University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> have established undergraduate and graduate degrees in technology-centered education.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Gagn.C3.A9">Robert Gagné</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Robert Gagné">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robert Gagné's work is widely used and cited in the design of instruction, as exemplified by more than 130 citations in prominent journals in the field during the period from 1985 through 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Synthesizing ideas from behaviorism and cognitivism, he provided a clear template, which is easy to follow for designing instructional events. Instructional designers who follow Gagné's theory will likely have tightly focused, efficient instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nine_events">Nine events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nine events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to Gagné, learning occurs in a series of learning events. Each of nine learning events are conditions for learning which must be accomplished before the next in order for learning to take place, termed . Similarly, instructional events should mirror the learning events:</p>
<ol>
<li>Gaining attention: To ensure reception of coming instruction, the teacher gives the learners a stimulus. Before the learners can start to process any new information, the instructor must gain the attention of the learners. This might entail using abrupt changes in the instruction.</li>
<li>Informing learners of objectives: The teacher tells the learner what they will be able to do because of the instruction. The teacher communicates the desired outcome to the group.</li>
<li>Stimulating recall of prior learning: The teacher asks for recall of existing relevant knowledge.</li>
<li>Presenting the stimulus: The teacher gives emphasis to distinctive features.</li>
<li>Providing learning guidance: The teacher helps the students in understanding (semantic encoding) by providing organization and relevance.</li>
<li>Eliciting performance: The teacher asks the learners to respond, demonstrating learning.</li>
<li>Providing feedback: The teacher gives informative feedback on the learners' performance.</li>
<li>Assessing performance: The teacher requires more learner performance, and gives feedback, to reinforce learning.</li>
<li>Enhancing retention and transfer: The teacher provides varied practice to generalize the capability.</li>
</ol>
<p>Some educators believe that Gagné's taxonomy of learning outcomes and events of instruction oversimplify the learning process by over-prescribing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However, using them as part of a complete instructional package can assist many educators in becoming more organized and staying focused on the instructional goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Robert Gagné’s work has been the foundation of instructional design since the beginning of the 1960s when he conducted research and developed training materials for the military. Among the first to coin the term “instructional design”, Gagné developed some of the earliest instructional design models and ideas. These models have laid the groundwork for more present-day instructional design models from theorists like Dick, Carey, and Carey (The Dick and Carey Systems Approach Model),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Jerold Kemp’s Instructional Design Model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instructional.com_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-instructional.com-30">[30]</a></sup> and David Merrill (Merrill’s First Principle of Instruction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instructional.com_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-instructional.com-30">[30]</a></sup> Each of these models are based on a core set of learning phases that include (1) activation of prior experience, (2) demonstration of skills, (3) application of skills, and (4) integration or these skills into real world activities. The figure below illustrates these five ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instructional.com_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-instructional.com-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gagné's main focus for instructional design was how instruction and learning could be systematically connected to the design of instruction. He emphasized the design principles and procedures that need to take place for effective teaching and learning. His initial ideas, along with the ideas of other early instructional designers were outlined in <i>Psychological Principles in Systematic Development</i>, written by Roberts B. Miller and edited by Gagné.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Gagné believed in internal learning and motivation which paved the way for theorists like Merrill, Li, and Jones who designed the Instructional Transaction Theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Reigeluth and Stein’s Elaboration Theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and most notably, Keller’s <a href="/wiki/Instructional_design#The_ARCS_Model_of_Motivational_Design" title="Instructional design">ARCS Model of Motivation and Design</a>.</p>
<p>Prior to Robert Gagné, learning was often thought of as a single, uniform process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> There was little or no distinction made between “learning to load a rifle and learning to solve a complex mathematical problem”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> Gagné offered an alternative view which developed the idea that different learners required different learning strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> Understanding and designing instruction based on a learning style defined by the individual brought about new theories and approaches to teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> Gagné 's understanding and theories of human learning added significantly to understanding the stages in cognitive processing and instructions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> For example, Gagné argued that instructional designers must understand the characteristics and functions of short-term and long-term memory to facilitate meaningful learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-5"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup> This idea encouraged instructional designers to include cognitive needs as a top-down instructional approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internettime.com_34-6"><a href="#cite_note-internettime.com-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gagné (1966) defines curriculum as a sequence of content units arranged in such a way that the learning of each unit may be accomplished as a single act, provided the capabilities described by specified prior units (in the sequence) have already been mastered by the learner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richey.2C_R._C._2000_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richey.2C_R._C._2000-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>His definition of curriculum has been the basis of many important initiatives in schools and other educational environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richey.2C_R._C._2000_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Richey.2C_R._C._2000-35">[35]</a></sup> In the late 1950s and early 1960s, Gagné had expressed and established an interest in applying theory to practice with particular interest in applications for teaching, training and learning. Increasing the effectiveness and efficiency of practice was of particular concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richey.2C_R._C._2000_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Richey.2C_R._C._2000-35">[35]</a></sup> His ongoing attention to practice while developing theory continues to influence education and training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richey.2C_R._C._2000_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-Richey.2C_R._C._2000-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gagné's work has had a significant influence on American education, and military and industrial training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-answers.com_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-answers.com-36">[36]</a></sup> Gagné was one of the early developers of the concept of instructional systems design which suggests the components of a lesson can be analyzed and should be designed to operate together as an integrated plan for instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-answers.com_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-answers.com-36">[36]</a></sup> In "Educational Technology and the Learning Process" (Educational Researcher, 1974), Gagné defined instruction as "the set of planned external events which influence the process of learning and thus promote learning.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-answers.com_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-answers.com-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning_design">Learning design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Learning design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of learning design arrived in the literature of technology for education in the late 1990s and early 2000s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> with the idea that "designers and instructors need to choose for themselves the best mixture of behaviourist and constructivist learning experiences for their online courses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> But the concept of learning design is probably as old as the concept of teaching. Learning design might be defined as "the description of the teaching-learning process that takes place in a unit of learning (e.g., a course, a lesson or any other designed learning event)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>As summarized by Britain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> learning design may be associated with:</p>
<ul>
<li>The concept of learning design</li>
<li>The implementation of the concept made by learning design specifications like PALO, <a href="/wiki/IMS_Learning_Design" title="IMS Learning Design">IMS Learning Design</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> LDL, <a href="/wiki/Simple_learning_design_2.0" title="Simple learning design 2.0">SLD 2.0</a>, etc.</li>
<li>The technical realisations around the implementation of the concept like TELOS, RELOAD LD-Author, etc.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ADDIE_process">ADDIE process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: ADDIE process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the most common model used for creating instructional materials is the <a href="/wiki/ADDIE_Model" title="ADDIE Model">ADDIE Model</a>. This acronym stands for the 5 phases contained in the model (Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, and Evaluate).</p>
<p><b>Brief History of ADDIE’s Development</b> – The ADDIE model was initially developed by Florida State University to explain “the processes involved in the formulation of an instructional systems development (ISD) program for military interservice training that will adequately train individuals to do a particular job and which can also be applied to any interservice curriculum development activity.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADDIE_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADDIE-42">[42]</a></sup> The model originally contained several steps under its five original phases (Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, and [Evaluation and] Control),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADDIE_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADDIE-42">[42]</a></sup> whose completion was expected before movement to the next phase could occur. Over the years, the steps were revised and eventually the model itself became more dynamic and interactive than its original hierarchical rendition, until its most popular version appeared in the mid-80s, as we understand it today.</p>
<p>The five phases are listed and explained below:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrendsIssues_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-TrendsIssues-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="410" data-file-width="768" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg/300px-ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg/450px-ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg/600px-ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ADDIE_Model_of_Design.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
ADDIE Model</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Analyze</b> – The first phase of content development is Analysis. Analysis refers to the gathering of information about one’s audience, the tasks to be completed, how the learners will view the content, and the project’s overall goals. The instructional designer then classifies the information to make the content more applicable and successful.</p>
<p><b>Design</b> – The second phase is the Design phase. In this phase, instructional designers begin to create their project. Information gathered from the analysis phase, in conjunction with the theories and models of instructional design, is meant to explain how the learning will be acquired. For example, the design phase begins with writing a learning objective. Tasks are then identified and broken down to be more manageable for the designer. The final step determines the kind of activities required for the audience in order to meet the goals identified in the Analyze phase.</p>
<p><b>Develop</b> – The third phase, Development, involves the creation of the activities that will be implemented. It is in this stage that the blueprints of the design phase are assembled.</p>
<p><b>Implement</b> – After the content is developed, it is then Implemented. This stage allows the instructional designer to test all materials to determine if they are functional and appropriate for the intended audience.</p>
<p><b>Evaluate</b> – The final phase, Evaluate, ensures the materials achieved the desired goals. The evaluation phase consists of two parts: formative and summative assessment. The ADDIE model is an iterative process of instructional design, which means that at each stage the designer can assess the project's elements and revise them if necessary. This process incorporates <a href="/wiki/Formative_assessment" title="Formative assessment">formative assessment</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Summative_assessment" title="Summative assessment">summative assessments</a> contain tests or evaluations created for the content being implemented. This final phase is vital for the instructional design team because it provides data used to alter and enhance the design.</p>
<p>Connecting all phases of the model are external and reciprocal revision opportunities. As in the internal Evaluation phase, revisions should and can be made throughout the entire process.</p>
<p>Most of the current instructional design models are variations of the ADDIE process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piskurich.2C_G.M._2006_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piskurich.2C_G.M._2006-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rapid_prototyping">Rapid prototyping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Rapid prototyping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An adaptation of the ADDIE model, which is used sometimes, is a practice known as <a href="/wiki/Rapid_prototyping" title="Rapid prototyping">rapid prototyping</a>.</p>
<p>Proponents suggest that through an iterative process the verification of the design documents saves time and money by catching problems while they are still easy to fix. This approach is not novel to the design of instruction, but appears in many design-related domains including software design, architecture, transportation planning, product development, message design, user experience design, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piskurich.2C_G.M._2006_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Piskurich.2C_G.M._2006-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saettler_1990_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saettler_1990-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stolovitch_Keeps_1999_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stolovitch_Keeps_1999-45">[45]</a></sup> In fact, some proponents of design prototyping assert that a sophisticated understanding of a problem is incomplete without creating and evaluating some type of prototype, regardless of the analysis rigor that may have been applied up front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In other words, up-front analysis is rarely sufficient to allow one to confidently select an instructional model. For this reason many traditional methods of instructional design are beginning to be seen as incomplete, naive, and even counter-productive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, some consider rapid prototyping to be a somewhat simplistic type of model. As this argument goes, at the heart of Instructional Design is the analysis phase. After you thoroughly conduct the analysis—you can then choose a model based on your findings. That is the area where most people get snagged—they simply do not do a thorough-enough analysis. (Part of Article By Chris Bressi on LinkedIn)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dick_and_Carey">Dick and Carey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Dick and Carey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another well-known instructional design model is <b>The Dick and Carey Systems Approach Model</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DickCarey2005_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-DickCarey2005-48">[48]</a></sup> The model was originally published in 1978 by Walter Dick and Lou Carey in their book entitled <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?hl=en&amp;lr=&amp;id=a0gdmLZwI8IC&amp;oi=fnd&amp;pg=PA71&amp;dq=The+Systematic+Design+of+Instruction" rel="nofollow"><i>The Systematic Design of Instruction</i></a>.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dick_Carey.png"><img alt="Dick Carey.png" data-file-height="668" data-file-width="2030" height="329" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dick_Carey.png/1000px-Dick_Carey.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dick_Carey.png/1500px-Dick_Carey.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Dick_Carey.png/2000px-Dick_Carey.png 2x" width="1000"/></a><br/>
Dick and Carey made a significant contribution to the instructional design field by championing a systems view of instruction, in contrast to defining instruction as the sum of isolated parts. The model addresses instruction as an entire system, focusing on the interrelationship between context, content, learning and instruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forest_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forest-49">[49]</a></sup> According to Dick and Carey, "Components such as the instructor, learners, materials, instructional activities, delivery system, and learning and performance environments interact with each other and work together to bring about the desired student learning outcomes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DickCarey2005_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-DickCarey2005-48">[48]</a></sup> The components of the Systems Approach Model, also known as the Dick and Carey Model, are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Identify Instructional Goal(s): A goal statement describes a skill, knowledge or attitude (SKA) that a learner will be expected to acquire</li>
<li>Conduct Instructional Analysis: Identify what a learner must recall and identify what learner must be able to do to perform particular task</li>
<li>Analyze Learners and Contexts: Identify general characteristics of the target audience, including prior skills, prior experience, and basic demographics; identify characteristics directly related to the skill to be taught; and perform analysis of the performance and learning settings.</li>
<li>Write Performance Objectives: Objectives consists of a description of the behavior, the condition and criteria. The component of an objective that describes the criteria will be used to judge the learner's performance.</li>
<li>Develop Assessment Instruments: Purpose of entry behavior testing, purpose of pretesting, purpose of post-testing, purpose of practive items/practive problems</li>
<li>Develop Instructional Strategy: Pre-instructional activities, content presentation, Learner participation, assessment</li>
<li>Develop and Select Instructional Materials</li>
<li>Design and Conduct Formative Evaluation of Instruction: Designers try to identify areas of the instructional materials that need improvement.</li>
<li>Revise Instruction: To identify poor test items and to identify poor instruction</li>
<li>Design and Conduct Summative Evaluation</li>
</ul>
<p>With this model, components are executed iteratively and in parallel, rather than linearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DickCarey2005_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-DickCarey2005-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guaranteed_Learning">Guaranteed Learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Guaranteed Learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The instructional design model, Guaranteed Learning, was formerly known as the Instructional Development Learning System (IDLS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esseff_.26_Esseff_1998_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esseff_.26_Esseff_1998-50">[50]</a></sup> The model was originally published in 1970 by Peter J. Esseff, PhD and Mary Sullivan Esseff, PhD in their book entitled <i>IDLS—Pro Trainer 1: How to Design, Develop, and Validate Instructional Materials</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peter (1968) &amp; Mary (1972) Esseff both received their doctorates in Educational Technology from the Catholic University of America under the mentorship of Dr. Gabriel Ofiesh, a founding father of the Military Model mentioned above. Esseff and Esseff synthesized existing theories to develop their approach to systematic design, "Guaranteed Learning" aka "Instructional Development Learning System" (IDLS). In 2015, the Drs. Esseffs created an eLearning course to enable participants to take the GL course online under the direction of Dr. Esseff. See <a class="external text" href="http://www.GuaranteedLearning.co" rel="nofollow">GuaranteedLearning.co</a> for further information (2015-3-13).</p>
<p>The components of the Guaranteed Learning Model are the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Design a task analysis</li>
<li>Develop criterion tests and performance measures</li>
<li>Develop interactive instructional materials</li>
<li>Validate the interactive instructional materials</li>
<li>Create simulations or performance activities (Case Studies, Role Plays, and Demonstrations)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other useful instructional design models include: the Smith/Ragan Model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> the Morrison/Ross/Kemp Model<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and the OAR Model of instructional design in higher education,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> as well as, Wiggins' theory of <a href="/wiki/Understanding_by_Design#Backward_design" title="Understanding by Design">backward design</a>.</p>
<p>Learning theories also play an important role in the design of instructional materials. Theories such as <a href="/wiki/Behaviorism" title="Behaviorism">behaviorism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constructivism_(learning_theory)" title="Constructivism (learning theory)">constructivism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_learning_theory" title="Social learning theory">social learning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognitivism_(psychology)" title="Cognitivism (psychology)">cognitivism</a> help shape and define the outcome of instructional materials.</p>
<p>Also see: Managing Learning in High Performance Organizations, by Ruth Stiehl and Barbara Bessey, from The Learning Organization, Corvallis, Oregon. <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0963745700">ISBN 0-9637457-0-0</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivational_design">Motivational design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Motivational design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Motivation is defined as an internal drive that activates behavior and gives it direction. The term motivation theory is concerned with the process that describe why and how human behavior is activated and directed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation_concepts">Motivation concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Motivation concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Intrinsic and Extrinsic Motivation</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Instrinsic: defined as the doing of an activity for its inherent satisfactions rather than for some separable consequence. When intrinsically motivated a person is moved to act for the fun or challenge entailed rather than because of external rewards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Intrinsic motivation reflects the desire to do something because it is enjoyable. If we are intrinsically motivated, we would not be worried about external rewards such as praise.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><i>Examples:</i> Writing short stories because you enjoy writing them, reading a book because you are curious about the topic, and playing chess because you enjoy effortful thinking</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Extrinsic: reflects the desire to do something because of external rewards such as awards, money and praise. People who are extrinsically motivated may not enjoy certain activities. They may only wish to engage in certain activities because they wish to receive some external reward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><i>Examples:</i> The writer who only writes poems to be submitted to poetry contests, a person who dislikes sales but accepts a sales position because he/she desires to earn an above average salary, and a person selecting a major in college based on salary and prestige, rather than personal interest.</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>John Keller</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> has devoted his career to researching and understanding motivation in instructional systems. These decades of work constitute a major contribution to the instructional design field. First, by applying motivation theories systematically to design theory. Second, in developing a unique problem-solving process he calls the ARCS Motivation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ARCS">ARCS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: ARCS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ARCS Model of Motivational Design was created by John Keller while he was researching ways to supplement the learning process with motivation. The model is based on Tolman's and Lewin's expectancy-value theory, which presumes that people are motivated to learn if there is value in the knowledge presented (i.e. it fulfills personal needs) and if there is an optimistic expectation for success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The model consists of four main areas: Attention, Relevance, Confidence, and Satisfaction.</p>
<p>Attention and relevance according to John Keller's ARCS motivational theory are essential to learning. The first 2 of 4 key components for motivating learners, attention and relevance can be considered the backbone of the ARCS theory, the latter components relying upon the former.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Attention">Attention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Attention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The attention mentioned in this theory refers to the interest displayed by learners in taking in the concepts/ideas being taught. This component is split into three categories: perceptual arousal, using surprise or uncertain situations; inquiry arousal, offering challenging questions and/or problems to answer/solve; and variability, using a variety of resources and methods of teaching. Within each of these categories, John Keller has provided further sub-divisions of types of stimuli to grab attention. Grabbing attention is the most important part of the model because it initiates the motivation for the learners. Once learners are interested in a topic, they are willing to invest their time, pay attention, and find out more.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Relevance">Relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Relevance, according to Keller, must be established by using language and examples that the learners are familiar with. The three major strategies Keller presents are goal-oriented, motive matching, and familiarity. Like the Attention category, Keller divided the three major strategies into subcategories, which provide examples of how to make a lesson plan relevant to the learner. Learners will throw concepts to the wayside if their attention cannot be grabbed and sustained and if relevance is not conveyed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Confidence">Confidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Confidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The confidence aspect of the ARCS model focuses on establishing positive expectations for achieving success among learners. The confidence level of learners is often correlated with motivation and the amount of effort put forth in reaching a performance objective. For this reason, it's important that learning design provides students with a method for estimating their probability of success. This can be achieved in the form of a syllabus and grading policy, rubrics, or a time estimate to complete tasks. Additionally, confidence is built when positive reinforcement for personal achievements is given through timely, relevant feedback.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Satisfaction">Satisfaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Satisfaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Finally, learners must obtain some type of satisfaction or reward from a learning experience. This satisfaction can be from a sense of achievement, praise from a higher-up, or mere entertainment. Feedback and reinforcement are important elements and when learners appreciate the results, they will be motivated to learn. Satisfaction is based upon motivation, which can be intrinsic or extrinsic. To keep learners satisfied, instruction should be designed to allow them to use their newly learned skills as soon as possible in as authentic a setting as possible.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivating_opportunities">Motivating opportunities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instructional_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Motivating opportunities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although Keller's ARCS model currently dominates instructional design with respect to learner motivation, in 2006 Hardré and Miller<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> proposed a need for a new design model that includes current research in human motivation, a comprehensive treatment of motivation, integrates various fields of psychology and provides designers the flexibility to be applied to a myriad of situations.</p>
<p>Hardré<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardr.C3.A9_2009_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardr.C3.A9_2009-60">[60]</a></sup> proposes an alternate model for designers called the Motivating Opportunities Model or MOM. Hardré's model incorporates cognitive, needs, and affective theories as well as social elements of learning to address learner motivation. MOM has seven key components spelling the acronym 'SUCCESS' – Situational, Utilization, Competence, Content, Emotional, Social, and Systemic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardr.C3.A9_2009_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hardr.C3.A9_2009-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
