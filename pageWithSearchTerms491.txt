<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Packaging and labeling</h1>

<p><b>Packaging</b> is the technology of enclosing or protecting products for distribution, storage, sale, and use. Packaging also refers to the process of designing, evaluating, and producing packages. Packaging can be described as a coordinated system of preparing goods for transport, warehousing, logistics, sale, and end use. Packaging contains, protects, preserves, transports, informs, and sells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In many countries it is fully integrated into government, business, institutional, industrial, and personal use.</p>
<p><b>Package labeling</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) or <b>labelling</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) is any written, electronic, or graphic communication on the package or on a separate but associated <a href="/wiki/Label" title="Label">label</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:180px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1871" data-file-width="1670" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png/178px-Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png/268px-Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png/357px-Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png 2x" width="178"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ritual_wine_vessel_-_The_Met.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bronze wine container from the 9th century BCE.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first packages used the natural materials available at the time: <a href="/wiki/Basket" title="Basket">baskets</a> of reeds, wineskins (<a href="/wiki/Bota_bag" title="Bota bag">bota bags</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wooden_box" title="Wooden box">wooden boxes</a>, pottery <a href="/wiki/Vase" title="Vase">vases</a>, ceramic <a href="/wiki/Amphora" title="Amphora">amphorae</a>, wooden <a href="/wiki/Barrel" title="Barrel">barrels</a>, woven bags, etc. Processed materials were used to form packages as they were developed: for example, early <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> vessels. The study of old packages is an important aspect of <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>.</p>
<p>The earliest recorded use of paper for packaging dates back to 1035, when a <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> traveler visiting markets in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> noted that vegetables, spices and hardware were wrapped in paper for the customers after they were sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tinning">Tinning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tinning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Tinning" title="Tinning">tinplate</a> for packaging dates back to the 18th century. The manufacture of tinplate was long a <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>; in 1667 <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Yarranton" title="Andrew Yarranton">Andrew Yarranton</a>, an English <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Crowley" title="Ambrose Crowley">Ambrose Crowley</a> brought the method to England where it was improved by ironmasters including <a href="/wiki/Philip_Foley" title="Philip Foley">Philip Foley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1697, <a href="/wiki/John_Hanbury_(1664%E2%80%931734)" title="John Hanbury (1664–1734)">John Hanbury</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> had a rolling mill at <a href="/wiki/Pontypool" title="Pontypool">Pontypool</a> for making "Pontypoole Plates".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The method pioneered there of rolling iron plates by means of cylinders enabled more uniform black plates to be produced than was possible with the former practice of <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammering</a>.</p>
<p>Tinplate boxes first began to be sold from ports in the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Channel" title="Bristol Channel">Bristol Channel</a> in 1725. The tinplate was shipped from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newport,_Monmouthshire" title="Newport, Monmouthshire">Newport, Monmouthshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By 1805, 80,000 boxes were made and 50,000 exported. <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">Tobacconists</a> in London began packaging snuff in metal-plated canisters from the 1760s onwards.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canning">Canning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:140px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1059" data-file-width="731" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg/138px-Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg/207px-Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg/276px-Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg 2x" width="138"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canning_stewpan_advertisement.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1914 magazine advertisement for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cookware" title="Cookware">cookware</a> with instructions for home canning.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With the discovery of the importance of airtight containers for food preservation by French inventor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_Appert" title="Nicholas Appert">Nicholas Appert</a>, the tin canning process was patented by British merchant <a href="/wiki/Peter_Durand" title="Peter Durand">Peter Durand</a> in 1810.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After receiving the patent, Durand did not himself follow up with canning food. He sold his patent in 1812 to two other Englishmen, <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Donkin" title="Bryan Donkin">Bryan Donkin</a> and John Hall, who refined the process and product and set up the world's first commercial canning factory on Southwark Park Road, London. By 1813, they were producing the first canned goods for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The progressive improvement in canning stimulated the 1855 invention of the <a href="/wiki/Can_opener" title="Can opener">can opener</a>. Robert Yeates, a cutlery and surgical instrument maker of Trafalgar Place West, Hackney Road, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, UK, devised a claw-ended can opener with a hand-operated tool that haggled its way around the top of metal cans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1858, another lever-type opener of a more complex shape was patented in the United States by <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Warner_(inventor)" title="Ezra Warner (inventor)">Ezra Warner</a> of <a href="/wiki/Waterbury,_Connecticut" title="Waterbury, Connecticut">Waterbury, Connecticut</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Paper-based_packaging">Paper-based packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Paper-based packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:156px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Packing_salt_packages.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="197" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Packing_salt_packages.jpg/154px-Packing_salt_packages.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/21/Packing_salt_packages.jpg 1.5x" width="154"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Packing_salt_packages.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Packing folding cartons of salt.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Set-up boxes were first used in the 16th century and modern <a href="/wiki/Folding_carton" title="Folding carton">folding cartons</a> date back to 1839. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrugated_box" title="Corrugated box">corrugated box</a> was produced commercially in 1817 in England. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrugated_paper" title="Corrugated paper">Corrugated (also called pleated) paper</a> received a British patent in 1856 and was used as a liner for tall hats. Scottish-born <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gair" title="Robert Gair">Robert Gair</a> invented the pre-cut <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">paperboard</a> box in 1890—flat pieces manufactured in bulk that folded into boxes. Gair's invention came about as a result of an accident: as a <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> printer and paper-bag maker during the 1870s, he was once printing an order of seed bags, and the metal ruler, normally used to crease bags, shifted in position and cut them. Gair discovered that by cutting and creasing in one operation he could make prefabricated paperboard boxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commercial paper bags were first manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, in 1844, and the American <a href="/wiki/Francis_Wolle" title="Francis Wolle">Francis Wolle</a> patented a machine for automated bag-making in 1852.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Packaging advancements in the early 20th century included <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">Bakelite</a> closures on <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottles</a>, transparent <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a> overwraps and panels on <a href="/wiki/Carton" title="Carton">cartons</a>. These innovations increased processing efficiency and improved <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">food safety</a>. As additional materials such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminum</a> and several types of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> were developed, they were incorporated into packages to improve performance and functionality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a> became the first university in the world to offer a degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging_Engineering" title="Packaging Engineering">Packaging Engineering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In-plant recycling has long been common for producing packaging materials. Post-consumer recycling of aluminum and paper-based products has been economical for many years: since the 1980s, post-consumer recycling has increased due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curbside_recycling" title="Curbside recycling">curbside recycling</a>, consumer awareness, and regulatory pressure.</p>

<p>Many prominent innovations in the packaging industry were developed first for military use. Some military supplies are packaged in the same commercial packaging used for general industry. Other military packaging must transport <a href="/wiki/Materiel" title="Materiel">materiel</a>, supplies, foods, etc. under severe distribution and storage conditions. Packaging problems encountered in World War II led to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Standard" title="United States Military Standard">Military Standard</a> or "mil spec" regulations being applied to packaging, which was then designated "military specification packaging". As a prominent concept in the military, mil spec packaging officially came into being around 1941, due to <a href="/wiki/Iceland_in_World_War_II" title="Iceland in World War II">operations in Iceland</a> experiencing critical losses, ultimately attributed to bad packaging. In most cases, mil spec packaging solutions (such as barrier materials, <a href="/wiki/Field_ration" title="Field ration">field rations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antistatic_bag" title="Antistatic bag">antistatic bags</a>, and various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping_crate" title="Shipping crate">shipping crates</a>) are similar to commercial grade packaging materials, but subject to more stringent performance and quality requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2003<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the packaging sector accounted for about two percent of the <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" title="Gross national product">gross national product</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a>. About half of this market was related to <a href="/wiki/Food_packaging" title="Food packaging">food packaging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_purposes_of_packaging_and_package_labels">The purposes of packaging and package labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The purposes of packaging and package labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Packaging and package labeling have several objectives<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Physical protection</b> – The objects enclosed in the package may require protection from, among other things, mechanical <a href="/wiki/Shock_(mechanics)" title="Shock (mechanics)">shock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_discharge" title="Electrostatic discharge">electrostatic discharge</a>, compression, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> etc.</li>
<li><b>Barrier protection</b> – A barrier to <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, dust, etc., is often required. <a href="/wiki/Permeation" title="Permeation">Permeation</a> is a critical factor in design. Some packages contain <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccants</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_absorber" title="Oxygen absorber">oxygen absorbers</a> to help extend shelf life. <a href="/wiki/Modified_atmosphere" title="Modified atmosphere">Modified atmospheres</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> or controlled atmospheres are also maintained in some food packages. Keeping the contents clean, fresh, <a href="/wiki/Asepsis" title="Asepsis">sterile</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and safe for the duration of the intended <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a> is a primary function. A barrier is also implemented in cases where segregation of two materials prior to end use is required, as in the case of special paints, glues, medical fluids, etc. At the consumer end, the packaging barrier is broken or measured amounts of material are removed for mixing and subsequent end use.</li>
<li><b>Containment or agglomeration</b> – Small objects are typically grouped together in one package for reasons of storage and selling efficiency. For example, a single box of 1000 pencils requires less physical handling than 1000 single pencils. <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">Liquids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powder_(substance)" title="Powder (substance)">powders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Granular_material" title="Granular material">granular materials</a> need containment.</li>
<li><b>Information transmission</b> – Packages and <a href="/wiki/Label" title="Label">labels</a> communicate how to use, transport, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycle</a>, or dispose of the package or product. With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">pharmaceuticals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medical</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical</a> products, some types of information are <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_labelling" title="Mandatory labelling">required</a> by government legislation. Some packages and labels also are used for <a href="/wiki/Track_and_trace" title="Track and trace">track and trace</a> purposes. Most items include their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_number" title="Serial number">serial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lot_number" title="Lot number">lot numbers</a> on the packaging, and in the case of food products, medicine, and some chemicals the packaging often contains an <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">expiry/best-before date</a>, usually in a shorthand form. Packages may indicate their construction material with a symbol.</li>
<li><b>Marketing</b> – Packaging and <a href="/wiki/Label" title="Label">labels</a> can be used by <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketers</a> to encourage potential buyers to purchase a product. Package <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a> and physical design have been important and constantly evolving phenomena for several decades. <a href="/wiki/Marketing_communications" title="Marketing communications">Marketing communications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a> are applied to the surface of the package and often to the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale_display" title="Point of sale display">point of sale display</a>. Most packaging is designed to reflect the brand's message and identity.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shampoo_packet,_single-serving.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="800" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg/170px-Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg/255px-Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg/340px-Shampoo_packet%2C_single-serving.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shampoo_packet,_single-serving.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A single-serving shampoo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sachet_(package)" title="Sachet (package)">packet</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b>Security</b> – Packaging can play an important role in reducing the <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a> risks of shipment. Packages can be made with improved <a href="/wiki/Tamper_resistance" title="Tamper resistance">tamper resistance</a> to deter manipulation and they can also have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamper-evident" title="Tamper-evident">tamper-evident</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> features indicating that tampering has taken place. Packages can be engineered to help reduce the risks of <a href="/wiki/Package_pilferage" title="Package pilferage">package pilferage</a> or the theft and resale of products: Some package constructions are more resistant to pilferage than other types, and some have pilfer-indicating seals. <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_consumer_goods" title="Counterfeit consumer goods">Counterfeit consumer goods</a>, unauthorized sales (diversion), material substitution and tampering can all be minimized or prevented with such anti-counterfeiting technologies. Packages may include <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authentication</a> seals and use <a href="/wiki/Security_printing" title="Security printing">security printing</a> to help indicate that the package and contents are not <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit" title="Counterfeit">counterfeit</a>. Packages also can include anti-theft devices such as dye-packs, <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">RFID</a> tags, or <a href="/wiki/Electronic_article_surveillance" title="Electronic article surveillance">electronic article surveillance</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> tags that can be activated or detected by devices at exit points and require specialized tools to deactivate. Using packaging in this way is a means of <a href="/wiki/Retail_loss_prevention" title="Retail loss prevention">retail loss prevention</a>.</li>
<li><b>Convenience</b> – Packages can have features that add <a href="/wiki/Convenience" title="Convenience">convenience</a> in distribution, handling, stacking, display, sale, opening, reclosing, using, dispensing, reusing, recycling, and ease of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">disposal</a></li>
<li><b>Portion control</b> – Single serving or single <a href="/wiki/Drug_delivery" title="Drug delivery">dosage</a> packaging has a precise amount of contents to control usage. Bulk commodities (such as salt) can be divided into packages that are a more suitable size for individual households. It also aids the control of inventory: selling sealed one-liter bottles of milk, rather than having people bring their own bottles to fill themselves.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging_types">Packaging types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Packaging types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Food_packages_(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1524" data-file-width="2136" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Food_packages_%281%29.jpg/220px-Food_packages_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Food_packages_%281%29.jpg/330px-Food_packages_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Food_packages_%281%29.jpg/440px-Food_packages_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Food_packages_(1).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Various types of household packaging for foods</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Packaging may be of several different types. For example, a <b>transport package</b> or <b>distribution package</b> can be the <a href="/wiki/Shipping_container" title="Shipping container">shipping container</a> used to ship, store, and handle the product or inner packages. Some identify a <b>consumer package</b> as one which is directed toward a consumer or household.</p>
<p>Packaging may be described in relation to the type of product being packaged: <a href="/wiki/Medical_device" title="Medical device">medical device</a> packaging, bulk <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical</a> packaging, <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drug</a> packaging, retail <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> packaging, military <a href="/wiki/Materiel" title="Materiel">materiel</a> packaging, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">pharmaceutical</a> packaging, etc.</p>
<p>It is sometimes convenient to categorize packages by layer or function: "primary", "secondary", etc.</p>
<ul>
<li>Primary packaging is the material that first envelops the product and holds it. This usually is the smallest unit of distribution or use and is the package which is in direct contact with the contents.</li>
<li>Secondary packaging is outside the primary packaging, and may be used to prevent pilferage or to group primary packages together.</li>
<li>Tertiary packaging is used for <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">bulk handling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouse</a> storage and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> shipping. The most common form is a <a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">palletized</a> <a href="/wiki/Unit_load" title="Unit load">unit load</a> that packs tightly into <a href="/wiki/Containerization" title="Containerization">containers</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>These broad categories can be somewhat arbitrary. For example, depending on the use, a <a href="/wiki/Shrink_wrap" title="Shrink wrap">shrink wrap</a> can be primary packaging when applied directly to the product, secondary packaging when used to combine smaller packages, or tertiary packaging when used to facilitate some types of distribution, such as to affix a number of cartons on a pallet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols_used_on_packages_and_labels">Symbols used on packages and labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Symbols used on packages and labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="389" data-file-width="291" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg/170px-HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg/255px-HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6e/HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HK_food_drink_Longevity_brand_Sweetened_Milk_Mar-2014_barcode_a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_code" title="Bar code">bar code</a> on a tin of condensed milk</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many types of symbols for package labeling are nationally and internationally standardized. For consumer packaging, symbols exist for product certifications (such as the <a href="/wiki/FCC_Declaration_of_Conformity" title="FCC Declaration of Conformity">FCC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%9CV" title="TÜV">TÜV</a> marks), <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proof_of_purchase" title="Proof of purchase">proof of purchase</a>, etc. Some requirements and symbols exist to communicate aspects of consumer rights and safety, for example the <a href="/wiki/CE_marking" title="CE marking">CE marking</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Estimated_sign" title="Estimated sign">estimated sign</a> that notes conformance to EU weights and measures accuracy regulations. Examples of environmental and recycling symbols include the <a href="/wiki/Recycling_symbol" title="Recycling symbol">recycling symbol</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Recycling_codes" title="Recycling codes">recycling code</a> (which could be a <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Dot_(symbol)" title="Green Dot (symbol)">"Green Dot"</a>. Food packaging may show <a href="/wiki/Food_contact_materials" title="Food contact materials">food contact material</a> symbols. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, products of animal origin which are intended to be consumed by humans have to carry standard, oval-shaped <a href="/wiki/EC_identification_and_health_marks" title="EC identification and health marks">EC identification and health marks</a> for food safety and quality insurance reasons.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Barcode" title="Barcode">Bar codes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Product_Code" title="Universal Product Code">Universal Product Codes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">RFID</a> labels are common to allow automated information management in <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retailing" title="Retailing">retailing</a>. <a href="/wiki/Country_of_Origin_Labeling" title="Country of Origin Labeling">Country of Origin Labeling</a> is often used. Some products might use <a href="/wiki/QR_code" title="QR code">QR codes</a> or similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrix_barcode" title="Matrix barcode">matrix barcodes</a>. Packaging may have visible <a href="/wiki/Printing_registration" title="Printing registration">registration marks</a> and other printing calibration and troubleshooting cues.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipping_container_labeling">Shipping container labeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Shipping container labeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Print_n_apply_ucc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Print &amp; Apply" corner wrap UCC (<a href="/wiki/GS1-128" title="GS1-128">GS1-128</a>) label application to a pallet load</div>

<p>Technologies related to shipping containers are identification codes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_codes" title="Bar codes">bar codes</a>, and electronic data interchange (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_Data_Interchange" title="Electronic Data Interchange">EDI</a>). These three core technologies serve to enable the business functions in the process of shipping containers throughout the distribution channel. Each has an essential function: identification codes either relate product information or serve as keys to other data, bar codes allow for the automated input of identification codes and other data, and EDI moves data between trading partners within the distribution channel.</p>
<p>Elements of these core technologies include <a href="/wiki/Universal_Product_Code" title="Universal Product Code">UPC</a> and <a href="/wiki/GS1" title="GS1">EAN</a> item identification codes, the SCC-14 (UPC shipping container code), the SSCC-18 (Serial Shipping Container Codes), Interleaved 2-of-5 and UCC/EAN-128 (newly designated <a href="/wiki/GS1-128" title="GS1-128">GS1-128</a>) bar code <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barcode_symbology" title="Barcode symbology">symbologies</a>, and ANSI ASC X12 and UN/EDIFACT EDI standards.</p>
<p>Small parcel carriers often have their own formats. For example, United Parcel Service has a <a href="/wiki/MaxiCode" title="MaxiCode">MaxiCode</a> 2-D code for parcel tracking.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">RFID</a> labels for shipping containers are also increasingly used. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> division, <a href="/wiki/Sam%27s_Club" title="Sam's Club">Sam's Club</a>, has also moved in this direction and is putting pressure on its suppliers to comply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shipments of <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_goods" title="Dangerous goods">hazardous materials</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_goods" title="Dangerous goods">dangerous goods</a> have special information and symbols (labels, placards, etc.) as required by UN, country, and specific carrier requirements. On transport packages, standardized symbols are also used to communicate handling needs. Some are defined in the <a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM</a> D5445 "Standard Practice for Pictorial Markings for Handling of Goods" and <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> 780 "Pictorial marking for handling of goods".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Package_development_considerations">Package development considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Package development considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Package design and development are often thought of as an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">new product development</a> process. Alternatively, development of a package (or component) can be a separate process, but must be linked closely with the product to be packaged. Package design starts with the identification of all the requirements: structural design, <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_assurance" title="Quality assurance">quality assurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, legal, regulatory, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, end-use, environmental, etc. The design criteria, performance (specified by <a href="/wiki/Package_testing" title="Package testing">package testing</a>), completion time targets, resources, and cost constraints need to be established and agreed upon. Package design processes often employ <a href="/wiki/Rapid_prototyping" title="Rapid prototyping">rapid prototyping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Document_automation" title="Document automation">document automation</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Distribution_differences.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="902" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bb/Distribution_differences.jpg/300px-Distribution_differences.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bb/Distribution_differences.jpg/450px-Distribution_differences.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bb/Distribution_differences.jpg/600px-Distribution_differences.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Distribution_differences.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Transport packaging needs to be matched to its <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> system. Packages designed for controlled shipments of uniform <a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">pallet</a> loads may not be suited to mixed shipments with <a href="/wiki/Express_mail" title="Express mail">express</a> carriers.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An example of how package design is affected by other factors is its relationship to <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>. When the distribution system includes individual shipments by a small parcel carrier, the sorting, handling, and mixed stacking make severe demands on the strength and protective ability of the transport package. If the logistics system consists of uniform palletized <a href="/wiki/Unit_load" title="Unit load">unit loads</a>, the structural design of the package can be designed to meet those specific needs, such as vertical stacking for a longer time frame. A package designed for one mode of shipment may not be suited to another.</p>
<p>With some types of products, the design process involves detailed regulatory requirements for the packaging. For example, any package components that may contact <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">foods</a> are designated <a href="/wiki/Food_contact_materials" title="Food contact materials">food contact materials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxicologist" title="Toxicologist">Toxicologists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_scientist" title="Food scientist">food scientists</a> need to verify that such packaging materials are allowed by applicable regulations. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging_engineer" title="Packaging engineer">Packaging engineers</a> need to verify that the completed package will keep the product safe for its intended <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a> with normal usage. Packaging processes, labeling, distribution, and sale need to be <a href="/wiki/Verification_and_validation" title="Verification and validation">validated</a> to assure that they comply with regulations that have the well being of the consumer in mind.</p>
<p>Sometimes the objectives of package development seem contradictory. For example, regulations for an <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drug</a> might require the package to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamper-evident" title="Tamper-evident">tamper-evident</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child-resistant_packaging" title="Child-resistant packaging">child resistant</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> These intentionally make the package difficult to open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The intended consumer, however, might be handicapped or elderly and unable to readily open the package. Meeting all goals is a challenge.</p>
<p>Package design may take place within a company or with various degrees of external <a href="/wiki/Packaging_engineering" title="Packaging engineering">packaging engineering</a>: <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">independent contractors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">consultants</a>, vendor evaluations, independent laboratories, contract packagers, total <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourcing</a>, etc. Some sort of formal <a href="/wiki/Project_planning" title="Project planning">project planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a> methodology is required for all but the simplest package design and development programs. An effective <a href="/wiki/Quality_management" title="Quality management">quality management</a> system and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verification_and_Validation" title="Verification and Validation">Verification and Validation</a> protocols are mandatory for some types of packaging and recommended for all.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_considerations">Environmental considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waste_hierarchy.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="550" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Waste_hierarchy.svg/300px-Waste_hierarchy.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Waste_hierarchy.svg/450px-Waste_hierarchy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Waste_hierarchy.svg/600px-Waste_hierarchy.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waste_hierarchy.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">waste hierarchy</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_packaging" title="Sustainable packaging">sustainable packaging</a></div>
<p>Package development involves considerations of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, environmental responsibility, and applicable <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> regulations. It may involve a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">life cycle assessment</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which considers the material and energy inputs and outputs to the package, the packaged product (contents), the packaging process, the <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a>, etc. It is necessary to know the relevant regulatory requirements for point of manufacture, sale, and use.</p>
<p>The traditional “three R’s” of reduce, reuse, and recycle are part of a <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">waste hierarchy</a> which may be considered in product and package development.</p>
<ul>
<li>Prevention – Waste prevention is a primary goal. Packaging should be used only where needed. Proper packaging can also help prevent waste. Packaging plays an important part in preventing loss or damage to the packaged product (contents). Usually, the energy content and material usage of the product being packaged are much greater than that of the package. A vital function of the package is to protect the product for its intended use: if the product is damaged or degraded, its entire energy and material content may be lost.</li>
<li>Minimization – (also "source reduction") The mass and volume of packaging (per unit of contents) can be measured and used as criteria for minimizing the package in the design process. Usually “reduced” packaging also helps minimize costs. Packaging engineers continue to work toward reduced packaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Reuse – <a href="/wiki/Reusable_packaging" title="Reusable packaging">Reusable packaging</a> is encouraged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Returnable packaging has long been useful (and economically viable) for closed loop logistics systems. Inspection, cleaning, repair and recouperage are often needed. Some manufacturers re-use the packaging of the incoming parts for a product, either as packaging for the outgoing product<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> or as part of the product itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>Recycling – <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">Recycling</a> is the reprocessing of materials (pre- and post-consumer) into new products. Emphasis is focused on recycling the largest primary components of a package: steel, aluminum, papers, plastics, etc. Small components can be chosen which are not difficult to separate and do not contaminate recycling operations. Packages can sometimes be designed to separate components to better facilitate recycling.</li>
<li>Energy recovery – <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">Waste-to-energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refuse-derived_fuel" title="Refuse-derived fuel">Refuse-derived fuel</a> in approved facilities make use of the heat available from incinerating the packaging components.</li>
<li>Disposal – <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">Incineration</a>, and placement in a sanitary <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> are undertaken for some materials. Certain US states regulate packages for toxic contents, which have the potential to contaminate emissions and ash from incineration and <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">leachate</a> from landfill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Packages should not be <a href="/wiki/Litter" title="Litter">littered</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Development of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_packaging" title="Sustainable packaging">sustainable packaging</a> is an area of considerable interest to <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organizations</a>, governments, consumers, packagers, and retailers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging_machines">Packaging machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Packaging_and_labeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Packaging machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Budweiser_Plant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Budweiser_Plant.jpg/400px-Budweiser_Plant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Budweiser_Plant.jpg/600px-Budweiser_Plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Budweiser_Plant.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Budweiser_Plant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Beer <a href="/wiki/Bottling_line" title="Bottling line">bottling lines</a></div>

<p>Choosing packaging machinery includes an assessment of technical capabilities, labor requirements, worker safety, <a href="/wiki/Maintainability" title="Maintainability">maintainability</a>, serviceability, <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">reliability</a>, ability to integrate into the packaging line, capital cost, floorspace, flexibility (change-over, materials, etc.), energy requirements, <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality</a> of outgoing packages, qualifications (for food, pharmaceuticals, etc.), throughput, efficiency, productivity, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Return_on_investment" title="Return on investment">return on investment</a>, etc.</p>
<p>Packaging machinery can be:</p>
<ol>
<li>purchased as standard, off-the-shelf equipment</li>
<li>purchased custom-made or custom-tailored to specific operations</li>
<li>manufactured or modified by in-house engineers and maintenance staff</li>
</ol>
<p>Efforts at packaging line <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a> increasingly use <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_controller" title="Programmable logic controller">programmable logic controllers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>.</p>
<p>Packaging machines may be of the following general types:</p>
<ul>
<li>Accumulating and collating machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blister_pack" title="Blister pack">Blister packs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skin_pack" title="Skin pack">skin packs</a> and vacuum packaging machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bottle_cap" title="Bottle cap">Bottle caps</a> equipment, over-capping, lidding, closing, seaming and sealing machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Box" title="Box">Box</a>, case and tray forming, packing, unpacking, closing and sealing machines</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartoning_machines" title="Cartoning machines">Cartoning machines</a></li>
<li>Cleaning, sterilizing, cooling and drying machines</li>
<li>Coding, printing, marking, stamping, and imprinting machines</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Converting" title="Converting">Converting</a> machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">Conveyor belts</a>, accumulating and related machines</li>
<li>Feeding, orienting, placing and related machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Filler_(packaging)" title="Filler (packaging)">Filling machines</a>: handling dry, powdered, solid, liquid, gas, or viscous products</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inspection" title="Inspection">Inspecting</a>: visual, sound, metal detecting, etc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Label_dispenser" title="Label dispenser">Label dispenser</a></li>
<li>Orienting, unscrambling machines</li>
<li>Package filling and closing machines</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">Palletizing</a>, depalletizing, <a href="/wiki/Unit_load" title="Unit load">unit load</a> assembly</li>
<li>Product identification: <a href="/wiki/Label" title="Label">labeling</a>, marking, etc.</li>
<li>Sealing machines: <a href="/wiki/Heat_sealer" title="Heat sealer">heat sealer</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slitter" title="Slitter">Slitting machines</a></li>
<li>Weighing machines: <a href="/wiki/Check_weigher" title="Check weigher">check weigher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multihead_weigher" title="Multihead weigher">multihead weigher</a></li>
<li>Wrapping machines: stretch wrapping, <a href="/wiki/Shrink_wrap" title="Shrink wrap">shrink wrap</a>, banding</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vertical_form_fill_sealing_machine" title="Vertical form fill sealing machine">Form, fill and seal machines</a></li>
<li>Other specialty machinery: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slitter" title="Slitter">slitters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perforate" title="Perforate">perforating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> cutters, parts attachment, etc.</li>
</ul>
