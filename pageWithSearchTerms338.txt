<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Interaction design</h1>

<p><b>Interaction design</b>, often abbreviated as <b>IxD</b>, is defined as "the practice of designing interactive digital products, environments, systems, and services."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xxxi,1</sup> While the digital side of this statement is true, Interaction design is also valid when creating physical (non-digital) products, covering the ideology of how a user may interact with it. Common topics that interaction design is most often associated with include <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Human–computer interaction">human–computer interaction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a>. While interaction design has an interest in form (similar to other design fields), its main area of focus rests on behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1</sup> Rather than analyzing how things are, interaction design synthesizes and imagines things as they might or ought to be. This element of interaction design is what clearly marks IxD as an aspect of a design field as opposed to a science or engineering field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xviii</sup></p>
<p>While disciplines like software engineering have a heavy focus on designing for technical stakeholders of a project, interaction design is more geared toward satisfying the needs and desires of the majority of users of a given product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xviii</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>interaction design</i> was first coined by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moggridge" title="Bill Moggridge">Bill Moggridge</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Verplank" title="Bill Verplank">Bill Verplank</a> in the mid-1980s, but it would be another 10 years before other designers rediscovered the term and the concept started to take hold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xviii</sup> To Verplank, it was an adaptation of the computer science term <i>user interface design</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a> profession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To Moggridge, it was an improvement over <i>soft-face</i>, which he had coined in 1984 to refer to the application of industrial design to products containing software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesigningInteractions_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DesigningInteractions-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest programs in design for interactive technologies were the Visible Language Workshop, started by Muriel Cooper at MIT in in 1975, and The Interactive Telecommunications Program founded at NYU in 1979 by Martin Elton and later headed by Red Burns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first academic program officially named "Interaction Design" was established at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> in 1994 as a Master of Design in Interaction Design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At the outset, the program focused mainly on screen interfaces, but has since shifted to a greater emphasis on the "big picture" aspects of interaction—people, organizations, culture, service, and system.</p>
<p>In 1990 Gillian Crampton Smith founded the Computer-related Design MA at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a> (RCA) in London, changed in 2005 to Design Interactions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> headed by Professor Anthony Dunne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2001 Crampton Smith helped found the <a href="/wiki/Interaction_Design_Institute_Ivrea" title="Interaction Design Institute Ivrea">Interaction Design Institute Ivrea</a>, a small institute in Olivetti's hometown in Northern Italy, dedicated solely to interaction design. The institute moved to Milan in October 2005 and merged courses with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domus_Academy,_Postgraduate_School_of_Design" title="Domus Academy, Postgraduate School of Design">Domus Academy</a>. In 2007, some of the people originally involved with IDII set up the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Institute_of_Interaction_Design" title="Copenhagen Institute of Interaction Design">Copenhagen Institute of Interaction Design</a> (CIID). After Ivrea Gillian Crampton Smith and Philip Tabor added the Interaction Design (IxD) track in the Visual and Multimedia Communication at Iuav, University of Venice, Italy, between 2006 and 2014.</p>
<p>In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Foundation_for_Strategic_Research" title="Swedish Foundation for Strategic Research">Swedish Foundation for Strategic Research</a> founded <a href="/wiki/The_Interactive_Institute" title="The Interactive Institute">The Interactive Institute</a> - a Swedish research institute in the field of interaction design.</p>
<p>Today, interaction design is taught in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_interaction_design_programs" title="List of interaction design programs">many schools worldwide</a>. It is also a topic frequently discussed at conferences such as Gamification 2013 held at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo_Stratford_Campus" title="University of Waterloo Stratford Campus">University of Waterloo Stratford Campus</a>, where author Stephen P. Anderson discussed Seductive Interaction Design, a fresh approach to designing sites and interactions based on the stages of seduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodologies">Methodologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methodologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goal-oriented_design">Goal-oriented design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Goal-oriented design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Goal-oriented design (or Goal-Directed™ design) "is concerned most significantly with satisfying the needs and desires of the people who will interact with a product or service."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutFace3_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFace3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xviii</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Cooper" title="Alan Cooper">Alan Cooper</a> argues in <i>The Inmates Are Running the Asylum</i> that we need to take a new approach to how interactive software based problems are solved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inmates_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inmates-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1</sup> The problems faced with designing computer based interfaces are fundamentally different from the challenges we face when designing interfaces for products that do not include software (e.g. hammers). Alan introduces the concept of cognitive friction, whereby we treat things as human when they are significantly complex enough that we cannot always understand how they behave. Computer interfaces are sufficiently complex as to be treated this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inmates_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inmates-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:22</sup></p>
<p>Currently, the common problem-solving approach is very much oriented towards solving individual problems from the perspective of a business or other interested parties. However, in order to achieve best practices for problem-solving, it is argued that we must truly understand the personal and objective goals of a user first.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personas">Personas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Persona_(user_experience)" title="Persona (user experience)">Personas</a> are archetypes that describe the various goals and observed behavior patterns among your potential users and customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Designing_for_the_Digital_Age_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Designing_for_the_Digital_Age-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A persona encapsulates and explains the most critical behavioral data in a way that designers and stakeholders can understand, remember, and relate to. Unlike simple lists of findings or other types of models, personas use storytelling to engage the social and emotional aspects of our brains, which helps team members either visualize the best product behavior or see why the recommended design is successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Designing_for_the_Digital_Age_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Designing_for_the_Digital_Age-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_dimensions">Cognitive dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cognitive dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_dimensions" title="Cognitive dimensions">cognitive dimensions</a> framework<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> provides a specialized vocabulary to evaluate and modify particular design solutions. Cognitive dimensions are designed as a lightweight approach to analysis of a design quality, rather than an in-depth, detailed description. They provide a common vocabulary for discussing many factors in notation, <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> or programming language design.</p>
<p>Dimensions provide high-level descriptions of the interface and how the user interacts with it; examples include <i>consistency</i>, <i>error-proneness</i>, <i>hard mental operations</i>, <i>viscosity</i> or <i>premature commitment</i>. These concepts aid the creation of new designs from existing ones through <i>design maneuvers</i> that alter the position of the design within a particular dimension.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Affective_interaction_design">Affective interaction design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Affective interaction design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the process of interaction design, designers must be aware of key aspects in their designs that influence emotional responses in target users. For instance, the need for products to convey positive emotions while avoiding negative ones is critical to product success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharp_2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharp_2007-13">[13]</a></sup> Other important aspects for consideration include motivational, learning, creative, social, and persuasive influences. One method that can help convey such aspects is the use of expressive interfaces. In software, for example, the use of dynamic icons, animations and sound can help communicate a state of operation, thus creating a sense of interactivity and feedback. Interface aspects such as fonts, color palettes, and graphical layouts can also influence an interface's perceived effectiveness; in fact, studies have shown that affective aspects can affect a user's perception of usability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharp_2007_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sharp_2007-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emotional and pleasure theories exist to explain people's responses to the use of interactive products. These include <a href="/wiki/Don_Norman" title="Don Norman">Don Norman</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emotional_design" title="Emotional design">emotional design</a> model, <a class="external text" href="http://changingminds.org/explanations/emotions/happiness/four_pleasures.htm" rel="nofollow">Patrick Jordan's pleasure model</a>, and <a class="external text" href="https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/technology-experience" rel="nofollow">McCarthy and Wright's Technology as Experience framework</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_five_dimensions">The five dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The five dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dimensions of interaction design were first introduced in the introduction of Moggridge's book <i>Designing Interactions.</i> Gillian Crampton Smith wrote that interaction design draws on four existing design languages, 1D, 2D, 3D, 4D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Kevin Silver later proposed the fifth dimension, behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1D:_Words">1D: Words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1D: Words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This dimension defines the interactions, such that words are the interaction that users interact with.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2D:_Visual_representations">2D: Visual representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2D: Visual representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Visual representations are the elements of an interface that the user interacts with; these may include but are not limited to "typography, diagrams, icons, and other graphics".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3D:_Physical_objects_or_space">3D: Physical objects or space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 3D: Physical objects or space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The third dimension of interaction design defines the objects or space "with which or within which users interact".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="4D:_Time">4D: Time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 4D: Time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The time with which or during which the user interacts with the interface is the fourth dimension. An example of this includes "content that changes over time such as sound, video, or animation".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="5D:_Behavior">5D: Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 5D: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The final dimension, behavior, defines the users actions in reaction to the interface and how they respond to it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_Design_Association">Interaction Design Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Interaction Design Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.ixda.org/" rel="nofollow">The Interaction Design Association (IxDA)</a> was created in 2003 to serve the international Interaction Design community. Currently, the organization has over 80,000 members and more than 173 local groups around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> IxDA hosts <a class="external text" href="http://interaction16.ixda.org/" rel="nofollow">Interaction</a> the annual interaction design conference, and the <a class="external text" href="http://awards.ixda.org/" rel="nofollow">Interaction Awards</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_disciplines">Related disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Related disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">Industrial design</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines-17">[17]</a></sup></dt>
<dd>The core principles of industrial design overlap with those of interaction design. Industrial designers use their knowledge of physical <a href="/wiki/Shape" title="Shape">form</a>, color, <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">human perception</a> and desire, <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a> to create a fit of an object with the person using it.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Human_factors_and_ergonomics" title="Human factors and ergonomics">Human factors and ergonomics</a></dt>
<dd>Certain basic principles of ergonomics provide grounding for interaction design. These include <a href="/wiki/Anthropometry" title="Anthropometry">anthropometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">biomechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kinesiology" title="Kinesiology">kinesiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> as they relate to human behavior in the built environment.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">Cognitive psychology</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines-17">[17]</a></sup></dt>
<dd>Certain basic principles of cognitive psychology provide grounding for interaction design. These include <a href="/wiki/Mental_model" title="Mental model">mental models</a>, mapping, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interface_metaphors" title="Interface metaphors">interface metaphors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Affordance" title="Affordance">affordances</a>. Many of these are laid out in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_Norman" title="Donald Norman">Donald Norman</a>'s influential book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Design_of_Everyday_Things" title="The Design of Everyday Things">The Design of Everyday Things</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Human–computer interaction">Human–computer interaction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines-17">[17]</a></sup></dt>
<dd>Academic research in human–computer interaction (HCI) includes methods for describing and testing the usability of interacting with an interface, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_dimensions" title="Cognitive dimensions">cognitive dimensions</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_walkthrough" title="Cognitive walkthrough">cognitive walkthrough</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Design_research" title="Design research">Design research</a></dt>
<dd>Interaction designers are typically informed through iterative cycles of user research. User research is used to identify the needs, motivations and behaviors of end users. They <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> with an emphasis on user goals and experience, and evaluate designs in terms of <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a> and affective influence.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">Architecture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-interactiondesign.com.au_-_disciplines_and_domaines-17">[17]</a></sup></dt>
<dd>As interaction designers increasingly deal with <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">ubiquitous computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_informatics" title="Urban informatics">urban informatics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_computing" title="Urban computing">urban computing</a>, the architects' ability to make, place, and create context becomes a point of contact between the disciplines.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/User_interface_design" title="User interface design">User interface design</a></dt>
<dd>Like user interface design and <a href="/wiki/Experience_design" title="Experience design">experience design</a>, interaction design is often associated with the design of system interfaces in a variety of media but concentrates on the aspects of the interface that define and present its behavior over time, with a focus on developing the system to respond to the user's experience and not the other way around.</dd>
</dl>
