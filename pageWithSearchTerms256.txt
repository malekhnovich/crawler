<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>, an <b>ontology</b> is a formal naming and definition of the types, properties, and interrelationships of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entities" title="Entities">entities</a> that really or fundamentally exist for a particular <a href="/wiki/Domain_of_discourse" title="Domain of discourse">domain of discourse</a>. It is thus a practical application of philosophical <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(general)" title="Taxonomy (general)">taxonomy</a>.</p>
<p>An ontology compartmentalizes the variables needed for some set of computations and establishes the relationships between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRG93_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRG93-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fields of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web" title="Semantic Web">Semantic Web</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_engineering" title="Systems engineering">systems engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomedical_informatics" title="Biomedical informatics">biomedical informatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_bookmarking" title="Enterprise bookmarking">enterprise bookmarking</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Information_architecture" title="Information architecture">information architecture</a> all create ontologies to limit complexity and to organize information. The ontology can then be applied to <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_definition">Etymology and definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a></i> has its origin in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and has been applied in many different ways. The word element <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/onto-" title="wiktionary:onto-">onto-</a></i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%A4%CE%BD" title="wiktionary:ὤν">ὤν</a>, ὄντος</i>, ("being", "that which is"), present participle of the verb <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BC%B0%CE%BC%CE%AF" title="wiktionary:εἰμί">εἰμί</a></i> ("be"). The core meaning within <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> is a model for describing the world that consists of a set of types, properties, and relationship types. There is also generally an expectation that the features of the model in an ontology should closely resemble the real world (related to the object).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>What many ontologies have in common in both computer science and in philosophy is the representation of entities, ideas, and events, along with their properties and relations, according to a system of categories. In both fields, there is considerable work on problems of <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_holism" title="Confirmation holism">ontological relativity</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">Quine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saul_Kripke" title="Saul Kripke">Kripke</a> in philosophy, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Sowa" title="John F. Sowa">Sowa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Guarino" title="Nicola Guarino">Guarino</a> in computer science),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and debates concerning whether a <a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">normative</a> ontology is viable (e.g., debates over <a href="/wiki/Foundationalism" title="Foundationalism">foundationalism</a> in philosophy, and over the <a href="/wiki/Cyc" title="Cyc">Cyc</a> project in AI). Differences between the two are largely matters of focus. Computer scientists are more concerned with establishing fixed, controlled vocabularies, while philosophers are more concerned with first principles, such as whether there are such things as <a href="/wiki/Essence" title="Essence">fixed essences</a> or whether enduring objects must be ontologically more primary than processes.</p>
<p>Other fields make ontological assumptions that are sometimes explicitly elaborated and explored. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_and_economics#Definition_and_ontology_of_economics" title="Philosophy and economics">definition and ontology of economics</a> (also sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>) is hotly debated especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist_economics" title="Marxist economics">Marxist economics</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> where it is a primary concern, but also in other subfields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Such concerns intersect with those of information science when a simulation or model is intended to enable decisions in the economic realm; for example, to determine what <a href="/wiki/Capital_asset" title="Capital asset">capital assets</a> are at risk and if so by how much (see <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a>). Some claim all social sciences have explicit ontology issues because they do not have hard <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">falsifiability</a> criteria like most models in physical sciences and that indeed the lack of such widely accepted hard falsification criteria is what defines a social or soft science.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, ontologies arise out of the branch of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, which deals with the nature of reality – of what exists. This fundamental branch is concerned with analyzing various types or modes of existence, often with special attention to the relations between <a href="/wiki/Particular" title="Particular">particulars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_(metaphysics)" title="Universal (metaphysics)">universals</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_and_extrinsic_properties_(philosophy)" title="Intrinsic and extrinsic properties (philosophy)">intrinsic and extrinsic properties</a>, and between <a href="/wiki/Essence" title="Essence">essence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">existence</a>. The traditional goal of ontological inquiry in particular is to divide the world "at its joints" to discover those fundamental categories or kinds into which the world’s objects naturally fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCB94_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCB94-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the second half of the 20th century, philosophers extensively debated the possible methods or approaches to building ontologies without actually <i>building</i> any very elaborate ontologies themselves. By contrast, computer scientists were building some large and robust ontologies, such as <a href="/wiki/WordNet" title="WordNet">WordNet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyc" title="Cyc">Cyc</a>, with comparatively little debate over <i>how</i> they were built.</p>
<p>Since the mid-1970s, researchers in the field of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> (AI) have recognized that capturing knowledge is the key to building large and powerful AI systems. AI researchers argued that they could create new ontologies as <a href="/wiki/Computational_model" title="Computational model">computational models</a> that enable certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Automated_reasoning" title="Automated reasoning">automated reasoning</a>. In the 1980s, the AI community began to use the term <i>ontology</i> to refer to both a theory of a modeled world and a component of knowledge systems. Some researchers, drawing inspiration from philosophical ontologies, viewed computational ontology as a kind of applied philosophy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG08_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TG08-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1990s, the widely cited Web page and paper "Toward Principles for the Design of Ontologies Used for Knowledge Sharing" by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Gruber" title="Tom Gruber">Tom Gruber</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRG95_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRG95-9">[9]</a></sup> is credited with a deliberate definition of <i>ontology</i> as a technical term in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>. Gruber introduced the term to mean a specification of a conceptualization:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>An ontology is a description (like a formal specification of a program) of the concepts and relationships that can formally exist for an agent or a community of agents. This definition is consistent with the usage of ontology as set of concept definitions, but more general. And it is a different sense of the word than its use in philosophy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRG01_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRG01-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Gruber (1993):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Ontologies are often equated with taxonomic hierarchies of classes, class definitions, and the subsumption relation, but ontologies need not be limited to these forms. Ontologies are also not limited to <a href="/wiki/Conservative_extension" title="Conservative extension">conservative definitions</a> — that is, definitions in the traditional logic sense that only introduce terminology and do not add any knowledge about the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> To specify a conceptualization, one needs to state axioms that do constrain the possible interpretations for the defined terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRG93_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TRG93-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontology_components" title="Ontology components">Ontology components</a></div>
<p>Contemporary ontologies share many structural similarities, regardless of the language in which they are expressed. As mentioned above, most ontologies describe individuals (instances), classes (concepts), attributes, and relations. In this section each of these components is discussed in turn.</p>
<p>Common components of ontologies include:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Individuals</dt>
<dd>Instances or objects (the basic or "ground level" objects)</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Class_(set_theory)" title="Class (set theory)">Classes</a></dt>
<dd>Sets, collections, concepts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_(computer_science)" title="Class (computer science)">classes in programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class (philosophy)">types of objects</a>, or kinds of things</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Attribute_(computing)" title="Attribute (computing)">Attributes</a></dt>
<dd>Aspects, properties, features, characteristics, or parameters that objects (and classes) can have</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relation_(mathematics)" title="Relation (mathematics)">Relations</a></dt>
<dd>Ways in which classes and individuals can be related to one another</dd>
<dt>Function terms</dt>
<dd>Complex structures formed from certain relations that can be used in place of an individual term in a statement</dd>
<dt>Restrictions</dt>
<dd>Formally stated descriptions of what must be true in order for some assertion to be accepted as input</dd>
<dt>Rules</dt>
<dd>Statements in the form of an if-then (antecedent-consequent) sentence that describe the logical inferences that can be drawn from an assertion in a particular form</dd>
<dt>Axioms</dt>
<dd>Assertions (including rules) in a <a href="/wiki/Logical_form" title="Logical form">logical form</a> that together comprise the overall theory that the ontology describes in its domain of application. This definition differs from that of "axioms" in <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">generative grammar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_logic" title="Formal logic">formal logic</a>. In those disciplines, axioms include only statements asserted as <i>a priori</i> knowledge. As used here, "axioms" also include the theory derived from axiomatic statements</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Event_(philosophy)" title="Event (philosophy)">Events</a></dt>
<dd>The changing of attributes or relations</dd>
</dl>
<p>Ontologies are commonly encoded using <a href="/wiki/Ontology_language" title="Ontology language">ontology languages</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain_ontology">Domain ontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Domain ontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A domain ontology (or domain-specific ontology) represents concepts which belong to part of the world. Particular meanings of terms applied to that domain are provided by domain ontology. For example, the word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/card" title="wikt:card">card</a></i> has many different meanings. An ontology about the domain of <a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">poker</a> would model the "<a href="/wiki/Playing_card" title="Playing card">playing card</a>" meaning of the word, while an ontology about the domain of <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">computer hardware</a> would model the "<a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punched card</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Video_card" title="Video card">video card</a>" meanings.</p>
<p>Since domain ontologies represent concepts in very specific and often eclectic ways, they are often incompatible. As systems that rely on domain ontologies expand, they often need to merge domain ontologies into a more general representation. This presents a challenge to the ontology designer. Different ontologies in the same domain arise due to different languages, different intended usage of the ontologies, and different perceptions of the domain (based on cultural background, education, ideology, etc.).</p>
<p>At present, merging ontologies that are not developed from a common foundation ontology is a largely manual process and therefore time-consuming and expensive. Domain ontologies that use the same foundation ontology to provide a set of basic elements with which to specify the meanings of the domain ontology elements can be merged automatically. There are studies on generalized techniques for merging ontologies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_Ontology_Repair_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dynamic_Ontology_Repair-12">[12]</a></sup> but this area of research is still largely theoretical.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_ontology">Upper ontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Upper ontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Upper_ontology" title="Upper ontology">Upper ontology</a></div>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_ontology_(computer_science)" title="Upper ontology (computer science)">upper ontology</a> (or foundation ontology) is a model of the common objects that are generally applicable across a wide range of domain ontologies. It usually employs a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_glossary" title="Core glossary">core glossary</a> that contains the terms and associated object descriptions as they are used in various relevant domain sets.</p>
<p>There are several standardized upper ontologies available for use, including <a href="/wiki/Basic_Formal_Ontology" title="Basic Formal Ontology">BFO</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BORO_method" title="BORO method">BORO method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Core" title="Dublin Core">Dublin Core</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Formal_Ontology" title="General Formal Ontology">GFO</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OpenCyc" title="OpenCyc">OpenCyc</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ResearchCyc" title="ResearchCyc">ResearchCyc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suggested_Upper_Merged_Ontology" title="Suggested Upper Merged Ontology">SUMO</a>, the Unified Foundational Ontology (UFO),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_ontology_(computer_science)#DOLCE_and_DnS" title="Upper ontology (computer science)">DOLCE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOLCE_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOLCE-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOLCE-OWL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOLCE-OWL-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/WordNet" title="WordNet">WordNet</a>, while considered an upper ontology by some, is not strictly an ontology. However, it has been employed as a linguistic tool for learning domain ontologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gellish" title="Gellish">Gellish</a> ontology is an example of a combination of an upper and a domain ontology.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering">Engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontology_engineering" title="Ontology engineering">Ontology engineering</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ontology_engineering" title="Ontology engineering">Ontology engineering</a> (or ontology building) is a subfield of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_engineering" title="Knowledge engineering">knowledge engineering</a>. It studies the ontology development process, the ontology life cycle, the methods and methodologies for building ontologies, and the tool suites and languages that support them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PFC04_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-PFC04-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DMN_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-DMN-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ontology engineering aims to make explicit the knowledge contained within software applications, and within enterprises and business procedures for a particular domain. Ontology engineering offers a direction towards solving the interoperability problems brought about by semantic obstacles, such as the obstacles related to the definitions of business terms and software classes. Ontology engineering is a set of tasks related to the development of ontologies for a particular domain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIS00_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PIS00-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editor">Editor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Editor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Ontology editors</b> are applications designed to assist in the creation or manipulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_(computer_science)" title="Ontology (computer science)">ontologies</a>. They often express ontologies in one of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_language_(computer_science)" title="Ontology language (computer science)">ontology languages</a>. Some provide export to other ontology languages however.</p>
<p>Among the most relevant criteria for choosing an ontology editor are the degree to which the editor abstracts from the actual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_language_(computer_science)" title="Ontology language (computer science)">ontology representation language</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Persistence_(computer_science)" title="Persistence (computer science)">persistence</a> and the visual navigation possibilities within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knowledge_model" title="Knowledge model">knowledge model</a>. Next come built-in <a href="/wiki/Inference_engine" title="Inference engine">inference engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_extraction" title="Information extraction">information extraction</a> facilities, and the support of meta-ontologies such as <a href="/wiki/OWL-S" title="OWL-S">OWL-S</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Core" title="Dublin Core">Dublin Core</a>, etc. Another important feature is the ability to import &amp; export foreign <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knowledge_representation" title="Knowledge representation">knowledge representation</a> languages for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_matching" title="Ontology matching">ontology matching</a>. Ontologies are developed for a specific purpose and application.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning">Learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontology_learning" title="Ontology learning">Ontology learning</a></div>
<p>Ontology learning is the automatic or semi-automatic creation of ontologies, including extracting a domain's terms from natural language text. As building ontologies manually is extremely labor-intensive and time consuming, there is great motivation to automate the process. Information extraction and text mining methods have been explored to automatically link ontologies to documents, e.g. in the context of the BioCreative challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontology_language" title="Ontology language">Ontology language</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ontology_language" title="Ontology language">ontology language</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">formal language</a> used to encode the ontology. There are a number of such languages for ontologies, both proprietary and standards-based:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Common_Algebraic_Specification_Language" title="Common Algebraic Specification Language">Common Algebraic Specification Language</a> is a general logic-based specification language developed within the IFIP working group 1.3 "Foundations of System Specifications" and functions as a de facto standard in the area of software specifications. It is now being applied to ontology specifications in order to provide modularity and structuring mechanisms.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_logic" title="Common logic">Common logic</a> is ISO standard 24707, a specification for a family of ontology languages that can be accurately translated into each other.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cyc" title="Cyc">Cyc</a> project has its own ontology language called <a href="/wiki/CycL" title="CycL">CycL</a>, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-order_predicate_calculus" title="First-order predicate calculus">first-order predicate calculus</a> with some higher-order extensions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DOGMA" title="DOGMA">DOGMA</a> (Developing Ontology-Grounded Methods and Applications) adopts the fact-oriented modeling approach to provide a higher level of semantic stability.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Gellish" title="Gellish">Gellish</a> language includes rules for its own extension and thus integrates an ontology with an ontology language.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/IDEF5" title="IDEF5">IDEF5</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> method to develop and maintain usable, accurate, domain ontologies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Knowledge_Interchange_Format" title="Knowledge Interchange Format">KIF</a> is a syntax for <a href="/wiki/First-order_logic" title="First-order logic">first-order logic</a> that is based on <a href="/wiki/S-expression" title="S-expression">S-expressions</a>. SUO-KIF is a derivative version supporting the <a href="/wiki/Suggested_Upper_Merged_Ontology" title="Suggested Upper Merged Ontology">Suggested Upper Merged Ontology</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meta-Object_Facility" title="Meta-Object Facility">MOF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language" title="Unified Modeling Language">UML</a> are standards of the <a href="/wiki/Object_Management_Group" title="Object Management Group">OMG</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Olog" title="Olog">Olog</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Category_theory" title="Category theory">category theoretic</a> approach to ontologies, emphasizing translations between ontologies using <a href="/wiki/Functor" title="Functor">functors</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Open_Biomedical_Ontologies" title="Open Biomedical Ontologies">OBO</a>, a language used for biological and biomedical ontologies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/OntoUML" title="OntoUML">OntoUML</a> is an ontologically well-founded profile of UML for conceptual modeling of domain ontologies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a> is a language for making ontological statements, developed as a follow-on from <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RDFS" title="RDFS">RDFS</a>, as well as earlier ontology language projects including <a href="/wiki/Ontology_Inference_Layer" title="Ontology Inference Layer">OIL</a>, <a href="/wiki/DARPA_Agent_Markup_Language" title="DARPA Agent Markup Language">DAML</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DAMLplusOIL" title="DAMLplusOIL">DAML+OIL</a>. OWL is intended to be used over the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, and all its elements (classes, properties and individuals) are defined as RDF <a href="/wiki/Web_resource" title="Web resource">resources</a>, and identified by <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Identifier" title="Uniform Resource Identifier">URIs</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rule_Interchange_Format" title="Rule Interchange Format">Rule Interchange Format</a> (RIF) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-Logic" title="F-Logic">F-Logic</a> combine ontologies and rules.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semantic_Application_Design_Language" title="Semantic Application Design Language">Semantic Application Design Language</a> (SADL)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> captures a subset of the expressiveness of <a href="/wiki/Web_Ontology_Language" title="Web Ontology Language">OWL</a>, using an English-like language entered via an <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_(software)" title="Eclipse (software)">Eclipse</a> Plug-in.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SBVR" title="SBVR">SBVR</a> (Semantics of Business Vocabularies and Rules) is an OMG standard adopted in industry to build ontologies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TOVE_Project" title="TOVE Project">TOVE Project</a>, TOronto Virtual Enterprise project</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_examples">Published examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Published examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>AURUM - Information Security Ontology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> An ontology for information security knowledge sharing, enabling users to collaboratively understand and extend the domain knowledge body. It may serve as a basis for automated information security risk and compliance management.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BabelNet" title="BabelNet">BabelNet</a>, a very large multilingual semantic network and ontology, lexicalized in many languages</li>
<li>Basic Formal Ontology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> a formal upper ontology designed to support scientific research</li>
<li>BioPAX,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> an ontology for the exchange and interoperability of biological pathway (cellular processes) data</li>
<li>BMO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> an e-Business Model Ontology based on a review of enterprise ontologies and business model literature</li>
<li>CCO and GexKB,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Application Ontologies (APO) that integrate diverse types of knowledge with the Cell Cycle Ontology (CCO) and the Gene Expression Knowledge Base (GexKB)</li>
<li>CContology (Customer Complaint Ontology),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> an e-business ontology to support online customer complaint management</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CIDOC_Conceptual_Reference_Model" title="CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model">CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model</a>, an ontology for <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>COSMO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> a Foundation Ontology (current version in OWL) that is designed to contain representations of all of the primitive concepts needed to logically specify the meanings of any domain entity. It is intended to serve as a basic ontology that can be used to translate among the representations in other ontologies or databases. It started as a merger of the basic elements of the OpenCyc and SUMO ontologies, and has been supplemented with other ontology elements (types, relations) so as to include representations of all of the words in the <a href="/wiki/Longman_Dictionary_of_Contemporary_English" title="Longman Dictionary of Contemporary English">Longman dictionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Defining_vocabulary" title="Defining vocabulary">defining vocabulary</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyc" title="Cyc">Cyc</a>, a large Foundation Ontology for formal representation of the universe of discourse</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Disease_Ontology" title="Disease Ontology">Disease Ontology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> designed to facilitate the mapping of diseases and associated conditions to particular medical codes</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_ontology_(computer_science)#DOLCE_and_DnS" title="Upper ontology (computer science)">DOLCE</a>, a Descriptive Ontology for Linguistic and Cognitive Engineering<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOLCE_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-DOLCE-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOLCE-OWL_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-DOLCE-OWL-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Drammar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Drammar (page does not exist)">Drammar</a>, ontology of drama</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dublin_Core" title="Dublin Core">Dublin Core</a>, a simple ontology for documents and publishing</li>
<li>Foundational, Core and Linguistic Ontologies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Foundational_Model_of_Anatomy" title="Foundational Model of Anatomy">Foundational Model of Anatomy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> an ontology for human anatomy</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FOAF_(software)" title="FOAF (software)">Friend of a Friend</a>, an ontology for describing persons, their activities and their relations to other people and objects</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_Ontology" title="Gene Ontology">Gene Ontology</a> for <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gellish_English_dictionary" title="Gellish English dictionary">Gellish English dictionary</a>, an ontology that includes a dictionary and taxonomy that includes an upper ontology and a lower ontology that focusses on industrial and business applications in engineering, technology and procurement. See also <a class="external text" href="http://gellish.wiki.sourceforge.net/" rel="nofollow">Gellish</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Source" title="Open Source">Open Source</a> project on SourceForge.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geopolitical_ontology" title="Geopolitical ontology">Geopolitical ontology</a>, an ontology describing geopolitical information created by <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a>(FAO). The geopolitical ontology includes names in multiple languages (English, French, Spanish, Arabic, Chinese, Russian and Italian); maps standard coding systems (UN, ISO, FAOSTAT, AGROVOC, etc.); provides relations among territories (land borders, group membership, etc.); and tracks historical changes. In addition, FAO provides web services &lt;<a class="external free" href="http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/webservices.asp?lang=en" rel="nofollow">http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/webservices.asp?lang=en</a>&gt; of geopolitical ontology and a module maker &lt;<a class="external free" href="http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/geoinfo/modulemaker/index.html" rel="nofollow">http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/geoinfo/modulemaker/index.html</a>&gt; to download modules of the geopolitical ontology into different formats (RDF, XML, and EXCEL). See more information on the <a href="/wiki/FAO_Country_Profiles" title="FAO Country Profiles">FAO Country Profiles</a> geopolitical ontology web page &lt;<a class="external free" href="http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/geoinfo.asp?lang=en" rel="nofollow">http://www.fao.org/countryprofiles/geoinfo.asp?lang=en</a>&gt;.</li>
<li>GOLD,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> General Ontology for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_linguistics" title="Descriptive linguistics">Linguistic Description</a></li>
<li>GUM (Generalized Upper Model),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> a linguistically motivated ontology for mediating between clients systems and natural language technology</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/IDEAS_Group" title="IDEAS Group">IDEAS Group</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> a formal ontology for enterprise architecture being developed by the Australian, Canadian, UK and U.S. Defence Depts.</li>
<li>Linkbase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> a formal representation of the biomedical domain, founded upon <a class="external text" href="http://www.ifomis.org/bfo/" rel="nofollow">Basic Formal Ontology</a>.</li>
<li>LPL, Lawson Pattern Language</li>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIFSTD" title="NIFSTD">NIFSTD</a> Ontologies from the <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience_Information_Framework" title="Neuroscience Information Framework">Neuroscience Information Framework</a>: a modular set of ontologies for the neuroscience domain. <li>OMNIBUS Ontology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> an ontology of learning, instruction, and instructional design</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ontology_for_Biomedical_Investigations" title="Ontology for Biomedical Investigations">Ontology for Biomedical Investigations</a>, an open access, integrated ontology for the description of biological and clinical investigations</li>
<li>ONSTR,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Ontology for Newborn Screening Follow-up and Translational Research <a class="external autonumber" href="https://nbsdc.org/pdf/ONSTR_ICBO2013_NikolicEtAl_CameraReadyPaper.pdf" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>, Newborn Screening Follow-up Data Integration Collaborative, Emory University, Atlanta, GA. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The development of ontologies for the Web has led to the emergence of services providing lists or directories of ontologies with search facility. Such directories have been called ontology libraries.</p>
<p>The following are libraries of human-selected ontologies.</p>
<ul>
<li>COLORE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> is an open repository of first-order ontologies in <a href="/wiki/Common_Logic" title="Common Logic">Common Logic</a> with formal links between ontologies in the repository.</li>
<li>DAML Ontology Library<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> maintains a legacy of ontologies in DAML.</li>
<li>Ontology Design Patterns portal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> is a wiki repository of reusable components and practices for ontology design, and also maintains a list of <i>exemplary ontologies</i>. Started within the <a class="external text" href="http://www.neon-project.org" rel="nofollow">NeOn</a> EU project.</li>
<li>Protégé Ontology Library<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> contains a set of OWL, Frame-based and other format ontologies.</li>
<li>SchemaWeb<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> is a directory of RDF schemata expressed in RDFS, OWL and DAML+OIL.</li>
</ul>
<p>The following are both directories and search engines. They include crawlers searching the Web for well-formed ontologies.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/OBO_Foundry" title="OBO Foundry">OBO Foundry</a> is a suite of interoperable reference ontologies in biology and biomedicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17989687_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17989687-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bioportal (ontology repository of NCBO)</li>
<li>OntoSelect<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Ontology Library offers similar services for RDF/S, DAML and OWL ontologies.</li>
<li>Ontaria<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> is a "searchable and browsable directory of semantic web data" with a focus on RDF vocabularies with OWL ontologies. (NB Project "on hold" since 2004).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swoogle" title="Swoogle">Swoogle</a> is a directory and search engine for all RDF resources available on the Web, including ontologies.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.oor.net" rel="nofollow">OOR</a> - the <a class="external text" href="http://OpenOntologyRepository.org" rel="nofollow">Open Ontology Repository initiative</a> - <a class="external free" href="http://oor.net" rel="nofollow">http://oor.net</a></li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.thezfiles.co.za/ROMULUS/" rel="nofollow">ROMULUS</a> is a foundational ontology repository aimed at improving semantic interoperability. Currently there are three foundational ontologies in the repository: DOLCE, BFO and GFO.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_applications">Examples of applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Examples of applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, ontologies can be used beneficially in</p>
<ul>
<li>enterprise applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A more concrete example is <a href="/wiki/SAPPHIRE_(Health_care)" title="SAPPHIRE (Health care)">SAPPHIRE (Health care)</a> or <i>Situational Awareness and Preparedness for Public Health Incidences and Reasoning Engines</i> which is a <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_information_system" title="Health information system">health information system</a> capable of tracking and evaluating situations and occurrences that may affect <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_information_systems" title="Geographic information systems">geographic information systems</a> bring together data from different sources and benefit therefore from ontological metadata which helps to connect the semantics of the data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology_(information_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Werner Ceusters has noted the confusion caused by the significant differences in the meaning of word ontology when used by philosophy compared with the use of the word ontology in computer science and advocates for greater precision in use of the word ontology so that members of the various disciplines using various definitions of the word ontology can communicate. He writes 'before one is able to answer the question 'what is an ontology?', one must provide first an answer to the question 'what does the word ontology mean?'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>It's also not clear how ontology fits with Schema on Read (NoSQL) databases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
