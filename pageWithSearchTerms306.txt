<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Operations research</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For the academic journal, see <a href="/wiki/Operations_Research_(journal)" title="Operations Research (journal)">Operations Research</a>.</div>
<p><b>Operations research</b>, or <b>operational research</b> in British usage, is a discipline that deals with the application of advanced analytical methods to help make better decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Further, the term 'operational analysis' is used in the British (and some British Commonwealth) military, as an intrinsic part of capability development, management and assurance. In particular, operational analysis forms part of the Combined Operational Effectiveness and Investment Appraisals (COEIA), which support British defence capability acquisition decision-making.</p>
<p>It is often considered to be a sub-field of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The terms <a href="/wiki/Management_science" title="Management science">management science</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_science" title="Decision science">decision science</a> are sometimes used as synonyms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Employing techniques from other mathematical sciences, such as <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical modeling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical analysis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_optimization" title="Mathematical optimization">mathematical optimization</a>, operations research arrives at optimal or near-optimal solutions to complex decision-making problems. Because of its emphasis on human-technology interaction and because of its focus on practical applications, operations research has overlap with other disciplines, notably <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">industrial engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operations_management" title="Operations management">operations management</a>, and draws on <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organizational_studies" title="Organizational studies">organization science</a>. Operations research is often concerned with determining the <a href="/wiki/Maxima_and_minima" title="Maxima and minima">maximum</a> (of profit, performance, or yield) or minimum (of loss, risk, or cost) of some real-world objective. Originating in military efforts before World War II, its techniques have grown to concern problems in a variety of industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsor.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsor.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Operational research (OR) encompasses a wide range of problem-solving techniques and methods applied in the pursuit of improved decision-making and efficiency, such as <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_optimization" title="Mathematical optimization">mathematical optimization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queueing_theory" title="Queueing theory">queueing theory</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">stochastic-process</a> models, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markov_Decision_Process" title="Markov Decision Process">Markov decision processes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Econometrics" title="Econometrics">econometric methods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_envelopment_analysis" title="Data envelopment analysis">data envelopment analysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_networks" title="Neural networks">neural networks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expert_System" title="Expert System">expert systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision_analysis" title="Decision analysis">decision analysis</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Analytic_hierarchy_process" title="Analytic hierarchy process">analytic hierarchy process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Nearly all of these techniques involve the construction of mathematical models that attempt to describe the system. Because of the computational and statistical nature of most of these fields, OR also has strong ties to <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analytics" title="Analytics">analytics</a>. Operational researchers faced with a new problem must determine which of these techniques are most appropriate given the nature of the system, the goals for improvement, and constraints on time and computing power.</p>
<p>The major subdisciplines in modern operational research, as identified by the journal <i>Operations Research</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Computing and information technologies</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Financial_engineering" title="Financial engineering">Financial engineering</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_science" title="Service science">service sciences</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain_management" title="Supply chain management">supply chain management</a></li>
<li>Policy modeling and public sector work</li>
<li>Revenue management</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">Simulation</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stochastics" title="Stochastics">Stochastic models</a></li>
<li>Transportation</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a discipline, operational research originated in the efforts of military planners during World War I (convoy theory and <a href="/wiki/Lanchester%27s_laws" title="Lanchester's laws">Lanchester's laws</a>). In the decades after the two world wars, the techniques were more widely applied to problems in business, industry and society. Since that time, operational research has expanded into a field widely used in industries ranging from petrochemicals to airlines, finance, logistics, and government, moving to a focus on the development of mathematical models that can be used to analyse and optimize complex systems, and has become an area of active academic and industrial research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsor.org_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsor.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_origins">Historical origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early work in operational research was carried out by individuals such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>. His research into the cost of transportation and sorting of mail led to England's <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Penny_Post" title="Uniform Penny Post">universal "Penny Post"</a> in 1840, and studies into the dynamical behaviour of railway vehicles in defence of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">GWR</a>'s broad gauge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Bridgman" title="Percy Bridgman">Percy Bridgman</a> brought operational research to bear on problems in physics in the 1920s and would later attempt to extend these to the social sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern operational research originated at the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Research_Establishment" title="Telecommunications Research Establishment">Bawdsey Research Station</a> in the UK in 1937 and was the result of an initiative of the station's superintendent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Percival_Rowe" title="Albert Percival Rowe">A. P. Rowe</a>. Rowe conceived the idea as a means to analyse and improve the working of the UK's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_warning_radar" title="Early warning radar">early warning radar</a> system, <a href="/wiki/Chain_Home" title="Chain Home">Chain Home</a> (CH). Initially, he analysed the operating of the radar equipment and its communication networks, expanding later to include the operating personnel's behaviour. This revealed unappreciated limitations of the CH network and allowed remedial action to be taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists in the United Kingdom including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_Maynard_Stuart_Blackett" title="Patrick Maynard Stuart Blackett">Patrick Blackett</a> (later Lord Blackett OM PRS), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cecil_Gordon_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cecil Gordon (scientist) (page does not exist)">Cecil Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solly_Zuckerman,_Baron_Zuckerman" title="Solly Zuckerman, Baron Zuckerman">Solly Zuckerman</a>, (later Baron Zuckerman OM, KCB, FRS), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conrad_Hal_Waddington" title="Conrad Hal Waddington">C. H. Waddington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Owen_Wansbrough-Jones" title="Owen Wansbrough-Jones">Owen Wansbrough-Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Yates" title="Frank Yates">Frank Yates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Bronowski" title="Jacob Bronowski">Jacob Bronowski</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a>, and in the United States with <a href="/wiki/George_Dantzig" title="George Dantzig">George Dantzig</a> looked for ways to make better decisions in such areas as <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> and training schedules</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern field of operational research arose during World War II. In the World War II era, operational research was defined as "a scientific method of providing executive departments with a quantitative basis for decisions regarding the operations under their control."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C67-3-4-48-para-1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-C67-3-4-48-para-1-10">[10]</a></sup> Other names for it included operational analysis (UK Ministry of Defence from 1962)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROCATWO291_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-PROCATWO291-11">[11]</a></sup> and quantitative management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> close to 1,000 men and women in Britain were engaged in operational research. About 200 operational research scientists worked for the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Blackett" title="Patrick Blackett">Patrick Blackett</a> worked for several different organizations during the war. Early in the war while working for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Establishment" title="Royal Aircraft Establishment">Royal Aircraft Establishment</a> (RAE) he set up a team known as the "Circus" which helped to reduce the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_artillery" title="Anti-aircraft artillery">anti-aircraft artillery</a> rounds needed to shoot down an enemy aircraft from an average of over 20,000 at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Britain" title="Battle of Britain">Battle of Britain</a> to 4,000 in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1941 Blackett moved from the RAE to the Navy, after first working with <a href="/wiki/RAF_Coastal_Command" title="RAF Coastal Command">RAF Coastal Command</a>, in 1941 and then early in 1942 to the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Blackett's team at Coastal Command's Operational Research Section (CC-ORS) included two future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> winners and many other people who went on to be pre-eminent in their fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> They undertook a number of crucial analyses that aided the war effort. Britain introduced the <a href="/wiki/Convoy" title="Convoy">convoy</a> system to reduce shipping losses, but while the principle of using warships to accompany merchant ships was generally accepted, it was unclear whether it was better for convoys to be small or large. Convoys travel at the speed of the slowest member, so small convoys can travel faster. It was also argued that small convoys would be harder for German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boats</a> to detect. On the other hand, large convoys could deploy more warships against an attacker. Blackett's staff showed that the losses suffered by convoys depended largely on the number of escort vessels present, rather than the size of the convoy. Their conclusion was that a few large convoys are more defensible than many small ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><span id="RAF_Coastal_Command.27s_Operational_Research_Section"></span>While performing an analysis of the methods used by <a href="/wiki/RAF_Coastal_Command" title="RAF Coastal Command">RAF Coastal Command</a> to hunt and destroy submarines, one of the analysts asked what colour the aircraft were. As most of them were from Bomber Command they were painted black for night-time operations. At the suggestion of CC-ORS a test was run to see if that was the best colour to camouflage the aircraft for daytime operations in the grey North Atlantic skies. Tests showed that aircraft painted white were on average not spotted until they were 20% closer than those painted black. This change indicated that 30% more submarines would be attacked and sunk for the same number of sightings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As a result of these findings Coastal Command changed their aircraft to using white undersurfaces.</p>

<p>Other work by the CC-ORS indicated that on average if the trigger depth of aerial-delivered <a href="/wiki/Depth_charge" title="Depth charge">depth charges</a> (DCs) were changed from 100 feet to 25 feet, the kill ratios would go up. The reason was that if a U-boat saw an aircraft only shortly before it arrived over the target then at 100 feet the charges would do no damage (because the U-boat wouldn't have had time to descend as far as 100 feet), and if it saw the aircraft a long way from the target it had time to alter course under water so the chances of it being within the 20-foot kill zone of the charges was small. It was more efficient to attack those submarines close to the surface when the targets' locations were better known than to attempt their destruction at greater depths when their positions could only be guessed. Before the change of settings from 100 feet to 25 feet, 1% of submerged U-boats were sunk and 14% damaged. After the change, 7% were sunk and 11% damaged. (If submarines were caught on the surface, even if attacked shortly after submerging, the numbers rose to 11% sunk and 15% damaged). Blackett observed "there can be few cases where such a great operational gain had been obtained by such a small and simple change of tactics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="RAF_Bomber_Command.27s_Operational_Research_Section"></span>Bomber Command's Operational Research Section (BC-ORS), analysed a report of a survey carried out by <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For the survey, Bomber Command inspected all bombers returning from bombing raids over Germany over a particular period. All damage inflicted by German <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">air defences</a> was noted and the recommendation was given that armour be added in the most heavily damaged areas. This recommendation was not adopted because the fact that the aircraft returned with these areas damaged indicated these areas were not vital, and adding armour to non-vital areas where damage is acceptable negatively affects aircraft performance. Their suggestion to remove some of the crew so that an aircraft loss would result in fewer personnel losses, was also rejected by RAF command. Blackett's team made the logical recommendation that the armour be placed in the areas which were completely untouched by damage in the bombers which returned. They reasoned that the survey was biased, since it only included aircraft that returned to Britain. The untouched areas of returning aircraft were probably vital areas, which, if hit, would result in the loss of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>When Germany organised its air defences into the <a href="/wiki/Kammhuber_Line" title="Kammhuber Line">Kammhuber Line</a>, it was realised by the British that if the RAF bombers were to fly in a <a href="/wiki/Bomber_stream" title="Bomber stream">bomber stream</a> they could overwhelm the night fighters who flew in individual cells directed to their targets by ground controllers. It was then a matter of calculating the statistical loss from collisions against the statistical loss from night fighters to calculate how close the bombers should fly to minimise RAF losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The "exchange rate" ratio of output to input was a characteristic feature of operational research. By comparing the number of flying hours put in by Allied aircraft to the number of U-boat sightings in a given area, it was possible to redistribute aircraft to more productive patrol areas. Comparison of exchange rates established "effectiveness ratios" useful in planning. The ratio of 60 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mine_(naval)" title="Mine (naval)">mines</a> laid per ship sunk was common to several campaigns: German mines in British ports, British mines on German routes, and United States mines in Japanese routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proceedings_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proceedings-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Operational research doubled the on-target bomb rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-29" title="B-29">B-29s</a> bombing Japan from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marianas_Islands" title="Marianas Islands">Marianas Islands</a> by increasing the training ratio from 4 to 10 percent of flying hours; revealed that wolf-packs of three United States submarines were the most effective number to enable all members of the pack to engage targets discovered on their individual patrol stations; revealed that glossy enamel paint was more effective camouflage for night fighters than traditional dull camouflage paint finish, and the smooth paint finish increased airspeed by reducing skin friction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proceedings_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Proceedings-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On land, the operational research sections of the Army Operational Research Group (AORG) of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Supply" title="Ministry of Supply">Ministry of Supply</a> (MoS) were landed in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Normandy in 1944</a>, and they followed British forces in the advance across Europe. They analysed, among other topics, the effectiveness of artillery, aerial bombing and anti-tank shooting.</p>

<p>With expanded techniques and growing awareness of the field at the close of the war, operational research was no longer limited to only operational, but was extended to encompass equipment procurement, training, logistics and infrastructure. Operations Research also grew in many areas other than the military once scientists learned to apply its principles to the civilian sector. With the development of the <a href="/wiki/Simplex_algorithm" title="Simplex algorithm">simplex algorithm</a> for <a href="/wiki/Linear_programming" title="Linear programming">linear programming</a> in 1947<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitt.edu_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pitt.edu-23">[23]</a></sup> and the development of computers over the next three decades, Operations Research can now "solve problems with hundreds of thousands of variables and constraints. Moreover, the large volumes of data required for such problems can be stored and manipulated very efficiently."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitt.edu_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-pitt.edu-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_science">Management science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Management science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Management_science" title="Management science">Management science</a></div>
<p>In 1967 <a href="/wiki/Stafford_Beer" title="Stafford Beer">Stafford Beer</a> characterized the field of management science as "the business use of operations research".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, in modern times the term management science may also be used to refer to the separate fields of <a href="/wiki/Organizational_studies" title="Organizational studies">organizational studies</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_strategy" title="Corporate strategy">corporate strategy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Like operational research itself, management science (MS) is an interdisciplinary branch of applied mathematics devoted to optimal decision planning, with strong links with economics, business, engineering, and other <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a>. It uses various <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>-based principles, <a href="/wiki/Strategy" title="Strategy">strategies</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytical_method" title="Analytical method">analytical methods</a> including <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical modeling</a>, statistics and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_algorithm" title="Numerical algorithm">numerical algorithms</a> to improve an organization's ability to enact rational and meaningful management decisions by arriving at optimal or near optimal solutions to complex decision problems. In short, management sciences help businesses to achieve their goals using the scientific methods of operational research.</p>
<p>The management scientist's mandate is to use rational, systematic, science-based techniques to inform and improve decisions of all kinds. Of course, the techniques of management science are not restricted to business applications but may be applied to military, medical, public administration, charitable groups, political groups or community groups.</p>
<p>Management science is concerned with developing and applying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_modeling" title="Scientific modeling">models</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concepts</a> that may prove useful in helping to illuminate management issues and solve managerial problems, as well as designing and developing new and better models of organizational excellence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LS_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-LS-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The application of these models within the corporate sector became known as management science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UTK_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-UTK-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operations_research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Applications of management science are abundant such as in airlines, manufacturing companies, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_organization" title="Service organization">service organizations</a>, military branches, and government. The range of problems and issues to which management science has contributed insights and solutions is vast. It includes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LS_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-LS-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Scheduling airlines, trains, buses etc.</li>
<li>Assignment (assigning crew to flights, trains or buses; employees to projects)</li>
<li>Facility location (deciding the most appropriate location for the new facilities such as a warehouse, factory or fire station)</li>
<li>Network flows (managing the flow of water from reservoirs)</li>
<li>Health service (information and supply chain management for health services)</li>
<li>Game theory (identifying, understanding and developing the strategies adopted by companies)</li>
</ul>
<p>Management science is also concerned with so-called "soft-operational analysis", which concerns methods for <a href="/wiki/Strategic_planning" title="Strategic planning">strategic planning</a>, strategic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_support" title="Decision support">decision support</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Problem_structuring_methods" title="Problem structuring methods">problem structuring methods</a>. In dealing with these sorts of challenges mathematical <a href="/wiki/Modeling_and_simulation" title="Modeling and simulation">modeling and simulation</a> are not appropriate or will not suffice. Therefore, during the past 30 years, a number of non-quantified modeling methods have been developed. These include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>stakeholder based approaches including <a href="/wiki/Metagame_analysis" title="Metagame analysis">metagame analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drama_theory" title="Drama theory">drama theory</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Morphological_analysis_(problem-solving)" title="Morphological analysis (problem-solving)">morphological analysis</a> and various forms of <a href="/wiki/Influence_diagram" title="Influence diagram">influence diagrams</a></li>
<li>approaches using cognitive mapping</li>
<li>the strategic choice approach</li>
<li>robustness analysis</li>
</ul>
