<p><b>Christopher Wolfgang Alexander</b> (born 4 October 1936 in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, Austria)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a widely influential <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a> and design theorist, and currently <a href="/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States#Professor_emeritus_and_emerita" title="Professors in the United States">emeritus professor</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>. His theories about the nature of human-centered <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> have had notable impacts across many fields beyond architecture, including <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and other fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-3">[3]</a></sup> Alexander has also designed and personally built over 100 buildings, both as an architect and a general contractor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the field of software, Alexander is regarded as the father of the <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern language</a> movement. The first <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a> – the technology behind <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> – led directly from Alexander's work, according to its creator, <a href="/wiki/Ward_Cunningham" title="Ward Cunningham">Ward Cunningham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C2_Wiki_Front_Page_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-C2_Wiki_Front_Page-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c2.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-c2.com-7">[7]</a></sup> Alexander's work has also influenced the development of <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile software development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In architecture, Alexander's work is used by a number of different contemporary architectural communities of practice, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Urbanist" title="New Urbanist">New Urbanist</a> movement, to help people to reclaim control over their own built environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, Alexander is controversial among some mainstream architects and critics, in part because his work is often harshly critical of much of contemporary architectural theory and practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nikos_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nikos-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexander is known for his many books on the design and building process, including <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_on_the_Synthesis_of_Form" title="Notes on the Synthesis of Form">Notes on the Synthesis of Form</a>, <a href="/wiki/A_City_is_Not_a_Tree" title="A City is Not a Tree">A City is Not a Tree</a></i> (first published as a paper and recently re-published in book form), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Timeless_Way_of_Building" title="The Timeless Way of Building">The Timeless Way of Building</a>, A New Theory of Urban Design,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oregon_Experiment" title="The Oregon Experiment">The Oregon Experiment</a>.</i> More recently he published the four-volume <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nature_of_Order" title="The Nature of Order">The Nature of Order</a>: An Essay on the Art of Building and the Nature of the Universe,</i> about his newer theories of "morphogenetic" processes, and <i>The Battle for the Life and Beauty of the Earth</i>, about the implementation of his theories in a large building project in Japan.</p>
<p>Alexander is perhaps best known for his 1977 book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Pattern_Language" title="A Pattern Language">A Pattern Language</a>,</i> a perennial seller some four decades after publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Reasoning that users are more sensitive to their needs than any architect could be,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> he produced and validated (in collaboration with his students Sara Ishikawa, <a href="/wiki/Murray_Silverstein" title="Murray Silverstein">Murray Silverstein</a>, Max Jacobson, Ingrid King, and Shlomo Angel) a "<a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern language</a>" to empower anyone to design and build at any scale.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a young child Alexander emigrated in fall 1938<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> with his parents from Austria to England, when his parents were forced to flee the Nazi regime. He spent much of his childhood in <a href="/wiki/Chichester" title="Chichester">Chichester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, England, where he began his education in the sciences. He moved from England to the United States in 1958 to study at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>. He moved to <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a> in 1963 to accept an appointment as Professor of Architecture, a position he would hold for almost 40 years. In 2002, after his retirement, Alexander moved to <a href="/wiki/Arundel" title="Arundel">Arundel</a>, England, where he continued to write, teach and build. Alexander is married to Margaret Moore Alexander, and he has two daughters, Sophie and Lily, by his former wife Pamela.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alexander attended Oundle school, England. In 1954, he was awarded the top open scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a> in chemistry and physics, and went on to read mathematics. He earned a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">Bachelor's degree</a> in Architecture and a <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">Master's degree</a> in Mathematics. He took his doctorate at Harvard (the first <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in Architecture ever awarded at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>), and was elected fellow at Harvard. During the same period he worked at MIT in transportation theory and computer science, and worked at Harvard in cognition and cognitive studies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alexander was elected Fellow of the Society of Fellows, Harvard University 1961-64; awarded the First Medal for Research by the American Institute of Architects, 1972; elected member of the Swedish Royal Academy, 1980; winner of the Best Building in Japan award, 1985; winner of the ACSA (<a href="/wiki/Association_of_Collegiate_Schools_of_Architecture" title="Association of Collegiate Schools of Architecture">Association of Collegiate Schools of Architecture</a>) Distinguished Professor Award, 1986 and 1987;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> invited to present the Louis Kahn Memorial Lecture, 1992; awarded the Seaside Prize, 1994; elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, 1996;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-3">[3]</a></sup> one of the two inaugural recipients of the Athena Award, given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_for_the_New_Urbanism" title="Congress for the New Urbanism">Congress for the New Urbanism</a> (CNU), 2006;. awarded (<i>in absentia</i>) the <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Scully_Prize" title="Vincent Scully Prize">Vincent Scully Prize</a> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Museum" title="National Building Museum">National Building Museum</a>, 2009; awarded the lifetime achievement award by the <a href="/wiki/Urban_Design_Group" title="Urban Design Group">Urban Design Group</a>, 2011; winner of the <a href="/wiki/Global_Award_for_Sustainable_Architecture" title="Global Award for Sustainable Architecture">Global Award for Sustainable Architecture</a>, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Author">Author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Timeless_Way_of_Building" title="The Timeless Way of Building">The Timeless Way of Building</a></i> (1979) described the perfection of use to which buildings could aspire:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There is one timeless way of building. It is a thousand years old, and the same today as it has ever been. The great traditional buildings of the past, the villages and tents and temples in which man feels at home, have always been made by people who were very close to the center of this way. It is not possible to make great buildings, or great towns, beautiful places, places where you feel yourself, places where you feel alive, except by following this way. And, as you will see, this way will lead anyone who looks for it to buildings which are themselves as ancient in their form, as the trees and hills, and as our faces are.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/A_Pattern_Language" title="A Pattern Language">A Pattern Language: Towns, Buildings, Construction</a></i> (1977) described a practical architectural system in a form that a theoretical mathematician or computer scientist might call a <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">generative grammar</a>.</p>
<p>The work originated from an observation that many medieval cities are attractive and harmonious. The authors said that this occurs because they were built to local regulations that required specific features, but freed the architect to adapt them to particular situations.</p>
<p>The book provides rules and pictures, and leaves decisions to be taken from the precise environment of the project. It describes exact methods for constructing practical, safe and attractive designs at every scale, from entire regions, through cities, neighborhoods, gardens, buildings, rooms, built-in furniture, and fixtures down to the level of doorknobs.</p>
<p>A notable value is that the architectural system consists only of classic patterns tested in the real world and reviewed by multiple architects for beauty and practicality.</p>
<p>The book includes all needed surveying and structural calculations, and a novel simplified building system that copes with regional shortages of wood and steel, uses easily stored inexpensive materials, and produces long-lasting classic buildings with small amounts of materials, design and labor. It first has users prototype a structure on-site in temporary materials. Once accepted, these are finished by filling them with very-low-density concrete. It uses <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaulted</a> construction to build as high as three stories, permitting very high densities.</p>
<p>This book's method was adopted by the University of Oregon, as described in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oregon_Experiment" title="The Oregon Experiment">The Oregon Experiment</a></i> (1975), and remains the official planning instrument. It has also been adopted in part by some cities as a building code.</p>
<p>The idea of a <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern language</a> appears to apply to any complex engineering task, and has been applied to some of them. It has been especially influential in <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_pattern_(computer_science)" title="Design pattern (computer science)">patterns</a> have been used to document collective knowledge in the field.</p>
<p><i>A New Theory of Urban Design</i> (1987) coincided with a renewal of interest in <a href="/wiki/Urbanism" title="Urbanism">urbanism</a> among architects, but stood apart from most other expressions of this by assuming a distinctly anti-masterplanning stance. An account of a design studio conducted with Berkeley students, it shows how convincing urban networks can be generated by requiring individual actors to respect only <i>local</i> rules, in relation to neighbours. A vastly undervalued part of the Alexander canon, <i>A New Theory</i> is important in understanding the generative processes which give rise to the <a href="/wiki/Shanty_town" title="Shanty town">shanty towns</a> latterly championed by <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Brand" title="Stewart Brand">Stewart Brand</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Neuwirth" title="Robert Neuwirth">Robert Neuwirth</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">the Prince of Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanson-Youn.C3.A9s_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanson-Youn.C3.A9s-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Nature_of_Order" title="The Nature of Order">The Nature of Order: An Essay on the Art of Building and the Nature of the Universe</a></i> (2003–04), which includes The <i>Phenomenon of Life</i>, <i>The Process of Creating Life</i>, <i>A Vision of a Living World</i> and <i>The Luminous Ground</i>, is Alexander's most comprehensive and elaborate work. In it, he puts forth a new theory about the nature of space and describes how this theory influences thinking about architecture, building, planning, and the way in which we view the world in general. The mostly static patterns from <i>A Pattern Language</i> have been amended by more dynamic sequences, which describe how to work towards patterns (which can roughly be seen as the end result of sequences). Sequences, like <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">patterns</a>, promise to be tools of wider scope than building (just as his theory of space goes beyond architecture).</p>
<p>The online publication <a class="external text" href="http://www.katarxis3.com/index.html" rel="nofollow">"Katarxis 3"</a> (September 2004) includes several essays by Christopher Alexander, as well as the legendary <a class="external text" href="http://www.katarxis3.com/Alexander_Eisenman_Debate.htm" rel="nofollow">debate</a> between Alexander and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Eisenman" title="Peter Eisenman">Peter Eisenman</a> from 1982.</p>
<p>Alexander's latest book, <i>The Battle for the Life and Beauty of the Earth: A Struggle Between Two World-Systems</i> (2012), is the story of the largest project he and his colleagues had ever tackled, the construction of a new High School/College campus in Japan. He also uses the project to connect with themes in his four-volume series. He contrasts his approach, (System A) with the construction processes endemic in the US and Japanese economies (System B). As Alexander describes it, System A is focused on enhancing the life/spirit of spaces within given constraints (land, budget, client needs, etc.) (drawings are sketches - decisions on placing buildings, materials used, finish and such are made in the field as construction proceeds, with adjustments as needed to meet overall budget); System B ignores, and tends to diminish or destroy that quality (architect responsible for drawings which the builder uses to build structures at the lowest possible cost). In the last few chapters he describes "centers" as a way of thinking about the connections among spaces, and about what brings more wholeness and life to a space.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_of_architecture">Works of architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Works of architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among Alexander's most notable built works are the Eishin Campus near <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> (the building process of which is outlined in his 2012 book <i>The Battle for the Life and Beauty of the Earth</i>); the West Dean Visitors Centre <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in West Sussex, England; the Julian Street Inn (a homeless shelter) in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> (both described in <i>Nature of Order</i>); the <a href="/wiki/Sala_House" title="Sala House">Sala House</a> and the Martinez House (experimental houses in Albany and Martinez, California made of lightweight concrete); the low-cost housing in <a href="/wiki/Mexicali" title="Mexicali">Mexicali, Mexico</a> (described in <i>The Production of Houses</i>); and several private houses (described and illustrated in <i>The Nature of Order</i>). Alexander's built work is characterized by a special quality (which he used to call "the quality without a name", but named "wholeness" in <i>Nature of Order</i>) that relates to human beings and induces feelings of belonging to the place and structure. This quality is found in the most loved traditional and historic buildings and urban spaces, and is precisely what Alexander has tried to capture with his sophisticated mathematical design theories. Paradoxically, achieving this connective human quality has also moved his buildings away from the abstract imageability valued in contemporary architecture, and this is one reason why his buildings are under-appreciated at present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nikos_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nikos-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>His former student and colleague <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mehaffy" title="Michael Mehaffy">Michael Mehaffy</a> wrote an <a class="external text" href="http://www.katarxis3.com/Gallery.htm" rel="nofollow">introductory essay</a> on Alexander's built work in the online publication <a class="external text" href="http://www.katarxis3.com/index.html" rel="nofollow">"Katarxis 3"</a>, which includes a <a class="external text" href="http://www.katarxis3.com/Gallery/nav.htm" rel="nofollow">gallery</a> of Alexander's major built projects through September 2004.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching">Teaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Teaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to his lengthy teaching career at Berkeley (during which a number of international students began to appreciate and apply his methods), Alexander was a key faculty member at both The Prince of Wales's Summer Schools in Civil Architecture (1990–1994) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Prince%27s_Foundation_for_the_Built_Environment" title="The Prince's Foundation for the Built Environment">The Prince's Foundation for the Built Environment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanson-Youn.C3.A9s_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hanson-Youn.C3.A9s-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexander's work has widely influenced architects; among those who acknowledge his influence are <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Susanka" title="Sarah Susanka">Sarah Susanka</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andres_Duany" title="Andres Duany">Andres Duany</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Witold_Rybczynski" title="Witold Rybczynski">Witold Rybczynski</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbm.org_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbm.org-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Robert_Campbell" title="Robert Campbell">Robert Campbell</a>, the Pulitzer Prize-winning architecture critic for the Boston Globe, stated that Alexander "has had an enormous critical influence on my life and work, and I think that's true of a whole generation of people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbm.org_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbm.org-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Architecture critic <a href="/wiki/Peter_Buchanan_(architect)" title="Peter Buchanan (architect)">Peter Buchanan</a>, in an essay for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Architectural_Review" title="The Architectural Review">The Architectural Review</a>'s 2012 campaign <i>The Big Rethink</i>, argues that Alexander's work as reflected in <i>A Pattern Language</i> is "thoroughly subversive and forward looking rather than regressive, as so many misunderstand it to be." He continues:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Even architects not immune to the charms of the places depicted, are loath to pursue the folksy aesthetic they see as implied and do not want to engage with such primitive construction – although the systemic collapse now unfolding may force that upon them. The daunting challenge for architects then, if such a thing is even possible to realise, would be to recreate in a more contemporary idiom both the richness and quality of experience suggested by the pattern language."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Many urban development projects continue to incorporate Alexander's ideas. For example, in the UK the developers <a href="/wiki/Living_Villages" title="Living Villages">Living Villages</a> have been highly influenced by Alexander's work and used <a href="/wiki/A_Pattern_Language" title="A Pattern Language">A Pattern Language</a> as the basis for the design of The Wintles in Bishops Castle, Shropshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Susanka" title="Sarah Susanka">Sarah Susanka's</a> "Not So Big House" movement adapts and popularizes Alexander's patterns and outlook.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_science">Computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alexander's <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_on_the_Synthesis_of_Form" title="Notes on the Synthesis of Form">Notes on the Synthesis of Form</a></i> was said to be required reading for researchers in computer science throughout the 1960s. It had an influence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naur_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-naur-27">[27]</a></sup> in the 1960s and 1970s on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Programming_language_design" title="Programming language design">programming language design</a>, modular programming, <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> and other design methodologies. Alexander's mathematical concepts and orientation were similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edsger_Dijkstra" title="Edsger Dijkstra">Edsger Dijkstra</a>'s influential <i>A Discipline of Programming</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The greatest influence of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Pattern_Language" title="A Pattern Language">A Pattern Language</a></i> in computer science is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_patterns" title="Design patterns">design patterns</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Alexander's philosophy of incremental, organic, coherent design also influenced the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accu_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-accu-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">Wiki</a> was invented<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C2_Wiki_Front_Page_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-C2_Wiki_Front_Page-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c2.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-c2.com-7">[7]</a></sup> to allow the Hillside Group to work on programming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_patterns" title="Design patterns">design patterns</a>. More recently the "deep geometrical structures" as discussed in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nature_of_Order" title="The Nature of Order">The Nature of Order</a></i> have been cited as having importance for <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented programming</a>, particularly in C++.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Will_Wright_(game_designer)" title="Will Wright (game designer)">Will Wright</a> wrote that Alexander's work was influential in the origin of the <i><a href="/wiki/SimCity" title="SimCity">SimCity</a></i> computer games, and in his later game <i><a href="/wiki/Spore_(2008_video_game)" title="Spore (2008 video game)">Spore</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alexander has often led his own software research, such as the 1996 Gatemaker project with <a href="/wiki/Greg_Bryant_(computer_scientist)" title="Greg Bryant (computer scientist)">Greg Bryant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_Alexander&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fourth volume of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nature_of_Order" title="The Nature of Order">The Nature of Order</a></i> approaches religious questions from a scientific and philosophical rather than mystical direction. In it, Alexander describes deep ties between the nature of matter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_perception" title="Human perception">human perception</a> of the universe, and the geometries people construct in buildings, cities, and artifacts. He suggests a crucial link between traditional practices and <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">beliefs</a>, and recent scientific advances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Despite his leanings toward <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">Deism</a>, and his naturalistic and anthropological approach to religion, Alexander maintains that he is a practicing member of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, believing it to embody a great deal of accumulated human truth within its rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
