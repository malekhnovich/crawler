<p>In <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <b>decision-making</b> is regarded as the <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive process</a> resulting in the selection of a belief or a course of action among several alternative possibilities. Every decision-making process produces a final choice; it may or may not prompt action. Decision-making is the process of identifying and choosing alternatives based on the values and preferences of the decision-maker.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Decision-making can be regarded as a problem-solving activity terminated by a solution deemed to be satisfactory. It is therefore a process which can be more or less <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a> or <a href="/wiki/Irrationality" title="Irrationality">irrational</a> and can be based on <a href="/wiki/Explicit_knowledge" title="Explicit knowledge">explicit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tacit_knowledge" title="Tacit knowledge">tacit knowledge</a>.</p>
<p>Human performance with regard to decisions has been the subject of active research from several perspectives:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">Psychological</a>: examining individual decisions in the context of a set of needs, preferences and values the individual has or seeks.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">Cognitive</a>: the decision-making process regarded as a continuous process integrated in the interaction with the environment.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Normative" title="Normative">Normative</a>: the analysis of individual decisions concerned with the logic of decision-making, or <a href="/wiki/Communicative_rationality" title="Communicative rationality">communicative rationality</a>, and the invariant choice it leads to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahneman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahneman-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A major part of decision-making involves the analysis of a finite set of alternatives described in terms of evaluative criteria. Then the task might be to rank these alternatives in terms of how attractive they are to the decision-maker(s) when all the criteria are considered simultaneously. Another task might be to find the best alternative or to determine the relative total priority of each alternative (for instance, if alternatives represent projects competing for funds) when all the criteria are considered simultaneously. Solving such problems is the focus of <a href="/wiki/Multiple-criteria_decision_analysis" title="Multiple-criteria decision analysis">multiple-criteria decision analysis</a> (MCDA). This area of decision-making, although very old, has attracted the interest of many researchers and practitioners and is still highly debated as there are many MCDA methods which may yield very different results when they are applied on exactly the same data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCDM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCDM-2">[2]</a></sup> This leads to the formulation of a <a href="/wiki/Decision-making_paradox" title="Decision-making paradox">decision-making paradox</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">Logical</a> decision-making is an important part of all science-based professions, where specialists apply their <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> in a given area to make informed decisions. For example, medical decision-making often involves a <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">diagnosis</a> and the selection of appropriate treatment. But <a href="/wiki/Naturalistic_decision-making" title="Naturalistic decision-making">naturalistic decision-making</a> research shows that in situations with higher time pressure, higher stakes, or increased ambiguities, experts may use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intuition_(psychology)" title="Intuition (psychology)">intuitive</a> decision-making rather than structured approaches. They may follow a <a href="/wiki/Recognition_primed_decision" title="Recognition primed decision">recognition primed decision</a> that fits their experience and arrive at a course of action without weighing alternatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The decision-maker's environment can play a part in the decision-making process. For example, environmental complexity is a factor that influences cognitive function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-necsi.edu_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-necsi.edu-3">[3]</a></sup> A complex environment is an environment with a large number of different possible states which come and go over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-its.caltech.edu_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-its.caltech.edu-4">[4]</a></sup> Studies done at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado" title="University of Colorado">University of Colorado</a> have shown that more complex environments correlate with higher cognitive function, which means that a decision can be influenced by the location. One experiment measured complexity in a room by the number of small objects and appliances present; a simple room had less of those things. Cognitive function was greatly affected by the higher measure of environmental complexity making it easier to think about the situation and make a better decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-necsi.edu_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-necsi.edu-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research about decision-making is also published under the label <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a>, in particular in European <a href="/wiki/Psychological_research" title="Psychological research">psychological research</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>It is important to differentiate between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Problem_analysis" title="Problem analysis">problem analysis</a> and decision-making. Traditionally, it is argued that problem analysis must be done first, so that the information gathered in that process may be used towards decision-making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2015)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Characteristics of problem analysis</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Analyze performance, what should the results be against what they actually are</li>
<li>Problems are merely deviations from performance standards</li>
<li>Problems must be precisely identified and described</li>
<li>Problems are caused by a change from a distinctive feature</li>
<li>Something can always be used to distinguish between what has and hasn't been affected by a cause</li>
<li>Causes of problems can be deduced from relevant changes found in analyzing the problem</li>
<li>Most likely cause of a problem is the one that exactly explains all the facts</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Characteristics of decision-making</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Objectives must first be established</li>
<li>Objectives must be classified and placed in order of importance</li>
<li>Alternative actions must be developed</li>
<li>The alternatives must be evaluated against all the objectives</li>
<li>The alternative that is able to achieve all the objectives is the tentative decision</li>
<li>The tentative decision is evaluated for more possible consequences</li>
<li>The decisive actions are taken, and additional actions are taken to prevent any adverse consequences from becoming problems and starting both systems (problem analysis and decision-making) all over again</li>
<li>There are steps that are generally followed that result in a decision model that can be used to determine an optimal production plan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>In a situation featuring conflict, <a href="/wiki/Role-playing" title="Role-playing">role-playing</a> may be helpful for predicting decisions to be made by involved parties<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_paralysis">Analysis paralysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Analysis paralysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Analysis_paralysis" title="Analysis paralysis">Analysis paralysis</a></div>
<p>Analysis paralysis is the state of over-analyzing (or over-thinking) a situation so that a decision or action is never taken, in effect paralyzing the outcome.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_overload">Information overload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Information overload">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Information_overload" title="Information overload">Information overload</a></div>
<p>Information overload is "a gap between the volume of information and the tools we have to assimilate" it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Excessive information affects problem processing and tasking, which affects decision-making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Crystal C. Hall and colleagues described an "illusion of knowledge", which means that as individuals encounter too much knowledge it can interfere with their ability to make rational decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-decision_analysis">Post-decision analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-decision analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evaluation and analysis of past decisions is complementary to decision-making. See also <a href="/wiki/Mental_accounting" title="Mental accounting">Mental accounting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Postmortem_documentation" title="Postmortem documentation">Postmortem documentation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Everyday_techniques">Everyday techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Everyday techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Decision-making techniques can be separated into two broad categories: <a href="/wiki/Group_decision-making" title="Group decision-making">group decision-making</a> techniques and individual decision-making techniques. Individual decision-making techniques can also often be applied by a group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Group">Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Consensus_decision-making" title="Consensus decision-making">Consensus decision-making</a> tries to avoid "winners" and "losers". Consensus requires that a majority approve a given course of action, but that the minority agree to go along with the course of action. In other words, if the minority opposes the course of action, consensus requires that the course of action be modified to remove objectionable features.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">Voting-based methods</a>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Majority" title="Majority">Majority</a> requires support from more than 50% of the members of the group. Thus, the bar for action is lower than with consensus.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" title="Plurality voting system">Plurality</a>, where the largest block in a group decides, even if it falls short of a majority.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Range_voting" title="Range voting">Range voting</a> lets each member score one or more of the available options. The option with the highest average is chosen. This method has experimentally been shown to produce the lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayesian_regret" title="Bayesian regret">Bayesian regret</a> among common voting methods, even when voters are strategic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Delphi_method" title="Delphi method">Delphi method</a> is structured communication technique for groups, originally developed for collaborative forecasting but has also been used for <a href="/wiki/Delphi_method#Use_in_policy-making" title="Delphi method">policy making</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dotmocracy" title="Dotmocracy">Dotmocracy</a> is a facilitation method that relies on the use of special forms called Dotmocracy Sheets to allow large groups to collectively brainstorm and recognize agreement on an unlimited number of ideas they have authored.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Participative_decision-making" title="Participative decision-making">Participative decision-making</a> occurs when an authority opens up the decision-making process to a group of people for a collaborative effort.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Decision_engineering" title="Decision engineering">Decision engineering</a> uses a visual map of the decision-making process based on <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">system dynamics</a> and can be automated through a decision modeling tool, integrating <a href="/wiki/Big_data" title="Big data">big data</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>, and expert knowledge as appropriate.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual">Individual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Individual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Decisional_balance_sheet" title="Decisional balance sheet">Decisional balance sheet</a>: listing the advantages and disadvantages (benefits and costs, pros and cons) of each option, as suggested by <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Protagoras_(dialogue)" title="Protagoras (dialogue)">Protagoras</a></i> and by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Simple prioritization: choosing the alternative with the highest probability-weighted <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a>. This may involve considering the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a> of different alternatives. See also <a href="/wiki/Decision_analysis" title="Decision analysis">Decision analysis</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Satisficing" title="Satisficing">Satisficing</a>: examining alternatives only until the first acceptable one is found. The opposite is <a href="/wiki/Maximization_(psychology)" title="Maximization (psychology)">maximizing</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimizing" title="Optimizing">optimizing</a>, in which many or all alternatives are examined in order to find the best option.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Argument_from_authority" title="Argument from authority">Acquiesce to a person in authority</a> or an "expert"; "<a href="/wiki/Superior_orders" title="Superior orders">just following orders</a>".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anti-authoritarianism" title="Anti-authoritarianism">Anti-authoritarianism</a>: taking the most opposite action compared to the advice of mistrusted authorities.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flipism" title="Flipism">Flipism</a> e.g. flipping a coin, cutting a deck of playing cards, and other random or coincidence methods – or prayer, <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">tarot</a> cards, astrology, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augurs" title="Augurs">augurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revelation</a>, or other forms of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, superstition or <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Automated_decision_support" title="Automated decision support">Automated decision support</a>: setting up criteria for automated decisions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">Decision support systems</a>: using <a href="/wiki/Decision-making_software" title="Decision-making software">decision-making software</a> when faced with highly complex decisions or when considering many stakeholders, categories, or other factors that affect decisions.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps">Steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GOFER">GOFER</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: GOFER">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1980s, psychologist Leon Mann and colleagues developed a decision-making process called GOFER, which they taught to adolescents, as summarized in the book <i>Teaching Decision Making To Adolescents</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The process was based on extensive earlier research conducted with psychologist <a href="/wiki/Irving_Janis" title="Irving Janis">Irving Janis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> GOFER is an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for five decision-making steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>Goals: Survey values and objectives.</li>
<li>Options: Consider a wide range of alternative actions.</li>
<li>Facts: Search for information.</li>
<li>Effects: Weigh the positive and negative consequences of the options.</li>
<li>Review: Plan how to implement the options.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DECIDE">DECIDE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: DECIDE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DECIDE" title="DECIDE">DECIDE</a></div>
<p>In 2008, Kristina Guo published the DECIDE model of decision-making, which has six parts:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><b>D</b>efine the problem</li>
<li><b>E</b>stablish or <b>E</b>numerate all the criteria (constraints)</li>
<li><b>C</b>onsider or <b>C</b>ollect all the alternatives</li>
<li><b>I</b>dentify the best alternative</li>
<li><b>D</b>evelop and implement a <a href="/wiki/Action_plan" title="Action plan">plan of action</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation"><b>E</b>valuate</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/monitoring" title="wikt:monitoring">monitor</a> the solution and examine <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> when necessary</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, Pam Brown of <a href="/wiki/Singleton_Hospital" title="Singleton Hospital">Singleton Hospital</a> in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, divided the decision-making process into seven steps:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Outline your goal and outcome.</li>
<li>Gather data.</li>
<li>Develop alternatives (i.e., brainstorming).</li>
<li>List pros and cons of each alternative.</li>
<li>Make the decision.</li>
<li>Immediately take action to implement it.</li>
<li>Learn from and reflect on the decision.</li>
</ol>
<p>In 2009, professor John Pijanowski described how the Arkansas Program, an ethics curriculum at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a>, used eight stages of <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">moral</a> decision-making based on the work of <a href="/wiki/James_Rest" title="James Rest">James Rest</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Establishing community: Create and nurture the relationships, norms, and procedures that will influence how problems are understood and communicated. This stage takes place prior to and during a moral dilemma.</li>
<li>Perception: Recognize that a problem exists.</li>
<li>Interpretation: Identify competing explanations for the problem, and evaluate the drivers behind those interpretations.</li>
<li>Judgment: Sift through various possible actions or responses and determine which is more justifiable.</li>
<li>Motivation: Examine the competing commitments which may distract from a more moral course of action and then prioritize and commit to moral values over other personal, institutional or social values.</li>
<li>Action: Follow through with action that supports the more justified decision.</li>
<li>Reflection in action.</li>
<li>Reflection on action.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Group_stages">Group stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Group stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to B. Aubrey Fisher, there are four stages or phases that should be involved in all group decision-making:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Orientation. Members meet for the first time and start to get to know each other.</li>
<li>Conflict. Once group members become familiar with each other, disputes, little fights and arguments occur. Group members eventually work it out.</li>
<li>Emergence. The group begins to clear up vague opinions by talking about them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reinforcement" title="Reinforcement">Reinforcement</a>. Members finally make a decision and provide justification for it.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is said that establishing critical norms in a group improves the quality of decisions, while the majority of opinions (called consensus norms) do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rational_and_irrational">Rational and irrational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Rational and irrational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, it is thought that if humans are rational and free to make their own decisions, then they would behave according to <a href="/wiki/Rational_choice_theory" title="Rational choice theory">rational choice theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schacter2011_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schacter2011-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:368–370</sup> Rational choice theory says that a person consistently makes choices that lead to the best situation for himself or herself, taking into account all available considerations including costs and benefits; the rationality of these considerations is from the point of view of the person himself, so a decision is not irrational just because someone else finds it questionable.</p>
<p>In reality, however, there are some factors that affect decision-making abilities and cause people to make irrational decisions – for example, to make contradictory choices when faced with the same problem <a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">framed</a> in two different ways (see also <a href="/wiki/Allais_paradox" title="Allais paradox">Allais paradox</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_and_personal_biases">Cognitive and personal biases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cognitive and personal biases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bias" title="Bias">Biases</a> usually creep into decision-making processes. Here is a list of commonly debated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_biases_in_judgment_and_decision-making" title="List of biases in judgment and decision-making">biases in judgment and decision-making</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Selective search for <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a>): People tend to be willing to gather facts that support certain conclusions but disregard other facts that support different conclusions. Individuals who are highly defensive in this manner show significantly greater left prefrontal cortex activity as measured by <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a> than do less defensive individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Premature termination of search for evidence: People tend to accept the first alternative that looks like it might work.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cognitive_inertia" title="Cognitive inertia">Cognitive inertia</a> is unwillingness to change existing thought patterns in the face of new circumstances.</li>
<li>Selective perception: People actively screen out information that they do not think is important (see also <a href="/wiki/Prejudice" title="Prejudice">Prejudice</a>). In one demonstration of this effect, discounting of arguments with which one disagrees (by judging them as untrue or irrelevant) was decreased by selective activation of right prefrontal cortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wishful_thinking" title="Wishful thinking">Wishful thinking</a> is a tendency to want to see things in a certain – usually positive – light, which can distort perception and thinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Choice-supportive_bias" title="Choice-supportive bias">Choice-supportive bias</a> occurs when people distort their memories of chosen and rejected options to make the chosen options seem more attractive.</li>
<li>Recency: People tend to place more attention on more recent information and either ignore or forget more distant information (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic_priming" title="Semantic priming">Semantic priming</a>). The opposite effect in the first set of data or other information is termed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primacy_effect" title="Primacy effect">primacy effect</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2015)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Repetition bias is a willingness to believe what one has been told most often and by the greatest number of different sources.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchoring_and_adjustment" title="Anchoring and adjustment">Anchoring and adjustment</a>: Decisions are unduly influenced by initial information that shapes our view of subsequent information.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Groupthink" title="Groupthink">Groupthink</a> is <a href="/wiki/Peer_pressure" title="Peer pressure">peer pressure</a> to conform to the opinions held by the group.</li>
<li>Source credibility bias is a tendency to reject a person's statement on the basis of a bias against the person, organization, or group to which the person belongs. People preferentially accept statement by others that they like (see also <a href="/wiki/Prejudice" title="Prejudice">Prejudice</a>).</li>
<li>Incremental decision-making and escalating commitment: People look at a decision as a small step in a process, and this tends to perpetuate a series of similar decisions. This can be contrasted with <i>zero-based decision-making</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Slippery_slope" title="Slippery slope">Slippery slope</a>).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attribution_theory" title="Attribution theory">Attribution asymmetry</a>: People tend to attribute their own success to internal factors, including abilities and talents, but explain their failures in terms of external factors such as bad luck. The reverse bias is shown when people explain others' success or failure.</li>
<li>Role fulfillment is a tendency to conform to others' decision-making expectations.</li>
<li>Underestimating <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Illusion_of_control" title="Illusion of control">illusion of control</a>: People tend to underestimate future uncertainty because of a tendency to believe they have more control over events than they really do.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)#Absolute_and_relative_influences" title="Framing (social sciences)">Framing bias</a>: This is best avoided by increasing <a href="/wiki/Numeracy" title="Numeracy">numeracy</a> and presenting data in several formats (for example, using both absolute and relative scales).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21792695_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21792695-25">[25]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunk-cost_fallacy" title="Sunk-cost fallacy">Sunk-cost fallacy</a> is a specific type of framing effect that affects decision-making. It involves an individual making a decision about a current situation based on what they have previously invested in the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schacter2011_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schacter2011-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:372</sup> An example of this would be an individual that is refraining from dropping a class that they are most likely to fail, due to the fact that they feel as though they have done so much work in the course thus far.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prospect_theory" title="Prospect theory">Prospect theory</a> involves the idea that when faced with a decision-making event, an individual is more likely to take on a risk when evaluating potential losses, and are more likely to avoid risks when evaluating potential gains. This can influence one's decision-making depending if the situation entails a threat, or opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schacter2011_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schacter2011-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:373</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Optimism_bias" title="Optimism bias">Optimism bias</a> is a tendency to overestimate the likelihood of positive events occurring in the future and underestimate the likelihood of negative life events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Such biased expectations are generated and maintained in the face of counter evidence through a tendency to discount undesirable information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> An optimism bias can alter risk perception and decision-making in many domains, ranging from finance to health.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reference_class_forecasting" title="Reference class forecasting">Reference class forecasting</a> was developed to eliminate or reduce cognitive biases in decision-making.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_styles">Cognitive styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cognitive styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optimizing_vs._satisficing">Optimizing vs. satisficing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Optimizing vs. satisficing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maximization_(psychology)" title="Maximization (psychology)">Maximization (psychology)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herbert_A._Simon" title="Herbert A. Simon">Herbert A. Simon</a> coined the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Bounded_rationality" title="Bounded rationality">bounded rationality</a>" to express the idea that human decision-making is limited by available information, available time and the mind's information-processing ability. Further psychological research has identified individual differences between two cognitive styles: <i><a href="/wiki/Maximization_(psychology)" title="Maximization (psychology)">maximizers</a></i> try to make an <a href="/wiki/Optimal_decision" title="Optimal decision">optimal decision</a>, whereas <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satisficer" title="Satisficer">satisficers</a></i> simply try to find a solution that is "good enough". Maximizers tend to take longer making decisions due to the need to maximize performance across all variables and make tradeoffs carefully; they also tend to more often regret their decisions (perhaps because they are more able than satisficers to recognise that a decision turned out to be sub-optimal).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intuitive_vs._rational">Intuitive vs. rational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Intuitive vs. rational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dual_process_theory" title="Dual process theory">Dual process theory</a></div>
<p>The psychologist <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Kahneman" title="Daniel Kahneman">Daniel Kahneman</a>, adopting terms originally proposed by the psychologists <a href="/wiki/Keith_Stanovich" title="Keith Stanovich">Keith Stanovich</a> and Richard West, has theorized that a person's decision-making is the result of an interplay between two kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_process" title="Cognitive process">cognitive processes</a>: an automatic intuitive system (called "System 1") and an effortful rational system (called "System 2"). System 1 is a bottom-up, fast, and implicit system of decision-making, while system 2 is a top-down, slow, and explicit system of decision-making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> System 1 includes simple <a href="/wiki/Heuristics_in_judgment_and_decision-making" title="Heuristics in judgment and decision-making">heuristics in judgment and decision-making</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Affect_heuristic" title="Affect heuristic">affect heuristic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Availability_heuristic" title="Availability heuristic">availability heuristic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Familiarity_heuristic" title="Familiarity heuristic">familiarity heuristic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Representativeness_heuristic" title="Representativeness heuristic">representativeness heuristic</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combinatorial_vs._positional">Combinatorial vs. positional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Combinatorial vs. positional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Styles and methods of decision-making were elaborated by <a href="/wiki/Aron_Katsenelinboigen" title="Aron Katsenelinboigen">Aron Katsenelinboigen</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Predispositioning_theory" title="Predispositioning theory">predispositioning theory</a>. In his analysis on styles and methods, Katsenelinboigen referred to the game of chess, saying that "chess does disclose various methods of operation, notably the creation of predisposition-methods which may be applicable to other, more complex systems."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Concept_of_Indeterminism_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Concept_of_Indeterminism-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5</sup></p>
<p>Katsenelinboigen states that apart from the methods (reactive and selective) and sub-methods (randomization, predispositioning, programming), there are two major styles: positional and combinational. Both styles are utilized in the game of chess. According to Katsenelinboigen, the two styles reflect two basic approaches to <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a>: deterministic (combinational style) and indeterministic (positional style). Katsenelinboigen's definition of the two styles are the following.</p>
<p>The combinational style is characterized by:</p>
<ul>
<li>a very narrow, clearly defined, primarily material goal; and</li>
<li>a program that links the initial position with the final outcome.</li>
</ul>
<p>In defining the combinational style in chess, Katsenelinboigen wrote: "The combinational style features a clearly formulated limited objective, namely the capture of material (the main constituent element of a chess position). The objective is implemented via a well-defined, and in some cases, unique sequence of moves aimed at reaching the set goal. As a rule, this sequence leaves no options for the opponent. Finding a combinational objective allows the player to focus all his energies on efficient execution, that is, the player's analysis may be limited to the pieces directly partaking in the combination. This approach is the crux of the combination and the combinational style of play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Concept_of_Indeterminism_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Concept_of_Indeterminism-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:57</sup></p>
<p>The positional style is distinguished by:</p>
<ul>
<li>a positional goal; and</li>
<li>a formation of semi-complete linkages between the initial step and final outcome.</li>
</ul>
<p>"Unlike the combinational player, the positional player is occupied, first and foremost, with the elaboration of the position that will allow him to develop in the unknown future. In playing the positional style, the player must evaluate relational and material parameters as independent variables. ... The positional style gives the player the opportunity to develop a position until it becomes pregnant with a combination. However, the combination is not the final goal of the positional player – it helps him to achieve the desirable, keeping in mind a predisposition for the future development. The <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhic_victory" title="Pyrrhic victory">pyrrhic victory</a> is the best example of one's inability to think positionally."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The positional style serves to:</p>
<ul>
<li>create a predisposition to the future development of the position;</li>
<li>induce the environment in a certain way;</li>
<li>absorb an unexpected outcome in one's favor; and</li>
<li>avoid the negative aspects of unexpected outcomes.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_Myers-Briggs_type">Influence of Myers-Briggs type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Influence of Myers-Briggs type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Briggs_Myers" title="Isabel Briggs Myers">Isabel Briggs Myers</a>, a person's decision-making process depends to a significant degree on their cognitive style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2015)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Myers developed a set of four bi-polar dimensions, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myers-Briggs_Type_Indicator" title="Myers-Briggs Type Indicator">Myers-Briggs Type Indicator</a> (MBTI). The terminal points on these dimensions are: <i>thinking</i> and <i>feeling</i>; <i>extroversion</i> and <i>introversion</i>; <i>judgment</i> and <i>perception</i>; and <i>sensing</i> and <i>intuition</i>. She claimed that a person's decision-making style correlates well with how they score on these four dimensions. For example, someone who scored near the thinking, extroversion, sensing, and judgment ends of the dimensions would tend to have a logical, analytical, objective, critical, and empirical decision-making style. However, some psychologists say that the MBTI lacks reliability and validity and is poorly constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other studies suggest that these national or <a href="/wiki/Cross-cultural_differences_in_decision-making" title="Cross-cultural differences in decision-making">cross-cultural differences in decision-making</a> exist across entire societies. For example, <a href="/wiki/Maris_Martinsons" title="Maris Martinsons">Maris Martinsons</a> has found that American, Japanese and Chinese business leaders each exhibit a distinctive national style of decision-making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neuroscience">Neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Decision-making is a region of intense study in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Systems_neuroscience" title="Systems neuroscience">systems neuroscience</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience" title="Cognitive neuroscience">cognitive neuroscience</a>. Several brain structures, including the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_cortex" title="Anterior cingulate cortex">anterior cingulate cortex</a> (ACC), <a href="/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex" title="Orbitofrontal cortex">orbitofrontal cortex</a> and the overlapping <a href="/wiki/Ventromedial_prefrontal_cortex" title="Ventromedial prefrontal cortex">ventromedial prefrontal cortex</a> are believed to be involved in decision-making processes. A <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a> study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> found distinctive patterns of neural activation in these regions depending on whether decisions were made on the basis of perceived personal <a href="/wiki/Volition_(psychology)" title="Volition (psychology)">volition</a> or following directions from someone else. Patients with damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ventromedial_prefrontal_cortex" title="Ventromedial prefrontal cortex">ventromedial prefrontal cortex</a> have difficulty making advantageous decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2015)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A common laboratory paradigm for studying neural decision-making is the <a href="/wiki/Two-alternative_forced_choice" title="Two-alternative forced choice">two-alternative forced choice</a> task (2AFC), in which a subject has to choose between two alternatives within a certain time. A study of a <a href="/wiki/Two-alternative_forced_choice" title="Two-alternative forced choice">two-alternative forced choice</a> task involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhesus_monkey" title="Rhesus monkey">rhesus monkeys</a> found that neurons in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parietal_cortex" title="Parietal cortex">parietal cortex</a> not only represent the formation of a decision<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> but also signal the degree of certainty (or "confidence") associated with the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Another recent study found that lesions to the ACC in the <a href="/wiki/Macaque" title="Macaque">macaque</a> resulted in impaired decision-making in the long run of reinforcement guided tasks suggesting that the ACC may be involved in evaluating past reinforcement information and guiding future action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> A 2012 study found that rats and humans can optimally accumulate incoming sensory evidence, to make statistically optimal decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">Emotion</a> appears able to aid the decision-making process. Decision-making often occurs in the face of <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> about whether one's choices will lead to benefit or harm (see also <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">Risk</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatic-marker_hypothesis" title="Somatic-marker hypothesis">somatic-marker hypothesis</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurobiology" title="Neurobiology">neurobiological</a> theory of how decisions are made in the face of uncertain outcome. This theory holds that such decisions are aided by emotions, in the form of bodily states, that are elicited during the deliberation of future consequences and that mark different options for behavior as being advantageous or disadvantageous. This process involves an interplay between neural systems that elicit emotional/bodily states and neural systems that map these emotional/bodily states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A recent lesion mapping study of 152 patients with focal brain lesions conducted by <a href="/wiki/Aron_K._Barbey" title="Aron K. Barbey">Aron K. Barbey</a> and colleagues provided evidence to help discover the neural mechanisms of <a href="/wiki/Emotional_intelligence" title="Emotional intelligence">emotional intelligence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although it is unclear whether the studies generalize to all processing, subconscious processes have been implicated in the initiation of conscious volitional movements. See the <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience_of_free_will" title="Neuroscience of free will">Neuroscience of free will</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_adolescents_vs._adults">In adolescents vs. adults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In adolescents vs. adults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">

<p>During their adolescent years, teens are known for their high-risk behaviors and rash decisions. Recent research<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has shown that there are differences in cognitive processes between adolescents and adults during decision-making. Researchers have concluded that differences in decision-making are not due to a lack of logic or reasoning, but more due to the immaturity of <a href="/wiki/Psychosocial" title="Psychosocial">psychosocial</a> capacities that influence decision-making. Examples of their undeveloped capacities which influence decision-making would be impulse control, emotion regulation, <a href="/wiki/Delayed_gratification" title="Delayed gratification">delayed gratification</a> and resistance to <a href="/wiki/Peer_pressure" title="Peer pressure">peer pressure</a>. In the past, researchers have thought that adolescent behavior was simply due to incompetency regarding decision-making. Currently, researchers have concluded that adults and adolescents are both competent decision-makers, not just adults. However, adolescents' competent decision-making skills decrease when psychosocial capacities become present.</p>
<p>Recent research<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has shown that risk-taking behaviors in adolescents may be the product of interactions between the socioemotional brain network and its <a href="/wiki/Executive_functions" title="Executive functions">cognitive-control network</a>. The socioemotional part of the brain processes social and emotional stimuli and has been shown to be important in <a href="/wiki/Reward_system" title="Reward system">reward processing</a>. The cognitive-control network assists in planning and self-regulation. Both of these sections of the brain change over the course of <a href="/wiki/Puberty" title="Puberty">puberty</a>. However, the socioemotional network changes quickly and abruptly, while the cognitive-control network changes more gradually. Because of this difference in change, the cognitive-control network, which usually regulates the socioemotional network, struggles to control the socioemotional network when psychosocial capacities are present.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When adolescents are exposed to social and emotional stimuli, their socioemotional network is activated as well as areas of the brain involved in reward processing. Because teens often gain a sense of reward from risk-taking behaviors, their repetition becomes ever more probable due to the reward experienced. In this, the process mirrors <a href="/wiki/Addiction" title="Addiction">addiction</a>. Teens can become addicted to risky behavior because they are in a high state of arousal and are rewarded for it not only by their own internal functions but also by their peers around them.</p>
<p>Adults are generally better able to control their risk-taking because their cognitive-control system has matured enough to the point where it can control the socioemotional network, even in the context of high arousal or when psychosocial capacities are present. Also, adults are less likely to find themselves in situations that push them to do risky things. For example, teens are more likely to be around peers who peer pressure them into doing things, while adults are not as exposed to this sort of social setting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>A recent study suggests that adolescents have difficulties adequately adjusting beliefs in response to bad news (such as reading that smoking poses a greater risk to health than they thought), but do not differ from adults in their ability to alter beliefs in response to good news.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> This creates biased beliefs, which may lead to greater risk taking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
