<p><b>Automotive design</b> is the profession involved in the development of the appearance, and to some extent the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>, of motor vehicles or more specifically road vehicles.</p>
<p>This most commonly refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a> but also refers to <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coach_(vehicle)" title="Coach (vehicle)">coaches</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">vans</a>. The functional design and development of a modern motor vehicle is typically done by a large team from many different disciplines included within <a href="/wiki/Automotive_engineering" title="Automotive engineering">automotive engineering</a>. Automotive design in this context is primarily concerned with developing the visual appearance or aesthetics of the vehicle, though it is also involved in the creation of the product concept. Automotive design is practiced by designers who usually have an art background and a degree in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a> or transportation design.</p>

<p>The task of the design team is usually split into three main aspects: exterior design, interior design, and color and <a href="/wiki/Trim_package" title="Trim package">trim</a> design. <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">Graphic design</a> is also an aspect of automotive design; this is generally shared amongst the design team as the lead designer sees fit. Design focuses not only on the isolated outer shape of automobile parts, but concentrates on the combination of form and function, starting from the vehicle package.</p>
<p>The aesthetic value will need to correspond to ergonomic functionality and utility features as well. In particular, vehicular electronic components and parts will give more challenges to automotive designers who are required to update on the latest information and knowledge associated with emerging vehicular gadgetry, particularly <a href="/wiki/Dashtop_mobile" title="Dashtop mobile">dashtop mobile</a> devices, like GPS navigation, <a href="/wiki/Satellite_radio" title="Satellite radio">satellite radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/HD_Radio" title="HD Radio">HD radio</a>, mobile TV, MP3 players, video playback, and smartphone interfaces. Though not all the new vehicular gadgets are to be designated as factory standard items, some of them may be integral to determining the future course of any specific vehicular models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exterior_design">Exterior design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exterior design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stylist responsible for the design of the exterior of the vehicle develops the proportions, shape, and surfaces of the vehicle. Exterior design is first done by a series of digital or manual drawings. Progressively, drawings that are more detailed are executed and approved by appropriate layers of management. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clay_(industrial_plasticine)" title="Clay (industrial plasticine)">Clay (industrial plasticine)</a> and or digital models are developed from, and along with the drawings. The data from these models are then used to create a full sized mock-up of the final design (<a href="/wiki/Body_in_white" title="Body in white">body in white</a>). With three- and five-axis CNC milling machines, the <a href="/wiki/Clay_modeling" title="Clay modeling">clay model</a> is first designed in a computer program and then "carved" using the machine and large amounts of clay. Even in times of high-class 3d software and virtual models on power walls, the clay model is still the most important tool to evaluate the design of a car and therefore used throughout the industry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior_design">Interior design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interior design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stylist responsible for the design of the vehicle interior develops the proportions, shape, placement, and surfaces for the instrument panel, seats, door trim panels, headliner, pillar trims, etc. Here the emphasis is on ergonomics and the comfort of the passengers. The procedure here is the same as with exterior design (sketch, digital model and clay model).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color_and_trim_design">Color and trim design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Color and trim design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The color and <a href="/wiki/Trim_package" title="Trim package">trim</a> (or color and materials) designer is responsible for the research, design, and development of all interior and exterior colors and materials used on a vehicle. These include <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">fabric</a> designs, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, grains, <a href="/wiki/Carpet" title="Carpet">carpet</a>, headliner, wood trim, and so on. <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">Color</a>, contrast, <a href="/wiki/Texture_(visual_arts)" title="Texture (visual arts)">texture</a>, and pattern must be carefully combined to give the vehicle a unique interior environment experience. Designers work closely with the exterior and interior designers.</p>
<p>Designers draw inspiration from other design disciplines such as: industrial design, <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a>, home furnishing, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Product_design" title="Product design">product design</a>. Specific research is done into global trends to design for projects two to three <a href="/wiki/Model_year" title="Model year">model years</a> in the future. Trend boards are created from this research in order to keep track of design influences as they relate to the automotive industry. The designer then uses this information to develop themes and concepts that are then further refined and tested on the vehicle models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphic_design">Graphic design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Graphic design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The design team also develops graphics for items such as: badges, decals, dials, switches, kick or tread strips, liveries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer-aided_styling_and_Class-A_development">Computer-aided styling and Class-A development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Computer-aided styling and Class-A development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sketches and rendering are transformed into 3D Digital surface modelling and rendering for real-time evaluation with Math data in initial stages. During the development process succeeding phases will require the 3D model fully developed to meet the aesthetic requirements of a designer and well as all engineering and manufacturing requirements. The fully developed CAS digital model will be re-developed for manufacturing meeting the Class-A surface standards that involves both technical as well as aesthetics. This data will be further developed by Product Engineering team. These modelers usually have a background in Industrial design or sometimes tooling engineering in case of some Class-A modelers. Autodesk Alias and ICEM Surf are the two most widely used software tools for Class-A development.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Styling_development_cycle">Styling development cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Styling development cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several manufacturers have slightly varied development cycles for designing an Automobile, but in practice these are the following.</p>
<ul>
<li>Design and User Research</li>
<li>Concept Development <a href="/wiki/Sketch_(drawing)" title="Sketch (drawing)">sketching</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CAS_(Computer_Aided_Styling)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CAS (Computer Aided Styling) (page does not exist)">Class A surfaces</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clay_modeling" title="Clay modeling">Clay modeling</a></li>
<li>Interior Buck Model</li>
<li>Vehicle ergonomics</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Class-A_Surface_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Class-A Surface Development (page does not exist)">Class A surfaces</a></li>
<li>Colour and Trim</li>
<li>Vehicle Graphics</li>
</ul>
<p>The design process occurs concurrently with other product Engineers who will be engineering the styling data for meeting performance, manufacturing and safety regulations. From mid-phase, back and forth interactions between the designers and product engineers culminates into a finished product be manufacturing ready.</p>
<p>Apart from this the Engineering team parallelly works in the following areas. Product Engineering (Body In White Sheetmetal Design and Plastic engineering), NVH Development team, <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">Prototype</a> development, <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">Powertrain</a> engineering, Physical Vehicle validation, Tool and Die development and <a href="/wiki/Production_line" title="Production line">Manufacturing process</a> design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_team">Development team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Development team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The styling team for a specific model consists of a chief designer and an Exterior as well as interior designer. In some cases all three roles are done by one designer. Several junior designers are involved in the development process as well who make specific contributions all overseen by the chief designer. Apart from this the Color and trim designer works closely with other designers. The Clay model team and Digital model team works closely with the styling team all located within the studio. Apart from this there would be studio head, studio managers and prototype engineers who would work across all teams in the studio. The total team size for developing a full sedan usually ranges from 25 to 40 members and the development time lasts for more than 24 months till signed-off for tooling and production. Thereafter a smaller team would be working until vehicle launch.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Integration of an automobile involves fitting together separate parts to form a <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> body or units and mounting these onto a frame, the <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">chassis</a>.</p>
<p>An automobile chassis basically comprises the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The body shell, which forms the skeleton of the vehicle.</li>
<li>The engine, is the power unit of the vehicle; which in the past has been in large part, the internal combustion engine.</li>
<li>Transmission system, which aids in transferring the drive from the engine to the wheels. Its main components are the clutch, gearbox, final drive, and differential.</li>
<li>Suspension system, which is used to connect the wheels to the body or chassis frame.</li>
<li>Steering</li>
<li>Brakes</li>
<li>Electrical equipment</li>
</ol>
<p>The chassis is complete in itself as a road vehicle. It can drive and control itself just as in case of a complete car and therefore, in many motor works, the chassis is usually tested on the road before the complete body of the vehicle is attached as the chassis alone can behave as the propulsion means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the United States, automotive design reached a turning point in 1942 when the American national automobile market began reaching saturation. To maintain unit sales, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> head <a href="/wiki/Alfred_P._Sloan" title="Alfred P. Sloan">Alfred P. Sloan Jr.</a> suggested annual model-year design changes to convince car owners that they needed to buy a new replacement each year, an idea borrowed from the bicycle industry (though Sloan usually gets the credit, or blame).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Critics called his strategy <a href="/wiki/Planned_obsolescence" title="Planned obsolescence">planned obsolescence</a>. Sloan preferred the term "dynamic obsolescence". This strategy had far-reaching effects on the auto business, the field of product design, and eventually the American economy.</p>
<p>The smaller automakers could not maintain the pace and expense of yearly re-styling. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> did not like the model-year change because he clung to an engineer's notions of simplicity, economics of scale, and design integrity. GM surpassed Ford's sales in 1931, and became the dominant company in the industry thereafter. The frequent design changes also made it necessary to use a <a href="/wiki/Body-on-frame" title="Body-on-frame">body-on-frame</a> rather than the lighter but less adaptable monocoque design used by most European automakers.</p>
<p>Some U.S. designers include <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Buehrig" title="Gordon Buehrig">Gordon Buehrig</a>, who was responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Auburn_Automobile" title="Auburn Automobile">Auburn</a> 851, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cord_Automobile" title="Cord Automobile">Cord</a> 810 and 812 (hence also the <a href="/wiki/Hupmobile" title="Hupmobile">Hupmobile</a> Skylark and the <a href="/wiki/Graham-Paige" title="Graham-Paige">Graham</a> Hollywood).</p>
<p>In the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a>'s innovations with aerodynamics helped launch the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Airflow" title="Chrysler Airflow">Chrysler Airflow</a> in 1934, which was revolutionary and radical compared to the contemporary vehicles. However, inadequate consumer acceptance of the advanced appearance of the cars forced a re-design of succeeding models of the Airflow. This marketing experience made the entire industry take note of the high risks involved in incorporating major design advancements into their production cars.</p>
<p>A major influence on American auto styling and marketing was <a href="/wiki/Harley_Earl" title="Harley Earl">Harley Earl</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who brought the <a href="/wiki/Car_tailfin" title="Car tailfin">tailfin</a> and other aeronautical design references to auto design starting with the rear fenders of the 1948 Cadillac. Another notable designer was <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> group's designer <a href="/wiki/Virgil_Exner" title="Virgil Exner">Virgil Exner</a>, who developed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_look" title="Forward look">Forward look</a> design in the mid-1950s. Exner is also credited with using wind tunnel testing to justify incorporating tailfins, thus moving the company away from boxy-looking cars into more aerodynamic and futuristic designs. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Loewy" title="Raymond Loewy">Raymond Loewy</a> was responsible for a number of <a href="/wiki/Studebaker" title="Studebaker">Studebaker</a> vehicles, including the <a href="/wiki/Studebaker_Starlight" title="Studebaker Starlight">Starlight</a> (including the bullet-nose).</p>
<p>Starting in the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Teague" title="Richard A. Teague">Richard A. Teague</a>, who spent most of his career with <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors Corporation</a> (AMC), originated the concept of using interchangeable body panels so as to create a wide array of different vehicles using the same stampings, starting with the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Cavalier" title="AMC Cavalier">AMC Cavalier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Teague was responsible for unique automotive designs such as the two-seat <a href="/wiki/AMC_AMX" title="AMC AMX">AMC AMX</a> muscle car, the subcompact <a href="/wiki/AMC_Gremlin" title="AMC Gremlin">Gremlin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Pacer" title="AMC Pacer">Pacer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/AMC_Matador" title="AMC Matador">Matador coupe</a>, as well as the original and market segment-creating, <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Cherokee_(XJ)" title="Jeep Cherokee (XJ)">Jeep Cherokee</a> (introduced in 1983, and built to 2001 in the U.S. and through 2005 in China).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally during the 1960s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford">Ford</a>'s first generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mustang" title="Ford Mustang">Ford Mustang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Thunderbird" title="Ford Thunderbird">Thunderbird</a> marked another era leading into new <a href="/wiki/Market_segmentation" title="Market segmentation">market segments</a> from <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>. The Ford Mustang achieved record sales in its first year of production and established the <a href="/wiki/Pony_car" title="Pony car">pony car</a> segment.</p>
<p>Personal injury litigation has had an effect on the design and appearance of the car in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, most of the automakers were concerned with mechanical reliability rather than its external appearance. Later, luxury and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> became a demand and also an effective marketing tool. Designs from each nation with its own strong cultural identity, reflected in their exterior and interior designs. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> slowed the progress, but after the early-1950s, Italian designers set the trend and remained the driving force until the early part of the 1980s.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> notable designs came from <a href="/wiki/Bugatti" title="Bugatti">Bugatti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avions_Voisin" title="Avions Voisin">Avions Voisin</a>. Of the mass selling cars <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a>, launched their vehicles with innovative designs and engineering and mostly aided by the styling of <a href="/wiki/Flaminio_Bertoni" title="Flaminio Bertoni">Flaminio Bertoni</a> as evident from <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_DS" title="Citroën DS">Citroën DS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After World War II, with the disappearance of the coach building industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> French automakers (except Citroën) followed British and other popular trends until they gained financial stability. During the 1980s, manufactures like <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> cultivated their own strong design identities with designers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_Le_Quement" title="Patrick Le Quement">Patrick Le Quement</a>. <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a>, which was dependent on <a href="/wiki/Pininfarina" title="Pininfarina">Pininfarina</a> since early post-war period, later established its own brand identity from the 1980s onwards. Its other company, Citroën, still retains its distinctive French innovations for its designs.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Great Britain was Europe's leading manufacturer of automobiles until the late-1960s. During that era there were more British-based automakers than in the rest of Europe combined. The British automobile industry catered to all segments ranging from compact, budget, sports, utility, and luxury-type cars. Car design in Britain was markedly different from other European designs largely because British designers were not influenced by other European art or design movements, as well as the British clay modelers used a different sweep set.</p>
<p>British cars until World War II were sold in most of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonies</a>. Innovations in vehicle packaging and chassis engineering combined with global familiarity with British designs meant vehicles were acceptable to public tastes at that time. British skilled resources like panel beaters, die machinists, and clay modelers were also available also partly due their involvement with motorsport industry.</p>
<p>Still during the 1960s, British manufacturers sought professional help from the Italians, such as <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Michelotti" title="Giovanni Michelotti">Giovanni Michelotti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Spada" title="Ercole Spada">Ercole Spada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pininfarina" title="Pininfarina">Pininfarina</a>. Notable British contributions to automobile designs were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morris_Mini" title="Morris Mini">Morris Mini</a> by <a href="/wiki/Alec_Issigonis" title="Alec Issigonis">Alec Issigonis</a>, several <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Cars" title="Jaguar Cars">Jaguar Cars</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Lyons" title="Sir William Lyons">Sir William Lyons</a> and Malcolm Sayer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> DB Series, and several cars from <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Motor_Company" title="Triumph Motor Company">Triumph</a> and <a href="/wiki/MG_Cars" title="MG Cars">MG</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford">Ford</a> Europe based in Great Britain, is notable for the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Sierra" title="Ford Sierra">Ford Sierra</a> line, a creation of <a href="/wiki/Uwe_Bahnsen" title="Uwe Bahnsen">Uwe Bahnsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lutz_(businessman)" title="Bob Lutz (businessman)">Robert Lutz</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_le_Qu%C3%A9ment" title="Patrick le Quément">Patrick le Quément</a>. Other British designers were <a href="/wiki/William_Towns" title="William Towns">William Towns</a> for <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> cars and <a href="/wiki/David_Bache" title="David Bache">David Bache</a> for <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Range_Rover" title="Range Rover">Range Rover</a> vehicles.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1608" data-file-width="2512" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg/220px-1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg/330px-1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg/440px-1972_BMW_2002_NY.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>Germany is often considered the birthplace of industrial design with <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> School of Design. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> regime closed down the design school. <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Porsche" title="Ferdinand Porsche">Ferdinand Porsche</a> and his family played a significant role in German design. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercedes_Benz" title="Mercedes Benz">Mercedes Benz</a> passenger cars were also in luxury segment and played more importance to aesthetics. After the 1980s, German design evolved into a distinctive Teutonic style often to complement their high engineered cars suited to Autobahns. The early German design clues of present-day owes some part to Italian designers like <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Michelotti" title="Giovanni Michelotti">Giovanni Michelotti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Spada" title="Ercole Spada">Ercole Spada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Sacco" title="Bruno Sacco">Bruno Sacco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Giorgetto_Giugiaro" title="Giorgetto Giugiaro">Giorgetto Giugiaro</a>. During the mid- and late-20th century one of the most influential coach builder/designer in Germany was Karmann.</p>
<p>German designs started gaining popularity after the 1980s, notable after the formation of <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a>. <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, which was dependent on <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Gandini" title="Marcello Gandini">Marcello Gandini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giorgetto_Giugiaro" title="Giorgetto Giugiaro">Giorgetto Giugiaro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Karmann" title="Karmann">Karmann</a>, later formed the contemporary design language along with Audi. BMW entered the automobile design with a sporty-looking everyday sedans using <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Michelotti" title="Giovanni Michelotti">Giovanni Michelotti</a>. These models were later enhanced by <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Spada" title="Ercole Spada">Ercole Spada</a> into the 1980s, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Klaus_Luthe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Klaus Luthe (page does not exist)">Klaus Luthe</a> until the mid-1990s. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> born designer <a href="/wiki/Chris_Bangle" title="Chris Bangle">Chris Bangle</a> was hired by BMW in the late-1990s to re-define the brand. Bangle incorporated new single press technology for compound curves to add controversial styling elements to his designs.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a> family contribution were instrumental in the evolution of Porsche cars, while the Italian designer <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Sacco" title="Bruno Sacco">Bruno Sacco</a> helped create various Mercedes Models from the 1960s to the 1990s.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rear_testarossa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rear_testarossa.jpg/220px-Rear_testarossa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rear_testarossa.jpg/330px-Rear_testarossa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Rear_testarossa.jpg/440px-Rear_testarossa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rear_testarossa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ferrari_Testarossa" title="Ferrari Testarossa">Ferrari Testarossa</a> from Pininfarina Studios by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Fioravanti_(engineer)" title="Leonardo Fioravanti (engineer)">Leonardo Fioravanti</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Italy, companies like Fiat and Alfa Romeo played a major role in car design. Many coach builders were dependent on these two major manufacturers. Italian manufacturers had a large presence in motorsports leading to several sport car manufacturers like <a href="/wiki/Ferrari" title="Ferrari">Ferrari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancia" title="Lancia">Lancia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maserati" title="Maserati">Maserati</a>, etc. During the late-1950s, Italian automobile designs gained global popularity coinciding with the modern fashion and architecture at that time around the world. Various design and technical schools in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a> turned out designers in large scale. By the late-1960s almost all Italian coach builders transformed into design studios catering to automakers around the world. The trend continued in the 1990s, when the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Korean</a> manufacturers sourced designs from these styling studios. One example is <a href="/wiki/Pininfarina" title="Pininfarina">Pininfarina</a>.</p>
<p>Italian designers whose designs services were sought globally are <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Michelotti" title="Giovanni Michelotti">Giovanni Michelotti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ercole_Spada" title="Ercole Spada">Ercole Spada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Sacco" title="Bruno Sacco">Bruno Sacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Gandini" title="Marcello Gandini">Marcello Gandini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Giorgetto_Giugiaro" title="Giorgetto Giugiaro">Giorgetto Giugiaro</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Scandinavia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ursaab.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="800" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ursaab.jpg/220px-Ursaab.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ursaab.jpg/330px-Ursaab.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Ursaab.jpg/440px-Ursaab.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ursaab.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ursaab, an early Saab concept illustrating an advanced headlamp treatment</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sweden had Volvo and Saab as domestic automakers, and the nation's northern location required that cars needed to withstand Nordic climate conditions. The Scandinavian design elements are known for their minimalism and simplicity. One of the early original Scandinavian designs was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saab_92001" title="Saab 92001">Saab 92001</a> by Sixten Sason and Gunnar Ljungström.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Automotive_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Czechoslovakia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tatra_T_77a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="578" data-file-width="713" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tatra_T_77a.jpg/220px-Tatra_T_77a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tatra_T_77a.jpg/330px-Tatra_T_77a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tatra_T_77a.jpg/440px-Tatra_T_77a.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tatra_T_77a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 1934 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czechoslovakian" title="Czechoslovakian">Czechoslovakian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatra_T77" title="Tatra T77">Tatra T77</a> is the first serial-produced aerodynamically designed automobile designed by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Ledwinka" title="Hans Ledwinka">Hans Ledwinka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jaray" title="Paul Jaray">Paul Jaray</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to World War II and until the early-1990s, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> had strong presence in the automotive industry with manufacturers like <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Auto" title="Škoda Auto">Skoda</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jawa_Motors" title="Jawa Motors">Jawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Cesk%C3%A1_zbrojovka_Strakonice" title="Česká zbrojovka Strakonice">CZ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Praga_(company)" title="Praga (company)">Praga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zetor" title="Zetor">Zetor</a>. Czech automobiles were generally known for their originality in mechanical simplicity and designs were remarkably <a href="/wiki/Bohemian" title="Bohemian">Bohemian</a> as evident from Tatra cars and Jawa motorcycles. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> regime, design started falling back and ultimately the domestic automakers ended up as subsidiaries of EU-based companies.</p>
