<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Entity–relationship model</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>An <b>entity–relationship model</b> (<b>ER model</b>) describes inter-related things of interest in a specific domain of knowledge. An ER model is composed of entity types (which classify the things of interest) and specifies relationships that can exist between instances of those entity types.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1425" data-file-width="1600" height="401" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png/450px-ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png/675px-ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png/900px-ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ER_Diagram_MMORPG.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An entity–relationship diagram for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> using Chen's notation.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> an ER model is commonly formed to represent things that a business needs to remember in order to perform business processes. Consequently, the ER model becomes an abstract <a href="/wiki/Data_modeling" title="Data modeling">data model</a> that defines a data or information structure that can be implemented in a <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>, typically a <a href="/wiki/Relational_database" title="Relational database">relational database</a>.</p>
<p>Entity–relationship modeling was developed for database design by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Chen" title="Peter Chen">Peter Chen</a> and published in a 1976 paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-1">[1]</a></sup> However, variants of the idea existed previously,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> some ER modelers show super and subtype entities connected by generalization-specialization relationships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and an ER model can be used also in the specification of domain-specific ontology.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An entity–relationship model is usually the result of systematic analysis to define and describe what is important to processes in an area of a business. It does not define the business processes; it only presents a business data schema in graphical form. It is usually drawn in a graphical form as boxes (<i>entities</i>) that are connected by lines (<i>relationships</i>) which express the associations and dependencies between entities. An ER model can also be expressed in a verbal form, for example: <i>one building may be divided into zero or more apartments, but one apartment can only be located in one building.</i></p>
<p>Entities may be characterized not only by relationships, but also by additional properties (<i>attributes</i>), which include identifiers called "primary keys". Diagrams created to represent attributes as well as entities and relationships may be called entity-attribute-relationship diagrams, rather than entity-relationship models.</p>
<p>An ER model is typically implemented as a <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>. In a simple relational database implementation, each row of a table represents one instance of an entity type, and each field in a table represents an attribute type. In a <a href="/wiki/Relational_database" title="Relational database">relational database</a> a relationship between entities is implemented by storing the primary key of one entity as a pointer or "foreign key" in the table of another entity</p>
<p>There is a tradition for ER/data models to be built at two or three levels of abstraction. Note that the conceptual-logical-physical hierarchy below is used in other kinds of specification, and is different from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_schema_approach" title="Three schema approach">three schema approach</a> to <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conceptual_data_model" title="Conceptual data model">Conceptual data model</a></dt>
<dd>This is the highest level ER model in that it contains the least granular detail but establishes the overall scope of what is to be included within the model set. The conceptual ER model normally defines master reference data entities that are commonly used by the organization. Developing an enterprise-wide conceptual ER model is useful to support documenting the <a href="/wiki/Data_architecture" title="Data architecture">data architecture</a> for an organization.</dd>
<dd>A conceptual ER model may be used as the foundation for one or more <i>logical data models</i> (see below). The purpose of the conceptual ER model is then to establish structural <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> commonality for the <a href="/wiki/Master_data" title="Master data">master data</a> entities between the set of logical ER models. The conceptual data model may be used to form commonality relationships between ER models as a basis for data model integration.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Logical_data_model" title="Logical data model">Logical data model</a></dt>
<dd>A logical ER model does not require a conceptual ER model, especially if the scope of the logical ER model includes only the development of a distinct information system. The logical ER model contains more detail than the conceptual ER model. In addition to master data entities, operational and transactional data entities are now defined. The details of each data entity are developed and the relationships between these data entities are established. The logical ER model is however developed independently of the specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Database_management_system" title="Database management system">database management system</a> into which it can be implemented.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Physical_data_model" title="Physical data model">Physical data model</a></dt>
<dd>One or more physical ER models may be developed from each logical ER model. The physical ER model is normally developed to be instantiated as a database. Therefore, each physical ER model must contain enough detail to produce a database and each physical ER model is technology dependent since each database management system is somewhat different.</dd>
<dd>The physical model is normally instantiated in the structural metadata of a database management system as relational database objects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Database_table" title="Database table">database tables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Database_index" title="Database index">database indexes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Unique_key" title="Unique key">unique key</a> indexes, and database constraints such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_key_constraint" title="Foreign key constraint">foreign key constraint</a> or a commonality constraint. The ER model is also normally used to design modifications to the relational database objects and to maintain the structural metadata of the database.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The first stage of <a href="/wiki/Information_system" title="Information system">information system</a> design uses these models during the <a href="/wiki/Requirements_analysis" title="Requirements analysis">requirements analysis</a> to describe information needs or the type of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> that is to be stored in a <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Data_modeling" title="Data modeling">data modeling</a> technique can be used to describe any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontology_(computer_science)" title="Ontology (computer science)">ontology</a> (i.e. an overview and classifications of used terms and their relationships) for a certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universe_of_discourse" title="Universe of discourse">area of interest</a>. In the case of the design of an information system that is based on a database, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conceptual_data_model" title="Conceptual data model">conceptual data model</a> is, at a later stage (usually called logical design), mapped to a <a href="/wiki/Logical_data_model" title="Logical data model">logical data model</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Relational_model" title="Relational model">relational model</a>; this in turn is mapped to a physical model during physical design. Note that sometimes, both of these phases are referred to as "physical design."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entity.E2.80.93relationship_modeling">Entity–relationship modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Entity–relationship modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>An <a href="/wiki/Entity" title="Entity">entity</a> may be defined as a thing capable of an independent existence that can be uniquely identified. An entity is an abstraction from the complexities of a domain. When we speak of an entity, we normally speak of some aspect of the real world that can be distinguished from other aspects of the real world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>An entity is a thing that exists either physically or logically. An entity may be a physical object such as a house or a car (they exist physically), an event such as a house sale or a car service, or a concept such as a customer transaction or order (they exist logically—as a concept). Although the term entity is the one most commonly used, following Chen we should really distinguish between an entity and an entity-type. An entity-type is a category. An entity, strictly speaking, is an instance of a given entity-type. There are usually many instances of an entity-type. Because the term entity-type is somewhat cumbersome, most people tend to use the term entity as a synonym for this term.</p>
<p>Entities can be thought of as <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>. Examples: a computer, an employee, a song, a mathematical theorem.</p>
<p>A relationship captures how entities are related to one another. Relationships can be thought of as <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>, linking two or more nouns. Examples: an <i>owns</i> relationship between a company and a computer, a <i>supervises</i> relationship between an employee and a department, a <i>performs</i> relationship between an artist and a song, a <i>proves</i> relationship between a mathematician and a conjecture.</p>
<p>The model's linguistic aspect described above is utilized in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declarative_programming_language" title="Declarative programming language">declarative</a> database <a href="/wiki/Query_language" title="Query language">query language</a> ERROL, which mimics <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> constructs. ERROL's <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a> and implementation are based on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reshaped_relational_algebra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reshaped relational algebra (page does not exist)">reshaped relational algebra</a> (RRA), a relational algebra that is adapted to the entity–relationship model and captures its linguistic aspect.</p>
<p>Entities and relationships can both have attributes. Examples: an <i>employee</i> entity might have a <i>Social Security Number</i> (SSN) attribute; the <i>proved</i> relationship may have a <i>date</i> attribute.</p>
<p>Every entity (unless it is a <a href="/wiki/Weak_entity" title="Weak entity">weak entity</a>) must have a minimal set of uniquely identifying attributes, which is called the entity's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_key" title="Primary key">primary key</a>.</p>
<p>Entity–relationship diagrams don't show single entities or single instances of relations. Rather, they show entity sets(all entities of the same entity type) and relationship sets(all relationships of the same relationship type). Example: a particular <i>song</i> is an entity. The collection of all songs in a database is an entity set. The <i>eaten</i> relationship between a child and her lunch is a single relationship. The set of all such child-lunch relationships in a database is a relationship set. In other words, a relationship set corresponds to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relation_(mathematics)" title="Relation (mathematics)">relation in mathematics</a>, while a relationship corresponds to a member of the relation.</p>
<p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Cardinality_(data_modeling)" title="Cardinality (data modeling)">cardinality constraints</a> on relationship sets may be indicated as well.</p>

<p>Physical view show how data is actually stored.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships.2C_roles_and_cardinalities">Relationships, roles and cardinalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relationships, roles and cardinalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Chen's original paper he gives an example of a relationship and its roles. He describes a relationship "marriage" and its two roles "husband" and "wife".</p>
<p>A person plays the role of husband in a marriage (relationship) and another person plays the role of wife in the (same) marriage. These words are nouns. That is no surprise; naming things requires a noun.</p>
<p>Chen's terminology has also been applied to earlier ideas. The lines, arrows and crow's-feet of some diagrams owes more to the earlier <a href="/wiki/Data_structure_diagram" title="Data structure diagram">Bachman diagrams</a> than to Chen's relationship diamonds.</p>
<p>Another common extension to Chen's model is to "name" relationships and roles as verbs or phrases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_naming">Role naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has also become prevalent to name roles with phrases such as <i>is the owner of</i> and <i>is owned by</i>. Correct nouns in this case are <i>owner</i> and <i>possession</i>. Thus <i>person plays the role of owner</i> and <i>car plays the role of possession</i> rather than <i>person plays the role of</i>, <i>is the owner of</i>, etc.</p>
<p>The use of nouns has direct benefit when generating physical implementations from semantic models. When a <i>person</i> has two relationships with <i>car</i> then it is possible to generate names such as <i>owner_person</i> and <i>driver_person</i>, which are immediately meaningful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardinalities">Cardinalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cardinalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modifications to the original specification can be beneficial. Chen described <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Look-across_cardinality&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Look-across cardinality (page does not exist)">look-across cardinalities</a>. As an aside, the <a href="/wiki/Barker%27s_notation" title="Barker's notation">Barker–Ellis</a> notation, used in Oracle Designer, uses same-side for minimum cardinality (analogous to optionality) and role, but look-across for maximum cardinality (the crows foot).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Merise" title="Merise">Merise</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Elmasri &amp; Navathe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> there is a preference for same-side for roles and both minimum and maximum cardinalities. Recent researchers (Feinerer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Dullea et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>) have shown that this is more coherent when applied to n-ary relationships of order greater than 2.</p>
<p>In Dullea et al. one reads "A 'look across' notation such as used in the UML does not effectively represent the semantics of participation constraints imposed on relationships where the degree is higher than binary."</p>
<p>In Feinerer it says "Problems arise if we operate under the look-across semantics as used for UML associations. Hartmann<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> investigates this situation and shows how and why different transformations fail." <i>(Although the "reduction" mentioned is spurious as the two diagrams 3.4 and 3.5 are in fact the same)</i> and also "As we will see on the next few pages, the look-across interpretation introduces several difficulties that prevent the extension of simple mechanisms from binary to n-ary associations."</p>

<p>Chen's notation for entity–relationship modeling uses rectangles to represent entity sets, and diamonds to represent relationships appropriate for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-class_object" title="First-class object">first-class objects</a>: they can have attributes and relationships of their own. If an entity set participates in a relationship set, they are connected with a line.</p>
<p>Attributes are drawn as ovals and are connected with a line to exactly one entity or relationship set.</p>
<p>Cardinality constraints are expressed as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>a double line indicates a <i>participation constraint</i>, <a href="/wiki/Total_relation" title="Total relation">totality</a> or <a href="/wiki/Surjective_function" title="Surjective function">surjectivity</a>: all entities in the entity set must participate in <i>at least one</i> relationship in the relationship set;</li>
<li>an arrow from entity set to relationship set indicates a <a href="/wiki/Unique_key" title="Unique key">key constraint</a>, i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injective_relation" title="Injective relation">injectivity</a>: each entity of the entity set can participate in <i>at most one</i> relationship in the relationship set;</li>
<li>a thick line indicates both, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Bijection" title="Bijection">bijectivity</a>: each entity in the entity set is involved in <i>exactly one</i> relationship.</li>
<li>an underlined name of an attribute indicates that it is a <a href="/wiki/Unique_key" title="Unique key">key</a>: two different entities or relationships with this attribute always have different values for this attribute.</li>
</ul>
<p>Attributes are often omitted as they can clutter up a diagram; other diagram techniques often list entity attributes within the rectangles drawn for entity sets.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crow.27s_foot_notation">Crow's foot notation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crow's foot notation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crow's foot notation is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barker%27s_Notation" title="Barker's Notation">Barker's Notation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structured_Systems_Analysis_and_Design_Method" title="Structured Systems Analysis and Design Method">Structured Systems Analysis and Design Method</a> (SSADM) and <a href="/wiki/Information_engineering" title="Information engineering">information engineering</a>. Crow's foot diagrams represent entities as boxes, and relationships as lines between the boxes. Different shapes at the ends of these lines represent the cardinality of the relationship.</p>
<p>Crow's foot notation was used in the consultancy practice <a href="/wiki/CACI" title="CACI">CACI</a>. Many of the consultants at CACI (including Richard Barker) subsequently moved to <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a> UK, where they developed the early versions of Oracle's <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_software_engineering" title="Computer-aided software engineering">CASE</a> tools, introducing the notation to a wider audience.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entity.E2.80.93relationships_and_semantic_modeling">Entity–relationships and semantic modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Entity–relationships and semantic modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_model">Semantic model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Semantic model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A semantic model is a model of concepts, it is sometimes called a "platform independent model". It is an intensional model. At the latest since <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Carnap</a>, it is well known that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"...the full meaning of a concept is constituted by two aspects, its intension and its extension. The first part comprises the embedding of a concept in the world of concepts as a whole, i.e. the totality of all relations to other concepts. The second part establishes the referential meaning of the concept, i.e. its counterpart in the real or in a possible world".</i></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_model">Extension model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extension model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An extensional model is one that maps to the elements of a particular methodology or technology, and is thus a "platform specific model". The UML specification explicitly states that associations in class models are extensional and this is in fact self-evident by considering the extensive array of additional "adornments" provided by the specification over and above those provided by any of the prior candidate "semantic modelling languages".<a class="external text" href="http://www.tdan.com/view-articles/8589" rel="nofollow">"UML as a Data Modeling Notation, Part 2"</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entity.E2.80.93relationship_origins">Entity–relationship origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Entity–relationship origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peter Chen, the father of ER modeling said in his seminal paper:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"<i>The entity-relationship model adopts the more natural view that the real world consists of entities and relationships. It incorporates some of the important semantic information about the real world.</i>" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chen-1">[1]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In his original 1976 article Chen explicitly contrasts entity–relationship diagrams with record modelling techniques:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"<i>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bachman_diagram" title="Bachman diagram">data structure diagram</a> is a representation of the organisation of records and is not an exact representation of entities and relationships.</i>"</dd>
</dl>
<p>Several other authors also support Chen's program:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_alignment">Philosophical alignment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Philosophical alignment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Chen is in accord with philosophic and theoretical traditions from the time of the Ancient Greek philosophers: Socrates, Plato and Aristotle (428 BC) through to modern <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/First-order_logic" title="First-order logic">logic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Peirce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Frege</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Russell</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> himself associates knowledge with the apprehension of unchanging Forms (The forms, according to Socrates, are roughly speaking archetypes or abstract representations of the many types of things, and properties) and their relationships to one another.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entity%E2%80%93relationship_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>ER assume information content that can readily be represented in a relational database. They describe only a relational structure for this information.</li>
<li>They are inadequate<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The tone or style of material in the vicinity of this tag may not be appropriate for Wikipedia. (July 2016)">tone</span></a></i>]</sup> for systems in which the information cannot readily be represented in relational form<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, such as with <a href="/wiki/Semi-structured_data" title="Semi-structured data">semi-structured data</a>.</li>
<li>For many systems, possible changes to information contained are nontrivial and important enough to warrant explicit specification.</li>
<li>Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> authors have extended ER modeling with constructs to represent change, an approach supported by the original author;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> an example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchor_Modeling" title="Anchor Modeling">Anchor Modeling</a>. An alternative is to model change separately, using a <a href="/wiki/Process_modeling" title="Process modeling">process modeling</a> technique. Additional techniques can be used for other aspects of systems. For instance, ER models roughly correspond to just 1 of the 14 different modeling techniques offered by <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language" title="Unified Modeling Language">UML</a>.</li>
<li>Even where it is suitable in principle, ER modeling is rarely used as a separate activity. One reason for this is today's abundance of tools to support diagramming and other design support directly on relational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Database_management_system" title="Database management system">database management systems</a>. These tools can readily extract database diagrams that are very close to ER diagrams from existing databases, and they provide alternative views on the information contained in such diagrams.</li>
<li>In a survey, Brodie and Liu<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> could not find a single instance of entity–relationship modeling inside a sample of ten Fortune 100 companies. Badia and Lemire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> blame this lack of use on the lack of guidance but also on the lack of benefits, such as lack of support for data integration.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_entity%E2%80%93relationship_model" title="Enhanced entity–relationship model">enhanced entity–relationship model</a> (EER modeling) introduces several concepts not in ER modeling, but are closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-oriented" title="Object-oriented">object-oriented</a> design, like <a href="/wiki/Is-a" title="Is-a">is-a</a> relationships.</li>
<li>For modelling <a href="/wiki/Temporal_database" title="Temporal database">temporal databases</a>, numerous ER extensions have been considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Similarly, the ER model was found unsuitable for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidimensional_database" title="Multidimensional database">multidimensional databases</a> (used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OLAP" title="OLAP">OLAP</a> applications); no dominant conceptual model has emerged in this field yet, although they generally revolve around the concept of <a href="/wiki/OLAP_cube" title="OLAP cube">OLAP cube</a> (also known as <i>data cube</i> within the field).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafanelli2003_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rafanelli2003-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
