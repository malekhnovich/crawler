<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Computational biology</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Computational biology</b> involves the development and application of data-analytical and theoretical methods, mathematical modeling and computational simulation techniques to the study of biological, behavioral, and social systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nih-1">[1]</a></sup> The field is broadly defined and includes foundations in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">applied mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_visualization" title="Scientific visualization">visualization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Computational biology is different from <a href="/wiki/Biological_computation" title="Biological computation">biological computation</a>, which is a subfield of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_engineering" title="Computer engineering">computer engineering</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioengineering" title="Bioengineering">bioengineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> to build <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>, but is similar to <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>, which is an interdisciplinary science using computers to store and process biological data.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Computational Biology, sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>, is the science of using biological data to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithms" title="Algorithms">algorithms</a> and relations among various biological systems. Prior to the advent of computational biology, biologists were unable to have access to large amounts of data. Researchers were able to develop analytical methods for interpreting biological information, but were unable to share them quickly among colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogeweg_2011_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hogeweg_2011-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bioinformatics began to develop in the early 1970s. It was considered the science of analyzing informatics processes of various biological systems. At this time, research in <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> was using network models of the human brain in order to generate new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithms" title="Algorithms">algorithms</a>. This use of biological data to develop other fields pushed biological researchers to revisit the idea of using computers to evaluate and compare large data sets. By 1982, information was being shared amongst researchers through the use of punch cards. The amount of data being shared began to grow exponentially by the end of the 1980s. This required the development of new computational methods in order to quickly analyze and interpret relevant information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogeweg_2011_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hogeweg_2011-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the late 1990s, computational biology has become an important part of developing emerging technologies for the field of biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The terms computational biology and evolutionary computation have a similar name, but are not to be confused. Unlike computational biology, evolutionary computation is not concerned with modeling and analyzing biological data. It instead creates algorithms based on the ideas of evolution across species. Sometimes referred to as genetic algorithms, the research of this field can be applied to computational biology. While evolutionary computation is not inherently a part of computational biology, Computational evolutionary biology is a subfield of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Computational biology has been used to help sequence the human genome, create accurate models of the human brain, and assist in modeling biological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogeweg_2011_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hogeweg_2011-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subfields">Subfields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subfields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_biomodeling">Computational biomodeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Computational biomodeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Modelling_biological_systems" title="Modelling biological systems">Modelling biological systems</a></div>
<p>Computational biomodeling is a field concerned with building <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">computer models</a> of biological systems. Computational biomodeling aims to develop and use visual simulations in order to assess the complexity of biological systems. This is accomplished through the use of specialized algorithms, and visualization software. These models allow for prediction of how systems will react under different environments. This is useful for determining if a system is robust. A robust biological system is one that “maintain their state and functions against external and internal perturbations”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kitano_2002_206.E2.80.9310_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kitano_2002_206.E2.80.9310-6">[6]</a></sup> which is essential for a biological system to survive. Computational biomodeling generates a large archive of such data, allowing for analysis from multiple users. While current techniques focus on small biological systems, researchers are working on approaches that will allow for larger networks to be analyzed and modeled. A majority of researchers believe that this will be essential in developing modern medical approaches to creating new drugs and gene therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kitano_2002_206.E2.80.9310_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kitano_2002_206.E2.80.9310-6">[6]</a></sup> A useful modelling approach is to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petri_nets" title="Petri nets">Petri nets</a> via tools such as esyN <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bean_2014_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bean_2014-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_genomics_.28Computational_genetics.29">Computational genomics (Computational genetics)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Computational genomics (Computational genetics)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computational_genomics" title="Computational genomics">Computational genomics</a></div>
<p>Computational genomics is a field within <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a> which studies the <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> of cells and organisms. It is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Computational_and_Statistical_Genetics" title="Computational and Statistical Genetics">Computational and Statistical Genetics</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a> is one example of computational genomics. This project looks to sequence the entire human genome into a set of data. Once fully implemented, this could allow for doctors to analyze the genome of an individual patient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This opens the possibility of personalized medicine, prescribing treatments based on an individual’s pre-existing genetic patterns. This project has created many similar programs. Researchers are looking to sequence the genomes of animals, plants, bacteria, and all other types of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the main ways that genomes are compared is by <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homology</a>. Homology is the study of biological structures and nucleotide sequences in different organisms that come from a common ancestor. Research suggests that between 80 and 90% of genes in newly sequenced prokaryotic genomes can be identified this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>This field is still in development. An untouched project in the development of computational genomics is the analysis of intergenic regions. Studies show that roughly 97% of the human genome consists of these regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Koonin_2001_155.E2.80.93158-9">[9]</a></sup> Researchers in computational genomics are working on understanding the functions of non-coding regions of the human genome through the development of computational and statistical methods and via large consortia projects such as <a href="/wiki/ENCODE" title="ENCODE">ENCODE</a> (The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements) and the Roadmap Epigenomics Project.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_neuroscience">Computational neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Computational neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Computational_neuroscience" title="Computational neuroscience">Computational neuroscience</a></div>
<p>Computational neuroscience is the study of brain function in terms of the information processing properties of the structures that make up the nervous system. It is a subset of the field of neuroscience, and looks to analyze brain data to create practical applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It looks to model the brain in order to examine specific types aspects of the neurological system. Various types of models of the brain include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Realistic Brain Models: These models look to represent every aspect of the brain, including as much detail at the cellular level as possible. Realistic models provide the most information about the brain, but also have the largest margin for error. More variables in a brain model create the possibility for more error to occur. These models do not account for parts of the cellular structure that scientists do not know about. Realistic brain models are the most computationally heavy and the most expensive to implement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sejnowski_1988_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sejnowski_1988-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Simplifying Brain Models: These models look to limit the scope of a model in order to assess a specific physical property of the neurological system. This allows for the intensive computational problems to be solved, and reduces the amount of potential error from a realistic brain model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sejnowski_1988_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sejnowski_1988-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>It is the work of computational neuroscientists to improve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithms" title="Algorithms">algorithms</a> and data structures currently used to increase the speed of such calculations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_pharmacology">Computational pharmacology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Computational pharmacology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Computational pharmacology (from a computational biology perspective) is “the study of the effects of genomic data to find links between specific genotypes and diseases and then screening drug data”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The pharmaceutical industry requires a shift in methods to analyze drug data. Pharmacologists were able to use Microsoft Excel to compare chemical and genomic data related to the effectiveness of drugs. However, the industry has reached what is referred to as the Excel barricade. This arises from the limited number of cells accessible on a spreadsheet. This development led to the need for computational pharmacology. Scientists and researchers develop computational methods to analyze these massive data sets. This allows for an efficient comparison between the notable data points and allows for more accurate drugs to be developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walter_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walter-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analysts project that if major medications fail due to patents, that computational biology will be necessary to replace current drugs on the market. Doctoral students in computational biology are being encouraged to pursue careers in industry rather than take Post-Doctoral positions. This is a direct result of major pharmaceutical companies needing more qualified analysts of the large data sets required for producing new drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walter_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walter-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_evolutionary_biology">Computational evolutionary biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Computational evolutionary biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Computational biology has assisted the field of evolutionary biology in many capacities. This includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Using DNA data to reconstruct the <a href="/wiki/Computational_phylogenetics" title="Computational phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> tree of life</li>
<li>Fitting <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> models (either forward time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Coalescent_theory" title="Coalescent theory">backward time</a>) to DNA data to make inferences about <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">demographic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">selective</a> history</li>
<li>Building <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> models of evolutionary systems from first principles in order to predict what is likely to evolve.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer_computational_biology">Cancer computational biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cancer computational biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cancer computational biology is a field that aims to determine the future mutations in cancer through an algorithmic approach to analyzing data. Research in this field has led to the use of high-throughput measurement. High throughput measurement allows for the gathering of millions of data points using robotics and other sensing devices. This data is collected from DNA, RNA, and other biological structures. Areas of focus include determining the characteristics of tumors, analyzing molecules that are deterministic in causing cancer, and understanding how the human genome relates to the causation of tumors and cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_and_tools">Software and tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Software and tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Computational Biologists use a wide range of software. These range from command line programs to graphical and web-based programs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_source_software">Open source software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Open source software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Open source software provides a platform to develop computational biological methods. Specifically, open source means that anybody can access software developed in research. <a href="/wiki/PLOS" title="PLOS">PLOS</a> cites four main reasons for the use of open source software including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reproducibility: This allows for researchers to use the exact methods used to calculate the relations between biological data.</li>
<li>Faster Development: developers and researchers do not have to reinvent existing code for minor tasks. Instead they can use pre-existing programs to save time on the development and implementation of larger projects.</li>
<li>Increased quality: Having input from multiple researchers studying the same topic provides a layer of assurance that errors will not be in the code.</li>
<li>Long-term availability: Open source programs are not tied to any businesses or patents. This allows for them to be posted to multiple web pages and ensure that they are available in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conferences">Conferences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conferences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several large conferences that are concerned with computational biology. Some notable examples are <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_Systems_for_Molecular_Biology" title="Intelligent Systems for Molecular Biology">Intelligent Systems for Molecular Biology</a> (ISMB), <a href="/wiki/European_Conference_on_Computational_Biology" title="European Conference on Computational Biology">European Conference on Computational Biology</a> (ECCB) and <a href="/wiki/Research_in_Computational_Molecular_Biology" title="Research in Computational Molecular Biology">Research in Computational Molecular Biology</a> (RECOMB).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journals">Journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous journals dedicated to computational biology. Some notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Computational_Biology" title="Journal of Computational Biology">Journal of Computational Biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/PLOS_Computational_Biology" title="PLOS Computational Biology">PLOS Computational Biology</a>. The PLOS computational biology journal is a peer-reviewed journal that has many notable research projects in the field of computational biology. They provide reviews on software, tutorials for open source software, and display information on upcoming computational biology conferences. <a href="/wiki/PLOS_Computational_Biology" title="PLOS Computational Biology">PLOS Computational Biology</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Open_access_journal" title="Open access journal">open access journal</a>. The publication may be openly used provided the author is cited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Recently a new open access journal <a class="external text" href="http://biopublisher.ca/index.php/cmb" rel="nofollow">Computational Molecular Biology</a> was launched.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_fields">Related fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Related fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Computational biology, <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_and_theoretical_biology" title="Mathematical and theoretical biology">mathematical biology</a> are all interdisciplinary approaches to the life sciences that draw from quantitative disciplines such as mathematics and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">NIH</a> describes computational/mathematical biology as the use of computational/mathematical approaches to address theoretical and experimental questions in biology and, by contrast, bioinformatics as the application of information science to understand complex life-sciences data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nih-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Specifically, the NIH defines</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Computational biology: The development and application of data-analytical and theoretical methods, mathematical modeling and computational simulation techniques to the study of biological, behavioral, and social systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nih-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Bioinformatics: Research, development, or application of computational tools and approaches for expanding the use of biological, medical, behavioral or health data, including those to acquire, store, organize, archive, analyze, or visualize such data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nih-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While each field is distinct, there may be significant overlap at their interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nih_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nih-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
