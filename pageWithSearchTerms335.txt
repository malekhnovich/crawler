<p><b>Social software</b>, also known as <b>Web 2.0 applications</b> or <b>social apps</b>, include communication tools and interactive tools often based on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Communication tools typically handle the capturing, storing and presentation of communication, usually written but increasingly including audio and video as well. Interactive tools handle mediated interactions between a pair or group of users. They focus on establishing and maintaining a connection among users, facilitating the mechanics of conversation and talk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-1">[1]</a></sup> Although we do not have a generally accepted definition but by social software we mean software that makes collaborative behaviour, the organisation and moulding of communities, self-expression, social interaction and feedback possible for individuals. Another important element of existing definition of "social software" is that it allows structured mediation of opinion between people, in a centralized or self-regulating manner. The most improved area for social software is that <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a> <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">applications</a> can all promote cooperation between people and the creation of online communities more than ever before.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instant_messaging">Instant messaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instant messaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">Instant messaging</a></div>
<p>An <i><a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a></i> application or <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">client</a> allows one to communicate with another person over a network in real time, in relative privacy. Popular, consumer-oriented clients include <a href="/wiki/AOL_Instant_Messenger" title="AOL Instant Messenger">AOL Instant Messenger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Hangouts" title="Google Hangouts">Google Hangouts</a>, <a href="/wiki/ICQ" title="ICQ">ICQ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meebo" title="Meebo">Meebo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MSN_Messenger" title="MSN Messenger">MSN Messenger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaim" title="Gaim">Pidgin</a> (formerly maig), and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Messenger" title="Yahoo! Messenger">Yahoo! Messenger</a>. Instant messaging software designed for use in business includes <a href="/wiki/IBM_Sametime" title="IBM Sametime">IBM Sametime</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensible_Messaging_and_Presence_Protocol" title="Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol">XMPP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Messenger" title="Microsoft Messenger">Microsoft Messenger</a>.</p>
<p>One can add friends to a contact or buddy list by entering the person's email address or messenger ID. If the person is online, their name will typically be listed as available for chat. Clicking on their name will activate a chat window with space to write to the other person, as well as read their reply.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Text_chat">Text chat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Text chat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Text_chat" title="Text chat">Text chat</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" title="Internet Relay Chat">Internet Relay Chat</a> (IRC) and other <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">online chat</a> technologies allow users to join and communicate with many people at once, publicly. Users may join a pre-existing chat room or create a new one about any topic. Once inside, you may type messages that everyone else in the room can read, as well as respond to/from others. Often there is a steady stream of people entering and leaving. Whether you are in another person's chat room or one you've created yourself, you are generally free to invite others online to join you in that room.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborative_software">Collaborative software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collaborative software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_software" title="Collaborative software">Collaborative software</a></div>
<p>The goal of collaborative software, also known as groupware, such as Moodle, Landing pages, Enterprise Architecture, and Sharepoint, is to allow subjects to share data – such as files, photos, text, etc. for the purpose of project work or school work. The intent is to first form a group and then have them collaborate among each other. Clay Shirky defines social software as "software that supports group interaction". Since groupware supports group interaction (once the group is formed), it would consider it to be social software.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_forums">Internet forums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Internet forums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">Internet forum</a></div>
<p>Originally modeled after the real-world paradigm of electronic <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_board_system" title="Bulletin board system">bulletin boards</a> of the world before internet was born, <i>internet forums</i> allow users to post a "topic" for others to review. Other users can view the topic and post their own comments in a linear fashion, one after the other. Most forums are public, allowing anybody to sign up at any time. A few are private, gated communities where new members must pay a small fee to join, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Something_Awful_Forums" title="Something Awful Forums">Something Awful Forums</a>.</p>
<p>Forums can contain many different categories in a hierarchy according to topics and subtopics. Other features include the ability to post images or files or to quote another user's post with special formatting in one's own post. Forums often grow in popularity until they can boast several thousand members posting replies to tens of thousands of topics continuously.</p>
<p>There are various standards and claimants for the market leaders of each software category. Various add-ons may be available, including translation and spelling correction software, depending on the expertise of the operators of the bulletin board. In some industry areas, the bulletin board has its own commercially successful achievements: free and paid hardcopy magazines as well as professional and amateur sites.</p>
<p>Current successful services have combined new tools with the older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newsgroup" title="Newsgroup">newsgroup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing list</a> paradigm to produce hybrids like <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Groups" title="Yahoo! Groups">Yahoo! Groups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Groups" title="Google Groups">Google Groups</a>. Also as a service catches on, it tends to adopt characteristics and tools of other services that compete. Over time, for example, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wiki_user_page&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wiki user page (page does not exist)">wiki user pages</a> have become social portals for individual users and may be used in place of other portal applications.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wikis">Wikis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wikis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikis" title="Wikis">Wikis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiki_software" title="Wiki software">Wiki software</a></div>
<p>In the past, web pages were only created and edited by web designers that had the technological skills to do so. Today there are many tools that can assist individuals with web content editing. Wikis allow novices to be on the same level as experienced web designers because wikis provide easy rules and guidelines. Wikis allow all individuals to work collaboratively on web content without having knowledge of any markup languages. A wiki is made up of many content pages that are created by its users. Wiki users are able to create, edit, and link related content pages together. The user community is based on the individuals that want to participate to improve the overall wiki. The participating users are in a democratic community were any users can edit other user's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples include <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiktionary" title="Wiktionary">Wiktionary</a>, the original <a href="/wiki/Portland_Pattern_Repository" title="Portland Pattern Repository">Portland Pattern Repository</a> wiki, <a href="/wiki/MeatballWiki" title="MeatballWiki">MeatballWiki</a>, CommunityWiki and <a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a>. For more detail on free and commercially available wiki systems see <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_wiki_software" title="Comparison of wiki software">Comparison of wiki software</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blogs">Blogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Blogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">Blog</a></div>
<p>Blogs, short for web logs, are like online journals for a particular person. The owner will post a message periodically, allowing others to comment. Topics often include the owner's daily life, views on politics or a particular subject important to them.</p>
<p>Blogs mean many things to different people, ranging from "online journal" to "easily updated personal website." While these definitions are technically correct, they fail to capture the power of blogs as social software. Beyond being a simple homepage or an online diary, some blogs allow comments on the entries, thereby creating a discussion forum. They also have blogrolls (i.e. links to other blogs which the owner reads or admires) and indicate their social relationship to those other bloggers using the <a href="/wiki/XHTML_Friends_Network" title="XHTML Friends Network">XFN</a> social relationship standard. <a href="/wiki/Pingback" title="Pingback">Pingback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trackback" title="Trackback">trackback</a> allow one blog to notify another blog, creating an inter-blog conversation. Blogs engage readers and can build a virtual community around a particular person or interest. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Slashdot" title="Slashdot">Slashdot</a>, <a href="/wiki/LiveJournal" title="LiveJournal">LiveJournal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blogger_(service)" title="Blogger (service)">BlogSpot</a>. Blogging has also become fashionable in business settings by companies who use software such as <a href="/wiki/IBM_Connections" title="IBM Connections">IBM Connections</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborative_real-time_editors">Collaborative real-time editors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Collaborative real-time editors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_real-time_editor" title="Collaborative real-time editor">Collaborative real-time editor</a></div>
<p>Simultaneous editing of a text or media file by different participants on a network was first demonstrated on research systems as early as the 1970s, but is now practical on a global network. Collaborative real-time editing is now utilized, for example, in film editing and on services such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Docs" title="Google Docs">Google Docs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction_markets">Prediction markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Prediction markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prediction_market" title="Prediction market">Prediction market</a></div>
<p>Many prediction market tools have become available (including some <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a>) that make it easy to predict and bet on future events. This a more formal version of social interaction, although it qualifies as a robust type of social software.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_network_services">Social network services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Social network services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_network_service" title="Social network service">Social network service</a></div>
<p>Social network services allow people to come together online and collaborate around shared interests, hobbies or causes. For example, some sites provide meeting organization facilities for people who practice the same sports. Other services enable business networking (<a href="/wiki/Ryze" title="Ryze">Ryze</a>, <a href="/wiki/XING" title="XING">XING</a> and <a href="/wiki/LinkedIn" title="LinkedIn">LinkedIn</a>) and social event meetups (<a href="/wiki/Meetup_(website)" title="Meetup (website)">Meetup</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buettner2016b_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buettner2016b-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some large <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wikis</a> have effectively become social network services by encouraging user pages and portals.</p>
<p>Anyone can create their own social networking service using hosted offerings like <a href="/wiki/Ning_(website)" title="Ning (website)">Ning</a>, or more flexible, installable software like <a href="/wiki/Dolphin_(social_networking_software)" title="Dolphin (social networking software)">Dolphin Pro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elgg_(software)" title="Elgg (software)">Elgg Social Networking Engine</a>, <a href="/wiki/BuddyPress" title="BuddyPress">BuddyPress</a>, <a href="/wiki/SocialEngine" title="SocialEngine">SocialEngine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxwall" title="Oxwall">Oxwall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Status.net" title="Status.net">Status.net</a> or <a href="/wiki/Concursive" title="Concursive">Concursive's</a> <a href="/wiki/ConcourseConnect" title="ConcourseConnect">ConcourseConnect</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_network_search_engines">Social network search engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Social network search engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social network search engines are a class of search engines that use social networks to organize, prioritize or filter search results. There are two subclasses of social network search engines: those that use <b>explicit</b> social networks and those that use <b>implicit</b> social networks.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Explicit</b> social network search engines allow people to find each other according to explicitly stated social relationships such as <a href="/wiki/XHTML_Friends_Network" title="XHTML Friends Network">XFN</a> social relationships. <a href="/wiki/XHTML_Friends_Network" title="XHTML Friends Network">XHTML Friends Network</a>, for example, allows people to share their relationships on their own sites, thus forming a decentralized/distributed online social network, in contrast to centralized social network services listed in the previous section.</li>
<li><b>Implicit</b> social network search engines allow people to filter search results based upon classes of social networks they trust, such as a shared political viewpoint. This was called an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Epistemic_filter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Epistemic filter (page does not exist)">epistemic filter</a> in the 1993 "State of the Future Report" from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Committee_for_the_United_Nations_University&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Committee for the United Nations University (page does not exist)">American Committee for the United Nations University</a> which predicted that this would become the dominant means of search for most users.</li>
</ul>
<p>Lacking trustworthy explicit information about such viewpoints, this type of social network search engine mines the web to infer the topology of online social networks. For example, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NewsTrove&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NewsTrove (page does not exist)">NewsTrove</a> search engine infers social networks from content - sites, blogs, pods and feeds - by examining, among other things, subject matter, link relationships and grammatical features to infer social networks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deliberative_social_networks">Deliberative social networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Deliberative social networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deliberative social networks are webs of discussion and debate for decision-making purposes. They are built for the purpose of establishing sustained relationships between individuals and their government. They rely upon informed opinion and advice that is given with a clear expectation of outcomes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_social_networks">Commercial social networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Commercial social networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commercial social networks are designed to support business transaction and to build a trust between an individual and a brand, which relies on opinion of product, ideas to make the product better, enabling customers to participate with the brands in promoting development, service delivery and a better customer experience.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An example of these networks is <a href="/wiki/Dell_IdeaStorm" title="Dell IdeaStorm">Dell IdeaStorm</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_guides">Social guides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Social guides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A social guide recommending places to visit or contains information about places in the real world such as coffee shops, restaurants and wifi hotspots, etc. One such application is <a href="/wiki/Wikivoyage" title="Wikivoyage">Wikivoyage</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_bookmarking">Social bookmarking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Social bookmarking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Social_bookmarking" title="Social bookmarking">Social bookmarking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_bookmarking" title="Enterprise bookmarking">Enterprise bookmarking</a></div>
<p>Some web sites allow users to post their list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_bookmark" title="Internet bookmark">bookmarks</a> or favorites websites for others to search and view them. These sites can also be used to meet others sharing common interests. Additionally, many social bookmarking sites allow users to browse through websites and content shared by other users based on popularity or category. As such, use of social bookmarking sites is an effective tool for <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">search engine optimization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_media_optimization" title="Social media optimization">social media optimization</a> for <a href="/wiki/Webmaster" title="Webmaster">webmasters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Examples include <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">digg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delicious_(website)" title="Delicious (website)">Delicious</a>, <a href="/wiki/StumbleUpon" title="StumbleUpon">StumbleUpon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">reddit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Furl" title="Furl">furl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enterprise_bookmarking" title="Enterprise bookmarking">Enterprise bookmarking</a> is a method of tagging and linking any information using an expanded set of tags to capture knowledge about data. It collects and indexes these tags in a web-infrastructure server residing behind the firewall. Users can share knowledge tags with specified people or groups, shared only inside specific networks, typically within an organization. Examples of this software are <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_Plaza" title="Knowledge Plaza">Knowledge Plaza</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jumper_2.0" title="Jumper 2.0">Jumper 2.0</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Lotus_Connections#Dogear" title="IBM Lotus Connections">IBM Dogear</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connectbeam" title="Connectbeam">Connectbeam</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_viewing">Social viewing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Social viewing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_viewing" title="Social viewing">Social viewing</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Social_viewing" title="Social viewing">Social viewing</a> allows multiple users to aggregate from multiple sources and view online videos together in a synchronized viewing experience.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_cataloging">Social cataloging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Social cataloging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_cataloging_application" title="Social cataloging application">Social cataloging application</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Social_cataloging_application" title="Social cataloging application">social cataloging</a> much like social bookmarking, this software is aimed towards academics. It allows the user to post a citation for an article found on the internet or a website, online database like Academic Search Premier or LexisNexis Academic University, a book found in a library catalog and so on. These citations can be organized into predefined categories or a new category defined by the user through the use of <a href="/wiki/Tag_(metadata)" title="Tag (metadata)">tags</a>. This allows academics researching or interested in similar areas to connect and share resources.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_libraries">Social libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Social libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This applications allows visitors to keep track of their collectibles, books, records and DVDs. Users can share their collections. Recommendations can be generated based on user ratings, using statistical computation and <a href="/wiki/Network_theory" title="Network theory">network theory</a>. Some sites offer a buddy system, as well as virtual "check outs" of items for borrowing among friends. <a href="/wiki/Folksonomy" title="Folksonomy">Folksonomy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tag_(metadata)" title="Tag (metadata)">tagging</a> is implemented on most of these sites.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_online_storage">Social online storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Social online storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social online storage applications allow their users to collaboratively create file archives containing files of any type. Files can either be edited online or from a local computer which has access to the storage system. Such systems can be built upon existing server infrastructure (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDrive" title="GDrive">GDrive</a>) or leverage idle resources by applying P2P technology (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wuala" title="Wuala">Wuala</a>). Such systems are social because they allow public file distribution and direct <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> with friends.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_network_analysis">Social network analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Social network analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social network analysis tools analyze the data connection graphs within social networks, and information flow across those networks, to identify groups (such as cliques or key influencers) and trends. They fall into two categories: professional research tools, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematica" title="Mathematica">Mathematica</a>, used by social scientists and statisticians, and consumer tools, such as <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Alpha" title="Wolfram Alpha">Wolfram Alpha</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which emphasise ease-of-use. See list at <a href="/wiki/Social_network_analysis_software" title="Social network analysis software">Social network analysis software</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_worlds">Virtual worlds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Virtual worlds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virtual_world" title="Virtual world">Virtual world</a></div>
<p>Virtual Worlds are services where it is possible to meet and interact with other people in a virtual environment reminiscent of the real world. Thus the term <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a>. Typically, the user manipulates an <a href="/wiki/Avatar_(computing)" title="Avatar (computing)">avatar</a> through the world, interacting with others using <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">chat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Voice_chat" title="Voice chat">voice chat</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Massively_multiplayer_online_games">Massively multiplayer online games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Massively multiplayer online games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game" title="Massively multiplayer online game">Massively multiplayer online game</a></div>
<p>MMOGs are virtual worlds (also known as virtual environments) that add various sorts of point systems, levels, competition and winners and losers to virtual world simulation. Commercial MMOGs (or, more accurately, massively multiplayer online role-playing games or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPGs</a>,) include <a href="/wiki/EverQuest" title="EverQuest">EverQuest</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-game_worlds">Non-game worlds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Non-game worlds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another development are the worlds that are less game-like or not <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a> at all. Games have points, winners and losers. Instead, some virtual worlds are more like social networking services like MySpace and Facebook, but with 3D simulation features. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Second_Life" title="Second Life">Second Life</a>, ActiveWorlds, <a href="/wiki/The_Sims_Online" title="The Sims Online">The Sims Online</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/There_(internet_service)" title="There (internet service)">There</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economies">Economies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Economies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virtual_economy" title="Virtual economy">Virtual economy</a></div>
<p>Very often a real economy emerges in these worlds, extending the non-physical <a href="/wiki/Service_economy" title="Service economy">service economy</a> within the world to service providers in the real world. Experts can design dresses or hairstyles for characters, go on routine missions for them and so on, and be paid in game money to do so. This emergence has resulted in expanding social possibility and also in increased incentives to cheat. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Second_Life" title="Second Life">Second Life</a>, the in-world economy is one of the primary features of the world. Some MMOG companies even have economists employed full-time (for example, CCP Games with <a href="/wiki/Eve_Online" title="Eve Online">Eve Online</a>) to monitor their in-game economic systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_specialized_social_applications">Other specialized social applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other specialized social applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many other applications with social software characteristics that facilitate human connection and collaboration in specific contexts. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Project_Management" title="Social Project Management">Social Project Management</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-learning" title="E-learning">e-learning</a> applications are among these.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_journalism">Politics and journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Politics and journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of social software for <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> has also expanded drastically especially over 2004–2006 to include a wide range of social software, often closely integrated with services like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phone_tree" title="Phone tree">phone trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a> forums and run by a candidate, party or <a href="/wiki/Caucus" title="Caucus">caucus</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_politics" title="Open politics">Open politics</a>, a variant of open-source governance, combines aspects of the <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">open content</a> movements, promoting <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a> methods claimed to be more open, less antagonistic, and more capable of determining what is in the <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a> with respect to <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> issues. It is a set of best practices from <a href="/wiki/Citizen_journalism" title="Citizen journalism">citizen journalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Participatory_democracy" title="Participatory democracy">participatory democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a>, informed by <a href="/wiki/E-democracy" title="E-democracy">e-democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Netroots" title="Netroots">netroots</a> experiments, applying argumentation framework for issue-based argument and a <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> which advocates the application of the philosophies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-source" title="Open-source">open-source</a> and open-content movements to <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> principles to enable any interested citizen to add to the creation of policy, as with a <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a> document. Legislation is democratically opened to the general citizenry, employing their <a href="/wiki/Collective_wisdom" title="Collective wisdom">collective wisdom</a> to benefit the decision-making process and improve democracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Open politics encompasses the <a href="/wiki/Open_government" title="Open government">open government</a> principle including those for public participation and engagement, such as the use of <a href="/wiki/IdeaScale" title="IdeaScale">IdeaScale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Moderator" title="Google Moderator">Google Moderator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantic_MediaWiki" title="Semantic MediaWiki">Semantic MediaWiki</a>, <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a>, and other software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Collective forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online_journalism" title="Online journalism">online journalism</a> have emerged more or less in parallel, in part to keep the political spin in check.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_communication_and_interactive_tools">Comparison of communication and interactive tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Comparison of communication and interactive tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Communication tools are generally <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/asynchronous" title="wikt:asynchronous">asynchronous</a>. By contrast, interactive tools are generally <a href="/wiki/Synchronization_(computer_science)" title="Synchronization (computer science)">synchronous</a>, allowing users to communicate in real time (phone, net phone, video chat) or near-synchronous (IM, text chat).</p>
<p>Communication involves the content of talk, speech or writing, whereas interaction involves the interest users establish in one another as individuals. In other words, a communication tool may want to make access and searching of text both simple and powerful. An interactive tool may want to present as much of a user's expression, performance and presence as possible. The organization of texts and providing access to archived contributions differs from the facilitation of interpersonal interactions between contributors enough to warrant the distinction in media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_presence">Virtual presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Virtual presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Telepresence" title="Telepresence">Telepresence</a></div>
<p>Widely viewed, <i>virtual presence</i> or <a href="/wiki/Telepresence" title="Telepresence">telepresence</a> means being present via intermediate technologies, usually radio, telephone, television or the internet. In addition, it can denote apparent physical appearance, such as voice, face and body language.</p>
<p>More narrowly, the term <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_presence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Virtual presence (page does not exist)">virtual presence</a> denotes presence on <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> locations which are identified by <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URLs</a>. People who are browsing a web site are considered to be virtually present at web locations. Virtual presence is a social software in the sense that people meet on the web by chance or intentionally. The ubiquitous (in the web space) communication transfers behavior patterns from the real world and <a href="/wiki/Virtual_world" title="Virtual world">virtual worlds</a> to the web. Research<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> has demonstrated effects<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> of online indicators</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debates_or_design_choices">Debates or design choices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Debates or design choices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Social software may be better understood as a <i>set</i> of debates or design choices, rather than any particular list of tools. Broadly conceived, there are many older media such as <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing lists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> fora that qualify as "social". However, most users of this term restrict its meaning to more recent software genres such as <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wikis</a>. Others suggest that the term <i>social software</i> is best used not to refer to a single type of software, but rather to the use of two or more modes of <a href="/wiki/Computer-mediated_communication" title="Computer-mediated communication">computer-mediated communication</a> that result in "community formation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In this view, people form online communities by combining one-to-one (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> and <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a>), one-to-many (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_pages" title="Web pages">Web pages</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blogs" title="Blogs">blogs</a>) and many-to-many (<a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wikis</a>) communication modes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Some groups schedule <a href="/wiki/Real_life" title="Real life">real life</a> meetings and so become "real" communities of people that share physical lives.</p>
<p>Most definers of social software agree that they seem to facilitate "bottom-up" community development. The system is classless and promotes those with abilities. Membership is voluntary, <a href="/wiki/Reputation" title="Reputation">reputations</a> are earned by winning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trust_(sociology)" title="Trust (sociology)">trust</a> of other members and the community's missions and governance are defined by the members themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Communities formed by "bottom-up" processes are often contrasted to the less vibrant collectivities formed by "top-down" software, in which users' roles are determined by an external authority and circumscribed by rigidly conceived software mechanisms (such as <a href="/wiki/Access_control" title="Access control">access rights</a>). Given small differences in policies, the same type of software can produce radically different social outcomes. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Tiki_Wiki_CMS_Groupware" title="Tiki Wiki CMS Groupware">Tiki Wiki CMS Groupware</a> has a fine-grained permission system of detailed access control so the site administrator can, on a page-by-page basis, determine which groups can view, edit or view the history. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/MediaWiki" title="MediaWiki">MediaWiki</a> avoids per-user controls, to keep most pages editable by most users and puts more information about users currently editing in its recent changes pages. The result is that Tiki can be used both by community groups who embrace the social paradigm of MediaWiki and by groups who prefer to have more content control.</p>
<p>By design, social software reflects the traits of <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networks</a> and is designed very consciously to let <a href="/wiki/Social_network_analysis" title="Social network analysis">social network analysis</a> work with a very compatible database. All social software systems create links between users, as persistent as the identity those users choose. Through these persistent links, a permanent community can be formed out of a formerly <a href="/wiki/Epistemic_community" title="Epistemic community">epistemic community</a>. The ownership and control of these links - who is linked and who isn't - is in the hands of the user. Thus, these links are asymmetrical - you might link to me, but I might not link to you.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Also, these links are functional, not decorative - you can choose not to receive any content from people you are not connected to, for example. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia_user_page&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wikipedia user page (page does not exist)">Wikipedia user pages</a> are a very good example and often contain extremely detailed information about the person who constructed them, including everything from their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mother_tongue" title="Mother tongue">mother tongue</a> to their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_purchasing" title="Moral purchasing">moral purchasing</a> preferences.</p>
<p>In late 2008, independent analyst firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMS_Watch" title="CMS Watch">CMS Watch</a> argued that a scenario-based (use-case) approach to examining social software would provide a useful way to evaluate tools and align business and technology needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Methods and tools for the development of social software are sometimes summarized under the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Software_Engineering" title="Social Software Engineering">Social Software Engineering</a>. However, this term is also used to describe lightweight and community-oriented development practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Constructivist learning theorists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vygotsky" title="Vygotsky">Vygotsky</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D.E._Leidner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="D.E. Leidner (page does not exist)">Leidner</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S.L._Jarvenpaa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="S.L. Jarvenpaa (page does not exist)">Jarvenpaa</a> have theorized that the process of expressing knowledge aids its creation and that conversations benefit the refinement of knowledge. Conversational <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge management</a> software fulfills this purpose because conversations, e.g. questions and answers, become the source of relevant knowledge in the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Conversational technologies are also seen as tools to support both individual knowledge workers and work units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many advocates of Social Software assume, and even actively argue, that users create actual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communities" title="Communities">communities</a>. They have adopted the term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online_communities" title="Online communities">online communities</a>" to describe the resulting social structures.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Christopher Allen supported this definition and traced the core ideas of this concept back through Computer Supported Cooperative or Collaborative Work (CSCW) in the 1990s, Groupware in the 1970s and 1980s, to Englebart's "augmentation" (1960s) and Bush's "Memex" (1940s). Although he identifies a "lifecycle" to this terminology that appears to reemerge each decade in a different form, this does not necessarily mean that social software is simply old wine in new bottles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The augmentation capabilities of social software were demonstrated in early internet applications for communication such as e-mail, newsgroups, groupware, virtual communities etc. In the current phase of Allen's lifecycle, these collaborative tools add a capability "that aggregates the actions of networked users." This points to a powerful dynamic that distinguishes social software from other group collaboration tools and as a component of Web 2.0 technology. Capabilities for content and behavior aggregation and redistribution present some of the more important potentials of this media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the next phase, academic experiments, Social Constructivism and the open source software movement are expected to be notable influences.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clay_Shirky" title="Clay Shirky">Clay Shirky</a> traces the origin of the term "social software" to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eric_Drexler" title="Eric Drexler">Eric Drexler's</a> 1987 discussion of "hypertext publishing systems" like the subsequent World Wide Web, and how systems of this kind could support software for public critical discussion, collaborative development, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_decision_making" title="Group decision making">group commitment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_filtering" title="Collaborative filtering">collaborative filtering</a> of content based on voting and rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Social technologies</i> (or <i>conversational technologies</i>) is a term used by organizations (particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Network-centric_organization" title="Network-centric organization">network-centric organizations</a></i>). It describes the technology that allows the storage and creation of knowledge through collaborative writing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1945, <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a> described a <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">hypertext</a>-like device called the "<a href="/wiki/Memex" title="Memex">memex</a>" in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" title="The Atlantic Monthly">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i> article <i><a href="/wiki/As_We_May_Think" title="As We May Think">As We May Think</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1962, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Engelbart" title="Douglas Engelbart">Douglas Engelbart</a> published his seminal work, "Augmenting Human Intellect: a conceptual framework." In this paper, he proposed using computers to augment training. With his colleagues at the Stanford Research Institute, Engelbart started to develop a computer system to augment human abilities, including learning. Debuting in 1968, the system was simply called the oNLine System (<a href="/wiki/NLS_(computer_system)" title="NLS (computer system)">NLS</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the same year, Dale McCuaig presented the initial concept of a global information network in his series of memos entitled "On-Line Man Computer Communication", written in August 1962. However, the actual development of the internet must be credited to <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Roberts_(scientist)" title="Lawrence Roberts (scientist)">Lawrence G. Roberts</a> of MIT,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAE_Draper_Citation_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAE_Draper_Citation-28">[28]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Kleinrock" title="Leonard Kleinrock">Leonard Kleinrock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kahn" title="Bob Kahn">Robert Kahn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinton_Cerf" title="Vinton Cerf">Vinton Cerf</a>.</p>
<p>In 1971,Jenna Imrie began a year-long demonstration of the <a href="/wiki/TICCIT" title="TICCIT">TICCIT</a> system among Reston, Virginia cable television subscribers. Interactive television services included informational and educational demonstrations using a touch-tone telephone. The <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> re-funded the <a href="/wiki/PLATO_(computer_system)" title="PLATO (computer system)">PLATO</a> project and also funded MITRE's proposal to modify its TICCIT technology as a computer-assisted instruction (CAI) system to support English and algebra at community colleges. MITRE subcontracted instructional design and courseware authoring tasks to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>. Also during this year, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Illich" title="Ivan Illich">Ivan Illich</a> described computer-based "learning webs" in his book <i>Deschooling Society</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Papert" title="Seymour Papert">Seymour Papert</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> published "Mindstorms: children, computers, and powerful ideas" (New York: Basic Books). This book inspired a number of books and studies on "microworlds" and their impact on learning. <a href="/wiki/BITNET" title="BITNET">BITNET</a> was founded by a consortium of US and Canadian universities. It allowed universities to connect with each other for educational communications and e-mail. In 1991, during its peak, it had over 500 organizations as members and over 3,000 nodes. Its use declined as the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> grew.</p>
<p>In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bates" title="Tony Bates">Tony Bates</a> published "<i>The Role of Technology in Distance Education</i>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bates-30">[30]</a></sup> reflecting (in 1986!) on ways forward for e-learning. He based this work on 15 years of operational use of computer networks at the Open University and nine years of systematic R&amp;D on CAL, viewdata/videotex, audio-graphic teleconferencing and computer conferencing. Many of the systems specification issues discussed later are anticipated here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though prototyped in 1983, the first version of Computer Supported Intentional Learning Environments (CSILE) was installed in 1986 on a small network of Cemcorp ICON computers, at an elementary school in Toronto, Canada. CSILE included text and graphical notes authored by different user levels (students, teachers, others) with attributes such as comments and thinking types which reflect the role of the note in the author's thinking. Thinking types included "my theory", "new information", and "I need to understand." CSILE later evolved into <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_Forum" title="Knowledge Forum">Knowledge Forum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, then a young British engineer working at CERN in Switzerland, circulated a proposal for an in-house online document sharing system which he described as a "web of notes with links." After the proposal was grudgingly approved by his superiors, he called the new system the World Wide Web.</p>
<p>In 1992, the CAPA (Computer Assisted Personalized Approach) system was developed at Michigan State University. It was first used in a 92-student physics class in the fall of 1992. Students accessed random personalized homework problems through <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">Telnet</a>.</p>
<p>In 2001, Adrian Scott founded <a href="/wiki/Ryze" title="Ryze">Ryze</a>, a free social networking website designed to link business professionals, particularly new entrepreneurs.</p>
<p>In February 2002, the suvi.org Addressbook started its service. It was the first service that connected people together. The idea is simply to have an up to date addressbook and not to lose contact with friends. Other people on the globe had the same idea. Friendster, Facebook and many other services were successors to this.</p>
<p>In April 2002, Jonathan Abrams created his profile on <a href="/wiki/Friendster" title="Friendster">Friendster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Hi5" title="Hi5">Hi5</a>, <a href="/wiki/LinkedIn" title="LinkedIn">LinkedIn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MySpace" title="MySpace">MySpace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/XING" title="XING">XING</a> were launched.</p>
<p>In February 2004, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> was launched.</p>
<p>In 2004, Levin (in Allen 2004, sec. 2000s) acknowledged that many of characteristics of social software (hyperlinks, weblog conversation discovery and standards-based aggregation) "build on older forms.". Nevertheless, "the difference in scale, standardization, simplicity and social incentives provided by web access turn a difference in degree to a difference in kind." Key technological factors underlying this difference in kind in the computer, network and information technologies are: filtered hypertext, ubiquitous web/computing, continuous internet connectivity, cheap, efficient and small electronics, content syndication strategies (RSS) and others. Additionally, the convergence of several major information technology systems for voice, data and video into a single system makes for expansive computing environments with far reaching effects.</p>
<p>In October 2005, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Andreessen" title="Marc Andreessen">Marc Andreessen</a> (after Netscape and Opsware) and <a href="/wiki/Gina_Bianchini" title="Gina Bianchini">Gina Bianchini</a> co-founded <a href="/wiki/Ning_(website)" title="Ning (website)">Ning</a>, an online platform where users can create their own social websites and networks. Ning now runs more than 275,000 networks, and is a "white label social networking providers, often being compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kickapps" title="Kickapps">Kickapps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brightcove" title="Brightcove">Brightcove</a>, rSitez and Flux.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/StudiVZ" title="StudiVZ">StudiVZ</a> was launched in November 2005.</p>
<p>In 2009, the Army's <a href="/wiki/Program_Executive_Office_Command_Control_Communications_Tactical" title="Program Executive Office Command Control Communications Tactical">Program Executive Office - Command, Control, and Communications Tactical (PEO-C3T)</a> founded <a href="/wiki/MilSuite" title="MilSuite">milSuite</a> capturing the concepts of Wiki, YouTube, Blogging, and connecting with other members of the DOD behind a secure firewall. This platform engages the premise of social networking while also facilitating <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open source software</a> with its purchase of <a class="external text" href="http://www.jivesoftware.com/" rel="nofollow">JIVE</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exponential_generation_of_resource_consuming_negative_externalities">Exponential generation of resource consuming negative externalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Exponential generation of resource consuming negative externalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a person or business sends a message to a network of people this generates an <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential process</a> that can consume considerable amounts of resources - most importantly human time. This can have a beneficial effect on those interested in the message, but can also consume time of people not interested in the message. It can also create in many a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_obligation" title="Social obligation">social obligation</a> to look - albeit briefly - at the message - particularly when it is from someone you know or consider to be a friend.</p>
<p>When a message is completely unwanted and unsolicited, this is a form of <a href="/wiki/Information_pollution" title="Information pollution">information pollution</a> and is often known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a>. When a message is from a network of friends, and wanted by some but not all, it generates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_externalities" title="Negative externalities">negative externalities</a> in that it consumes valuable resources (time).</p>
<p>Some examples : Bill sends an email or social message to 20 friends. Of these 2 are very interested, 8 become interested, the rest aren't interested but may read all or part of the message anyway, spending their time. Some of these 20 people will forward the message to their friends. The process repeats - resulting in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exponential_time#Exponential_time" title="Exponential time">exponentially</a> increasing consumption of time by those uninterested in the message (as well as an exponentially increasing consumption of time by people who are or become interested - which may distract them from other more productive tasks). Eventually, when the expected number of people forwarding a message drops below 1, the process dies out, but in the interim it may circulate widely - resulting in a potentially massive waste of resources. Much of the time wasted will be due to a sense of social obligation to at least scan or check on the title of the message.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_networking_in_a_work_environment">Social networking in a work environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Social networking in a work environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Bill works for ACME company and sends out an email memo or network message to 20 coworkers. Some have to read the message (for example if Bill is their boss or a senior person in the hierarchy), others will just scan it - even if they are uninterested. Some may comment on it - sharing the response with multiple recipients, others may forward it to others. Some may not want to read the message, but may feel obligated to read and respond. The outgoing process of sharing or forwarding takes very little time, but may produce exponentially growing time demands on others. Over time, employees may find more of their time devoted to social networking demands at work - including scanning, reading, commenting upon, forwarding, and responding to messages. These social work-obligations may crowd out more productive activities resulting in longer hours with less efficiency.</p>
<p>In a sense, social networking at work is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Dilbert" title="Dilbert">large ongoing group meeting</a>. Sometimes excellent results occur, but other times major amounts of time are wasted. Sometimes output benefits from everyone's input and ongoing consultation, other times, individual work without constant obligation to check in and gain consensus may be more productive. The output of a <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">"committee"</a> is sometimes worse than that of an individual or small team.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_overload_and_arbitrary_filtering_of_communication">Information overload and arbitrary filtering of communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Information overload and arbitrary filtering of communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As information supply increases, the average time spent evaluating individual content has to decrease. Eventually, much communication is summarily ignored - based on very arbitrary and rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heuristics" title="Heuristics">heuristics</a> that will filter out the information for example by category. Bad information crowds out the good - much the way SPAM often crowds out potentially useful unsolicited communications. (See also the main article on <a href="/wiki/Information_overload" title="Information overload">Information overload</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Downsides_of_ubiquitous_social_networking">Downsides of ubiquitous social networking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Downsides of ubiquitous social networking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyberbullying">Cyberbullying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_software&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Cyberbullying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyberbullying" title="Cyberbullying">Cyberbullying</a></div>
<p>Cyber bullying is different than conventional bullying. Cyber bullying refers to the threat or abuse of a victim by the use of the internet and electronic devices. Victims of cyber bullying can be targeted over social media, email, or text messages. These attacks are typically aggressive, and repetitive in nature. Internet bullies can make multiple email, social media, etc. accounts to attack a victim. Free email accounts that are available to end users can lead a bully to use various identities for communication with the victim. Cyber bullying percentages have grown exponentially because of the use of technology among younger users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cyber Bullying Statistics 2014</p>
<p>25 percent of teenagers report that they have experienced repeated bullying via their cell phone or on the internet. Over half (52 percent) off young people report being cyber bullied. Embarrassing or damaging photographs taken without the knowledge or consent of the subject has been reported by 11 percent of adolescents and teens. Of the young people who reported cyber bullying incidents against them, one-third (33 percent) of them reported that their bullies issued online threats. Often, both bullies and cyber bullies turn to hate speech to victimize their target. One-tenth of all middle school and high school students have been on the receiving end of ‘hate terms' hurled against them. Over half (55 percent) of all teens who use social media have witnessed outright bullying via that medium. An astounding 95 percent of teens who witnessed bullying on social media report that others, like them, have ignored the behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
