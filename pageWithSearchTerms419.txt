<p>The <b>waterfall model</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequential</a> (non-iterative) <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> process, used in <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development processes</a>, in which progress is seen as flowing steadily downwards (like a <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfall</a>) through the phases of conception, initiation, <a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">design</a>, construction, <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">testing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">production/implementation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_maintenance" title="Software maintenance">maintenance</a>. Despite the development of new software development process models, the Waterfall method is still the dominant process model with over a third of software developers still using it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The waterfall development model originates in the <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> industries: highly structured physical environments in which after-the-fact changes are prohibitively costly, if not impossible. Because no formal software development methodologies existed at the time, this hardware-oriented model was simply adapted for software development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benington_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-benington-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterfall_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known presentation describing use of similar phases in software engineering was held by Herbert D. Benington at Symposium on advanced programming methods for digital computers on 29 June 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This presentation was about the development of software for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi_Automatic_Ground_Environment" title="Semi Automatic Ground Environment">SAGE</a>. In 1983 the paper was republished with a foreword by Benington pointing out that the process was not in fact performed in a strict top-down fashion, but depended on a prototype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benington_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-benington-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first formal description of the waterfall model is often cited as a 1970 article by <a href="/wiki/Winston_W._Royce" title="Winston W. Royce">Winston W. Royce</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royce_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-royce-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> although Royce did not use the term <i>waterfall</i> in that article. Royce presented this model as an example of a flawed, non-working model; which is how the term is generally used in writing about software development—to describe a critical view of a commonly used software development practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest use of the term "waterfall" may have been in a 1976 paper by Bell and Thayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> captured this approach in <a href="/wiki/DOD-STD-2167A" title="DOD-STD-2167A">DOD-STD-2167A</a>, their standards for working with software development contractors, which stated that "the contractor shall implement a software development cycle that includes the following six phases: Preliminary Design, Detailed Design, Coding and Unit Testing, Integration, and Testing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Model">Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterfall_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Royce's original waterfall model, the following phases are followed in order:</p>
<ol>
<li>System and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_Requirements_Specification" title="Software Requirements Specification">software requirements</a>: captured in a <a href="/wiki/Product_requirements_document" title="Product requirements document">product requirements document</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Requirements_analysis" title="Requirements analysis">Analysis</a>: resulting in <a href="/wiki/Model-driven_software_development" title="Model-driven software development">models</a>, <a href="/wiki/Database_schema" title="Database schema">schema</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Business_rule" title="Business rule">business rules</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">Design</a>: resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">software architecture</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">Coding</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unit_testing" title="Unit testing">proving</a>, and <a href="/wiki/System_integration" title="System integration">integration</a> of software</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">Testing</a>: the systematic discovery and <a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">debugging</a> of <a href="/wiki/Software_bug" title="Software bug">defects</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Computer_operator" title="Computer operator">Operations</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Installation_(computer_programs)" title="Installation (computer programs)">installation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_migration" title="Data migration">migration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technical_support" title="Technical support">support</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Software_maintenance" title="Software maintenance">maintenance</a> of complete systems</li>
</ol>
<p>Thus the waterfall model maintains that one should move to a phase only when its preceding phase is reviewed and verified.</p>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Modified_waterfall_models" title="Modified waterfall models">modified waterfall models</a> (including Royce's final model), however, can include slight or major variations on this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royce_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-royce-4">[4]</a></sup> These variations included returning to the previous cycle after flaws were found downstream, or returning all the way to the design phase if downstream phases deemed insufficient.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_arguments">Supporting arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterfall_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Supporting arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Time spent early in the software production cycle can reduce costs at later stages. For example, a problem found in the early stages (such as requirements specification) is cheaper to fix than the same bug found later on in the process (by a factor of 50 to 200).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rapid_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rapid-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In common practice, waterfall methodologies result in a project schedule with 20–40% of the time invested for the first two phases, 30–40% of the time to coding, and the rest dedicated to testing and implementation. The actual project organisation needs to be highly structured. Most medium and large projects will include a detailed set of procedures and controls, which regulate every process on the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A further argument for the waterfall model is that it places emphasis on documentation (such as requirements documents and design documents) as well as <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a>. In less thoroughly designed and documented methodologies, knowledge is lost if team members leave before the project is completed, and it may be difficult for a project to recover from the loss. If a fully working design document is present (as is the intent of Big Design Up Front and the waterfall model), new team members or even entirely new teams should be able to familiarise themselves by reading the documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The waterfall model provides a structured approach; the model itself progresses linearly through discrete, easily understandable and explainable phases and thus is easy to understand; it also provides easily identifiable milestones in the development process. It is perhaps for this reason that the waterfall model is used as a beginning example of a development model in many software engineering texts and courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is argued that the waterfall model can be suited to projects where requirements and scope are fixed, the product itself is firm and stable, and the technology is clearly understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waterfall_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Clients may not know exactly what their requirements are before they see working software and so change their requirements, leading to redesign, redevelopment, and retesting, and increased costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Designers may not be aware of future difficulties when designing a new software product or feature, in which case it is better to revise the design than persist in a design that does not account for any newly discovered constraints, requirements, or problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to the perceived problems with the <i>pure</i> waterfall model, <a href="/wiki/Modified_waterfall_models" title="Modified waterfall models">modified waterfall models</a> were introduced, such as "Sashimi (Waterfall with Overlapping Phases), Waterfall with Subprojects, and Waterfall with Risk Reduction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rapid_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-rapid-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some organisations, such as the United States Department of Defense, now have a stated preference against waterfall-type methodologies, starting with <a href="/wiki/MIL-STD-498" title="MIL-STD-498">MIL-STD-498</a>, which encourages <i>evolutionary acquisition</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iterative_and_Incremental_Development" title="Iterative and Incremental Development">Iterative and Incremental Development</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>While advocates of <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile software development</a> argue the waterfall model is an ineffective process for developing software, some sceptics suggest that the waterfall model is a false argument used purely to market <i>alternative</i> development methodologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
