<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>, a <b>software design pattern</b> is a general reusable solution to a commonly occurring problem within a given context in <a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">software design</a>. It is not a finished design that can be transformed directly into <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source</a> or <a href="/wiki/Machine_code" title="Machine code">machine</a> code. It is a description or template for how to solve a problem that can be used in many different situations. Design patterns are formalized <a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">best practices</a> that the programmer can use to solve common problems when designing an application or system.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-oriented" title="Object-oriented">Object-oriented</a> design patterns typically show relationships and <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interactions</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_(computer_science)" title="Class (computer science)">classes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Object_(computer_science)" title="Object (computer science)">objects</a>, without specifying the final application classes or objects that are involved. Patterns that imply mutable state may be unsuited for <a href="/wiki/Functional_programming" title="Functional programming">functional programming</a> languages, some patterns can be rendered unnecessary in languages that have built-in support for solving the problem they are trying to solve, and object-oriented patterns are not necessarily suitable for non-object-oriented languages.</p>
<p>Design patterns may be viewed as a structured approach to <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a> intermediate between the levels of a <a href="/wiki/Programming_paradigm" title="Programming paradigm">programming paradigm</a> and a concrete <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Design patterns reside in the domain of modules and interconnections. At a higher level there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architectural_pattern_(computer_science)" title="Architectural pattern (computer science)">architectural patterns</a> which are larger in scope, usually describing an overall pattern followed by an entire system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R.C.Martin_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-R.C.Martin-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many types of design patterns, for instance <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Algorithm_strategy_patterns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Algorithm strategy patterns (page does not exist)">Algorithm strategy patterns</a></dt>
<dd>Addressing concerns related to high-level strategies describing how to exploit application characteristics on a computing platform.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
<dt><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_design_patterns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Computational design patterns (page does not exist)">Computational design patterns</a></dt>
<dd>Addressing concerns related to key computation identification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution_patterns" title="Execution patterns">Execution patterns</a></dt>
<dd>Which address issues related to lower-level support of application execution, including strategies for executing streams of tasks and for the definition of building blocks to support task synchronization.</dd>
<dt><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Implementation_strategy_patterns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Implementation strategy patterns (page does not exist)">Implementation strategy patterns</a></dt>
<dd>Addressing concerns related to implementing source code to support
<ol>
<li>program organization, and</li>
<li>the common data structures specific to parallel programming.</li>
</ol>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_design_patterns" title="Structural design patterns">Structural design patterns</a></dt>
<dd>Addressing concerns related to global structures of applications being developed.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Patterns originated as an <a href="/wiki/Pattern_(architecture)" title="Pattern (architecture)">architectural concept</a> by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Alexander" title="Christopher Alexander">Christopher Alexander</a> (1977/79). In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Kent_Beck" title="Kent Beck">Kent Beck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ward_Cunningham" title="Ward Cunningham">Ward Cunningham</a> began experimenting with the idea of applying patterns to programming – specifically <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern languages</a> – and presented their results at the <a href="/wiki/OOPSLA" title="OOPSLA">OOPSLA</a> conference that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith1987_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith1987-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beck1987_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beck1987-7">[7]</a></sup> In the following years, Beck, Cunningham and others followed up on this work.</p>
<p>Design patterns gained popularity in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> after the book <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_Patterns_(book)" title="Design Patterns (book)"><i>Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software</i></a> was published in 1994 by the so-called "Gang of Four" (Gamma et al.), which is frequently abbreviated as "GoF". That same year, the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pattern_Languages_of_Programming" title="Pattern Languages of Programming">Pattern Languages of Programming</a> Conference was held and the following year, the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Pattern_Repository" title="Portland Pattern Repository">Portland Pattern Repository</a> was set up for documentation of design patterns. <p>Although design patterns have been applied practically for a long time, formalization of the concept of design patterns languished for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baroni2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baroni2003-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Design patterns can speed up the development process by providing tested, proven development paradigms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Effective software design requires considering issues that may not become visible until later in the implementation. Reusing design patterns helps to prevent subtle issues that can cause major problems<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, and it also improves code readability for coders and architects who are familiar with the patterns.</p>
<p>In order to achieve flexibility, design patterns usually introduce additional levels of <a href="/wiki/Indirection" title="Indirection">indirection</a>, which in some cases may complicate the resulting designs and hurt application performance.</p>
<p>By definition, a pattern must be programmed anew into each application that uses it. Since some authors see this as a step backward from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_reuse" title="Software reuse">software reuse</a> as provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_componentry" title="Software componentry">components</a>, researchers have worked to turn patterns into components. Meyer and Arnout were able to provide full or partial componentization of two-thirds of the patterns they attempted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyer2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meyer2006-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Software design techniques are difficult to apply to a broader range of problems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Design patterns provide general solutions, <a href="/wiki/Documentation" title="Documentation">documented</a> in a format that does not require specifics tied to a particular problem.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Design patterns are composed of several sections (see <a href="#Documentation">§ Documentation</a> below). Of particular interest are the Structure, Participants, and Collaboration sections. These sections describe a <i>design motif</i>: a prototypical <i>micro-architecture</i> that developers copy and adapt to their particular designs to solve the recurrent problem described by the design pattern. A micro-architecture is a set of program constituents (e.g., classes, methods...) and their relationships. Developers use the design pattern by introducing in their designs this prototypical micro-architecture, which means that micro-architectures in their designs will have structure and organization similar to the chosen design motif.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain-specific_patterns">Domain-specific patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Domain-specific patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Efforts have also been made to codify design patterns in particular domains, including use of existing design patterns as well as domain specific design patterns. Examples include <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> design patterns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Information_visualization" title="Information visualization">information visualization</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> secure design,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "secure usability",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Web design <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and business model design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pattern_Languages_of_Programming" title="Pattern Languages of Programming">Pattern Languages of Programming</a> Conference proceedings <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> include many examples of domain-specific patterns.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_list">Classification and list</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification and list">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Design patterns were originally grouped into the categories: <a href="/wiki/Creational_pattern" title="Creational pattern">creational patterns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_pattern" title="Structural pattern">structural patterns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_pattern" title="Behavioral pattern">behavioral patterns</a>, and described using the concepts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delegation_(programming)" title="Delegation (programming)">delegation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregation_(object-oriented_programming)" title="Aggregation (object-oriented programming)">aggregation</a>, and consultation. For further background on object-oriented design, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coupling_(computer_science)" title="Coupling (computer science)">coupling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cohesion_(computer_science)" title="Cohesion (computer science)">cohesion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inheritance_(computer_science)" title="Inheritance (computer science)">inheritance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interface_(object-oriented_programming)" title="Interface (object-oriented programming)">interface</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymorphism_in_object-oriented_programming" title="Polymorphism in object-oriented programming">polymorphism</a>. Another classification has also introduced the notion of <a href="/wiki/Architectural_pattern" title="Architectural pattern">architectural design pattern</a> that may be applied at the architecture level of the software such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model%E2%80%93View%E2%80%93Controller" title="Model–View–Controller">Model–View–Controller</a> pattern.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Documentation">Documentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Documentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The documentation for a design pattern describes the context in which the pattern is used, the forces within the context that the pattern seeks to resolve, and the suggested solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GabrielHillside_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-GabrielHillside-28">[28]</a></sup> There is no single, standard format for documenting design patterns. Rather, a variety of different formats have been used by different pattern authors. However, according to <a href="/wiki/Martin_Fowler" title="Martin Fowler">Martin Fowler</a>, certain pattern forms have become more well-known than others, and consequently become common starting points for new pattern-writing efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler2006-29">[29]</a></sup> One example of a commonly used documentation format is the one used by <a href="/wiki/Erich_Gamma" title="Erich Gamma">Erich Gamma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Helm" title="Richard Helm">Richard Helm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Johnson_(computer_scientist)" title="Ralph Johnson (computer scientist)">Ralph Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Vlissides" title="John Vlissides">John Vlissides</a> (collectively known as the "Gang of Four", or GoF for short) in their book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_Patterns_(book)" title="Design Patterns (book)">Design Patterns</a></i>. It contains the following sections:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Pattern Name and Classification:</b> A descriptive and unique name that helps in identifying and referring to the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Intent:</b> A description of the goal behind the pattern and the reason for using it.</li>
<li><b>Also Known As:</b> Other names for the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Motivation (Forces):</b> A scenario consisting of a problem and a context in which this pattern can be used.</li>
<li><b>Applicability:</b> Situations in which this pattern is usable; the context for the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Structure:</b> A graphical representation of the pattern. <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language#UML_Class_Diagram" title="Unified Modeling Language">Class diagrams</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interaction_diagram" title="Interaction diagram">Interaction diagrams</a> may be used for this purpose.</li>
<li><b>Participants:</b> A listing of the classes and objects used in the pattern and their roles in the design.</li>
<li><b>Collaboration:</b> A description of how classes and objects used in the pattern interact with each other.</li>
<li><b>Consequences:</b> A description of the results, side effects, and trade offs caused by using the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Implementation:</b> A description of an implementation of the pattern; the solution part of the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Sample Code:</b> An illustration of how the pattern can be used in a programming language.</li>
<li><b>Known Uses:</b> Examples of real usages of the pattern.</li>
<li><b>Related Patterns:</b> Other patterns that have some relationship with the pattern; discussion of the differences between the pattern and similar patterns.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of design patterns has been criticized in several ways.</p>
<p>The design patterns may just be a sign of some missing features of a given programming language (<a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a> or <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a> for instance). <a href="/wiki/Peter_Norvig" title="Peter Norvig">Peter Norvig</a> demonstrates that 16 out of the 23 patterns in the <i>Design Patterns</i> book (which is primarily focused on C++) are simplified or eliminated (via direct language support) in <a href="/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language)" title="Lisp (programming language)">Lisp</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dylan_(programming_language)" title="Dylan (programming language)">Dylan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norvig1998_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norvig1998-30">[30]</a></sup> Related observations were made by Hannemann and Kiczales who implemented several of the 23 design patterns using an <a href="/wiki/Aspect-oriented_programming" title="Aspect-oriented programming">aspect-oriented programming language</a> (AspectJ) and showed that code-level dependencies were removed from the implementations of 17 of the 23 design patterns and that aspect-oriented programming could simplify the implementations of design patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hannemann_2002_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hannemann_2002-31">[31]</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Paul_Graham_(computer_programmer)" title="Paul Graham (computer programmer)">Paul Graham's</a> essay "Revenge of the Nerds".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham2002_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham2002-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moreover, inappropriate use of patterns may unnecessarily increase complexity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CodeComplete2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-CodeComplete2-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
