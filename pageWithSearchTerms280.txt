<p><b>Frederick Phillips Brooks, Jr.</b> (born April 19, 1931) is an American computer architect, <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, best known for managing the development of <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System/360" title="System/360">System/360</a> family of computers and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS/360" title="OS/360">OS/360</a> software support package, then later writing candidly about the process in his seminal book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month" title="The Mythical Man-Month">The Mythical Man-Month</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmm_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mmm-5">[5]</a></sup> Brooks has received many awards, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Technology" title="National Medal of Technology">National Medal of Technology</a> in 1985 and the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dblp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shustek2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shustek2015-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Brooks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham</a>, North Carolina, he attended <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, graduating in 1953 with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">Physics</a>, and he received a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in Applied Mathematics (Computer Science) from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1956, supervised by <a href="/wiki/Howard_H._Aiken" title="Howard H. Aiken">Howard Aiken</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathgene_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mathgene-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Brooks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brooks joined <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> in 1956, working in <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie</a>, New York and <a href="/wiki/Yorktown,_New_York" title="Yorktown, New York">Yorktown</a>, New York. He worked on the architecture of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_7030_Stretch" title="IBM 7030 Stretch">IBM 7030 Stretch</a>, a $10m scientific supercomputer of which nine were sold, and the <a href="/wiki/IBM_7950_Harvest" title="IBM 7950 Harvest">IBM 7950 Harvest</a> computer for the National Security Agency. Subsequently, he became manager for the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System/360" title="System/360">System/360</a> family of computers and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS/360" title="OS/360">OS/360</a> software package. During this time he coined the term <a href="/wiki/Computer_architecture" title="Computer architecture">computer architecture</a>.</p>
<p>It was in <i>The Mythical Man-Month</i> that Brooks made the now-famous statement: "Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later." This has since come to be known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooks%27s_law" title="Brooks's law">Brooks's law</a></i>. In addition to <i>The Mythical Man-Month</i>, Brooks is also known for the paper <i><a href="/wiki/No_Silver_Bullet" title="No Silver Bullet">No Silver Bullet</a> â Essence and Accident in Software Engineering</i>.</p>
<p>In 1964, Brooks accepted an invitation to come to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a> and found the University's computer science department. He chaired it for 20 years. As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fred_Brooks&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> he was still engaged in active research there, primarily in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtual_environments" title="Virtual environments">virtual environments</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_visualization" title="Scientific visualization">scientific visualization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBM_archive_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBM_archive-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2010 interview by Kevin Kelly for an article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in Wired Magazine, Brooks was asked "What do you consider your greatest technological achievement?" Brooks responded "The most important single decision I ever made was to change the IBM 360 series from a 6-<a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">byte</a> to an 8-bit byte, thereby enabling the use of lowercase letters. That change propagated everywhere."</p>
<p>A "20th anniversary" edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month" title="The Mythical Man-Month">The Mythical Man-Month</a></i> with four additional chapters was published in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As well as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month" title="The Mythical Man-Month">The Mythical Man-Month</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmm_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mmm-5">[5]</a></sup> Brooks has authored or co-authored many books and <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer reviewed</a> papers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dblp_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-6">[6]</a></sup> including <i>Automatic Data Processing</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/No_Silver_Bullet" title="No Silver Bullet">No Silver Bullet</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Computer Architecture</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <i>The Design of Design</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His contributions to <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Humanâcomputer interaction">Humanâcomputer interaction</a> are described in Ben Shneiderman's HCI pioneers website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
