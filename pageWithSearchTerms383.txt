<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Knowledge</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Knowledge_(disambiguation)" title="Knowledge (disambiguation)">Knowledge (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Knowledge</b> is a familiarity, <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a> or understanding of someone or something, such as <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">descriptions</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skills" title="Skills">skills</a>, which is acquired through <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> or <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> by <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceiving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Discovery_(observation)" title="Discovery (observation)">discovering</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>.</p>
<p>Knowledge can refer to a theoretical or practical understanding of a subject. It can be implicit (as with practical skill or expertise) or explicit (as with the theoretical understanding of a subject); it can be more or less formal or systematic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, the study of knowledge is called <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>; the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> famously defined knowledge as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justified_true_belief" title="Justified true belief">justified true belief</a>", though this definition is now agreed by most analytic philosophers to be problematic because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gettier_problems" title="Gettier problems">Gettier problems</a>. However, several definitions of knowledge and theories to explain it exist.</p>
<p>Knowledge acquisition involves complex <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> processes: <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reasoning" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> while knowledge is also said to be related to the capacity of <i>acknowledgment</i> in human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_of_knowledge">Theories of knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories of knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="1674" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg/220px-Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg/330px-Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg/440px-Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knowledge-Reid-Highsmith.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Reid_(painter)" title="Robert Reid (painter)">Robert Reid</a>, <i>Knowledge</i> (1896). <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson_Building" title="Thomas Jefferson Building">Thomas Jefferson Building</a>, Washington, D.C.</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">Epistemology</a></div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The eventual demarcation of philosophy from science was made possible by the notion that philosophy's core was "theory of knowledge," a theory distinct from the sciences because it was their <i>foundation</i>... Without this idea of a "theory of knowledge," it is hard to imagine what "philosophy" could have been in the age of modern science.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rorty" title="Richard Rorty">Richard Rorty</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophy_and_the_Mirror_of_Nature" title="Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature">Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature</a></i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The definition of knowledge is a matter of ongoing <a href="/wiki/Debate" title="Debate">debate</a> among <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosophers</a> in the field of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>. The classical definition, described but not ultimately endorsed by <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> specifies that a <a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement (logic)">statement</a> must meet three <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/criterion" title="wikt:criterion">criteria</a> in order to be considered knowledge: it must be <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_justification" title="Theory of justification">justified</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">true</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">believed</a>. Some claim that these conditions are not sufficient, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gettier_case" title="Gettier case">Gettier case</a> examples allegedly demonstrate. There are a number of alternatives proposed, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nozick" title="Robert Nozick">Robert Nozick</a>'s arguments for a requirement that knowledge 'tracks the truth' and <a href="/wiki/Simon_Blackburn" title="Simon Blackburn">Simon Blackburn</a>'s additional requirement that we do not want to say that those who meet any of these conditions 'through a defect, flaw, or failure' have knowledge. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Kirkham" title="Richard Kirkham">Richard Kirkham</a> suggests that our definition of knowledge requires that the evidence for the belief necessitates its truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast to this approach, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a> observed, following <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_paradox" title="Moore's paradox">Moore's paradox</a>, that one can say "He believes it, but it isn't so," but not "He knows it, but it isn't so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He goes on to argue that these do not correspond to distinct mental states, but rather to distinct ways of talking about conviction. What is different here is not the mental state of the speaker, but the activity in which they are engaged. For example, on this account, to <i>know</i> that the kettle is boiling is not to be in a particular state of mind, but to perform a particular task with the statement that the kettle is boiling. Wittgenstein sought to bypass the difficulty of definition by looking to the way "knowledge" is used in natural languages. He saw knowledge as a case of a <a href="/wiki/Family_resemblance" title="Family resemblance">family resemblance</a>. Following this idea, "knowledge" has been reconstructed as a cluster concept that points out relevant features but that is not adequately captured by any definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communicating_knowledge">Communicating knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Communicating knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg/220px-Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg/330px-Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg/440px-Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Los portadores de la antorcha (The Torch-Bearers)</i> – Sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Anna_Hyatt_Huntington" title="Anna Hyatt Huntington">Anna Hyatt Huntington</a> symbolizing the transmission of knowledge from one generation to the next (<a href="/wiki/Complutense_University_of_Madrid" title="Complutense University of Madrid">Ciudad Universitaria, Madrid, Spain</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Symbolic_linguistic_representation" title="Symbolic linguistic representation">Symbolic representations</a> can be used to indicate meaning and can be thought of as a dynamic process. Hence the transfer of the symbolic representation can be viewed as one <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ascription" title="wikt:ascription">ascription</a> process whereby knowledge can be transferred. Other forms of communication include observation and imitation, verbal exchange, and audio and video recordings. Philosophers of language and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Semioticians&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Semioticians (page does not exist)">semioticians</a> construct and analyze theories of knowledge transfer or communication.</p>
<p>While many would agree that one of the most universal and significant tools for the transfer of knowledge is writing and reading (of many kinds), argument over the usefulness of the written word exists nonetheless, with some scholars skeptical of its impact on societies. In his collection of essays <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technopoly:_the_Surrender_of_Culture_to_Technology" title="Technopoly: the Surrender of Culture to Technology">Technopoly</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Postman" title="Neil Postman">Neil Postman</a> demonstrates the argument against the use of writing through an excerpt from Plato's work <i><a href="/wiki/Phaedrus_(dialogue)" title="Phaedrus (dialogue)">Phaedrus</a></i> (Postman, Neil (1992) <i>Technopoly</i>, Vintage, New York, pp 73). In this excerpt, the scholar <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> recounts the story of Thamus, the Egyptian king and Theuth the inventor of the written word. In this story, Theuth presents his new invention "writing" to King Thamus, telling Thamus that his new invention "will improve both the <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a> and memory of the Egyptians" (Postman, Neil (1992) Technopoly, Vintage, New York, pp 74). King Thamus is skeptical of this new invention and rejects it as a tool of recollection rather than retained knowledge. He argues that the written word will infect the Egyptian people with fake knowledge as they will be able to attain facts and stories from an external source and will no longer be forced to mentally retain large quantities of knowledge themselves (Postman, Neil (1992) <i>Technopoly</i>, Vintage, New York,pp 74).</p>
<p>Classical early modern theories of knowledge, especially those advancing the influential empiricism of the philosopher John Locke, were based implicitly or explicitly on a model of the mind which likened ideas to words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This analogy between language and thought laid the foundation for a graphic conception of knowledge in which the mind was treated as a table, a container of content, that had to be stocked with facts reduced to letters, numbers or symbols. This created a situation in which the spatial alignment of words on the page carried great cognitive weight, so much so that educators paid very close attention to the visual structure of information on the page and in notebooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Media theorists like Andrew Robinson emphasise that the visual depiction of knowledge in the modern world was often seen as being 'truer' than oral knowledge. This plays into a longstanding analytic notion in the Western intellectual tradition in which verbal communication is generally thought to lend itself to the spread of falsehoods as much as written communication. It is harder to preserve records of what was said or who originally said it – usually neither the source nor the content can be verified. Gossip and rumors are examples prevalent in both media. As to the value of writing, the extent of human knowledge is now so great, and the people interested in a piece of knowledge so separated in time and space, that writing is considered central to capturing and sharing it.</p>
<p>Major libraries today can have millions of books of knowledge (in addition to works of fiction). It is only recently that audio and video technology for recording knowledge have become available and the use of these still requires replay equipment and electricity. Verbal teaching and handing down of knowledge is limited to those who would have contact with the transmitter or someone who could interpret written work. Writing is still the most available and most universal of all forms of recording and transmitting knowledge. It stands unchallenged as mankind's primary technology of knowledge transfer down through the ages and to all cultures and languages of the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (May 2013)">disputed</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Knowledge#Writing_as_the_.22primary_technology_of_knowledge_transfer.22_.28disputed_text.29" title="Talk:Knowledge">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Situated_knowledge">Situated knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Situated knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Situated knowledge is knowledge specific to a particular situation. It is a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Donna_Haraway" title="Donna Haraway">Donna Haraway</a> as an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a> approaches of "successor science" suggested by <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Harding" title="Sandra Harding">Sandra Harding</a>, one which "offers a more adequate, richer, better account of a world, in order to live in it well and in critical, reflexive relation to our own as well as others' practices of domination and the unequal parts of privilege and oppression that makes up all positions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haraway1988_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haraway1988-10">[10]</a></sup> This situation partially transforms science into a <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Escobar_(anthropologist)" title="Arturo Escobar (anthropologist)">Arturo Escobar</a> explains as, "neither fictions nor supposed facts." This narrative of situation is historical textures woven of fact and fiction, and as Escobar explains further, "even the most neutral scientific domains are narratives in this sense," insisting that rather than a purpose dismissing science as a trivial matter of contingency, "it is to treat (this narrative) in the most serious way, without succumbing to its mystification as 'the truth' or to the ironic <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a> common to many critiques."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Haraway's argument stems from the limitations of the human <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a>, as well as the overemphasis of the <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">sense of vision</a> in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>. According to Haraway, <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a> in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> has been, "used to signify a leap out of the marked body and into a conquering gaze from nowhere." This is the "gaze that mythically inscribes all the marked bodies, that makes the unmarked category claim the power to see and not be seen, to represent while escaping representation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haraway1988_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haraway1988-10">[10]</a></sup> This causes a limitation of views in the position of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> itself as a potential player in the creation of knowledge, resulting in a position of "modest witness". This is what Haraway terms a "god trick", or the aforementioned representation while escaping representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In order to avoid this, "Haraway perpetuates a tradition of thought which emphasizes the importance of the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(philosophy)" title="Subject (philosophy)">subject</a> in terms of both ethical and political accountability".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some methods of generating knowledge, such as <a href="/wiki/Trial_and_error" title="Trial and error">trial and error</a>, or learning from <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a>, tend to create highly situational knowledge. One of the main attributes of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> is that the theories it generates are much less situational than knowledge gained by other methods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Situational knowledge is often embedded in language, culture, or traditions. This integration of situational knowledge is an allusion to the community, and its attempts at collecting subjective perspectives into an embodiment "of views from somewhere." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haraway1988_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haraway1988-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Knowledge generated through experience is called knowledge "a posteriori", meaning afterwards. The pure existence of a term like "a posteriori" means this also has a counterpart. In this case, that is knowledge "a priori", meaning before. The knowledge prior to any experience means that there are certain "assumptions" that one takes for granted. For example, if you are being told about a <a href="/wiki/Chair" title="Chair">chair</a>, it is clear to you that the chair is in <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>, that it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_dimensional_space" title="Three dimensional space">3D</a>. This knowledge is not knowledge that one can "forget", even someone suffering from amnesia experiences the world in 3D.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Even though Haraway's arguments are largely based on <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist studies</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haraway1988_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Haraway1988-10">[10]</a></sup> this idea of different worlds, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skeptic</a> stance of situated knowledge is present in the main arguments of <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralism</a>. Fundamentally, both argue the <a href="/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy)" title="Contingency (philosophy)">contingency</a> of knowledge on the presence of <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>; <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, as well as the rejection of universal rules or laws or elementary structures; and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> as an inherited trait of <a href="/wiki/Objectification" title="Objectification">objectification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_knowledge">Partial knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Partial knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One discipline of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> focuses on partial knowledge. In most cases, it is not possible to understand an information domain exhaustively; our knowledge is always <i>incomplete</i> or partial. Most real problems have to be solved by taking advantage of a partial understanding of the problem context and problem data, unlike the typical math problems one might solve at school, where all data is given and one is given a complete understanding of formulas necessary to solve them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>This idea is also present in the concept of <a href="/wiki/Bounded_rationality" title="Bounded rationality">bounded rationality</a> which assumes that in real life situations people often have a limited amount of information and make decisions accordingly.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intuition_(psychology)" title="Intuition (psychology)">Intuition</a> is the ability to acquire partial knowledge without <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">inference</a> or the use of <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> An individual may "know" about a situation and be unable to explain the process that led to their knowledge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_knowledge">Scientific knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scientific knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Francis_Bacon_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1337" data-file-width="814" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Francis_Bacon_2.jpg/170px-Francis_Bacon_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Francis_Bacon_2.jpg/255px-Francis_Bacon_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/Francis_Bacon_2.jpg/340px-Francis_Bacon_2.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Francis_Bacon_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Bacon" title="Sir Francis Bacon">Sir Francis Bacon</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Scientia_potentia_est" title="Scientia potentia est">Knowledge is Power</a>"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The development of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> has made a significant contribution to how knowledge of the physical world and its phenomena is acquired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To be termed scientific, a method of <a href="/wiki/Inquiry" title="Inquiry">inquiry</a> must be based on gathering <a href="/wiki/Observable" title="Observable">observable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurable</a> <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> subject to specific principles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reasoning" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a> and experimentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The scientific method consists of the collection of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> through <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimentation</a>, and the formulation and testing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotheses" title="Hypotheses">hypotheses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Science, and the nature of scientific knowledge have also become the subject of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">Philosophy</a>. As science itself has developed, scientific knowledge now includes a broader usage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_science" title="Hard and soft science">soft sciences</a> such as biology and the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a> — discussed elsewhere as <a href="/wiki/Meta-epistemology" title="Meta-epistemology">meta-epistemology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Genetic_epistemology" title="Genetic epistemology">genetic epistemology</a>, and to some extent related to "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_cognitive_development" title="Theory of cognitive development">theory of cognitive development</a>". Note that "<a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>" is the study of knowledge and how it is acquired. Science is "the process used everyday to logically complete thoughts through inference of facts determined by calculated experiments." <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Bacon" title="Sir Francis Bacon">Sir Francis Bacon</a> was critical in the historical development of the scientific method; his works established and popularized an inductive methodology for scientific inquiry. His famous aphorism, "<a href="/wiki/Scientia_potentia_est" title="Scientia potentia est">knowledge is power</a>", is found in the Meditations Sacrae (1597).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until recent times, at least in the Western tradition, it was simply taken for granted that knowledge was something possessed only by humans — and probably <i>adult</i> humans at that. Sometimes the notion might stretch to (ii) <i>Society-as-such</i>, as in (e.g.) "the knowledge possessed by the Coptic culture" (as opposed to its individual members), but that was not assured either. Nor was it usual to consider <i>unconscious</i> knowledge in any systematic way until this approach was popularized by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other biological domains where "knowledge" might be said to reside, include: (iii) the <i>immune system</i>, and (iv) in the <i>DNA of the genetic code</i>. See the list of four "epistemological domains":   <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Popper</a>, (1975);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and Traill (2008:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Table S, page 31)—also references by both to <a href="/wiki/Niels_Kaj_Jerne" title="Niels Kaj Jerne">Niels Jerne</a>.</p>
<p>Such considerations seem to call for a separate definition of "knowledge" to cover the biological systems. For biologists, knowledge must be usefully <i>available</i> to the system, though that system need not be conscious. Thus the criteria seem to be:</p>
<ul>
<li>The system should apparently be dynamic and self-organizing (unlike a mere book <i>on its own</i>).</li>
<li>The knowledge must constitute some sort of representation of "the outside world",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> or ways of dealing with it (directly or indirectly).</li>
<li>Some way must exist for the system to access this information quickly enough for it to be useful.</li>
</ul>
<p>Scientific knowledge may not involve a claim to <a href="/wiki/Certainty" title="Certainty">certainty</a>, maintaining <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a> means that a scientist will never be absolutely certain when they are correct and when they are not. It is thus an irony of proper <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> that one must doubt even when correct, in the hopes that this practice will lead to greater convergence on the <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a> in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_meaning_of_knowledge">Religious meaning of knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious meaning of knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many expressions of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, knowledge is one of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_gifts_of_the_Holy_Spirit" title="Seven gifts of the Holy Spirit">seven gifts of the Holy Spirit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_the_knowledge_of_good_and_evil" title="Tree of the knowledge of good and evil">tree of the knowledge of good and evil</a> contained the knowledge that separated Man from God: "And the LORD God said, Behold, the man is become as one of us, to know good and evil..." (<a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder2/?book=Genesis&amp;verse=3:22&amp;src=KJV">Genesis 3:22</a>)</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnosticism</a>, divine knowledge or <a href="/wiki/Gnosis" title="Gnosis">gnosis</a> is hoped to be attained.</p>
<p>विद्या दान (Vidya Daan) i.e. knowledge sharing is a major part of <a href="/wiki/D%C4%81na" title="Dāna">Daan</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Niyama" title="Niyama">tenet</a> of all <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">Dharmic Religions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> Scriptures present two kinds of knowledge, <i>Paroksh Gyan</i> and <i>Prataksh Gyan</i>. <i>Paroksh Gyan</i> (also spelled <i><a href="/wiki/Paroksha" title="Paroksha">Paroksha</a>-Jnana</i>) is secondhand knowledge: knowledge obtained from books, hearsay, etc. <i>Prataksh Gyan</i> (also spelled <i>Prataksha-Jnana</i>) is the knowledge borne of direct experience, i.e., knowledge that one discovers for oneself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jnana_yoga" title="Jnana yoga">Jnana yoga</a> ("path of knowledge") is one of three main types of yoga expounded by <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">Bhagavad Gita</a>. (It is compared and contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Bhakti#Bhakti_Yoga" title="Bhakti">Bhakti Yoga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karma_yoga" title="Karma yoga">Karma yoga</a>.)</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, knowledge (Arabic: علم, <i>ʿilm</i>) is given great significance. "The Knowing" (<i>al-ʿAlīm</i>) is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_the_Qur%27an" title="Names of God in the Qur'an">99 names</a> reflecting distinct attributes of <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> asserts that knowledge comes from God (<a class="external text" href="http://www.usc.edu/org/cmje/religious-texts/quran/verses/002-qmt.php#002.239" rel="nofollow">2:239</a>) and various <i><a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a></i> encourage the acquisition of knowledge. <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> is reported to have said "Seek knowledge from the cradle to the grave" and "Verily the men of knowledge are the inheritors of the prophets". Islamic scholars, theologians and jurists are often given the title <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema">alim</a></i>, meaning "knowledgeble".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> tradition, knowledge (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a>: דעת <i>da'ath</i>) is considered one of the most valuable traits a person can acquire. Observant Jews recite three times a day in the <a href="/wiki/Amidah" title="Amidah">Amidah</a> "Favor us with knowledge, understanding and discretion that come from you. Exalted are you, Existent-One, the gracious giver of knowledge." The <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">Tanakh</a> states, "A wise man gains power, and a man of knowledge maintains power", and "knowledge is chosen above gold".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_measure_of_religiosity_.28in_sociology_of_religion.29">As a measure of religiosity (in sociology of religion)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: As a measure of religiosity (in sociology of religion)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the sociologist <a href="/wiki/Mervin_F._Verbit" title="Mervin F. Verbit">Mervin Verbit</a>, knowledge may be understood as one of the key components of religiosity. Religious knowledge itself may be broken down into four dimensions:</p>
<ul>
<li>content</li>
<li>frequency</li>
<li>intensity</li>
<li>centrality</li>
</ul>
<p>The content of one's religious knowledge may vary from person to person, as will the degree to which it may occupy the person's mind (frequency), the intensity of the knowledge, and the centrality of the information (in that religious tradition, or to that individual).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
