<p><b>Pattern theory</b>, formulated by <a href="/wiki/Ulf_Grenander" title="Ulf Grenander">Ulf Grenander</a>, is a mathematical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formalism_(mathematics)" title="Formalism (mathematics)">formalism</a> to describe knowledge of the world as patterns. It differs from other approaches to <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> in that it does not begin by prescribing <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> and machinery to recognize and classify patterns; rather, it prescribes a vocabulary to articulate and recast the pattern concepts in precise language.</p>
<p>In addition to the new algebraic vocabulary, its statistical approach is novel in its aim to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Identify the <a href="/wiki/Latent_variable" title="Latent variable">hidden variables</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Data_set" title="Data set">data set</a> using real world data rather than artificial stimuli, which was previously commonplace.</li>
<li>Formulate prior distributions for hidden variables and models for the observed variables that form the vertices of a Gibbs-like graph.</li>
<li>Study the randomness and variability of these graphs.</li>
<li>Create the basic classes of stochastic models applied by listing the deformations of the patterns.</li>
<li>Synthesize (sample) from the models, not just analyze signals with it.</li>
</ul>
<p>Broad in its mathematical coverage, Pattern Theory spans algebra and statistics, as well as local topological and global entropic properties.</p>
<p>The Brown University Pattern Theory Group was formed in 1972 by <a class="external text" href="http://www.dam.brown.edu/pattern/ug.html" rel="nofollow">Ulf Grenander</a>. Many mathematicians are currently working in this group, noteworthy among them being the <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">Fields Medalist</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Mumford" title="David Mumford">David Mumford</a>. Mumford regards Grenander as his "guru" in this subject.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example:_Natural_Language_Grammar">Example: Natural Language Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Example: Natural Language Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>We begin with an example to motivate the algebraic definitions that follow.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grammar_automaton.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="514" data-file-width="467" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Grammar_automaton.png/200px-Grammar_automaton.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Grammar_automaton.png/300px-Grammar_automaton.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Grammar_automaton.png/400px-Grammar_automaton.png 2x" width="200"/></a>

Grammar automaton</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:155px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Generator_list.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="216" data-file-width="153" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Generator_list.png" width="153"/></a>

<p>If we want to represent language patterns, the most immediate candidate for primitives might be words. However, <a href="/wiki/Set_phrase" title="Set phrase">set phrases</a>, such as “in order to”, immediately indicate the inappropriateness of words as atoms. In searching for other primitives, we might try the rules of <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>. We can represent grammars as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_state_automata" title="Finite state automata">finite state automata</a> or <a href="/wiki/Context-free_grammar" title="Context-free grammar">context-free grammars</a>. Below is a sample finite state grammar automaton.</p>
<p>The following phrases are generated from a few simple rules of the automaton and programming code in pattern theory:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>the boy who owned the small cottage went to the deep forest</i></dd>
<dd><i>the prince walked to the lake</i></dd>
<dd><i>the girl walked to the lake and the princess went to the lake</i></dd>
<dd><i>the pretty prince walked to the dark forest</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>To create such sentences, rewriting rules in finite state automata act as <a href="/wiki/Generator_(mathematics)" title="Generator (mathematics)">generators</a> to create the sentences as follows: if a machine starts in state 1, it goes to state 2 and writes the word “the”. From state 2, it writes one of 4 words: prince, boy, princess, girl, chosen at random. The probability of choosing any given word is given by the <a href="/wiki/Markov_chain" title="Markov chain">Markov chain</a> corresponding to the automaton. Such a simplistic automaton occasionally generates more awkward sentences</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>the evil evil prince walked to the lake</i></dd>
<dd><i>the prince walked to the dark forest and the prince walked to a forest and the princess who lived in some big small big cottage who owned the small big small house went to a forest</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>From the finite state diagram we can infer the following generators (shown at right) that creates the signal. A generator is a 4-tuple: current state, next state, word written, probability of written word when there are multiple choices. That is, each generator is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_transition" title="State transition">state transition</a> arrow of <a href="/wiki/State_diagram" title="State diagram">state diagram</a> for a Markov chain.</p>
<p>Imagine that a configuration of generators are strung together linearly so its output forms a sentence, so each generator "bonds" to the generators before and after it. Denote these bonds as 1a,1b,2a,2b,…12a,12b. Each numerical label corresponds to the automaton's state and each letter "a" and "b" corresponds to the inbound and outbound bonds. Then the following bond table (left) is equivalent to the automaton diagram. For the sake of simplicity, only half of the bond table is shown—the table is actually symmetric.</p>

<p>As one can tell from this example, and typical of signals that are studied, identifying the primitives and bond tables requires some thought. The example highlights another important fact not readily apparent in other signals problems: that a configuration is not the signal that is observed; rather, its image as a sentence is observed. Herein lies a significant justification for distinguishing an observable from a non-observable construct. Additionally, it provides an algebraic structure to associate with <a href="/wiki/Hidden_Markov_model" title="Hidden Markov model">hidden Markov models</a>. In sensory examples such as the vision example below, the hidden configurations and observed images are much more similar, and such a distinction may not seem justified. Fortunately, the grammar example reminds us of this distinction.</p>
<p>A more sophisticated example can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Link_grammar" title="Link grammar">link grammar</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>.</p>

<p>3. An <b>image</b> (C mod R) captures the notion of an observed Configuration, as opposed to one which exists independently from any perceptual apparatus. Images are configurations distinguished only by their external bonds, inheriting the configuration’s composition and similarities transformations. Formally, images are equivalence classes partitioned by an Identification Rule "~" with 3 properties:</p>
<ol>
<li>ext(c) = ext(c') whenever c~c'</li>
<li>sc~sc' whenever c~c'</li>
<li>sigma(c1,c2) ~ sigma(c1',c2') whenever c1~c1', c2~c2' are all regular.</li>
</ol>
<p>A configuration corresponding to a physical stimulus may have many images, corresponding to many observer perception's identification rule.</p>
<p>4. A <b>pattern</b> is the repeatable components of an image, defined as the S-invariant subset of an image. Similarities are reference transformations we use to define patterns, e.g. rigid body transformations. At first glance, this definition seems suited for only texture patterns where the minimal sub-image is repeated over and over again. If we were to view an image of an object such as a dog, it is not repeated, yet seem like it seems familiar and should be a pattern. (Help needed here).</p>
<p>5. A <b>deformation</b> is a transformation of the original image that accounts for the noise in the environment and error in the perceptual apparatus. Grenander identifies 4 types of deformations: noise and blur, multi-scale superposition, domain warping, and interruptions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grenander’s Pattern Theory treatment of <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_inference" title="Bayesian inference">Bayesian inference</a> in seems to be skewed towards on image reconstruction (e.g. content addressable memory). That is given image I-deformed, find I. However, Mumford’s interpretation of Pattern Theory is broader and he defines PT to include many more well-known statistical methods. Mumford’s criteria for inclusion of a topic as Pattern Theory are those methods "characterized by common techniques and motivations", such as the <a href="/wiki/Hidden_Markov_model" title="Hidden Markov model">HMM</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expectation_Maximization" title="Expectation Maximization">EM algorithm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_programming" title="Dynamic programming">dynamic programming</a> circle of ideas. Topics in this section will reflect Mumford's treatment of Pattern Theory. His principle of statistical Pattern Theory are the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use real world signals rather than constructed ones to infer the hidden states of interest.</li>
<li>Such signals contain too much complexity and artifacts to succumb to a purely deterministic analysis, so employ stochastic methods too.</li>
<li>Respect the natural structure of the signal, including any symmetries, independence of parts, marginals on key statistics. Validate by sampling from the derived models by and infer hidden states with Bayes’ rule.</li>
<li>Across all modalities, a limited family of deformations distort the <i>pure patterns</i> into real world signals.</li>
<li>Stochastic factors affecting an observation show strong conditional independence.</li>
</ul>
<p>Statistical PT makes ubiquitous use of conditional probability in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayes_theorem" title="Bayes theorem">Bayes theorem</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidden_Markov_models" title="Hidden Markov models">Markov</a> Models. Both these concepts are used to express the relation between hidden states (configurations) and observed states (images). Markov Models also captures the local properties of the stimulus, reminiscent of the purpose of bond table for regularity.</p>
<p>The generic set up is the following: Let <i>s</i> = the hidden state of the data that we wish to know. <i>i</i> = observed image. Bayes theorem gives</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>p</i> (<i>s</i> | <i>i</i> ) <i>p</i>(<i>i</i>) = <i>p</i> (<i>s</i>, <i>i</i> ) = <i>p</i> (<i>i</i>|<i>s</i> ) <i>p</i>(<i>s</i>)</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>To analyze the signal (recognition): fix i, maximize p, infer s.</dd>
<dd>To synthesize signals (sampling): fix s, generate i's, compare w/ real world images</dd>
</dl>
<p>The following conditional probability examples illustrates these methods in action:</p>

<p>MAP ~ MDL (MDL offers a glimpse of why the MAP probabilistic formulation make sense analytically)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditional_probability_for_hidden-observed_states">Conditional probability for hidden-observed states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conditional probability for hidden-observed states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bayes_Theorem_for_Machine_translation">Bayes Theorem for Machine translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bayes Theorem for Machine translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Supposing we want to translate French sentences to English. Here, the hidden configurations are English sentences and the observed signal they generate are French sentences. Bayes theorem gives <i>p</i>(<i>e</i>|<i>f</i>)<i>p</i>(<i>f</i>) = <i>p</i>(<i>e</i>, <i>f</i>) = <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>)<i>p</i>(<i>e</i>) and reduces to the fundamental equation of machine translation: maximize <i>p</i>(<i>e</i>|<i>f</i>) = <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>)<i>p</i>(<i>e</i>) over the appropriate <i>e</i> (note that <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>) is independent of <i>e</i>, and so drops out when we maximize over <i>e</i>). This reduces the problem to three main calculations for:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>p</i>(<i>e</i>) for any given <i>e</i>, using the <i>N</i>-gram method and dynamic programming</li>
<li><i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>) for any given <i>e</i> and <i>f</i>, using alignments and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EM_algorithm" title="EM algorithm">expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm</a></li>
<li><i>e</i> that maximizes the product of 1 and 2, again, using dynamic programming</li>
</ol>
<p>The analysis seems to be symmetric with respect to the two languages, and if we think can calculate <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>), why not turn the analysis around and calculate <i>p</i>(<i>e</i>|<i>f</i>) directly? The reason is that during the calculation of <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>) the asymmetric assumption is made that source sentence be well formed and we cannot make any such assumption about the target translation because we do not know what it will translate into.</p>
<p>We now focus on <i>p</i>(<i>f</i>|<i>e</i>) in the three-part decomposition above. The other two parts, <i>p</i>(<i>e</i>) and maximizing <i>e</i>, uses similar techniques as the <i>N</i>-gram model. Given a French-English translation from a large training data set (such data sets exists from the Canadian parliament),</p>
<pre>
       NULL   And    the    program      has    been    implemented
                     Le     programme    a ete     mis en application
</pre>
<p>the sentence pair can be encoded as an <i>alignment</i> (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 6, 6) that reads as follows: the first word in French comes from the second English word, the second word in French comes from the 3rd English word, and so forth. <p>To illustrate an EM algorithm, first set the desired parameter uniformly, that is</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>t</i>(<i>x</i> | <i>b</i> ) = <i>t</i>(<i>y</i> | <i>b</i> ) = <i>t</i>(<i>x</i> | <i>c</i> ) = <i>t</i>(<i>y</i> | <i>c</i> ) = <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span></dd>
</dl>
<p>Then EM iterates as follows</p>
<div class="center">
<div class="thumb tnone">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:812px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Em_iterations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="810" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/10/Em_iterations.jpg" width="810"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Iterations of an EM algorithm</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The alignment probability for the “crossing alignment” (where <i>b</i> connects to <i>y</i>) got a boost from the second sentence pair <i>b</i>/<i>y</i>. That further solidified <i>t</i>(<i>y</i> | <i>b</i>), but as a side effect also boosted <i>t</i>(<i>x</i> | <i>c</i>), because <i>x</i> connects to <i>c</i> in that same “crossing alignment.” The effect of boosting <i>t</i>(<i>x</i> | <i>c</i>) necessarily means downgrading <i>t</i>(<i>y</i> | <i>c</i>) because they sum to one. So, even though <i>y</i> and <i>c</i> co-occur, analysis reveals that they are not translations of each other. With real data, EM also is subject to the usual local extremum traps.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="HMMs_for_speech_recognition">HMMs for speech recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: HMMs for speech recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ski_recording.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="286" data-file-width="679" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4b/Ski_recording.jpg/400px-Ski_recording.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4b/Ski_recording.jpg/600px-Ski_recording.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4b/Ski_recording.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ski_recording.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vibrational breakdown of "ski"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For decades, speech recognition seemed to hit an impasse as scientists sought descriptive and analytic solution. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound wave</a> p(t) below is produced by speaking the word “ski”.</p>
<p>Its four distinct segments has very different characteristics. One can choose from many levels of generators (hidden variables): the intention of the speaker’s brain, the state of the mouth and vocal cords, or the ‘phones’ themselves. Phones are the generator of choice to be inferred and it encodes the word in a noisy, highly variable way. Early work on speech recognition attempted to make this inference deterministically using logical rules based on binary features extracted from p(t). For instance, the table below shows some of the features used to distinguish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_consonants" title="English consonants">English consonants</a>.</p>
<p>In real situations, the signal is further complicated by background noises such as cars driving by or artifacts such as a cough in mid sentence (mumford’s 2nd underpinning). The deterministic rule-based approach failed and the state of the art (e.g. Dragon Naturally Speaking) is to use a family of precisely tuned HMMs and a Bayesian MAP estimators to do better. Similar stories played out in vision, and other stimulus categories.</p>
