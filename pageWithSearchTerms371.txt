<p><b>Computational finance</b> is a branch of applied <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> that deals with problems of practical interest in <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seydel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seydel-1">[1]</a></sup> Some slightly different definitions are the study of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> currently used in finance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFRL_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CFRL-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer programs</a> that realize financial <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">models</a> or <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Computational finance emphasizes practical <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical methods</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">mathematical proofs</a> and focuses on techniques that apply directly to <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic analyses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miranda_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miranda-4">[4]</a></sup> It is an interdisciplinary field between <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_finance" title="Mathematical finance">mathematical finance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical methods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ugur_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ugur-5">[5]</a></sup> Two major areas are efficient and accurate computation of <a href="/wiki/Fair_value" title="Fair value">fair values</a> of <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">financial securities</a> and the modeling of <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">stochastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">price series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duan-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computational_finance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The birth of computational finance as a discipline can be traced to <a href="/wiki/Harry_Markowitz" title="Harry Markowitz">Harry Markowitz</a> in the early 1950s. Markowitz conceived of the portfolio selection problem as an exercise in mean-variance optimization. This required more computer power than was available at the time, so he worked on useful algorithms for approximate solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Markowitz_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Markowitz-7">[7]</a></sup> Mathematical finance began with the same insight, but diverged by making simplifying assumptions to express relations in simple <a href="/wiki/Closed-form_expression" title="Closed-form expression">closed forms</a> that did not require sophisticated computer science to evaluate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, hedge fund managers such as <a href="/wiki/Edward_O._Thorp" title="Edward O. Thorp">Ed Thorp</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poundstone_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Poundstone-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Goodkin" title="Michael Goodkin">Michael Goodkin</a> (working with Harry Markowitz, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Samuelson" title="Paul Samuelson">Paul Samuelson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_C._Merton" title="Robert C. Merton">Robert C. Merton</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodkin_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodkin-10">[10]</a></sup> pioneered the use of computers in <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">arbitrage</a> trading. In academics, sophisticated computer processing was needed by researchers such as <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Fama" title="Eugene Fama">Eugene Fama</a> in order to analyze large amounts of financial data in support of the <a href="/wiki/Efficient-market_hypothesis" title="Efficient-market hypothesis">efficient-market hypothesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1970s, the main focus of computational finance shifted to <a href="/wiki/Valuation_of_options" title="Valuation of options">options pricing</a> and analyzing <a href="/wiki/Mortgage_loan" title="Mortgage loan">mortgage</a> <a href="/wiki/Securitization" title="Securitization">securitizations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">[11]</a></sup> In the late 1970s and early 1980s, a group of young <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_analyst" title="Quantitative analyst">quantitative practitioners</a> who became known as “rocket scientists” arrived on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> and brought along <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a>. This led to an explosion of both the amount and variety of computational finance applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehlers_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehlers-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of the new techniques came from <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">signal processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">speech recognition</a> rather than traditional fields of computational economics like <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_optimization" title="Mathematical optimization">optimization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">time series</a> analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehlers_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ehlers-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of the 1980s, the winding down of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> brought a large group of displaced <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">applied mathematicians</a>, many from behind the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>, into finance. These people become known as “<a href="/wiki/Financial_engineering" title="Financial engineering">financial engineers</a>” (“quant” is a term that includes both rocket scientists and financial engineers, as well as quantitative portfolio managers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown1-13">[13]</a></sup> This led to a second major extension of the range of computational methods used in finance, also a move away from personal computers to <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-11">[11]</a></sup> Around this time computational finance became recognized as a distinct academic subfield. The first degree program in computational finance was offered by Carnegie Mellon University in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CM_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CM-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the last 20 years, the field of computational finance has expanded into virtually every area of finance, and the demand for practitioners has grown dramatically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seydel_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seydel-1">[1]</a></sup> Moreover, many specialized companies have grown up to supply computational finance software and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodkin_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodkin-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
