<p>In <a href="/wiki/Software_project_management" title="Software project management">software project management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">software testing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>, <b>verification and validation</b> (<b>V&amp;V</b>) is the process of checking that a software system meets specifications and that it fulfills its intended purpose. It may also be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Software_quality_control" title="Software quality control">software quality control</a>. It is normally the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">software testers</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development lifecycle</a>. In simple terms, software verification is: "Assuming we should build X, does our software actually achieve its goals without any bugs or gaps?" On the other hand, software validation is: "Was X actually what we should have built? Does X actually meet the high level requirements?"</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Validation checks that the product design satisfies or fits the intended use (high-level checking), i.e., the software meets the user requirements. This is done through <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_testing" title="Dynamic testing">dynamic testing</a> and other forms of review.</p>
<p>Verification and validation are not the same thing, although they are often confused. <a href="/wiki/Barry_Boehm" title="Barry Boehm">Boehm</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boehm1989_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boehm1989-1">[1]</a></sup> succinctly expressed the difference between</p>
<ul>
<li>Verification: Are we building the product right?</li>
<li>Validation: Are we building the right product?</li>
</ul>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model" title="Capability Maturity Model">Capability Maturity Model</a> (CMMI-SW v1.1),</p>
<ul>
<li>Software Verification: The process of evaluating software to determine whether the products of a given development phase satisfy the conditions imposed at the start of that phase. [IEEE-STD-610]</li>
<li>Software Validation: The process of evaluating software during or at the end of the development process to determine whether it satisfies specified requirements. [IEEE-STD-610]</li>
</ul>
<p>In other words, software verification is ensuring that the product has been built according to the requirements and design specifications, while software validation ensures that the product actually meets the user's needs, and that the specifications were correct in the first place. Software verification ensures that "you built it right". Software validation ensures that "you built the right thing". Software validation confirms that the product, as provided, will fulfill its intended use.</p>
<p>From testing perspective:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fault – wrong or missing function in the code.</li>
<li>Failure – the manifestation of a fault during execution.</li>
<li>Malfunction – according to its specification the system does not meet its specified functionality.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both verification and validation are related to the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Software_quality_assurance" title="Software quality assurance">software quality assurance</a>. By themselves, verification and validation do not guarantee software quality; planning, <a href="/wiki/Traceability" title="Traceability">traceability</a>, configuration management and other aspects of software engineering are required.</p>
<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Modeling_and_simulation" title="Modeling and simulation">modeling and simulation</a> (M&amp;S) community, the definitions of verification, validation and accreditation are similar:</p>
<ul>
<li>M&amp;S Verification is the process of determining that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">computer model</a>, simulation, or federation of models and simulations implementations and their associated data accurately represent the developer's conceptual description and specifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Missile_Defense_Agency_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Missile_Defense_Agency-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>M&amp;S Validation is the process of determining the degree to which a model, simulation, or federation of models and simulations, and their associated data are accurate representations of the real world from the perspective of the intended use(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Missile_Defense_Agency_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Missile_Defense_Agency-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Accreditation" title="Accreditation">Accreditation</a> is the formal certification that a model or simulation is acceptable to be used for a specific purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Missile_Defense_Agency_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Missile_Defense_Agency-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The definition of M&amp;S validation focuses on the accuracy with which the M&amp;S represents the real-world intended use(s). Determining the degree of M&amp;S accuracy is required because all M&amp;S are approximations of reality, and it is usually critical to determine if the degree of approximation is acceptable for the intended use(s). This stands in contrast to software validation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_methods">Classification of methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification of methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission-critical" title="Mission-critical">mission-critical</a> software systems, where flawless performance is absolutely necessary, <a href="/wiki/Formal_methods" title="Formal methods">formal methods</a> may be used to ensure the correct operation of a system. However, often for non-mission-critical software systems, formal methods prove to be very costly<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and an alternative method of software V&amp;V must be sought out. In such cases, <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_methods" title="Syntactic methods">syntactic methods</a> are often used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_cases">Test cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Test cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Test_case" title="Test case">Test case</a></div>
<p>A test case is a tool used in the process. Test cases may be prepared for software verification and software validation to determine if the product was built according to the requirements of the user. Other methods, such as reviews, may be used early in the life cycle to provide for software validation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_Verification_and_Validation">Independent Verification and Validation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Independent Verification and Validation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Software verification and validation often is carried out by a separate group from the development team. In such cases, the process is called "independent verification and validation", or simply IV&amp;V.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_environment">Regulatory environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_verification_and_validation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regulatory environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Verification and validation must meet the compliance requirements of law regulated industries, which is often guided by government agencies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpsv_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gpsv-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dgi21_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dgi21-4">[4]</a></sup> or industrial administrative authorities. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a> requires software versions and <a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">patches</a> to be validated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gicn_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gicn-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
