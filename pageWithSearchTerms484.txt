<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Transition design</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Transition design</b> proposes <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> led societal transition towards more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> futures. It applies an understanding of the interconnectedness of social, economic, political and natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systems" title="Systems">systems</a> to address problems that exist at all levels of scale in ways that improve <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. Such complex and interconnected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked_problems" title="Wicked problems">wicked problems</a> can include <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">economic inequality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> loss, decline of <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</p>
<p>Transition design leverages the power of interdependency and <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a> with the aim of transforming entire lifestyles, making these more convivial and participatory, and harmonising them with the natural environment. It emphasizes the temporal element of all such design solutionsâ€”how they relate to the traditional and <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> cultures of the past, and how they might unfold, develop and connect over short, medium and long horizons of time.</p>
<p>Transition design aims to intervene in social, economic, political and technological systems so as to assist people to satisfy their needs in ways that establish mutually beneficial relationships between people, the natural environment and the built and designed world. This process is informed in particular by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_systems_theory" title="Living systems theory">living systems theory</a>, employing such concepts as <a href="/wiki/Self-organization" title="Self-organization">self-organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interdependence" title="Interdependence">interdependence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holarchy" title="Holarchy">holarchy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>. It involves changing the ways in which people earn their livelihood, and changing the organization of business, manufacturing, agriculture, finance, healthcare, education and travel. Transition design aims to cultivate lifestyles and forms of <a href="/wiki/Everyday_life" title="Everyday life">everyday life</a> in which fundamental needs can be satisfied in integrated, place-based ways, encouraging symbiotic relationships between communities, and between communities and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_designers">Transition designers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transition designers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transition designers can come from all walks-of-life and backgrounds, regardless of whether they are formally trained designers. They use the tools and processes of <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> to re-conceive entire lifestyles, rather than focusing on problems within existing, mostly unsustainable, social, economic and political paradigms. Designers assume a similar role in transition design as they do in <a href="/wiki/Service_design" title="Service design">service design</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_design" title="Social design">social design</a>: the designer is a facilitator of emergent solutions to problems rather than an expert who conceives and delivers blueprints and finished solutions. Transition designers are especially focussed on potentialities for beneficial change that exist within systems, and on making modest interventions which can ramify throughout entire systems.</p>
<p>Because complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked_problems" title="Wicked problems">wicked problems</a> are multi-faceted, their solutions require not only the skills and knowledge of designers, but also knowledge from the spectrum of disciplinary fields (science, philosophy, psychology, social science, anthropology and the humanities etc.). Transition design is therefore a collaborative process that is informed by knowledge from outside design. It emphasizes the need for <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinarity" title="Transdisciplinarity">transdisciplinarity</a> and for the reintegration of knowledge.</p>
<p>Transition design solutions, however, should not simply represent the collaborative efforts of groups of experts and specialists; since solutions must be place-specific and ecosystem appropriate, they must incorporate local knowledge, participation and commitment. Just as transition design solutions need not only represent the reintegration of knowledge, they should also represent its recontextualization. Just as engineering has been used as a metaphor to describe the large scale, top-down and managerial influence of social phenomena (designer, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planner" title="Urban planner">urban planner</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technocrat" title="Technocrat">technocrat</a> and policy maker as <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_(political_science)" title="Social engineering (political science)">social engineers</a>) so the transition designer might be compared to a gardener. Like a good gardener, the transition designer has an intimate understanding of and feeling for a particular place and its ecosystem, of the relationships between its different parts, of what its particular needs are, of what will and will not flourish, and of how it might grow and develop over long periods of time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_approaches">Comparison with other approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison with other approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In designing for the relationship between people, nature, and the things that people make and do; in designing for short, mid and long horizons of time and in acknowledgement of vernacular, historical and indigenous cultures; and in designing place/ecosystem specific solutions, transition design can be seen as a logical development of a trend in design over recent decades that emphasizes relationship and context. Within mainstream design this trend has included <a href="/wiki/Service_design" title="Service design">service design</a> (or design for service), <a href="/wiki/Social_design" title="Social design">social design</a> (or design for social innovation) and <a href="/wiki/Interaction_design" title="Interaction design">interaction design</a>, whilst outside of mainstream design this trend has included <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_design" title="Ecological design">ecological design</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_School_of_Design" title="Carnegie Mellon School of Design">School of Design at Carnegie Mellon University (CMU)</a>, transition design is taught alongside design for service and design for social Innovation, in the expectation that designers will be able to move freely between these three areas of practice and research, according to the scope of the problem that they hope to address. Thus design for service represents moderate change within existing paradigms and systems; design for social innovation represents significant change within emerging paradigms and systems; and transition design represents radical change within future paradigms and systems.</p>
<p>These areas of design practise and research can be located on a continuum, and the movement along this continuum (from design for service, through design for social innovation to transition design) represents an expansion of the scale of time, depth of engagement, and context to include social, political, economic and environmental concerns. Transition design's foregrounding of relationship, context and wholeness represents the incorporation into design of an ecological or holistic worldview and gives it several defining features:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_systems_theory" title="Living systems theory">Living systems theory</a> is used as both an approach to understanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked_problems" title="Wicked problems">wicked problems</a> and designing solutions to address them.</li>
<li>Designs solutions that protect and restore both social and natural ecosystems through the creation of mutually beneficial relationships between people, the things they do and make and the natural environment.</li>
<li>Sees everyday life and lifestyles as the most important/fundamental context for design.</li>
<li>Advocates place and ecosystem based, but globally networked solutions; seeks solutions that connect place to planet.</li>
<li>Designs solutions for short, medium and long horizons of time and all levels of scale of everyday life.</li>
<li>Looks for emergent possibilities within problem contexts and amplifies grassroots solutions already underway.</li>
<li>Links existing solutions together so that they can function as steps in a larger transition vision.</li>
<li>Distinguishes between 'wants/desires' and genuine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Needs" title="Needs">needs</a> and bases solutions on maximizing and integrating satisfiers for the widest range of needs.</li>
<li>Sees the designer's own mindset and posture as an essential component of transition designing.</li>
<li>Calls for the reintegration and recontextualization of diverse transdisciplinary knowledge.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Framework">Framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Transition design framework (Irwin, Tonkinwise &amp; Kossoff, 2013)</div>

<p>Terry Irwin, Head of the School of Design at Carnegie Mellon University, with Cameron Tonkinwise, Director of Design Studies and Chair of the doctoral program at Carnegie Mellon University, and Gideon Kossoff, Adjunct Professor in the School of Design at Carnegie Mellon University, have together developed a framework which organizes transition design into four mutually influencing areas: Vision for Transition; Theories of Change; Posture/Mindset and New Ways of Designing. This framework was first disseminated at a talk given by Irwin, Kossoff, and Tonkinwise at 'Head, Heart, Hand', the 2013 national <a href="/wiki/AIGA" title="AIGA">AIGA</a> design conference in Minneapolis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_term_.27transition.27">Use of the term 'transition'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use of the term 'transition'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transition design acknowledges that we are living in 'transitional times'. Its central premise is the need for societal transitions to more sustainable futures and the belief that design has a key role to play in these transitions. The use of the word 'transition' is suggested and reinforced by several contemporary discourses, projects and networks. In various ways these initiatives explore how change/transition manifests and can be initiated and directed, or are actively engaged in trying to bring about transition to more sustainable futures. Such initiatives have influenced transition design in several ways, and include:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_town_network">Transition town network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transition town network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transition_town" title="Transition town">Transition towns</a>, an international network of communities who are working to develop local <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(organizational)" title="Resilience (organizational)">resilience</a>, and expand their capacity to respond to and 'bounce back' from external disruptionsâ€”crises such as an interruption to energy supplies, economic downturns or climate change. Transition Towns are, for example, developing locally based food and energy systems and local currencies and businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> By corollary, transition design involves a type of social engagement and community organizing that situates projects and initiatives within the context of long-term visions for specific places and ecosystems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_management">Transition management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transition management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transition_management_(governance)" title="Transition management (governance)">Socio-technical transition management</a>, a body of theory which represents the convergence of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> research, technology forecasting, social ecological impact analysis and the fields of social history and construction of technology. It studies the <a href="/wiki/Coevolution" title="Coevolution">coevolution</a> of technologies and their uses in order to conceive how innovations can be introduced into society to enable new ways of living and working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Recently it has incorporated <a href="/wiki/Social_practice_theory" title="Social practice theory">social practice theory</a> and is beginning explicit use of ideas about designing. By corollary, transition design should involve a deep understanding of the social history of technology, and a post-planning approach to how the introductions of new technologies impact society and vice versa.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Great_Transition">The Great Transition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Great Transition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Transition" title="Great Transition">Great Transition</a>, was a term first used in 1964 by the economist and systems theorist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Boulding" title="Kenneth Boulding">Kenneth Boulding</a>. In 1995 the <a href="/wiki/Global_Scenario_Group" title="Global Scenario Group">Global Scenario Group</a> began to produce a series of reports identifying multiple future-based planetary scenarios and various strategies for change that could lead to the 'Great Transition' (improved quality of life, reduced poverty and inequity, human solidarity, enriched cultures and protection of the biosphere).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In recent years the <a href="/wiki/Tellus_Institute" title="Tellus Institute">Tellus Institute</a> has launched the <a href="/wiki/Great_Transition" title="Great Transition">Great Transition Initiative</a> (GTI), an international network of more than 40 scholars and activists who seek to develop and mobilize a planet-wide citizens transition movement. The concept of the 'Great Transition' has also been adopted by several leading think tanks, such as the <a href="/wiki/New_Economics_Foundation" title="New Economics Foundation">New Economics Foundation</a>. By corollary, transition design future based scenarios and the means and tools by which local communities can transition towards these within a planetary context.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_in_complex_and_living_systems">Transition in complex and living systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transition in complex and living systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of 'transition' in transition design alludes to the dynamic nature of complex, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a>, self-organizing and interdependent systems such as ecosystems and organisms. The '<a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>' that occurs as such systems undergo stresses or 'perturbations' from their environment, can lead them to display unexpected, unpredictable and 'emergent' new forms of behaviour and structure. By corollary, transition design incorporates living systems theory/<a href="/wiki/Ecological_literacy" title="Ecological literacy">ecoliteracy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in order to prepare designers for work within complex social systems, and to understand how these are embedded in natural systems. Transition design leverages the potential for self-organization, interdependence and emergence within such non-linear systems, and anticipates the possibility of rapid systemic change that can be triggered by modest interventions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmopolitan_localism">Cosmopolitan localism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cosmopolitan localism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transition design focuses on the need for <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitanism" title="Cosmopolitanism">cosmopolitan</a> <a href="/wiki/Localism_(politics)" title="Localism (politics)">localism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> a place-based lifestyle in which solutions to global problems are designed for local circumstances and tailored to specific social and ecological contexts. Its objective is to foster a global network of mutually supportive communities (neighbourhoods, villages, towns, cities and regions) who share and exchange knowledge, ideas, skills, technology, culture and (where socially and ecologically sustainable) resources. Cosmopolitan localism fosters a creative, reciprocal relationship between the local and the global. It addresses the problem of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globalisation" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a>, which tends to subsume local cultures and economies into homogenised and unsustainable global systems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> whilst avoiding the pitfalls of localisation, such as <a href="/wiki/Parochialism" title="Parochialism">parochialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isolationism" title="Isolationism">isolationism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_development">Initial development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Initial development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of transition design was first developed by Gideon Kossoff in a chapter called 'Holism and the Reconstitution of Everyday Life: A Framework for the Transition to a Sustainable Society' in the book, <i>Grow Small, Think Beautiful: Ideas for a Sustainable World from <a href="/wiki/Schumacher_College" title="Schumacher College">Schumacher College</a></i>, edited by Stephan Harding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This chapter was a summary of Kossoff's PhD thesis, also entitled <i>Holism and the Reconstitution of Everyday Life</i> (2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The term 'transition design' has since been adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_School_of_Design" title="Carnegie Mellon School of Design">Carnegie Mellon School of Design</a> and incorporated into its curriculum as one of three areas within which design is taught and researched at the undergraduate, graduate and doctoral levels (Design for Service, Design for Social Innovation and Transition Design).</p>
<p>In his PhD thesis, <i>Holism and the Reconstitution of Everyday Life</i>, Gideon Kossoff used the term transition design to describe the process of using design thinking and process to assist the transition to a sustainable society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This early framing of transition design integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic_science" title="Holistic science">holistic science</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systems" title="Systems">systems</a> theories,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Goethean_science" title="Goethean science">Goethean science</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> with a tradition of non-authoritarian social and political philosophy that includes figures such as <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">Martin Buber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jane_Jacobs" title="Jane Jacobs">Jane Jacobs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> These figures can be called 'radical holists' since they use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organicist" title="Organicist">organicist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">holistic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> principles to underpin their advocacy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decentralized" title="Decentralized">decentralized</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mutualism_(movement)" title="Mutualism (movement)">mutualistic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Participatory" title="Participatory">participatory</a> social, political and economic structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> This 'radical holist' approach has been validated by holistic science which defines wholes in terms of dynamic and creative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-organisation" title="Self-organisation">self-organisation</a>, mutualistic interdependencies, and a reciprocal relationship between wholes and the parts of which they are constituted. These principles are at the heart of an emerging ecological or holistic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldview" title="Worldview">worldview</a> that has been articulated by <a href="/wiki/Fritjof_Capra" title="Fritjof Capra">Fritjof Capra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Tarnas" title="Richard Tarnas">Richard Tarnas</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Abram" title="David Abram">David Abram</a> and many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Transition design can be thought of as a way of using this ecological worldview to address many contemporary problems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_everyday_life">In everyday life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In everyday life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transition design, in its initial development, focused on everyday life, since this is the context within which ecological, social and economic problems arise, and this is the arena within which such problems must be resolved. It therefore drew on various critiques of everyday life such as those made by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre" title="Henri Lefebvre">Henri Lefebvre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Debord" title="Guy Debord">Guy Debord</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakhtin" title="Mikhail Bakhtin">Mikhail Bakhtin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>This framing of transition design proposes that in order to be sustainable, everyday life will need to be organised according to holistic principles. Everyday life arises as people strive to satisfy their material and non-material <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Needs" title="Needs">needs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It is more likely to be sustainable when communities control the satisfaction of their needs at all levels of scaleâ€”households, neighbourhoods, villages, cities, regionsâ€”'The Domains of Everyday Life'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In traditional, pre-industrial communities, the Domains emerged as people endeavoured to satisfy their needs. Embodying the social processes of such cultures, the Domains were to varying degrees, self-organising, participatory, semi-autonomous and mutualistic, and everyday life as a whole consisted of nested networks of households, villages, neighbourhoods and regions. These characteristics are shared with living, whole and ecological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> With the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modernity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, control of need satisfaction was largely appropriated by centralized institutions. When this happens, the Domains lose their role as loci for the satisfaction of the needs of their inhabitants, and they no longer emerge as vital, integrated, semi-autonomous forms: households, neighbourhoods, cities and regions are typically hollowed and fragmented. Their decline leads to many social, ecological, economic and political problems and to the unsustainability of everyday life. There is therefore a direct relationship between sustainability, community and place-based control of need satisfaction and holism in everyday life: the transition to a sustainable society requires the reconstitution and reinvention of the Domains of Everyday Life. An additional Domain, that of the Planet, has emerged in modernity and its development could give everyday life a cosmopolitanism it lacked in pre-industrial societies.</p>
<p>Any place-based initiative that promotes holism in everyday life, protecting and recovering control by communities of the process of need satisfaction, will help the transition process. It is the task of transition design to facilitate this at all levels of scale, revitalising and improving the quality of everyday life through the development of nested, networked, integrated, semi-autonomous and self-organizing households, neighbourhoods, cities and regions.</p>
<p>Aside this background, the <a class="external text" href="http://transformap.co/" rel="nofollow">TransforMap</a> initiative emerged with the objective to visualise socio-technical transition initiatives that occur in the domain of everyday life on a geographical map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transition_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transition design was first introduced in 2014 at Carnegie Mellon University's School of Design where it informs projects, curricula and programs at the undergraduate, graduate and doctoral levels. On March 7, 2015 a Transition Design Symposium was held at CMU's School of Design. This was structured around the position papers of twenty academics from a wide range of disciplines (e.g. anthropology, sociology, architecture, psychology, human computer interaction, philosophy, design and design studies). Each position paper was a response to a 'Provocation and Briefing' document written by Professor Terry Irwin, Dr. Cameron Tonkinwise and Dr. Gideon Kossoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
