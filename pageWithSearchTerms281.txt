<p><b>Tom DeMarco</b> (born August 20, 1940) is an American <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, author, and consultant on <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> topics. He was an early developer of <a href="/wiki/Structured_analysis" title="Structured analysis">structured analysis</a> in the 1970s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_DeMarco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tom DeMarco was born in <a href="/wiki/Hazleton,_Pennsylvania" title="Hazleton, Pennsylvania">Hazleton, Pennsylvania</a>. He received a BSEE degree in Electrical Engineering from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, a M.S. from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Diploma" title="Diploma">diplôme</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> at the Sorbonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtlanticSystemsBio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AtlanticSystemsBio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_DeMarco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DeMarco started working at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Telephone_Laboratories" title="Bell Telephone Laboratories">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a> in 1963, where he participated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESS-1" title="ESS-1">ESS-1</a> project to develop the first large scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_Switching_System" title="Electronic Switching System">Electronic Switching System</a>, which became installed in telephone offices all over the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeM02_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeM02-2">[2]</a></sup> Later in the 1960s he started working for a French IT consulting firm, where he worked on the development of a <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_system" title="Conveyor system">conveyor system</a> for the new merchandise mart at La Villette in Paris, and in the 1970s on the development of on-line banking systems in Sweden, Holland, France and New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISRC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISRC-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s DeMarco was one of the major figures in the development of structured analysis and structured design in software engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward1995_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ward1995-4">[4]</a></sup> In January 1978 he published <i>Structured Analysis and System Specification</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeMarco1978_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeMarco1978-5">[5]</a></sup> a major milestone in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward1995_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ward1995-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Lister" title="Tim Lister">Tim Lister</a>, Stephen McMenamin, John F. Palmer, James Robertson and Suzanne Robertson, he founded the consulting firm "The Atlantic Systems Guild" in New York. The firm initially shared offices with the Dorset House publisher <a href="/wiki/Edward_Yourdon" title="Edward Yourdon">Edward Yourdon</a>. Their company developed into a New York- and London-based consulting company specializing in methods and management of software development.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>DeMarco has lectured and consulted throughout the Americas, Europe, Africa, Australia and the Far East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DorsetHouseBio_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-DorsetHouseBio-6">[6]</a></sup>Additionally he was technical advisor at <a href="/wiki/ZeniMax_Media" title="ZeniMax Media">ZeniMax Media</a> Parent company of video game publisher <a href="/wiki/Bethesda_Softworks" title="Bethesda Softworks">Bethesda Softworks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is a member of the ACM and a Fellow of the IEEE. He lives in <a href="/wiki/Camden,_Maine" title="Camden, Maine">Camden, Maine</a>, and is<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> a principal of the Atlantic Systems Guild, and a fellow and Senior Consultant of the <a href="/wiki/Cutter_Consortium" title="Cutter Consortium">Cutter Consortium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtlanticSystemsBio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AtlanticSystemsBio-1">[1]</a></sup> DeMarco was the 1986 recipient of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Warnier_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Warnier Prize (page does not exist)">Warnier Prize</a> for "lifetime contribution to the field of computing", and the 1999 recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Award" title="Stevens Award">Stevens Award</a> for "contribution to the methods of software development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtlanticSystemsBio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AtlanticSystemsBio-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_DeMarco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his spare time, DeMarco is an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_technician" title="Emergency medical technician">emergency medical technician</a>, certified by his home state and by the <a href="/wiki/National_Registry_of_Emergency_Medical_Technicians" title="National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians">National Registry of EMTs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DorsetHouseBio_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-DorsetHouseBio-6">[6]</a></sup> He is also founding member of the Penobscot Compact, operating under the auspices of the Maine State Aspirations Program, in which local employers contribute the paid time of their employees to tutor students in the public schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
