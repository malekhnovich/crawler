<p><b>Project management</b> is the discipline of initiating, planning, executing, controlling, and closing the <a href="/wiki/Work_(project_management)" title="Work (project management)">work</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Project_team" title="Project team">team</a> to achieve specific goals and meet specific success criteria. A <a href="/wiki/Project" title="Project">project</a> is a temporary endeavor designed to produce a unique product, service or result with a defined beginning and end (usually time-constrained, and often constrained by funding or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deliverables" title="Deliverables">deliverables</a>) undertaken to meet unique goals and objectives, typically to bring about beneficial change or added value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The temporary nature of projects stands in contrast with <a href="/wiki/Business_operations" title="Business operations">business as usual (or operations)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which are repetitive, permanent, or semi-permanent functional activities to produce products or services. In practice, the <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> of these two systems is often quite different, and as such requires the development of distinct technical skills and management strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cattani2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cattani2011-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary challenge of project management is to achieve all of the project goals within the given constraints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This information is usually described in a user or project manual, which is created at the beginning of the development process. The primary constraints are <a href="/wiki/Scope_(project_management)" title="Scope (project management)">scope</a>, time, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Quality" title="Quality">quality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">budget</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMI_10_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMI_10-6">[6]</a></sup> The secondary — and more ambitious — challenge is to <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">optimize</a> the <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">allocation</a> of necessary inputs and integrate them to meet pre-defined objectives.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trajan%27s_Column_(Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="586" data-file-width="864" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png/280px-Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png/420px-Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png/560px-Trajan%27s_Column_%28Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress%29.png 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trajan%27s_Column_(Roman_Soldiers_Building_a_Fortress).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Roman soldiers building a fortress, <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan's Column</a> 113 AD</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Until 1900, civil engineering projects were generally managed by creative architects, engineers, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_builder_(occupation)" title="Master builder (occupation)">master builders</a> themselves, for example, <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a> (first century BC), <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a> (1632–1723), <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a> (1757–1834) and <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Isambard Kingdom Brunel</a> (1806–1859).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was in the 1950s that organizations started to systematically apply project management tools and techniques to complex engineering projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YHK05_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-YHK05-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Henri_Gannt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="282" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Henri_Gannt.jpg/170px-Henri_Gannt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Henri_Gannt.jpg/255px-Henri_Gannt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Henri_Gannt.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Henri_Gannt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Henry_Gantt" title="Henry Gantt">Henry Gantt</a> (1861–1919), the father of planning and control techniques</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a discipline, project management developed from several fields of application including civil construction, engineering, and heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_(military)" title="Defense (military)">defense</a> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Two forefathers of project management are <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gantt" title="Henry Gantt">Henry Gantt</a>, called the father of planning and control techniques,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> who is famous for his use of the <a href="/wiki/Gantt_chart" title="Gantt chart">Gantt chart</a> as a project management tool (alternatively <i>Harmonogram</i> first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Karol_Adamiecki" title="Karol Adamiecki">Karol Adamiecki</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>); and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Fayol" title="Henri Fayol">Henri Fayol</a> for his creation of the five management functions that form the foundation of the body of knowledge associated with project and program management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Both Gantt and Fayol were students of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Winslow_Taylor" title="Frederick Winslow Taylor">Frederick Winslow Taylor</a>'s theories of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_management" title="Scientific management">scientific management</a>. His work is the forerunner to modern project management tools including <a href="/wiki/Work_breakdown_structure" title="Work breakdown structure">work breakdown structure</a> (WBS) and <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">resource allocation</a>.</p>
<p>The 1950s marked the beginning of the modern project management era where core engineering fields come together to work as one. Project management became recognized as a distinct discipline arising from the management discipline with engineering model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClGa06_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClGa06-13">[13]</a></sup> In the United States, prior to the 1950s, projects were managed on an ad-hoc basis, using mostly <a href="/wiki/Gantt_chart" title="Gantt chart">Gantt charts</a> and informal techniques and tools. At that time, two mathematical <a href="/wiki/Schedule_(project_management)" title="Schedule (project management)">project-scheduling</a> models were developed. The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_Path_Method" title="Critical Path Method">Critical Path Method</a>" (CPM) was developed as a joint venture between <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Remington_Rand" title="Remington Rand">Remington Rand Corporation</a> for managing plant maintenance projects. And the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Program_Evaluation_and_Review_Technique" title="Program Evaluation and Review Technique">Program Evaluation and Review Technique</a>" or PERT, was developed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Booz_Allen_Hamilton" title="Booz Allen Hamilton">Booz Allen Hamilton</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polaris_missile" title="Polaris missile">Polaris missile</a> submarine program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>PERT and CPM are very similar in their approach but still present some differences. CPM is used for projects that assume deterministic activity times; the times at which each activity will be carried out are known. PERT, on the other hand, allows for stochastic activity times; the times at which each activity will be carried out are uncertain or varied. Because of this core difference, CPM and PERT are used in different contexts. These mathematical techniques quickly spread into many private enterprises.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pert_chart_colored.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="308" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pert_chart_colored.svg/240px-Pert_chart_colored.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pert_chart_colored.svg/360px-Pert_chart_colored.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Pert_chart_colored.svg/480px-Pert_chart_colored.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pert_chart_colored.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PERT" title="PERT">PERT network chart</a> for a seven-month project with five milestones</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the same time, as project-scheduling models were being developed, technology for project cost <a href="/wiki/Estimation_(project_management)" title="Estimation (project management)">estimating</a>, cost management and engineering economics was evolving, with pioneering work by Hans Lang and others. In 1956, the American Association of Cost Engineers (now <a href="/wiki/AACE_International" title="AACE International">AACE International</a>; the Association for the Advancement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_Engineering" title="Cost Engineering">Cost Engineering</a>) was formed by early practitioners of project management and the associated specialties of planning and scheduling, cost estimating, and cost/schedule control (project control). AACE continued its pioneering work and in 2006 released the first integrated process for portfolio, program and project management (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Cost_Management" title="Total Cost Management">Total Cost Management</a> Framework).</p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Project_Management_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Project Management Association (page does not exist)">International Project Management Association</a> (IPMA) was founded in Europe in 1967,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> as a federation of several national project management associations. IPMA maintains its federal structure today and now includes member associations on every continent except Antarctica. IPMA offers a Four Level Certification program based on the IPMA Competence Baseline (ICB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The ICB covers technical, contextual, and behavioral competencies.</p>
<p>In 1969, the <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Institute" title="Project Management Institute">Project Management Institute</a> (PMI) was formed in the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> PMI publishes <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Guide_to_the_Project_Management_Body_of_Knowledge" title="A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge">A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge</a></i> (PMBOK Guide), which describes project management practices that are common to "most projects, most of the time." PMI also offers multiple certifications.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches">Approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of approaches for managing project activities including lean, iterative, incremental, and phased approaches.</p>
<p>Regardless of the methodology employed, careful consideration must be given to the overall project objectives, timeline, and cost, as well as the roles and responsibilities of all participants and <a href="/wiki/Project_stakeholder" title="Project stakeholder">stakeholders</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_traditional_approach">The traditional approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The traditional approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A traditional phased approach identifies a sequence of steps to be completed. In the "traditional approach",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wysocki_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-wysocki-18">[18]</a></sup> five developmental components of a project can be distinguished (four stages plus control):</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Project_Management_(phases).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="276" data-file-width="852" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Project_Management_%28phases%29.png/320px-Project_Management_%28phases%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Project_Management_%28phases%29.png/480px-Project_Management_%28phases%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Project_Management_%28phases%29.png/640px-Project_Management_%28phases%29.png 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Project_Management_(phases).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical development phases of an engineering project</div>
</div>
</div>
<ol>
<li>initiation</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Project_planning" title="Project planning">planning</a> and design</li>
<li>execution and construction</li>
<li>monitoring and controlling systems</li>
<li>completion and finish point</li>
</ol>
<p>Many industries use variations of these project stages. For example, when working on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brick-and-mortar" title="Brick-and-mortar">brick-and-mortar</a> design and construction, projects will typically progress through stages like pre-planning, conceptual design, schematic design, design development, construction drawings (or contract documents), and construction administration. In <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a>, this approach is often known as the <a href="/wiki/Waterfall_model" title="Waterfall model">waterfall model</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy70_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roy70-19">[19]</a></sup> i.e., one series of <a href="/wiki/Task_(project_management)" title="Task (project management)">tasks</a> after another in linear sequence. In software development many organizations have adapted the <a href="/wiki/Rational_Unified_Process" title="Rational Unified Process">Rational Unified Process</a> (RUP) to fit this methodology, although RUP does not require or explicitly recommend this practice. Waterfall development works well for small, well-defined projects, but often fails in larger projects of undefined and ambiguous nature. The <a href="/wiki/Cone_of_Uncertainty" title="Cone of Uncertainty">Cone of Uncertainty</a> explains some of this as the planning made on the initial phase of the project suffers from a high degree of uncertainty. This becomes especially true as software development is often the realization of a new or novel product. In projects where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Requirements" title="Requirements">requirements</a> have not been finalized and can change, <a href="/wiki/Requirements_management" title="Requirements management">requirements management</a> is used to develop an accurate and complete definition of the behavior of software that can serve as the basis for software development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stellman05_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stellman05-20">[20]</a></sup> While the terms may differ from industry to industry, the actual stages typically follow common steps to <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a>—"defining the problem, weighing options, choosing a path, implementation and evaluation."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PRINCE2">PRINCE2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: PRINCE2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PRINCE2" title="PRINCE2">PRINCE2</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prince2_procces_model_.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="410" data-file-width="654" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bc/Prince2_procces_model_.jpg/300px-Prince2_procces_model_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bc/Prince2_procces_model_.jpg/450px-Prince2_procces_model_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bc/Prince2_procces_model_.jpg/600px-Prince2_procces_model_.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prince2_procces_model_.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/PRINCE2" title="PRINCE2">PRINCE2</a> process model</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>PRINCE2 is a structured approach to project management released in 1996 as a generic project management method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It combines the original PROMPT methodology (which evolved into the PRINCE methodology) with IBM's MITP (managing the implementation of the total project) methodology. PRINCE2 provides a method for managing projects within a clearly defined framework.</p>
<p>PRINCE2 focuses on the definition and delivery of products, in particular their quality requirements. As such, it defines a successful project as being output-oriented (not activity- or task-oriented) through creating an agreed set of products<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-managing_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-managing-22">[22]</a></sup> that define the scope of the project and provides the basis for planning and control, that is, how then to coordinate people and activities, how to design and supervise product delivery, and what to do if products and therefore the scope of the project has to be adjusted if it does not develop as planned.</p>
<p>In the method, each process is specified with its key inputs and outputs and with specific goals and activities to be carried out to deliver a project's outcomes as defined by its Business Case. This allows for continuous assessment and adjustment when deviation from the Business Case is required.</p>
<p>PRINCE2 provides a common language for all participants in the project. The governance framework of PRINCE2 – its roles and responsibilities – are fully described and require tailoring to suit the complexity of the project and skills of the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-managing_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-managing-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_chain_project_management">Critical chain project management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical chain project management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Critical_chain_project_management" title="Critical chain project management">Critical chain project management</a></div>
<p>Critical chain project management (CCPM) is a method of planning and managing project execution designed to deal with uncertainties inherent in managing projects, while taking into consideration limited availability of <a href="/wiki/Resource_(project_management)" title="Resource (project management)">resources</a> (physical, human skills, as well as management &amp; support capacity) needed to execute projects.</p>
<p>CCPM is an application of the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_constraints" title="Theory of constraints">theory of constraints</a> (TOC) to projects. The goal is to increase the flow of projects in an organization (<a href="/wiki/Throughput" title="Throughput">throughput</a>). Applying the first three of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Constraints#The_five_focusing_steps" title="Theory of Constraints">five focusing steps</a> of TOC, the system constraint for all projects is identified as are the resources. To exploit the constraint, tasks on the critical chain are given priority over all other activities. Finally, projects are planned and managed to ensure that the resources are ready when the critical chain tasks must start, subordinating all other resources to the critical chain.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Project_plan" title="Project plan">project plan</a> should typically undergo <a href="/wiki/Resource_leveling" title="Resource leveling">resource leveling</a>, and the longest sequence of resource-constrained tasks should be identified as the critical chain. In some cases, such as managing contracted sub-projects, it is advisable to use a simplified approach without resource leveling.</p>
<p>In multi-project environments, resource leveling should be performed across projects. However, it is often enough to identify (or simply select) a single "drum". The drum can be a resource that acts as a constraint across projects, which are staggered based on the availability of that single resource.</p>
<p>One can also use a "virtual drum" by selecting a task or group of tasks (typically <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Integration_points&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Integration points (page does not exist)">integration points</a>) and limiting the number of projects in execution at that stage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process-based_management">Process-based management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Process-based management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Process-based_management" title="Process-based management">Process-based management</a></div>
<p>The incorporation of process-based management has been driven by the use of Maturity models such as the <a href="/wiki/OPM3" title="OPM3">OPM3</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMMI" title="CMMI">CMMI</a> (capability maturity model integration; see <a href="/wiki/File:Capability_Maturity_Model.jpg" title="File:Capability Maturity Model.jpg">this example</a> of a predecessor) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO_15504" title="ISO 15504">ISO/IEC15504</a> (SPICE – software process improvement and capability estimation). Unlike SEI's CMM, the OPM3 maturity model describes how to make project management processes capable of performing successfully, consistently, and predictably in order to enact the strategies of an organization <a class="external text" href="http://www.opmexperts.com/opm3/" rel="nofollow">.</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lean_project_management">Lean project management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lean project management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lean_project_management" title="Lean project management">Lean project management</a></div>
<p>Lean project management uses the principles from <a href="/wiki/Lean_manufacturing" title="Lean manufacturing">lean manufacturing</a> to focus on delivering value with less waste and reduced time.</p>

<p>In critical studies of project management it has been noted that several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PERT" title="PERT">PERT</a> based models are not well suited for the multi-project company environment of today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most of them are aimed at very large-scale, one-time, non-routine projects, and currently all kinds of management are expressed in terms of projects.</p>
<p>Using complex models for "projects" (or rather "tasks") spanning a few weeks has been proven to cause unnecessary costs and low maneuverability in several cases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The generalization of Extreme Programming to other kinds of projects is <a href="/wiki/Extreme_project_management" title="Extreme project management">extreme project management</a>, which may be used in combination with the <a href="/wiki/Process_modeling" title="Process modeling">process modeling</a> and management principles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_interaction_management" title="Human interaction management">human interaction management</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_realization_management">Benefits realization management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Benefits realization management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benefits_realisation_management" title="Benefits realisation management">Benefits realisation management</a></div>
<p>Benefits realization management (BRM) enhances normal project management techniques through a focus on outcomes (the benefits) of a project rather than products or outputs, and then measuring the degree to which that is happening to keep a project on track. This can help to reduce the risk of a completed project being a failure by delivering agreed upon requirements/outputs but failing to deliver the <i><b>benefits</b></i> of those requirements.</p>
<p>In addition, BRM practices aim to ensure the alignment between project outcomes and business strategies. The effectiveness of these practices is supported by recent research evidencing BRM practices influencing project success from a strategic perspective across different countries and industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example of delivering a project to requirements might be agreeing to deliver a computer system that will process staff data and manage payroll, holiday and staff personnel records. Under BRM the agreement might be to achieve a specified reduction in staff hours required to process and maintain staff data.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes"><span id="Project_development_stages"></span> Processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Project_development_stages.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="368" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Project_development_stages.jpg/230px-Project_development_stages.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Project_development_stages.jpg/345px-Project_development_stages.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Project_development_stages.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Project_development_stages.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The project development stages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VA03_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-VA03-24">[24]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditionally, project management includes a number of elements: four to five project management process groups, and a control system. Regardless of the methodology or terminology used, the same basic project management processes or stages of development will be used. Major process groups generally include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMI_10_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-PMI_10-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Initiation</li>
<li>Planning</li>
<li>Production or execution</li>
<li>Monitoring and controlling</li>
<li>Closing</li>
</ul>
<p>In project environments with a significant exploratory element (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>), these stages may be supplemented with decision points (go/no go decisions) at which the project's continuation is debated and decided. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Phase%E2%80%93gate_model" title="Phase–gate model">Phase–gate model</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiating">Initiating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Initiating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="185" data-file-width="643" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/280px-Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/420px-Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/560px-Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Initiating_Process_Group_Processes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Initiating process group processes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VA03_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-VA03-24">[24]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The initiating processes determine the nature and scope of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> If this stage is not performed well, it is unlikely that the project will be successful in meeting the business’ needs. The key project controls needed here are an understanding of the business environment and making sure that all necessary controls are incorporated into the project. Any deficiencies should be reported and a recommendation should be made to fix them.</p>
<p>The initiating stage should include a plan that encompasses the following areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>analyzing the business <a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">needs/requirements</a> in measurable goals</li>
<li>reviewing of the current <a href="/wiki/Business_operations" title="Business operations">operations</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Financial_analysis" title="Financial analysis">financial analysis</a> of the costs and benefits including a <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">budget</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_analysis" title="Stakeholder analysis">stakeholder analysis</a>, including users, and support personnel for the project</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Project_charter" title="Project charter">project charter</a> including costs, tasks, deliverables, and schedules</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning">Planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the initiation stage, the project is planned to an appropriate level of detail (see <a href="/wiki/File:Planning_Process_Group_Activities.jpg" title="File:Planning Process Group Activities.jpg">example of a flow-chart</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VA03_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-VA03-24">[24]</a></sup> The main purpose is to plan time, cost and resources adequately to estimate the work needed and to effectively manage risk during project execution. As with the Initiation process group, a failure to adequately plan greatly reduces the project's chances of successfully accomplishing its goals.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Project_planning" title="Project planning">Project planning</a> generally consists of<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>determining how to plan (e.g. by level of detail or <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Wave_planning" title="Rolling Wave planning">Rolling Wave planning</a>);</li>
<li>developing the <a href="/wiki/Scope_statement" title="Scope statement">scope statement</a>;</li>
<li>selecting the planning team;</li>
<li>identifying deliverables and creating the work breakdown structure;</li>
<li>identifying the activities needed to complete those deliverables and networking the activities in their logical sequence;</li>
<li>estimating the resource requirements for the activities;</li>
<li>estimating time and cost for activities;</li>
<li>developing the schedule;</li>
<li>developing the budget;</li>
<li>risk planning;</li>
<li>gaining formal approval to begin work.</li>
</ul>
<p>Additional processes, such as planning for communications and for scope management, identifying roles and responsibilities, determining what to purchase for the project and holding a kick-off meeting are also generally advisable.</p>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">new product development</a> projects, conceptual design of the operation of the final product may be performed concurrent with the project planning activities, and may help to inform the planning team when identifying deliverables and planning activities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Executing">Executing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Executing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

This phase involves proper allocation, co-ordination and management of human resources and any other resources such as material and budgets. The output of this phase is the project deliverables.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring_and_controlling">Monitoring and controlling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Monitoring and controlling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="883" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/320px-Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/480px-Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg/640px-Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monitoring_and_Controlling_Process_Group_Processes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monitoring and controlling process group processes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VA03_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-VA03-24">[24]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Monitoring and controlling consists of those processes performed to observe project execution so that potential problems can be identified in a timely manner and corrective action can be taken, when necessary, to control the execution of the project. The key benefit is that project performance is observed and measured regularly to identify variances from the project management plan.</p>
<p>Monitoring and controlling includes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL00_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPL00-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Measuring the ongoing project activities ('where we are');</li>
<li>Monitoring the project variables (cost, effort, scope, etc.) against the project management plan and the project performance baseline (<i>where we should be</i>);</li>
<li>Identifying corrective actions to address issues and risks properly (<i>How can we get on track again</i>);</li>
<li>Influencing the factors that could circumvent integrated change control so only approved changes are implemented.</li>
</ul>
<p>In multi-phase projects, the monitoring and control process also provides feedback between project phases, in order to implement corrective or preventive actions to bring the project into compliance with the project management plan.</p>
<p>Project maintenance is an ongoing process, and it includes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMI_10_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-PMI_10-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Continuing support of end-users</li>
<li>Correction of errors</li>
<li>Updates to the product over time</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Project_Management_(project_control).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="493" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png/240px-Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png/360px-Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png/480px-Project_Management_%28project_control%29.png 2x" width="240"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Project_Management_(project_control).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monitoring and controlling cycle</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In this stage, <a href="/wiki/Auditor" title="Auditor">auditors</a> should pay attention to how effectively and quickly user problems are resolved.</p>
<p>Over the course of any construction project, the work scope may change. Change is a normal and expected part of the construction process. Changes can be the result of necessary design modifications, differing site conditions, material availability, contractor-requested changes, value engineering and impacts from third parties, to name a few. Beyond executing the change in the field, the change normally needs to be documented to show what was actually constructed. This is referred to as change management. Hence, the owner usually requires a final record to show all changes or, more specifically, any change that modifies the tangible portions of the finished work. The record is made on the contract documents – usually, but not necessarily limited to, the design drawings. The end product of this effort is what the industry terms as-built drawings, or more simply, "as built." The requirement for providing them is a norm in construction contracts. Construction document management is a highly important task undertaken with the aid an online or desktop software system, or maintained through physical documentation. The increasing legality pertaining to the construction industries maintenance of correct documentation has caused the increase in the need for document management systems.</p>
<p>When changes are introduced to the project, the viability of the project has to be re-assessed. It is important not to lose sight of the initial goals and targets of the projects. When the changes accumulate, the forecasted result may not justify the original proposed investment in the project. Successful project management identifies these components, and tracks and monitors progress so as to stay within time and budget frames already outlined at the commencement of the project.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Closing includes the formal acceptance of the project and the ending thereof. Administrative activities include the archiving of the files and documenting lessons learned.</p>
<p>This phase consists of:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMI_10_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-PMI_10-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Contract closure</b>: Complete and settle each contract (including the resolution of any open items) and close each contract applicable to the project or project phase.</li>
<li><b>Project close</b>: Finalize all activities across all of the process groups to formally close the project or a project phase</li>
</ul>
<p>Also included in this phase is the Post Implementation Review. This is a vital phase of the project for the project team to learn from experiences and apply to future projects. Normally a Post Implementation Review consists of looking at things that went well and analyzing things that went badly on the project to come up with lessons learned.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_controlling_and_project_control_systems">Project controlling and project control systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Project controlling and project control systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Project controlling (also known as <a href="/wiki/Cost_engineering" title="Cost engineering">Cost Engineering</a>) should be established as an independent function in project management. It implements verification and controlling function during the processing of a project in order to reinforce the defined performance and formal goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The tasks of project controlling are also:</p>
<ul>
<li>the creation of infrastructure for the supply of the right information and its update</li>
<li>the establishment of a way to communicate disparities of project parameters</li>
<li>the development of project information technology based on an intranet or the determination of a project key performance indicator system (KPI)</li>
<li>divergence analyses and generation of proposals for potential project regulations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>the establishment of methods to accomplish an appropriate project structure, project workflow organization, project control and governance</li>
<li>creation of transparency among the project parameters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Fulfillment and implementation of these tasks can be achieved by applying specific methods and instruments of project controlling. The following methods of project controlling can be applied:</p>
<ul>
<li>investment analysis</li>
<li>cost–benefit analysis</li>
<li>value benefit analysis</li>
<li>expert surveys</li>
<li>simulation calculations</li>
<li>risk-profile analysis</li>
<li>surcharge calculations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Milestone_(project_management)" title="Milestone (project management)">milestone</a> trend analysis</li>
<li>cost trend analysis</li>
<li>target/actual-comparison<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Project control is that element of a project that keeps it on track, on-time and within budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL00_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-JPL00-27">[27]</a></sup> Project control begins early in the project with planning and ends late in the project with post-implementation review, having a thorough involvement of each step in the process. Projects may be audited or reviewed while the project is in progress. Formal audits are generally risk or compliance-based and management will direct the objectives of the audit. An examination may include a comparison of approved project management processes with how the project is actually being managed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Each project should be assessed for the appropriate level of control needed: too much control is too time consuming, too little control is very risky. If project control is not implemented correctly, the cost to the business should be clarified in terms of errors and fixes.</p>
<p>Control systems are needed for cost, <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a>, quality, communication, time, change, procurement, and human resources. In addition, auditors should consider how important the projects are to the <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a>, how reliant the stakeholders are on controls, and how many controls exist. Auditors should review the development process and procedures for how they are implemented. The process of development and the quality of the final product may also be assessed if needed or requested. A business may want the auditing firm to be involved throughout the process to catch problems earlier on so that they can be fixed more easily. An auditor can serve as a controls consultant as part of the development team or as an independent auditor as part of an audit.</p>
<p>Businesses sometimes use formal systems development processes. These help assure that systems are developed successfully. A formal process is more effective in creating strong controls, and auditors should review this process to confirm that it is well designed and is followed in practice. A good formal systems development plan outlines:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Strategy" title="Strategy">strategy</a> to align development with the organization's broader objectives</li>
<li>Standards for new systems</li>
<li>Project management policies for timing and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budgeting" title="Budgeting">budgeting</a></li>
<li>Procedures describing the process</li>
<li>Evaluation of quality of change</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topics">Topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_managers">Project managers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Project managers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Project_manager" title="Project manager">project manager</a> is a professional in the field of project management. Project managers can have the responsibility of the planning, execution, and closing of any project, typically relating to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Construction_industry" title="Construction industry">construction industry</a>, engineering, architecture, <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, and telecommunications. Many other fields of production engineering, design engineering, and heavy industrial have project managers.</p>
<p>A project manager is the person accountable for accomplishing the stated project objectives. Key project management responsibilities include creating clear and attainable project objectives, building the project requirements, and managing the <a href="/wiki/Project_management_triangle" title="Project management triangle">triple constraint</a> for projects, which is cost, time, and scope.</p>
<p>A project manager is often a client representative and has to determine and implement the exact needs of the client, based on knowledge of the firm they are representing. The ability to adapt to the various internal procedures of the contracting party, and to form close links with the nominated representatives, is essential in ensuring that the key issues of cost, time, quality and above all, client satisfaction, can be realized.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_management_types">Project management types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Project management types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Project management can apply to any project, but it is often tailored to accommodate the specific needs of different and highly specialized industries. For example, the construction industry, which focuses on the delivery of things like buildings, roads, and bridges, has developed its own specialized form of project management that it refers to as <i>Construction project management</i> and for which project managers can become trained and certified in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contruction_Project_Management_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contruction_Project_Management-33">[33]</a></sup> The Information technology industry has also evolved to develop its own form of Project management that is referred to as <i>IT Project management</i> and which specializes in the delivery of technical assets and services that are required to pass through various lifecycle phases such as planning, design, development, testing, and deployment. <i>Biotechnology project management</i> focuses on the intricacies of biotechnology research and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>For each type of project management, project managers develop and utilize repeatable templates that are specific to the industry they're dealing with. This allows project plans to become very thorough and highly repeatable, with the specific intent to increase quality, lower delivery costs, and lower time to deliver project results.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_Management">Risk Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Risk Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Project_risk_management" title="Project risk management">Project risk management</a></div>
<p>The United States Department of Defense states; "Cost, Schedule, Performance, and Risk," are the four elements through which Department of Defense acquisition professionals make trade-offs and track program status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There are also <a href="/wiki/Project_management#International_standards" title="Project management">international standards</a>. Risk management applies proactive identification (see <a href="/wiki/Risk_management_tools" title="Risk management tools">tools</a>) of future problems and understanding of their consequences allowing <a href="/wiki/Predictive_analytics" title="Predictive analytics">predictive</a> decisions about projects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_breakdown_structure">Work breakdown structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Work breakdown structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Work_breakdown_structure" title="Work breakdown structure">Work breakdown structure</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Work_breakdown_structure" title="Work breakdown structure">work breakdown structure</a> (WBS) is a <a href="/wiki/Tree_structure" title="Tree structure">tree structure</a> that shows a subdivision of effort required to achieve an objective—for example a program, project, and contract. The WBS may be hardware-, product-, service-, or <a href="/wiki/Business_process" title="Business process">process</a>-oriented (see an example in a <a href="/wiki/File:NASA_NF_533_reporting_structure.jpg" title="File:NASA NF 533 reporting structure.jpg">NASA reporting structure (2001)</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA01_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA01-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>A WBS can be developed by starting with the end objective and successively subdividing it into manageable components in terms of size, <a href="/wiki/Duration_(project_management)" title="Duration (project management)">duration</a>, and responsibility (e.g., systems, subsystems, components, tasks, sub-tasks, and work packages), which include all steps necessary to achieve the objective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stellman05_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stellman05-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The work breakdown structure provides a common framework for the natural development of the overall planning and control of a contract and is the basis for dividing work into definable increments from which the statement of work can be developed and technical, schedule, cost, and labor hour reporting can be established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA01_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA01-36">[36]</a></sup> The work breakdown structure can be displayed in two forms one in form of a table with subdivision of tasks two in form of an organisational chart.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_standards">International standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: International standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been several attempts to develop project management standards, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>ISO 21500: 2012 – Guidance on project management. This is the first project management ISO.</li>
<li>ISO 31000: 2009 – Risk management. Risk management is 1 of the 10 knowledge areas of either ISO 21500 or PMBoK5 concept of project management.</li>
<li>ISO/IEC/IEEE 16326-2009 – Systems and Software Engineering—Life Cycle Processes—Project Management<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model" title="Capability Maturity Model">Capability Maturity Model</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a>.</li>
<li>GAPPS, <a href="/wiki/Global_Alliance_for_Project_Performance_Standards" title="Global Alliance for Project Performance Standards">Global Alliance for Project Performance Standards</a> – an open source standard describing COMPETENCIES for project and program managers.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Guide_to_the_Project_Management_Body_of_Knowledge" title="A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge">A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Institute" title="Project Management Institute">Project Management Institute</a> (PMI)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/HERMES_method" title="HERMES method">HERMES method</a>, Swiss general project management method, selected for use in Luxembourg and international organizations.</li>
<li>The ISO standards <a href="/wiki/ISO_9000" title="ISO 9000">ISO 9000</a>, a family of standards for quality management systems, and the <a href="/wiki/ISO_10006" title="ISO 10006">ISO 10006</a>:2003, for Quality management systems and guidelines for quality management in projects.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PRINCE2" title="PRINCE2">PRINCE2</a>, Projects IN Controlled Environments.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Association_for_Project_Management" title="Association for Project Management">Association for Project Management</a> Body of Knowledge<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_Software_Process" title="Team Software Process">Team Software Process</a> (TSP) from the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Cost_Management" title="Total Cost Management">Total Cost Management</a> Framework, AACE International's Methodology for Integrated Portfolio, Program and Project Management.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/V-Model" title="V-Model">V-Model</a>, an original systems development method.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Logical_framework_approach" title="Logical framework approach">Logical framework approach</a>, which is popular in international development organizations.</li>
<li>[Australian Institute of Project Management] AIPM has 4 levels of certification; CPPP, CPPM, CPPD &amp; CPPE for Certified Practicing Project ... Partner, Manager, Director and Executive.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_portfolio_management">Project portfolio management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Project portfolio management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Project_portfolio_management" title="Project portfolio management">Project portfolio management</a></div>
<p>An increasing number of organizations are using, what is referred to as, <a href="/wiki/Project_portfolio_management" title="Project portfolio management">project portfolio management</a> (PPM) as a means of selecting the right projects and then using project management techniques<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AH04_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-AH04-39">[39]</a></sup> as the means for delivering the outcomes in the form of benefits to the performing private or not-for-profit organization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_management_software">Project management software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Project management software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Project_management_software" title="Project management software">Project management software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_management_information_system" title="Project management information system">Project management information system</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Project_management_software" title="Project management software">Project management software</a> is software used to help plan, organize, and manage resource pools, develop resource estimates and implement plans. Depending on the sophistication of the software, functionality may include <a href="/wiki/Software_development_effort_estimation" title="Software development effort estimation">estimation</a> and planning, <a href="/wiki/Schedule_(workplace)" title="Schedule (workplace)">scheduling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_control" title="Cost control">cost control</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budget_management" title="Budget management">budget management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">resource allocation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collaboration_software" title="Collaboration software">collaboration software</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision-making_software" title="Decision-making software">decision-making</a>, <a href="/wiki/Workflow_management_system" title="Workflow management system">workflow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Risk_management_tools" title="Risk management tools">risk</a>, quality, <a href="/wiki/Documentation" title="Documentation">documentation</a> and/or administration systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_project_management">Virtual project management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Virtual project management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virtual_team" title="Virtual team">Virtual team</a></div>
<p>Virtual program management (VPM) is management of a project done by a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_team" title="Virtual team">virtual team</a>, though it rarely may refer to a project implementing a virtual environment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It is noted that managing a virtual project is fundamentally different from managing traditional projects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> combining concerns of telecommuting and global collaboration (culture, timezones, language).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
