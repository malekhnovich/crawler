<p><b>Sensory design</b> aims to establish an overall diagnosis of the <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sensory perceptions</a> of a product, and define appropriate means to <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> or redesign it on that basis. It involves an observation of the diverse and varying situations in which a given product or object is used in order to measure the users' overall opinion of the product, its positive and negative aspects in terms of <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">tactility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">appearance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">sound</a> and so on.</p>
<p>Sensory assessment aims to quantify and describe, in a systematic manner, all human <a href="/wiki/Perception#Types" title="Perception">perceptions</a> when confronted with a product or object. Contrary to traditional laboratory analysis, a sensory analysis of a product is either carried out by a panel of <a href="/wiki/Psychological_testing" title="Psychological testing">trained testers</a> , or by specialized test equipment designed to mimic the perception of humans.</p>
<p>The result allows researchers to establish a list of specifications and to set out a precise and quantified requirement. These are applied to materials and objects using various criteria:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">Touch</a>, textures, compliance, friction.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">Vision</a> color, luminosity, shape, pattern.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">Sounds</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wiki:movement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wiki:movement (page does not exist)">movements</a> made when a product is handled;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">Smell</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">Taste</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperature</a> and perceived thermal properties</li>
</ul>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Transportation">Use in Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use in Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transportation</a> sphere, these two branches of <a href="/wiki/Sensory_analysis" title="Sensory analysis">sensory analysis</a> translate into sometimes minor enhancements to the design for a vehicle interior, information system, or station environment to smooth some of the rougher edges of the travel experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Railway_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Railway-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, specialized air purifying equipment can be used to design a more pleasant odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interior_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Interior-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Food_and_Beverage">Use in Food and Beverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in Food and Beverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sensory design plays a critical role the modern food and beverage industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The food and beverage industry attempts to maintain specific sensory experiences. In addition to smell and flavor, the color of food (e.g. ripe fruits),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and texture of food (e.g. potato chips) are critical. Even the environment is important as "Color affects the appetite, in essence, the taste of food".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interior_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Interior-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensory_Design_Technologies">Sensory Design Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sensory Design Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While classically limited to the perception of trained sensory experts, advances in sensors and computation have allowed objective quantified measurements of sensory information to be acquired, quantified and communicated leading to improved design communication, translation from prototype to production, and quality assurance. Sensory areas that have been objectively quantified include vision, touch, and smell.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision">Vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In vision both light and color are considered in sensory design. Early <a href="/wiki/Light_meter" title="Light meter">light meters</a> (called extinction meters) relied on the human eye to gauge and quantify the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity_function" title="Luminosity function">amount of light</a>. Subsequently analog and digital light meters have been popularized for photography. Work by Lawrence Herbert in the 1960s lead to a systematic combination of lighting and color samples required to quantify colors by human eye. This became the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Pantone" title="Pantone">Pantone Matching System</a>. Combining this with specialized light meters allowed digital color meters to be invented and popularized.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Touch">Touch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Touch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Touch plays an important role in a variety of products and is increasingly considered in product design and marketing efforts and has led to a more scientific approach to tactile design and marketing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Classical the field of tribology has developed various tests to evaluate interacting surfaces in relative motion with a focus on measuring friction, lubrication, and wear. However these measurements do not correlate with human perception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayesian_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bayesian-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternative methods for evaluating how materials feel were first popularized from work initiated at Kyoto University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Kawabata evaluation system developed six measurements <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> of how fabrics feel. The SynTouch Standard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is a system developed using a biomimetic tactile sensor which quantifies fifteen dimensions of touch based on testing over 500 materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bayesian_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bayesian-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smell">Smell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Smell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Measuring odors has remained difficult. A variety of techniques have been attempted but “Most measures have had a subjective component that makes them anachronistic with modern methodology in experimental behavioral science, indeterminate regarding the extent of individual differences, unusable with infra humans and of unproved ability to discern small differences”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> New methods for robotic exploration of smell are being proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
