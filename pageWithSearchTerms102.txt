<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Enterprise architecture framework</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>enterprise architecture framework</b> (<b>EA framework</b>) defines how to create and use an <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_architecture" title="Enterprise architecture">enterprise architecture</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Architecture_framework" title="Architecture framework">architecture framework</a> provides principles and practices for creating and using the architecture description of a system. It structures architects' thinking by dividing the architecture description into domains, layers or views, and offers models - typically matrices and diagrams - for documenting each view. This allows for making systemic design decisions on all the components of the system and making long-term decisions around new design, requirements, sustainability and support. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enterprise_Architecture_Purpose_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enterprise_Architecture_Purpose-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Enterprise architecture regards the enterprise as a large and complex system or <a href="/wiki/System_of_systems" title="System of systems">system of systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To manage the scale and complexity of this system, an architectural framework provides tools and approaches that help architects abstract from the level of detail that builders work at to bring enterprise design tasks into focus and produce valuable architecture description documentation.</p>
<p>The components of an architecture framework provide structured guidance that is divided into three main areas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SM03_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SM03-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Descriptions of architecture: how to document the enterprise as a system from several viewpoints. Each view describes one slice of the architecture; it includes those entities and relationships that address particular concerns of interest to particular stakeholders; it may take the form of a list, a table, a diagram, or a higher level of composite of such.</li>
<li>Methods for designing architecture: processes that architects follow. Usually, an overarching enterprise architecture process, composed of phases, breaks into lower-level processes composed of finer grained activities. A process is defined by its objectives, inputs, phases (steps or activities) and outputs. It may be supported by approaches, techniques, tools, principles, rules, and practices.</li>
<li>Organization of architects: guidance on the team structure, the governance of the team, the skills, experience and training needed.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>

<p>Since the 1970s people working in IS/IT have looked for ways to engage business people – to enable business roles and processes - and to influence investment in business information systems and technologies – with a view to the wide and long term benefit of the enterprise. Many of the aims, principles, concepts and methods now employed in EA frameworks were established in the 1980s, and can be found in IS and IT architecture frameworks published in that decade and the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB_2013_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GB_2013-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1980, IBM’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_Systems_Planning" title="Business Systems Planning">Business Systems Planning</a> (BSP) was promoted as a method for analyzing and designing an organization’s information architecture, with the goals:</p>
<ol>
<li>understand the issues and opportunities with the current applications and technical architecture;</li>
<li>develop a future state and migration path for the technology that supports the enterprise;</li>
<li>provide business executives with a direction and decision making framework for IT capital expenditures;</li>
<li>provide information system (IS) with a blueprint for development.</li>
</ol>
<p>In 1982, when working for IBM and with BSP, John Zachman was perhaps the first to mention Enterprise Architecture in the public domain. Then and in later papers, Zachman used the word enterprise as a synonym for business. "Although many popular information systems planning methodologies, design approaches, and various tools and techniques do not preclude or are not inconsistent with enterprise-level analysis, few of them explicitly address or attempt to define enterprise architectures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JZ_1982_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JZ_1982-8">[8]</a></sup> However, in this article the term "Enterprise Architecture" was mentioned only once without any specific definition and all subsequent works of Zachman used the term "Information Systems Architecture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JZ_1987_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JZ_1987-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JZ_1992_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-JZ_1992-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=PRISM_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="PRISM architecture framework (page does not exist)">PRISM architecture framework</a> was developed as a result of the research project sponsored by a group of companies including IBM, which was seemingly the first published EA framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTORY_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HISTORY-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, John Zachman, who was a marketing specialist at IBM, published a paper, <i>A Framework for Information Systems Architecture</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JZ_1987_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-JZ_1987-9">[9]</a></sup> The paper provided a classification scheme for artifacts that describe (at several levels of abstraction) the what, how, where, who, when and why of information systems. Given IBM already employed BSP, Zachman had no need to provide planning process. The paper did not mention enterprise architecture.</p>
<p>In 1989, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) published the <a href="/wiki/NIST_Enterprise_Architecture_Model" title="NIST Enterprise Architecture Model">NIST Enterprise Architecture Model</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This was a five-layer reference model that illustrates the interrelationship of business, information system, and technology domains. It was promoted within the U.S. federal government. It was not an EA framework as we see it now, but it helped to establish the notion of dividing EA into architecture domains or layers. The NIST Enterprise Architecture Model seemingly was the first publication that consistently used the term "Enterprise Architecture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTORY_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-HISTORY-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, the term "Enterprise Architecture" was formally defined for the first time as an architecture that "defines and interrelates data, hardware, software, and communications resources, as well as the supporting organization required to maintain the overall physical structure required by the architecture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTORY_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-HISTORY-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, a paper by Zachman and Sowa <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JZ_1992_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-JZ_1992-10">[10]</a></sup> started thus "John Zachman introduced a framework for information systems architecture (ISA) that has been widely adopted by systems analysts and database designers." The term enterprise architecture did not appear. The paper was about using the ISA framework to describe, “...the overall information system and how it relates to the enterprise and its surrounding environment.” The word enterprise was used as a synonym for business.</p>
<p>In 1993, Stephen Spewak’s book <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enterprise_Architecture_Planning" title="Enterprise Architecture Planning">Enterprise Architecture Planning</a> (EAP) defined a process for defining architectures for the use of information in support of the business and the plan for implementing those architectures. The business mission is the primary driver. Then the data required to satisfy the mission. Then the applications built to store and provide that data. Finally the technology to implement the applications. Enterprise Architecture Planning is a data-centric approach to architecture planning. An aim is to improve data quality, access to data, adaptability to changing requirements, data interoperability and sharing, and cost containment. EAP has its roots in IBM’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_Systems_Planning" title="Business Systems Planning">Business Systems Planning</a> (BSP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HISTORY_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-HISTORY-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, the Open Group selected <a href="/wiki/TAFIM" title="TAFIM">TAFIM</a> from the US DoD as a basis for development of The Open Group Architecture Framework (TOGAF), where architecture meant IT architecture. TOGAF started out taking a strategic and enterprise-wide, but technology-oriented, view. It emerged from the desire to rationalize a messy IT estate. Right up to version 7, TOGAF was still focused on defining and using a Technical Reference Model (or foundation architecture) to define the platform services required from the technologies that an entire enterprise uses to support business applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB_2013_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-GB_2013-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, the US <i>IT Management Reform Act</i>, more commonly known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinger-Cohen_Act" title="Clinger-Cohen Act">Clinger-Cohen Act</a>, repeatedly directed that a US federal government agency’s investment in IT must be mapped to identifiable business benefits. In addition, it made the agency CIO responsible for, “...developing, maintaining and facilitating the implementation of a sound and integrated IT architecture for the executive agency.”</p>
<p>By 1997, Zachman had renamed and refocused his ISA framework as an EA framework; it remained a classification scheme for descriptive artifacts, not a process for planning systems or changes to systems.</p>
<p>In 1998, The Federal CIO Council began developing the Federal Enterprise Architecture Framework (FEAF) in accordance with the priorities enunciated in Clinger-Cohen and issued it in 1999. FEAF was a process much like TOGAF’s ADM, in which “The architecture team generates a sequencing plan for the transition of systems, applications, and associated business practices predicated upon a detailed gap analysis [between baseline and target architectures].”</p>
<p>In 2001, the US Chief CIO council published <i>A practical guide to Federal Enterprise Architecture</i>, which starts, “An enterprise architecture (EA) establishes the Agency-wide roadmap to achieve an Agency’s mission through optimal performance of its core business processes within an efficient information technology (IT) environment." At that point, the processes in TOGAF, FEAF, EAP and BSP were clearly related.</p>
<p>In 2002/3, in its <i>Enterprise Edition</i>, TOGAF 8 shifted focus from the technology architecture layer to the higher business, data and application layers. It introduced structured analysis, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_Engineering" title="Information Engineering">Information Engineering</a>, which features, for example, mappings of organization units to business functions and data entities to business functions. Today, business functions are often called business capabilities. And many enterprise architects regard their business function/capability hierarchy/map as the fundamental Enterprise Architecture artifact. They relate data entities, use cases, applications and technologies to the functions/capabilities.</p>
<p>In 2006, the popular book <i>Enterprise Architecture As Strategy <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></i> reported the results of work by MIT’s Center for Information System Research. This book emphasises the need for enterprise architects to focus on core business processes ("Companies excel because they've [decided] which processes they must execute well, and have implemented the IT systems to digitise those processes.") and to engage business managers with the benefits that strategic cross-organisational process integration and/or standardisation could provide.</p>
<p>A 2008 research project for the development of professional certificates in enterprise and solution architecture by the <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a> (BCS) showed that enterprise architecture has always been inseparable from information system architecture, which is natural, since business people need information to make decisions and carry out business processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB_2013_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-GB_2013-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, the TOGAF 9.1. specification says: "Business planning at the strategy level provides the initial direction to enterprise architecture."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Normally, the business principles, business goals, and strategic drivers of the organization are defined elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB_2013_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-GB_2013-7">[7]</a></sup> In other words, Enterprise Architecture is not a business strategy, planning or management methodology. Enterprise Architecture strives to align business information systems technology with given business strategy, goals and drivers. The TOGAF 9.1 specification clarified, that, "A complete enterprise architecture description should contain all four architecture domains (business, data, application, technology), but the realities of resource and time constraints often mean there is not enough time, funding, or resources to build a top-down, all-inclusive architecture description encompassing all four architecture domains, even if the enterprise scope is [...] less than the full extent of the overall enterprise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TOGAF" title="TOGAF">TOGAF</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> is the most popular Architecture framework (judged by published certification numbers) that some assume it defines EA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB_2013_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-GB_2013-7">[7]</a></sup> However, some still use the term Enterprise Architecture as a synonym for Business Architecture, rather than covering all four architecture domains - business, data, applications and technology.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_building_governance">Architecture and building governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Architecture and building governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People who remodel a home are aware that the architect produces detailed drawings that specify plumbing, electrical, and building construction information for the entire structure. The architect responsible for the design produces (or oversees others who produce) blueprints for each phase of the project—from structural changes to size and layout of rooms. Moreover, to successfully complete the project, the architect operates within a framework of building codes. City or county inspections ensure the work complies with building codes.</p>
<p>Enterprise architecture works in a similar manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToCu01_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ToCu01-18">[18]</a></sup> An architecture description document can be thought of as the blueprint for the procurement and realization of a system. But an enterprise architecture includes more than just an abstract description of the system's structure and behavior. It includes also principles, policies and standards (akin to building codes) that ensure that systems are soundly constructed. Governance of the enterprise architecture and of its implementation requires organization and processes (akin to city and county inspectors and the processes for checking building improvement projects).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_domain">Architecture domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Architecture domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="523" data-file-width="1039" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg/320px-Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg/480px-Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg/640px-Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Layers_of_the_Enterprise_Architecture.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Layers of the enterprise architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hwe06_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hwe06-19">[19]</a></sup></div>

<p>Since Stephen Spewak's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enterprise_Architecture_Planning" title="Enterprise Architecture Planning">Enterprise Architecture Planning</a> (EAP) in 1993, and perhaps before then, it has been normal to divide enterprises architecture into four <a href="/wiki/Architecture_domain" title="Architecture domain">architecture domains</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Business_architecture" title="Business architecture">Business architecture</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Data_architecture" title="Data architecture">Data architecture</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Applications_architecture" title="Applications architecture">Applications architecture</a>,</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Technology_architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Technology architecture (page does not exist)">Technology architecture</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Note that the applications architecture is about the choice of and relationships between applications in the enterprise's application portfolio, not about the internal architecture of a single application (which is often called application architecture).</p>
<p>Many EA frameworks combine data and application domains into a single (digitized) information system layer, sitting below the business (usually a human activity system) and above the technology (the platform IT infrastructure).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers_of_the_enterprise_architecture">Layers of the enterprise architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Layers of the enterprise architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FEA_Reference_Models.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="234" data-file-width="740" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/FEA_Reference_Models.jpg/360px-FEA_Reference_Models.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/FEA_Reference_Models.jpg/540px-FEA_Reference_Models.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/FEA_Reference_Models.jpg/720px-FEA_Reference_Models.jpg 2x" width="360"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FEA_Reference_Models.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Example of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_enterprise_architecture" title="Federal enterprise architecture">federal enterprise architecture</a>, which has defined five architectural layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WH05_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-WH05-20">[20]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>For many years, it has been common to regard the architecture domains as layers, with the idea that each layer contains components that execute processes and offer services to the layer above. This way of looking at the architecture domains was evident in TOGAF v1 (1996), which encapsulated the technology component layer behind the platform services defined in the "Technical Reference Model" - very much according to the philosophy of TAFIM and POSIX.</p>
<p>The view of architecture domains as layers can be presented thus:</p>
<ul>
<li>Environment (the external entities and activities monitored, supported or directed by the business).</li>
<li>Business Layer (business functions offering services to each other and to external entities).</li>
<li>Data Layer (Business information and other valuable stored data)</li>
<li>Information System Layer (business applications offering information services to each other and to business functions)</li>
<li>Technology Layer (generic hardware, network and platform applications offering platform services to each other and to business applications).</li>
</ul>
<p>Each layer <i>delegates</i> work to the layer below. In each layer, the components, the processes and the services can be defined at a coarse-grained level and decomposed into finer-grained components, processes and services. The graphic shows a variation on this theme.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components_of_enterprise_architecture_framework">Components of enterprise architecture framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Components of enterprise architecture framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to three major framework components discussed above.</p>
<ol>
<li>Description advice: some kind of Architecture Artifacts Map or Viewpoint Library</li>
<li>Process advice: some kind of Architecture Development Method, with supporting guidance.</li>
<li>Organization advice: including an EA Governance Model</li>
</ol>
<p>An Ideal EA Framework should feature:</p>
<ol>
<li>Business Value Measurement Metrics</li>
<li>EA Initiative Model</li>
<li>EA Maturity Model</li>
<li>Enterprise Communication Model</li>
</ol>
<p>Most modern EA Frameworks (e.g. TOGAF, ASSIMPLER, EAF) include most of the above. Zachman has always focused on architecture description advice.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_architecture_domains_and_subdomains">Enterprise architecture domains and subdomains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Enterprise architecture domains and subdomains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="511" data-file-width="723" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG/320px-Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG/480px-Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG/640px-Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enterprise_Architecture_Domain_Reference_Architecture.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Enterprise architecture reference architecture with sub domains</div>
</div>

<p>The application and technology domains (not to be confused with business domains) are characterized by domain capabilities and domain services. The capabilities are supported by the services. The application services are also referred to in <a href="/wiki/Service-oriented_architecture" title="Service-oriented architecture">service-oriented architecture</a> (SOA). The technical services are typically supported by software products.</p>
<p>The data view starts with the data classes which can be decomposed into data subjects which can be further decomposed into data entities. The basic data model type which is most commonly used is called merda (master entity relationship diagrams assessment, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entity-relationship_model" title="Entity-relationship model">entity-relationship model</a>). The Class, subject and entity forms a hierarchical view of data. Enterprises may have millions of instances of data entities.</p>
<p>The Enterprise Architecture Reference Traditional Model offers clear distinction between the architecture domains (business, information/data, application/integration and technical/infrastructure). These domains can be further divided into Sub domain disciplines. An example of the EA domain and sub domains is in the image on the right.</p>
<p>Many enterprise architecture teams consist of Individuals with Skills aligned with the Enterprise Architecture Domains and sub-domain disciplines. Here are some examples: enterprise business architect, enterprise documentational architect, enterprise application architect, enterprise infrastructure architect, enterprise information architect, etc.</p>
<p>An example of the list of reference architecture patterns in the application and information architecture domains are available at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architectural_pattern_(computer_science)" title="Architectural pattern (computer science)">Architectural pattern (computer science)</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="View_model">View model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: View model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:356px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="708" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg/354px-4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg/531px-4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg/708px-4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg.png 2x" width="354"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:4%2B1_Architectural_View_Model.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4%2B1" title="4+1">4+1 view model of architecture</a>.</div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/View_model" title="View model">view model</a> is a framework that defines the set of views or approaches used in <a href="/wiki/Systems_analysis" title="Systems analysis">systems analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_design" title="Systems design">systems design</a>, or the construction of an <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_architecture" title="Enterprise architecture">enterprise architecture</a>.</p>
<p>Since the early 1990s there have been a number of efforts to define standard approaches for describing and analyzing system architectures. Many of the recent Enterprise Architecture frameworks have some kind of set of views defined, but these sets are not always called <i>view models</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization">Standardization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Standardization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the best-known standard in the field of <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">software architecture</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_architecture" title="System architecture">system architecture</a> started life as <a href="/wiki/IEEE_1471" title="IEEE 1471">IEEE 1471</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE_Standard" title="IEEE Standard">IEEE Standard</a> for describing the <i>architecture of a software-intensive system</i> approved in 2000.</p>
<p>In its latest version, the standard is published as <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_42010" title="ISO/IEC 42010">ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2011</a>. The standard defines an architecture framework as <i>conventions, principles and practices for the description of architectures established within a specific domain of application and/or community of stakeholders</i>, and proposes an architecture framework is specified by:</p>
<ol>
<li>the relevant stakeholders in the domain,</li>
<li>the types of concerns arising in that domain,</li>
<li>architecture viewpoints framing those concerns and</li>
<li>correspondence rules integrating those viewpoints cited before.</li>
</ol>
<p>Architecture frameworks conforming to the standard can include additional methods, tools, definitions, and practices beyond those specified.</p>

<ul>
<li>ASSIMPLER Framework – an architecture framework, based on the work of Mandar Vanarse at Wipro in 2002</li>
<li>Avancier Methods (AM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Processes and documentation advice for enterprise and solution architects, supported by training and certification.</li>
<li>BRM (Build-Run-Manage) Framework - an architecture framework created by Sanjeev "Sunny" Mishra during his early days at IBM in 2000.</li>
<li>Capgemini <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Architecture_Framework" title="Integrated Architecture Framework">Integrated Architecture Framework</a> (IAF) – from <a href="/wiki/Capgemini" title="Capgemini">Capgemini</a> company in 1993</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dragon1" title="Dragon1">Dragon1</a> - An open Visual Enterprise Architecture Method recently recognized by The Open Group as Architecture Framework</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DYA_framework" title="DYA framework">DYA</a> framework developed by <a href="/wiki/Sogeti" title="Sogeti">Sogeti</a> since 2004.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_Enterprise" title="Dynamic Enterprise">Dynamic Enterprise</a> Enterprise architecture concept based on Web 2.0 technology</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extended_Enterprise_Architecture_Framework" title="Extended Enterprise Architecture Framework">Extended Enterprise Architecture Framework</a> - from Institute For Enterprise Architecture Developments in 2003</li>
<li>EACOE Framework <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.eacoe.org" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> – an Enterprise Architecture framework, as an elaboration of the work of John Zachman</li>
<li>IBM <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_FrameWork" title="Information FrameWork">Information FrameWork</a> (IFW) – conceived by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Evernden" title="Roger Evernden">Roger Evernden</a> in 1996</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/OBASHI" title="OBASHI">OBASHI</a> – the OBASHI Business &amp; IT methodology and framework</li>
<li>Pragmatic Enterprise Architecture Framework (PEAF)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pragmatic_EA_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pragmatic_EA-31">[31]</a></sup> - part of Pragmatic Family of Frameworks developed by Kevin Lee Smith, Pragmatic EA, from 2008</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Purdue_Enterprise_Reference_Architecture" title="Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture">Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Theodore_J._Williams" title="Theodore J. Williams">Theodore J. Williams</a> at the Purdue University early 1990s.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SAP_Enterprise_Architecture_Framework" title="SAP Enterprise Architecture Framework">SAP Enterprise Architecture Framework</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Service-oriented_modeling#Service-oriented_modeling_framework" title="Service-oriented modeling">Service-oriented modeling framework (SOMF)</a>, based on the work of <a href="/wiki/Michael_B.T._Bell" title="Michael B.T. Bell">Michael Bell</a></li>
<li>Solution Architecting Mechanism (SAM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> – A coherent architecture framework consisting of a set of integral modules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tony_Shan_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tony_Shan-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zachman_Framework" title="Zachman Framework">Zachman Framework</a> – an architecture framework, based on the work of <a href="/wiki/John_Zachman" title="John Zachman">John Zachman</a> at IBM in the 1980s</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_architecture_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite that EA frameworks have been widely discussed and strongly associated with the very notion of EA, their real practical value has been questioned:</p>
<ul>
<li>Historical analysis of EA publications shows that EA frameworks are "nothing more than typical <a href="/wiki/Management_fad" title="Management fad">management fads</a> aggressively promoted by consulting companies and gurus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Research shows that EA frameworks "appear theoretical and impossible to implement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vivek_Kundra" title="Vivek Kundra">Vivek Kundra</a>, the federal CIO of the United States, argued that EA frameworks "are worse than useless."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Jason Bloomberg reports that EA frameworks only waste architects' time instead of solving real problems. "Frameworks are cocaine for executives - they give them a huge rush and then they move to the next framework."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
