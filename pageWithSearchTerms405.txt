<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Technology transfer</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Technology transfer</b>, also called <b>transfer of technology</b> (<b>TOT</b>), is the process of transferring (disseminating) <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> from the places and <a href="/wiki/Ingroups_and_outgroups" title="Ingroups and outgroups">ingroups</a> of its origination to wider distribution among more people and places. It occurs along various axes: among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universities" title="Universities">universities</a>, from universities to businesses, from large businesses to smaller ones, from <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> to businesses, across <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a>, both formally and informally, and both openly and surreptitiously. Often it occurs by concerted effort to share skills, knowledge, technologies, methods of manufacturing, samples of manufacturing, and facilities among <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> and other institutions to ensure that scientific and technological developments are accessible to a wider range of users who can then further develop and exploit the technology into new products, processes, applications, materials, or services. It is closely related to (and may arguably be considered a subset of) <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_transfer" title="Knowledge transfer">knowledge transfer</a>. Horizontal transfer is the movement of technologies from one area to another. At present<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> transfer of technology (TOT) is primarily horizontal. Vertical transfer occurs when technologies are moved from applied research centers to research and development departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Technology transfer is promoted at conferences organized by such groups as the <a href="/wiki/Ewing_Marion_Kauffman_Foundation" title="Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation">Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation</a> and the Association of University Technology Managers, and at "challenge" competitions by organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Advancing_Innovation" title="Center for Advancing Innovation">Center for Advancing Innovation</a> in Maryland. Local venture capital organizations such as the Mid-Atlantic Venture Association (MAVA) also sponsor conferences at which investors assess the potential for commercialization of technology.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Technology_brokering" title="Technology brokering">Technology brokers</a> are people who discovered how to bridge the emergent worlds and apply scientific concepts or processes to new situations or circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A related term, used almost synonymously, is "technology <a href="/wiki/Valorisation" title="Valorisation">valorisation</a>". While conceptually the practice has been utilized for many years (in ancient times, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> was notable for applying science to practical problems), the present-day volume of research, combined with high-profile failures at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> and elsewhere<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, has led to a focus on the process itself.</p>
<p>Whereas technology transfer can involve the dissemination of <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">highly complex technology</a> from capital-intensive origins to low-capital recipients (and can involve aspects of dependency and fragility of systems), it also can involve <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a>, not necessarily high-tech or expensive, that is better disseminated, yielding <a href="/wiki/Robustness" title="Robustness">robustness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">independence</a> of systems.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_process">Transfer process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Technology_transfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transfer process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many companies, universities and governmental organizations now have an Office of Technology Transfer (TTO, also known as "Tech Transfer" or "TechXfer") dedicated to identifying research which has potential commercial interest and strategies for how to exploit it. For instance, a research result may be of scientific and commercial interest, but <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> are normally only issued for practical processes, and so someone—not necessarily the researchers—must come up with a specific practical process. Another consideration is commercial value; for example, while there are many ways to accomplish <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>, the ones of commercial value are those that generate more energy than they require to operate.</p>
<p>The process to commercially exploit research varies widely. It can involve licensing agreements or setting up joint ventures and partnerships to share both the risks and rewards of bringing new technologies to market. Other corporate vehicles, e.g. spin-outs, are used where the host organization does not have the necessary will, resources or skills to develop a new technology. Often these approaches are associated with raising of <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> (VC) as a means of funding the development process, a practice more common in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> than in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, which has a more conservative approach to VC funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Research_spin-off" title="Research spin-off">Research spin-off</a> companies are a popular vehicle of commercialisation in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, where the rate of licensing of Canadian university research remains far below that of the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Technology transfer offices may work on behalf of research institutions, governments and even large multinationals. Where start-ups and spin-outs are the clients, commercial fees are sometimes waived in lieu of an equity stake in the business. As a result of the potential complexity of the technology transfer process, technology transfer organizations are often multidisciplinary, including economists, engineers, lawyers, marketers and scientists. The dynamics of the technology transfer process has attracted attention in its own right, and there are several dedicated societies and journals.</p>
<p>There has been a marked increase in technology transfer intermediaries specialized in their field since 1980, stimulated in large part by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayh-Dole_Act" title="Bayh-Dole Act">Bayh-Dole Act</a> and equivalent legislation in other countries, which provided additional incentives for research exploitation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">Drawbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Technology_transfer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite incentives to move research into production, the practical aspects are sometimes difficult to perform in practice. Using DoD <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technology_Readiness_Level" title="Technology Readiness Level">Technology Readiness Levels</a> as a criterion (for example), research tends to focus on TRL (technology readiness level) 1-3 while readiness for production tends to focus on TRL 6-7 or higher. Bridging TRL-3 to TRL-6 has proven to be difficult in some organizations. Attempting to rush research (prototypes) into production (fully tested under diverse conditions, reliable, maintainable, etc.) tends to be more costly and time-consuming than expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
