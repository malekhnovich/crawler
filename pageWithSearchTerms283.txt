<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Michael A. Jackson</h1>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other people named Michael Jackson, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson_(disambiguation)" title="Michael Jackson (disambiguation)">Michael Jackson (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Michael Anthony Jackson</b> (born 1936) is a British <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, and independent <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a> consultant in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, England. He is also part-time researcher at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT%26T_Labs_Research" title="AT&amp;T Labs Research">AT&amp;T Labs Research</a>, Florham Park, NJ, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>, and visiting research <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_A._Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jackson was educated at <a href="/wiki/Harrow_School" title="Harrow School">Harrow School</a> where he was taught by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Strachey" title="Christopher Strachey">Christopher Strachey</a> and wrote his first program under Strachey's guidance. From 1954 to 1958 he studied classics (known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greats" title="Greats">Greats</a>") at <a href="/wiki/Merton_College,_Oxford" title="Merton College, Oxford">Merton College, Oxford</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCreg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-2">[2]</a></sup> a fellow student, two years ahead of him, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C._A._R._Hoare" title="C. A. R. Hoare">C. A. R. Hoare</a>. They shared an interest in <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, which was studied as part of Greats at Oxford.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, Jackson developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jackson_Structured_Programming" title="Jackson Structured Programming">Jackson Structured Programming</a> (JSP). In the 1980s, with John Cameron, he developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jackson_System_Development" title="Jackson System Development">Jackson System Development</a> (JSD). Then, in the 1990s, he developed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Problem_Frames_Approach" title="Problem Frames Approach">Problem Frames Approach</a>. In collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Zave" title="Pamela Zave">Pamela Zave</a>, he created "Distributed Feature Composition",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> a virtual architecture for specification and implementation of telecommunication services.</p>
<p>Jackson received the <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Award" title="Stevens Award">Stevens Award</a> for Software Development Methods in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961 Jackson married Judith Wendy Blackburn;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCreg_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MCreg-2">[2]</a></sup> they have four sons, one of whom, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Jackson_(computer_scientist)" title="Daniel Jackson (computer scientist)">Daniel</a>, is also a computer scientist based at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_A._Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jackson has developed a series of methods. Each of these methods covers a wider scope than the previous one, and builds on ideas that appeared, but were not fully developed, in the previous one. Reading his books in sequence allows you to follow the evolution of his thinking.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jackson_Structured_Programming">Jackson Structured Programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_A._Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jackson Structured Programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jackson_Structured_Programming" title="Jackson Structured Programming">Jackson Structured Programming</a> (JSP) was the first software development method that Jackson developed. It is a program design method, and was described in his book <i>Principles of Program Design</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PoPD_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PoPD-6">[6]</a></sup> JSP covers the design of individual programs, but not systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jackson_System_Development">Jackson System Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_A._Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jackson System Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jackson_System_Development" title="Jackson System Development">Jackson System Development</a> (JSD) was the second software development method that Jackson developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> JSD is a system development method not just for individual programs, but for entire systems. JSD is most readily applicable to information systems, but it can easily be extended to the development of real-time embedded systems. JSD was described in his book <i>System Development</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problem_Frames_Approach">Problem Frames Approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_A._Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Problem Frames Approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Problem Analysis or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Problem_Frames_Approach" title="Problem Frames Approach">Problem Frames Approach</a> was the third software development method that Jackson developed. It concerns itself with aspects of developing all kinds of software, not just information systems. It was first sketched in his book <i>Software Requirements and Specifications</i>, and described much more fully in his book <i>Problem Frames</i>. The First International Workshop on Applications and Advances in Problem Frames<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> was held as part of ICSE’04 held in Edinburgh, Scotland.</p>

<p>His books include:</p>
