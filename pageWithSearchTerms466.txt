<p><b>Graphic design</b> is the process of visual communication and problem-solving through the use of <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a>. The field is considered a subset of <a href="/wiki/Visual_communication" title="Visual communication">visual communication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communication_design" title="Communication design">communication design</a>, but sometimes the term "graphic <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>" is used synonymously. Graphic designers create and combine symbols, images and text to form visual representations of ideas and messages. They use <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> techniques to create <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">visual compositions</a>. Common uses of graphic design include <a href="/wiki/Corporate_design" title="Corporate design">corporate design</a> (logos and branding), editorial design (magazines, newspapers and books), <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">web design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication_design" title="Communication design">communication design</a>, product <a href="/wiki/Packaging_and_labeling" title="Packaging and labeling">packaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Signage" title="Signage">signage</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>While Graphic Design as a discipline has a relatively recent history, first coined by <a href="/wiki/William_Addison_Dwiggins" title="William Addison Dwiggins">William Addison Dwiggins</a> in 1922, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> graphic design-like activities span the history of humankind: from the caves of <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a>, to Rome's <a href="/wiki/Trajan%27s_Column" title="Trajan's Column">Trajan's Column</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscripts</a> of the Middle Ages, to the dazzling neon lights of <a href="/wiki/Ginza" title="Ginza">Ginza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. In "Babylon, artisans pressed cuneiform inscriptions into clay bricks or tablets which were used for construction. The bricks gave information such as the name of the reigning monarch, the builder, or some other dignitary". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Arguably, this could have been identified as the first billboard or road sign announcing the name of the governor of a state or mayor of the city today. The Egyptians developed communication by hieroglyphics which used picture symbols dating as far back as 136 B.C. found on the Rosetta Stone. "The Rosetta stone, found by one of Napoleon's engineers was an advertisement for the Egyptian ruler, Ptolemy as the "true Son of the Sun, the Father of the Moon, and the Keeper of the Happiness of Men"" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> The Egyptians also invented <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, paper made from reeds found along the Nile, on which they transcribed advertisements more common among their people at the time. During the "<a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>", a period of time spanning from 500 <small>AD</small> to 1450 <small>AD</small>, monks kept symbols and writing alive while much of the citizenry did not engage in progressive learning, reading or writing.</p>
<p>In both this lengthy history and in the relatively recent explosion of <a href="/wiki/Visual_communication" title="Visual communication">visual communication</a> in the 20th and 21st centuries, the distinction between <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, art, graphic design and <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine art</a> is sometimes blurred. After all, they share many of the same elements, theories, principles, practices and <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>, and sometimes the same benefactor or client. In <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Advertising,&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Advertising, (page does not exist)">advertising,</a> the ultimate objective is the sale of goods and services. In graphic design, "the essence is to give order to information, form to ideas, expression, and feeling to artifacts that document human experience."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The history of graphic design in the United States began with Benjamin Franklin who used this paper The Pennsylvania Gazette, to master the art of publicity. Not only did he promote his own books, he used the paper to influence what he thought the masses should read, as well. "Benjamin Franklin's ingenuity gained in strength as did his cunning and in 1737 he had replaced his counterpart in Pennsylvania, Andrew Bradford as postmaster and printer after a competition he instituted and therefore won. He showed his prowess by running an ad in his <i>General Magazine and the Historical Chronicle of British Plantations in America</i> (the precursor to the Saturday Evening Post) that stressed the benefits offered by a stove he invented, named called the <i>Pennsylvania Fireplace</i>. His invention is still sold today and is known as <i>the Franklin Stove</i>. "<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>American Advertising was as American as primitive plumbing as it imitated British newspapers and magazines. Newspapers offered 3 blocks of text telling society of the slaves that ran away from their masters. Advertisements were printed in scrambled type and uneven lines which made it difficult to read. It was Benjamin Franklin that changed this by adding 14-point type of the first line of the advertisement; although later it was shortened and centered, making a real heading. Franklin's use of type was masterful; he instinctively knew what <i>"appealed to the eye"</i>. It was Franklin who added illustrations, something that not even London printers had done and was considered most advanced. Benjamin Franklin was the first to invent logos, which were early symbols that announced such advertisements as opticians with golden spectacles. Benjamin Franklin taught the advertisers that the use of detail was important to tell the story of their products. The idea of telling a story grew a monster as some began advertising in 10-20 lines adding color, names, varieties, and sizes of the goods they offered. The early advertisements tell us a lot about the culture, the thoughts and conditions that the colonists faced during the establishment of this great nation in its advertising infancy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trading_cards_in_the_17th_and_18th_century">Trading cards in the 17th and 18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trading cards in the 17th and 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 17th- and 18th-century, advertising moved into trading cards, which had replaced handwritten announcements pasted on walls. This practice became widespread, and since space was limited, the new posters would be pasted over others that had been slapped up only minutes before. A precursor to today's advertising code of ethics developed. It started as an agreement that no advertisement would be posted over another if the paste was still damp (this form of paste dried very slowly). At best these ads gained only a few days of exposure. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were clear examples of bias in <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> <a href="/wiki/Classified_advertising" title="Classified advertising">classified advertising</a> early on when many disparaging notices placed by husbands and masters of slaves when their wives or slaves ran away. The advertisers were not bound by codes of ethics but only through society. The truth was in-between the words that were used. However, the ads of the time did give an accurate picture of society and were used as a way to keep people accountable to themselves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Basic_copyediting" title="Wikipedia:Basic copyediting"><span title="This needs copy editing. (September 2016)">needs copy edit</span></a></i>]</sup> The habits, practices and principles are engraved in advertising and through viewing historical advertisements we reunite with ages past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup> Colonial merchants, like merchants of today, went where the customers were. The trading post was the store and the peddler, a "walking catalog". A quote by <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Farrar" title="Frederic Farrar">Frederic Farrar</a>, historian and newspaper salesman stated in October 1975 that, "Without newspapers there would have been no American Revolution and without advertising there would have been no newspaper".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_advent_of_printing">The advent of printing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The advent of printing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_printing" title="History of printing">History of printing</a></div>
<p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> (618–907) between the 7th and 9th century AD, wood blocks were cut to print on textiles and later to reproduce Buddhist texts. A Buddhist scripture printed in 868 is the earliest known printed book. Beginning in the 11th century, longer scrolls and books were produced using <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a> printing making books widely available during the Song dynasty (960–1279).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although printing from <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a> had been introduced couple of centuries earlier then 17th century. Then also the handbills or trade cards were printed from wood or copper engravings during 17th-18th century. They generally announced the business and its location. Hogarth, a famous painter from England also doubled his skill in engraving being one of the first to design for business trade.</p>
<p>A new age dawned in Mainz Germany, in the year 1448, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Gutenberg" title="Johann Gutenberg">Johann Gutenberg</a> introduced moveable type in a new metal alloy. The invention of the printing press opened up a new era of commerce and trade. Previously, the most common way of advertising was word of mouth. Print expedited advertising methods in an explosive way. In France and London, for example, criers announced products for sale just as ancient Romans had done. This new invention replaced the maddening street noise. Visual representations were on the horizon as tradesmen desired to use this method to convey to the buying public the items they had for sale and their need to persuade the purchasers to use their products.</p>
<p>The printing press made books widely available in Europe. The book design of <a href="/wiki/Aldus_Manutius" title="Aldus Manutius">Aldus Manutius</a> developed the book structure which would become the foundation of western publication design. This era of graphic design is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance_Humanism" title="Renaissance Humanism">Humanist</a> or Old Style. Additionally, it was William Caxton, England's first owner of a printing press that printed religious books and had trouble selling them. That brought him to discover the use of the left over pages and utilize them in announcing the sale of the books and post them on the church doors. This particular form of posting to the doors was termed <i>"squis"</i> or <i>"pin up" posters,</i> in approximately 1612 as the first form of print advertising in Europe<i>.</i> The term <i>Siquis</i> actually came from the Roman era when public notices were posted stating <i>"if anybody..."</i>, which is Latin for <i>"si quis"</i>. These printed announcements were followed by later public registers of <i>wants</i> called <i>want ads</i> and in some areas such as the first periodical in Paris advertising was termed "advices". The "Advices" were what we know today as <i>want ad media</i> or <i>advice columns</i>.</p>
<p>Later in 1638, British North America gets a printing press, from England. The printing press arrived at Harvard University, Cambridge Massachusetts. More then 52 years passed before another printing press arrived in Boston to Benjamin Harris, a London Bookseller who had immigrated to Boston. Benjamin Harris tried his hand at publishing a newspaper in serial form. Harris' attempt at publishing resulted in a paper entitled <i>'Publick Occurrences Both Foreign and Domestick'</i>. It was four pages long and suppressed by the government after its first edition.</p>
<p>John Campbell is credited for the first newspaper, the <i>'Boston News-Letter'</i>, which appeared in 1704. The paper was known during the revolution as <i>"Weeklies"</i>. It was termed so, as it took 13 hours for the printing ink to dry. Therefore, printing both sides of the paper would have required more than a day of drying time alone, plus the time to set the type, print and distribute. <i>'The solution was to first, print the ads and then to print the news on the other side the day before publication. The paper was for pages long having ads on at least 20%-30% of the total paper, (pages one and four) the hot news was located on the inside.'</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> The initial use of the <i>Boston News-Letter</i> carried Campbell's own solicitations for advertising from his readers. Campbell's first paid advertisement was in his third edition, May 7 or 8th, 1704. Two of the first ads were for stolen merchandise which were two anvils and the third was for real estate in Long Island Oyster Bay, Owned by William Bradford, a pioneer printer in New York, the first to sell something of value. <a href="/wiki/William_Bradford_(Colonial_printer)" title="William Bradford (Colonial printer)">William Bradford</a>, later publishes his first newspaper in 1725, New York's first, The New-York Gazette. William Bradford's son, preceded him in Philadelphia publishing the <i>American Weekly Mercury</i>, 1719. <i>The Mercury</i> and William Brooker's <i>Massachusetts Gazette</i>, published a day earlier were the 2 newspapers to be published following the <i>Boston News-Letter</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergence_of_the_design_industry">Emergence of the design industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emergence of the design industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late 19th-century Europe, especially in the United Kingdom, the first official publication of a printed design was released which marked the separation of graphic design from fine art.</p>
<p>In 1849, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cole" title="Henry Cole">Henry Cole</a> became one of the major forces in <a href="/wiki/Design_education" title="Design education">design education</a> in Great Britain, informing the government of the importance of design in his <i>Journal of Design and Manufactures</i>. He organized the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Exhibition" title="Great Exhibition">Great Exhibition</a> as a celebration of modern industrial technology and Victorian design.</p>
<p>From 1891 to 1896, <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>' Kelmscott Press published books that are some of the most significant of the graphic design products of the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a>, and made a very lucrative business of creating books of great stylistic refinement and selling them to the public. Morris created a market for works of graphic design in their own right to create a profession for this new type of art for aspiring individuals who wish to earn a living through these skills. The work of the Kelmscott Press is characterized by its obsession with historical styles. This historicism was, however, important as it amounted to the first significant reaction to the stale state of nineteenth-century graphic design. Morris' work, along with the rest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_Press" title="Private Press">Private Press</a> movement, directly influenced <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> and is indirectly responsible for developments in early twentieth century graphic design in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccarthy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mccarthy-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The term "graphic design" first appeared in print in the 1922 essay "New Kind of Printing Calls for New Design" by <a href="/wiki/William_Addison_Dwiggins" title="William Addison Dwiggins">William Addison Dwiggins</a>, an American book designer in the early 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Raffe's <i>Graphic Design</i>, published in 1927, is considered to be the first book to use "Graphic Design" in its title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The signage in the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> is a classic design example<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> of the modern era and used a typeface designed by Edward Johnston in 1916.</p>
<p>In the 1920s, Soviet <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(art)" title="Constructivism (art)">constructivism</a> applied 'intellectual production' in different spheres of production. The movement saw individualistic art as useless in revolutionary Russia and thus moved towards creating objects for utilitarian purposes. They designed buildings, theater sets, posters, fabrics, clothing, furniture, logos, menus, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jan_Tschichold" title="Jan Tschichold">Jan Tschichold</a> codified the principles of <a href="/wiki/Modern_typography" title="Modern typography">modern typography</a> in his 1928 book, <i>New Typography</i>. He later repudiated the philosophy he espoused in this book as being fascistic, but it remained very influential.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Tschichold, <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> typographers such as <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Bayer" title="Herbert Bayer">Herbert Bayer</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy" title="László Moholy-Nagy">László Moholy-Nagy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/El_Lissitzky" title="El Lissitzky">El Lissitzky</a> have greatly influenced graphic design as we know it today. They pioneered production techniques<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and stylistic devices used throughout the twentieth century. The following years saw graphic design in the modern style gain widespread acceptance and application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A booming post-World War II American economy established a greater need for graphic design, mainly advertising and packaging. The emigration of the German <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> school of design to Chicago in 1937 brought a "mass-produced" minimalism to America; sparking a wild fire of "modern" <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> and design. Notable names in mid-century modern design include <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Frutiger" title="Adrian Frutiger">Adrian Frutiger</a>, designer of the <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a> <a href="/wiki/Univers" title="Univers">Univers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frutiger_(typeface)" title="Frutiger (typeface)">Frutiger</a>; <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rand" title="Paul Rand">Paul Rand</a>, who, from the late 1930s until his death in 1996, took the principles of the Bauhaus and applied them to popular advertising and logo design, helping to create a uniquely American approach to European minimalism while becoming one of the principal pioneers of the subset of graphic design known as <a href="/wiki/Corporate_identity" title="Corporate identity">corporate identity</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Josef_M%C3%BCller-Brockmann" title="Josef Müller-Brockmann">Josef Müller-Brockmann</a>, who designed posters in a severe yet accessible manner typical of the 1950s and 1970s era.</p>
<p>The growth of the professional graphic design industry has grown in parallel with the rise of <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerism</a>. This has raised some concerns and criticisms, notably from within the graphic design community with the <a href="/wiki/First_Things_First_1964_manifesto" title="First Things First 1964 manifesto">First Things First manifesto</a>. First launched by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Garland" title="Ken Garland">Ken Garland</a> in 1964, it was re-published as the <a href="/wiki/First_Things_First_2000_manifesto" title="First Things First 2000 manifesto">First Things First 2000 manifesto</a> in 1999 in the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Emigre_(magazine)" title="Emigre (magazine)">Emigre</a></i> 51<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> stating "We propose a reversal of priorities in favor of more useful, lasting and democratic forms of communication - a mindshift away from product marketing and toward the exploration and production of a new kind of meaning. The scope of debate is shrinking; it must expand. Consumerism is running uncontested; it must be challenged by other perspectives expressed, in part, through the visual languages and resources of design."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Both editions attracted signatures from respected design practitioners and thinkers, for example; <a href="/wiki/Rudy_VanderLans" title="Rudy VanderLans">Rudy VanderLans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erik_Spiekermann" title="Erik Spiekermann">Erik Spiekermann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Lupton" title="Ellen Lupton">Ellen Lupton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rick_Poynor" title="Rick Poynor">Rick Poynor</a>. The 2000 manifesto was also notably published in <a href="/wiki/Adbusters" title="Adbusters">Adbusters</a>, known for its strong critiques of visual culture.</p>

<p>From <a href="/wiki/Traffic_sign" title="Traffic sign">road signs</a> to technical schematics, from interoffice <a href="/wiki/Memorandum" title="Memorandum">memorandums</a> to reference <a href="/wiki/User_guide" title="User guide">manuals</a>, graphic design enhances transfer of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> and visual messages. <a href="/wiki/Readability" title="Readability">Readability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legibility" title="Legibility">legibility</a> is enhanced by improving the visual presentation and layout of text.</p>
<p>Design can also aid in selling a <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">product</a> or <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">idea</a> through effective visual communication. It is applied to products and elements of company identity like <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logos</a>, colors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging" title="Packaging">packaging</a>, and text. Together these are defined as <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">branding</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>). Branding has increasingly become important in the range of services offered by many graphic designers, alongside <a href="/wiki/Corporate_identity" title="Corporate identity">corporate identity</a>. Whilst the terms are often used interchangeably, branding is more strictly related to the identifying mark or trade name for a product or service, whereas corporate identity can have a broader meaning relating to the structure and ethos of a company, as well as to the company's external image. Graphic designers will often form part of a team working on corporate identity and branding projects. Other members of that team can include marketing professionals, communications consultants and commercial writers.</p>
<p>Textbooks are designed to present subjects such as geography, science, and math. These publications have layouts which illustrate <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diagram" title="Diagram">diagrams</a>. A common example of graphics in use to educate is diagrams of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_anatomy" title="Human anatomy">human anatomy</a>. Graphic design is also applied to layout and formatting of educational material to make the information more accessible and more readily understandable.</p>
<p>Graphic wayfinding signage systems have become important for large public spaces such as airports and convention centers. These systems often depend on graphic design to communicate information quickly and economically through a color or symbol that can be read and followed from a distance (as opposed to large amounts of text). Such <a href="/wiki/Environmental_graphic_design" title="Environmental graphic design">environmental graphic design</a> systems allow people to navigate unfamiliar spaces. The term "architectural graphics" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Davis_Doggett" title="Jane Davis Doggett">Jane Davis Doggett</a>, pioneer designer of airport wayfinding systems, but the term more commonly used in 2014 is environmental graphics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Experiential_Graphics_Magazine_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Experiential_Graphics_Magazine-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Graphic design is applied in the <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a> industry in decoration, scenery, and visual story telling. Other examples of design for entertainment purposes include novels, comic books, DVD covers, <a href="/wiki/Opening_credits" title="Opening credits">opening credits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Closing_credits" title="Closing credits">closing credits</a> in <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">filmmaking</a>, and programs and props on stage. This could also include artwork used for T-shirts and other items screenprinted for sale.</p>
<p>From scientific journals to news reporting, the presentation of opinion and facts is often improved with graphics and thoughtful compositions of visual information - known as <a href="/wiki/Information_design" title="Information design">information design</a>. Newspapers, magazines, blogs, television and film documentaries may use graphic design to inform and entertain. With the advent of the web, information designers with experience in interactive tools such as <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Adobe Flash</a> are increasingly being used to illustrate the background to news stories.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Skills">Skills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Skills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A graphic design project may involve the stylization and presentation of existing <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">text</a> and either preexisting <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">imagery</a> or images developed by the graphic designer. Artistic pieces can be incorporated in both traditional and digital form, which involves the use of visual arts, typography, and page layout techniques for publications and marketing. For example, a newspaper story begins with the journalists and photojournalists and then becomes the graphic designer's job to organize the page into a reasonable layout and determine if any other graphic elements should be required. In a magazine article or advertisement, often the graphic designer or <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">art director</a> will commission photographers or illustrators to create original pieces just to be incorporated into the design layout. Or the designer may utilize stock imagery or photography. Contemporary design practice has been extended to the modern computer, for example in the use of <a href="/wiki/WYSIWYG" title="WYSIWYG">WYSIWYG</a> user interfaces, often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Interactive_design" title="Interactive design">interactive design</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia</a> design. Another aspect of graphic design is to have good research skills, analyzing a work of art and simultaneously seeing it in new ways. Graphic Design need skills such as power to convince the audience and selling the design. Communication is a key part in graphic design. The process of graphic design include the "process school" which is an approach to the subject that is concerned with the actual process of communication; it especially highlights the channels and media through which messages are transmitted and by which senders and receivers encode and decode. Semiotic School on the other hand, is message as a construction of signs which through interaction with receivers, produces meaning; communication as an agent. The process school is like the way in which a message is brought out to society.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typography">Typography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Typography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">Typography</a></div>
<p>Typography is the art, craft and techniques of type design, modifying type <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyphs" title="Glyphs">glyphs</a>, and arranging type. Type glyphs (characters) are created and modified using a variety of illustration techniques. The arrangement of type is the selection of typefaces, point size, tracking (the space between all characters used), kerning (the space between two specific characters), and leading (line spacing).</p>
<p>Typography is performed by typesetters, compositors, typographers, graphic artists, art directors, and clerical workers. Until the Digital Age, typography was a specialized occupation. Digitization opened up typography to new generations of visual designers and lay users.</p>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Page_layout">Page layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Page layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The page layout aspect of graphic design deals with the arrangement of elements (content) on a page, such as image placement, and text layout and style. Beginning from early <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated</a> pages in hand-copied books of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and proceeding down to intricate modern magazine and catalogue layouts, structured page design has long been a consideration in printed material and more recently in electronic displays such as web pages. Elements usually consist of <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">type</a> (text), <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a> (pictures), and (with print media) occasionally place-holder graphics for elements that are not printed with ink such as <a href="/wiki/Die_(manufacturing)" title="Die (manufacturing)">die</a>/<a href="/wiki/Laser_cutting" title="Laser cutting">laser cutting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foil_stamping" title="Foil stamping">foil stamping</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embossing_(paper)" title="Embossing (paper)">blind embossing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Printmaking">Printmaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Printmaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">Printmaking</a></div>
<p>Printmaking is the process of making artworks by printing on <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> and other materials or surfaces. Except in the case of monotyping, the process is capable of producing multiples of the same piece, which is called a print. Each piece is not a copy but an original since it is not a reproduction of another work of art and is technically known as an impression. Painting or <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a>, on the other hand, creates a unique original piece of artwork. Prints are created from a single original surface, known technically as a matrix. Common types of matrices include: plates of metal, usually copper or zinc for engraving or etching; stone, used for lithography; blocks of wood for woodcuts, linoleum for linocuts and fabric plates for screen-printing. But there are many other kinds, discussed below. Works printed from a single plate create an edition, in modern times usually each signed and numbered to form a limited edition. Prints may also be published in book form, as artist's books. A single print could be the product of one or multiple techniques.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pencils_hb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pencils_hb.jpg/220px-Pencils_hb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pencils_hb.jpg/330px-Pencils_hb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Pencils_hb.jpg/440px-Pencils_hb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Aside from technology, graphic design requires <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judgment" title="Judgment">judgment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a>. Critical, observational, quantitative and analytic thinking are required for design layouts and <a href="/wiki/Artistic_rendering" title="Artistic rendering">rendering</a>. If the executor is merely following a solution (e.g. sketch, script or instructions) provided by another designer (such as an <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">art director</a>), then the executor is not usually considered the designer.</p>
<p>The method of presentation (e.g. arrangement, style, medium) may be equally important to the design. The appropriate development and presentation tools can substantially change how an audience perceives a project. The image or layout is produced using external <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_(arts)" title="Media (arts)">traditional media</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_drawing_tools" title="Technical drawing tools">guides</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_image_editing" title="Digital image editing">digital image editing</a> tools on computers. Tools in <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a> often take on traditional names such as "scissors" or "<a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">pen</a>". Some graphic design tools such as a <a href="/wiki/Grid_(graphic_design)" title="Grid (graphic design)">grid</a> are used in both traditional and digital form.</p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing" title="Desktop publishing">desktop publishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphic_art_software" title="Graphic art software">graphic art software</a> applications introduced a generation of designers to computer image manipulation and creation that had previously been manually executed. Computer graphic design enabled designers to instantly see the effects of layout or typographic changes, and to simulate the effects of traditional media without requiring a lot of space. However, traditional tools such as <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">pencils</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">markers</a> are useful even when computers are used for finalization; a designer or art director may hand sketch numerous concepts as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_process" title="Creative process">creative process</a>. Some of these sketches may even be shown to a client for early stage approval, before the designer develops the idea further using a computer and graphic design software tools.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">Computers</a> are considered an indispensable tool in the graphic design industry. Computers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_application" title="Software application">software applications</a> are generally seen by <a href="/wiki/Creative_professional" title="Creative professional">creative professionals</a> as more effective production tools than traditional methods. However, some designers continue to use manual and traditional tools for production, such as <a href="/wiki/Milton_Glaser" title="Milton Glaser">Milton Glaser</a>.</p>
<p>New ideas can come by way of experimenting with tools and methods. Some designers explore ideas using pencil and paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Others use many different mark-making tools and resources from computers to sculpture as a means of inspiring creativity. One of the key features of graphic design is that it makes a tool out of appropriate image selection in order to possibly convey meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computers_and_the_creative_process">Computers and the creative process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Computers and the creative process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some debate whether computers enhance the creative process of graphic design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Rapid production from the computer allows many designers to explore multiple ideas quickly with more detail than what could be achieved by traditional hand-rendering or <a href="/wiki/Paste_up" title="Paste up">paste-up</a> on paper, moving the designer through the creative process more quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, being faced with limitless choices does not help isolate the best design solution and can lead to endless iterations with no clear design outcome.</p>
<p>A graphic designer may use <a href="/wiki/Sketch_(drawing)" title="Sketch (drawing)">sketches</a> to explore multiple or complex ideas quickly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> without the distractions and complications of software.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Hand-rendered <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_layout" title="Comprehensive layout">comps</a> are often used to get approval for an idea execution before a designer invests time to produce finished visuals on a computer or in paste-up. The same <a href="/wiki/Thumbnail" title="Thumbnail">thumbnail</a> sketches or rough drafts on paper may be used to rapidly refine and produce the idea on the computer in a hybrid process. This hybrid process is especially useful in <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> design<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> where a software <a href="/wiki/Learning_curve" title="Learning curve">learning curve</a> may detract from a creative thought process. The traditional-design/computer-production hybrid process may be used for freeing one's creativity in <a href="/wiki/Page_layout" title="Page layout">page layout</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_development_(visual_arts)" title="Image development (visual arts)">image development</a> as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the early days of computer publishing, many "traditional" graphic designers relied on computer-savvy <a href="/wiki/Production_artist" title="Production artist">production artists</a> to produce their ideas from sketches, without needing to learn the computer skills themselves. However, this practice has been increasingly less common since the advent of <a href="/wiki/Desktop_publishing" title="Desktop publishing">desktop publishing</a> over 30 years ago. The use of computers and graphics software is now taught in most graphic design courses.</p>
<p>Nearly all of the popular and "industry standard" software programs used for graphic design since the early 1990s are products of <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">Adobe Systems</a> Incorporated. <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">Adobe Photoshop</a> (a raster-based program for photo editing) and <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Illustrator" title="Adobe Illustrator">Adobe Illustrator</a> (a vector-based program for drawing) are often used in the final stage of the electronic design process. Raster images may have been edited in Adobe Photoshop, logos and illustrations in Adobe Illustrator, and the final product assembled in one of the major page layout programs, including <a href="/wiki/Adobe_InDesign" title="Adobe InDesign">Adobe InDesign</a>. Most graphic designers entering the field since about 1990 are expected to be proficient in at least one or two of these programs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_Design_Fields">Related Design Fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Related Design Fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interface_design">Interface design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Interface design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/User_interface_design" title="User interface design">User interface design</a></div>
<p>Since the advent of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and computer software development, many graphic designers have become involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interface_design" title="Interface design">interface design</a>, in an environment commonly referred to as a Graphical User Interface (GUI). This has included <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">web design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_design" title="Software design">software design</a>, when <a href="/wiki/End_user" title="End user">end user</a> <a href="/wiki/Interactivity" title="Interactivity">interactivity</a> is a design consideration of the layout or interface. Combining visual communication skills with the interactive communication skills of user interaction and online branding, graphic designers often work with <a href="/wiki/Software_developer" title="Software developer">software developers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_developer" title="Web developer">web developers</a> to create both the <a href="/wiki/Look_and_feel" title="Look and feel">look and feel</a> of a web site or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_application" title="Software application">software application</a> and enhance the interactive experience of the user or web site visitor. An important aspect of interface design is <a href="/wiki/Icon_design" title="Icon design">icon design</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="User_experience_design">User experience design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: User experience design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/User_experience_design" title="User experience design">User experience design</a></div>
<p>User experience design considers how a user interacts with and responds to an interface, service or product and adjusts it accordingly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiential_graphic_design">Experiential graphic design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Experiential graphic design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Graphic_Design" title="Environmental Graphic Design">Environmental Graphic Design</a></div>
<p>Experiential graphic design is the application of communication skills to the build environment. This area of graphic design requires practitioners to possess knowledge of creating large scale physical installations which have to be manufactured and withstand the same sort of environmental conditions as buildings. As such, it is a cross-disciplinary collaborative process involving designers, fabricators, city planners, architects, manufacturers and large project building teams.</p>
<p>Experiential graphic designers try to solve problems that people encounter while interacting with buildings and space. Examples of practice areas for environmental graphic designers are <a href="/wiki/Wayfinding" title="Wayfinding">wayfinding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placemaking" title="Placemaking">placemaking</a>, branded environments, exhibitions and museum displays, public installations and digital environments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupations">Occupations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Occupations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design_occupations" title="Graphic design occupations">Graphic design occupations</a></div>
<p>Graphic design career paths cover all ends of the <a href="/wiki/Creative_industries" title="Creative industries">creative spectrum</a> and often overlap. Employment within Graphic Design involves workers performing specialized tasks, such as design services, publishing, advertising, and public relations, while receiving a median pay of $53,026.00 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The main job responsibility of a Graphic Designer is the arrangement of visual elements in some type of media. The main job titles within the industry can vary and are often country specific. They can include <a href="/wiki/Graphic_designer" title="Graphic designer">graphic designer</a>, art director, <a href="/wiki/Creative_director" title="Creative director">creative director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">animator</a> and the entry level <a href="/wiki/Production_artist" title="Production artist">production artist</a>. Depending on the industry served, the responsibilities may have different titles such as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTP_associate" title="DTP associate">DTP Associate</a>" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_Artist" title="Graphic Artist">Graphic Artist</a>", but despite changes in title, graphic design principles remain consistent. The responsibilities may come from, or lead to, specialized skills such as <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interactive_design" title="Interactive design">interactive design</a>. Today's graduating graphic design students are normally exposed to all of these areas of graphic design and urged to become familiar with all of them as well in order to be competitive. Graphic designers will face strong competition when applying for positions, in which organizations are looking for candidates with convincing talents and a college-level education; field requirements consist of having strong portfolio and a bachelor's degree. Due to the increase in technology, employment rates within computer systems design are expecting a 35% increase, while traditional designs, such as publications, are facing slower rates of 16% or less.</p>
<p>Graphic designers can work in a variety of environments. Whilst many will work within companies devoted specifically to the industry, such as design consultancies or branding agencies, others may work within publishing, marketing or other communications companies. Increasingly, especially since the introduction of personal computers to the industry, many graphic designers have found themselves working within non-design oriented organizations, as in-house designers. Graphic designers may also work as free-lance designers, working on their own terms, prices, ideas, etc.</p>
<p>A graphic designer reports to the <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">art director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creative_director" title="Creative director">creative director</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senior_media_creative" title="Senior media creative">senior media creative</a>. As a designer becomes more senior, they may spend less time designing media and more time leading and directing other designers on broader creative activities, such as <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> development and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_identity" title="Corporate identity">corporate identity</a> development. They are often expected to interact more directly with clients, for example taking and interpreting briefs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crowdsourcing_in_graphic_design">Crowdsourcing in graphic design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graphic_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Crowdsourcing in graphic design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing_creative_work" title="Crowdsourcing creative work">Crowdsourcing creative work</a></div>
<p>Jeff Howe of Wired Magazine first used the term "crowdsourcing" in his 2006 article, "The Rise of Crowdsourcing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It spans such creative domains as graphic design, architecture, apparel design, writing, illustration etc.; Tasks may be assigned to individuals or a group and may be categorized as convergent or divergent. An example of a divergent task is generating a large number of designs for a poster. An example of a convergent task is selecting one poster design.</p>
