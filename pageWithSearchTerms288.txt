<p><b>Edward Nash Yourdon</b> (April 30, 1944 – January 20, 2016) was an American <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">computer consultant</a>, author and lecturer, and pioneer in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_engineering_methodology" title="Software engineering methodology">software engineering methodology</a>. He was known as one of the lead developers of the <a href="/wiki/Structured_analysis" title="Structured analysis">structured analysis</a> techniques of the 1970s and as a co-developer of the Yourdon/Whitehead method for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-oriented" title="Object-oriented">object-oriented</a> analysis/design in the late 1980s and the Coad/Yourdon methodology for object-oriented analysis/design in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio99_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio99-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yourdon obtained his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">B.S.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">applied mathematics</a> from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) in 1965, and did graduate work in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> at MIT and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polytechnic_Institute_of_New_York_University" title="Polytechnic Institute of New York University">Polytechnic Institute of New York</a>.</p>
<p>In 1964 Yourdon started working at <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> developing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FORTRAN" title="FORTRAN">FORTRAN</a> programs for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PDP-5" title="PDP-5">PDP-5</a> minicomputer and later <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembler</a> for the <a href="/wiki/PDP-8" title="PDP-8">PDP-8</a>. Later in the 1960s and early 1970s after working at a small consulting firm and as independent consultant, in 1974 Yourdon founded his own consulting firm, YOURDON Inc. to provide educational, publishing, and consulting services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio99_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio99-1">[1]</a></sup> After he sold this firm in 1986 he served on the Board of multiple IT consultancy corporations, and was advisor on several research project in the software industry throughout the 1990s.</p>
<p>In June 1997, Yourdon was inducted into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Hall_of_Fame&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Computer Hall of Fame (page does not exist)">Computer Hall of Fame</a>, along with such notables as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Martin_(author)" title="James Martin (author)">James Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grace_Hopper" title="Grace Hopper">Grace Hopper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Weinberg" title="Gerald Weinberg">Gerald Weinberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHFYourdon_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHFYourdon-2">[2]</a></sup> In December 1999 the <i>Crosstalk: The Journal of Defense Software Engineering,</i> named him one of the ten most influential people in the software field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1990s, Yourdon became the center of controversy over his beliefs that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y2K" title="Y2K">Y2K</a>-related computer problems could result in severe software failures that would culminate in widespread social collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to the efforts of Yourdon and thousands of dedicated technologists, developers and project managers, these potential critical system failure points were successfully remediated, thus avoiding the problems Yourdon and others identified early enough to make a difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the new millennium, Yourdon became Faculty Fellow at the Information Systems Research Center of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Texas" title="University of North Texas">University of North Texas</a> as well as Fellow of the Business Technology Trends Council for the <a href="/wiki/Cutter_Consortium" title="Cutter Consortium">Cutter Consortium</a>, where he also was editor of the Cutter IT Journal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After developing <a href="/wiki/Structured_analysis" title="Structured analysis">structured analysis</a> techniques of the 1970s, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-oriented" title="Object-oriented">object-oriented</a> analysis/design in the late 1980s and 1990s, in the new millennium Yourdon has specialized in <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_engineering_methodologies" title="Software engineering methodologies">software engineering methodologies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a> development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also founded and published <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Programmer" title="American Programmer">American Programmer</a> magazine (now titled <i>Cutter IT Journal</i>). He is the author of the book <i><a href="/wiki/Decline_and_Fall_of_the_American_Programmer" title="Decline and Fall of the American Programmer">Decline and Fall of the American Programmer</a></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yourdon_Inc.">Yourdon Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Yourdon Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, Yourdon founded the consulting firm Yourdon Inc. in New York, which provided consulting, educational and publishing in the field of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>. In the early 1980s, the company had multiple offices in North America and Europe and a staff of 150 people. They trained over 250,000 people in the topics of structured programming, structured design, structured analysis, logical data modeling and project management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio99_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio99-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, Yourdon sold the consulting company. It later became part of the Canadian (Québec) software company <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CGI_Informatique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CGI Informatique (page does not exist)">CGI Informatique</a>, which is now itself part of <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>. The publishing division had published over 150 books on software engineering topics before it became part of Prentice Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio99_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bio99-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yourdon_structured_method">Yourdon structured method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yourdon structured method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1980s Yourdon developed the Yourdon structured method (YSM) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSADM" title="SSADM">SSADM</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Function_model" title="Function model">functional structuring</a>. The method supports two distinct design phases: analysis and design. YSM includes three discrete steps: the feasibility study; essential modeling; and implementation modeling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It offers a series of models:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The behavioral model: states that system behavior can be described in three ways: functions, dynamics and relationships.</li>
<li>The processor environment model (PEM): describes the allocation of computing functions in processor hardware.</li>
<li>The software environment model (SEM): defines the software architecture and its effects from each processor.</li>
<li>The code organizational model (COM): shows the modular structure of each task</li>
</ul>
<p>The Yourdon structured method (YSM) and <a href="/wiki/Structured_analysis_and_design_technique" title="Structured analysis and design technique">structured analysis and design technique</a> (SADT) are examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structured_design" title="Structured design">structured design</a> methods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Year_2000_.28Y2K.29_problem">Year 2000 (Y2K) problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Year 2000 (Y2K) problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the late 1990s, he was one of the leading proponents of the theory that the '<a href="/wiki/Year_2000_problem" title="Year 2000 problem">Y2K bug</a>' could lead to a collapse of civilization, or at least protracted economic depression and technological breakdown on a wide scale. He wrote several books on the subject, including <i>Time Bomb 2000</i> (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0130205192">ISBN 0-13-020519-2</a>), and produced at least one video putting forth that theory (and offering advice on how to survive the coming crisis). Yourdon took a lot of heat when his dire predictions (vigorously refuted by many experts in advance) failed to materialize in any form. This blunder may have caused him to lose credibility with many in the software industry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_and_death">Final years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Final years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his final years, Yourdon served as an internationally recognized expert witness and computer consultant specializing in project management, software engineering methodologies, and Web 2.0 development. He died on January 20, 2016, as a result of a post-surgical blood infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Yourdon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yourdon was married to Toni Nash. He had three children; daughter Jennifer, and sons Jamie and David. He also had four grandchildren; Liam Christopher, Owen Edward, Edward Roland ("Teddy"), and Elliot Ann. Yourdon had five sisters; Toni, Teri, Tina, Aleda, and Patrice.</p>
