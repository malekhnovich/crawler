<p><b>Barry W. Boehm</b> (born 1935) is an American <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, distinguished professor <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about.usc.edu_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-about.usc.edu-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of computer science, industrial and systems engineering; the TRW Professor of Software Engineering; and founding director of the Center for Systems and Software Engineering at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>. He is known for his many contributions to the area of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boehm received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1957, and a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">M.S.</a> in 1961, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> in 1964, both in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boe97_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boe97-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1955 he started working as a programmer-analyst at <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a>. In 1959 he switched to the <a href="/wiki/RAND_Corporation" title="RAND Corporation">RAND Corporation</a>, where he was head of the Information Sciences Department until 1973. From 1973 to 1989 he was chief scientist of the Defense Systems Group at <a href="/wiki/TRW_Inc." title="TRW Inc.">TRW Inc.</a>. From 1989 to 1992 he served within the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD) as director of the <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> Information Science and Technology Office, and as director of the DDR&amp;E Software and Computer Technology Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boe97_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boe97-3">[3]</a></sup> Since 1992 he is TRW Professor of Software Engineering, Computer Science Department, and director, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">USC</a> Center for Systems and Software Engineering, formerly Center for Software Engineering.</p>
<p>He has served on the board of several scientific journals, including the IEEE Transactions on Software Engineering, Computer, IEEE Software, ACM Computing Reviews, Automated Software Engineering, Software Process, and Information and Software Technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boe97_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boe97-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent awards for Barry Boehm include the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Secretary_of_Defense_Award_for_Excellence" title="Office of the Secretary of Defense Award for Excellence">Office of the Secretary of Defense Award for Excellence</a> in 1992, the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Quality" title="American Society for Quality">ASQC</a> Lifetime Achievement Award in 1994, the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> Distinguished Research Award in Software Engineering in 1997, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> International <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Award" title="Stevens Award">Stevens Award</a>. He is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIAA" title="AIAA">AIAA</a> Fellow, an <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="List of Fellows of the Association for Computing Machinery">ACM Fellow</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> Fellow, and a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>. He received the Mellon Award for Excellence in Mentoring in 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the IEEE Simon Ramo Medal in 2010. He was appointed as a distinguished professor on January 13, 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about.usc.edu_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-about.usc.edu-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boehm's research interests include <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development process</a> modeling, software <a href="/wiki/Requirements_engineering" title="Requirements engineering">requirements engineering</a>, software <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architectures</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_metrics" title="Software metrics">software metrics</a> and cost models, <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> environments, and knowledge-based software engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boe97_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boe97-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>His contributions to the field, according to Boehm (1997) himself, include "the Constructive Cost Model (<a href="/wiki/COCOMO" title="COCOMO">COCOMO</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Spiral_model" title="Spiral model">Spiral Model</a> of the software process, the Theory W (win-win) approach to software management and requirements determination and two advanced software engineering environments: the TRW Software Productivity System and Quantum Leap Environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boe97_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Boe97-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_versus_hardware_costs">Software versus hardware costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Software versus hardware costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an important 1973 report entitled "Ada - The Project : The DoD High Order Language Working Group" to the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">DARPA</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Boehm predicted that software costs would overwhelm hardware costs. DARPA had expected him to predict that hardware would remain the biggest problem, encouraging them to invest in even larger computers. The report inspired a change of direction in computing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_economics">Software economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Software economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

It relates software development effort for a program, in Person-Months (PM), to <i>Thousand Source Lines of Code</i> (KSLOC).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spiral_model">Spiral model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spiral model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spiral_model_(Boehm,_1988).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="730" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png/160px-Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png/240px-Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png/320px-Spiral_model_%28Boehm%2C_1988%29.png 2x" width="160"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Boehm also created the <a href="/wiki/Spiral_model" title="Spiral model">spiral model</a> of software development, in which the phases of development are repeatedly revisited. This iterative <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development process</a> influenced <a href="/wiki/MBASE" title="MBASE">MBASE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wideband_Delphi">Wideband Delphi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wideband Delphi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boehm refined the <a href="/wiki/Delphi_method" title="Delphi method">Delphi method</a> of estimation to include more group iteration, making it more suitable for certain classes of problems, such as software development. This variant is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wideband_Delphi" title="Wideband Delphi">Wideband Delphi</a> method.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incremental_Commitment_Model">Incremental Commitment Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Boehm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Incremental Commitment Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Incremental Commitment Model (ICM) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is a system design, developmental, and evolution process for the 21st Century systems. The systems' types cover a wide range from COTS based systems to "routine" Information Systems to human intensive and life or safety critical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> It was only in 1998, after the development of the ICM that Barry Boehm along with A Winsor Brown started to focus on reconciling it with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=WinWin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="WinWin (page does not exist)">WinWin</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spiral_Model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spiral Model (page does not exist)">Spiral Model</a> and its incarnation in <a href="/wiki/MBASE" title="MBASE">MBASE</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the follow-on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lean_MBASE&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lean MBASE (page does not exist)">Lean MBASE</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and working towards an Incremental Commitment Model for Software (ICMS) by adapting the existing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=WinWin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="WinWin (page does not exist)">WinWin</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spiral_Model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spiral Model (page does not exist)">Spiral Model</a> support tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2008, the evolving ICM for Software with its risk-driven anchor point decisions, proved very useful to several projects which ended up having unusual life cycle phase sequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
