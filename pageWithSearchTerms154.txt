<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraneous_examples">Extraneous examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extraneous examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The many pages for specific Design Patterns have a chronic excess of examples. Many pages are more than 80% example; at least <a href="/wiki/Singleton_pattern" title="Singleton pattern">one pattern</a> has examples in 13 languages with a total of 16 examples! While some examples are different in important ways between families of languages (such as strongly-typed versus dynamically-typed), I have a strong feeling that language advocates are adding me-too examples that detract from explaining the particular pattern. I think Java is probably the best example language for OO design patterns since Java examples won't be cluttered with memory-management code but are strongly typed, so it is clear what inherits what. Full disclosure: My own personal languages of choice are C++ and Python and I am not too familiar with Java. I've changed a few <i>algorithm</i> examples from pseudocode to Python when a working Python example could be almost as easy to understand as pseudocode. However, for OO design patterns, I really think a widely-used strongly-typed garbage-collected language is most appropriate, and that leaves me with Java.</p>
<p>If people feel strongly about having examples in every possible language, I would argue that the examples should be forked off onto separate pages or moved to wikibooks. But certainly 16 examples in 13 languages is not the best way to describe the <a href="/wiki/Singleton_pattern" title="Singleton pattern">singleton pattern</a>. <a href="/wiki/User:BenFrantzDale" title="User:BenFrantzDale">—Ben FrantzDale</a> (<a href="/wiki/User_talk:BenFrantzDale" title="User talk:BenFrantzDale">talk</a>) 13:47, 8 April 2009 (UTC)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_pattern_category">Fundamental pattern category</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fundamental pattern category">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>I'm not sure about considering encapsulation, inheritance, and exceptions to be design patterns. Encapsulation and inheritance are general directions for structuring code more than they are patterns, re-usable elements of software. They are philosophies more than patterns or blueprints. Patterns (not limited to computer science) must be unanimously recognizable. For example, an architectural design's use of the golden ratio is non-disputable. I've never seen "fundamental patterns" used anywhere outside of that cited college lecture presentation and the book it cites, Barbara Liskov's "Program Development in Java". It strikes me that the author wished to coin a new term; perhaps someone who read it can provide another opinion. My [<a class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/webhp?hl=en&amp;btnG=Google+Search#hl=en&amp;q=%22fundamental+pattern%22&amp;btnG=Google+Search&amp;aq=f&amp;oq=%22fundamental+pattern%22&amp;aqi=&amp;fp=1mZ_-PL2Zjc%7CGoogle" rel="nofollow">query</a>] yielded few relevant results. Has the term 'fundamental pattern' gained community acceptance or popular usage? And if so, do—and why do—encapsulation and inheritance qualify? Any thoughts? Also, the page <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_pattern" title="Fundamental pattern">Fundamental pattern</a> lists <a href="/wiki/Proxy_pattern" title="Proxy pattern">Proxy pattern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Facade_pattern" title="Facade pattern">Facade pattern</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Composite_pattern" title="Composite pattern">Composite pattern</a>; all three of which are listed as structural patterns in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_pattern_(computer_science)" title="Design pattern (computer science)">this page</a>; and it doesn't list inheritance or encapsulation. <a href="/wiki/User:Dmyersturnbull" title="User:Dmyersturnbull"> <font color="#005000">dm</font><font color="#555555">yers</font><font color="#005000">t</font><font color="#555555">urnbull</font> </a> ⇒ <a href="/wiki/User_talk:Dmyersturnbull" title="User talk:Dmyersturnbull">talk</a> 03:59, 4 June 2009 (UTC)</p>

<p>In the field of computer science, there exist some criticisms regarding the concept of design patterns.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Workarounds_for_missing_language_features">Workarounds for missing language features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Workarounds for missing language features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Users of <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_programming_language" title="Dynamic programming language">dynamic programming languages</a> have discussed many design patterns as workarounds for the limitations of languages such as <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a> and <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norvig_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norvig-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Visitor_pattern" title="Visitor pattern">Visitor pattern</a> need not be implemented in a language that supports <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multimethods" title="Multimethods">multimethods</a>. The purpose of Visitor is to add new operations to existing classes without modifying those classes. In C++, a class is declared as a syntactic structure with a specific and closed set of methods. In a language with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multimethods" title="Multimethods">multimethods</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Common_Lisp" title="Common Lisp">Common Lisp</a>, methods for a class are outside of the class structure, and one may add new methods without modifying it.</p>
<p>Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Decorator_pattern" title="Decorator pattern">Decorator pattern</a> amounts to implementing dynamic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delegation_(programming)" title="Delegation (programming)">delegation</a>, as found in Common Lisp, <a href="/wiki/Objective-C" title="Objective-C">Objective-C</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self_(programming_language)" title="Self (programming language)">Self</a> and <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>.</p>
<p>Many patterns imply <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-orientation</a> or more generally mutable state, and so are meaningless in <a href="/wiki/Functional_programming" title="Functional programming">functional programming</a> style, in which data is immutable or treated as such. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Iterator_pattern" title="Iterator pattern">Iterator pattern</a> is a generalisation of <a href="/wiki/For_loop" title="For loop">'for' loops</a>, an inherently <a href="/wiki/Procedural_programming" title="Procedural programming">imperative</a> notion.</p>

For instance, cognitive scientists would talk about "mental representations", and could ask questions that design pattern people could not, like are some mental representations more "difficult" than others, such as, do they take up more processing time? This could then have relevance to software engineering because it could tell us, somewhat, if some "design patterns" give us more trouble than others). In other words, software design patterns (Gang of Four, Alexander's ideas, etc.) to me seem disconnected from the rest of our knowledge. This is a bad thing. If anything it justifies them less.</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="design_patterns_vs_algorithms">design patterns <i>vs</i> algorithms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: design patterns vs algorithms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If a design pattern is a "a general reusable <b>solution</b> to a commonly occurring problem", then in what way(s) does it differ from an algorithm? This article doesn't do much to explain exactly <i>what</i> a design pattern is. A simple example would be a good start. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=User:WilliamSommerwerck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="User:WilliamSommerwerck (page does not exist)">WilliamSommerwerck</a> (<a href="/wiki/User_talk:WilliamSommerwerck" title="User talk:WilliamSommerwerck">talk</a>) 19:13, 7 September 2011 (UTC)</p>
<dl>
<dd>As a quick answer, I would say that design patterns differ from algorithm in that design patterns solve software engineering problems rather than computer science problems. That is, they are templates for managing complexity that arrives from interaction among software components. There are many ways to implement <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depth_first_search" title="Depth first search">depth first search</a>, so even though the algorithm may be the same, on implementation may be far preferable from a software-design perspective in terms of software reusability, etc. That's a sketch of an answer, anyway. 20:38, 7 September 2011 (UTC)</dd>
<dd>Looking up <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a>, it is described as "a finite list of well-defined instructions", I would say that is different enough. Unfortunately, design patterns are an advanced subject and this is partly why explaining them is so difficult. Typically, one or more design patterns may be used in an algorithm solution expressed in a particular computer language. Books are written on the subject.</dd>
<dd>Like classic Chinese martial arts movies, one needs good <a href="/wiki/Kung_fu_(term)" title="Kung fu (term)">kung fu (term)</a>, not just basic knowledge of how to move your arms and legs. – <a href="/wiki/User:Rfrankla" title="User:Rfrankla">rfrankla</a> (<a href="/wiki/User_talk:Rfrankla" title="User talk:Rfrankla">talk</a>) 10:42, 21 April 2014 (UTC)</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languish.3F">Languish?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Languish?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Although design patterns have been applied practically for a long time, formalization of the concept of design patterns languished for several years..."</p>
<p>Languished? For several years? I *think* what is meant here is that "Although design patterns have been applied practically for a long time, it was only recently that they were formalized." <a href="/wiki/User:GeneCallahan" title="User:GeneCallahan">GeneCallahan</a> (<a href="/wiki/User_talk:GeneCallahan" title="User talk:GeneCallahan">talk</a>) 18:34, 23 December 2014 (UTC)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="IVSR">IVSR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: IVSR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In several design patterns the C# examples have been changed to include the text IVSR, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mediator_pattern" title="Mediator pattern">Mediator pattern</a>. What is IVSR? When searching online I don't get any useful results. Could it be that someone is trying to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:PROMO" title="Wikipedia:PROMO">self promote</a>? If so, it needs to be removed. Otherwise it might be useful to explain what IVSR is. <small class="autosigned">— Preceding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Signatures" title="Wikipedia:Signatures">unsigned</a> comment added by <a href="/wiki/Special:Contributions/46.44.173.1" title="Special:Contributions/46.44.173.1">46.44.173.1</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=User_talk:46.44.173.1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="User talk:46.44.173.1 (page does not exist)">talk</a>) 12:02, 24 February 2015 (UTC)</small></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The History section ends with the following claim:"Although design patterns have been applied practically for a long time, formalization of the concept of design patterns languished for several years." The reference given does NOT (as far as I can see from <i>Baroni, et al's</i> .pdf) support the claim. Also, the claim is so vague as to be meaningless. Languished when? between 1700 and 1900? Between 1987 and 1991? Note that Kent Beck's home page (cited in Baroni) at <a class="external free" href="http://c2.com/ppr/about/author/kent.html" rel="nofollow">http://c2.com/ppr/about/author/kent.html</a> makes a couple of claims that could verify his contention that he spoke about the use of formal design patterns for building software. But it is certainly not peer reviewed and is imho a dubious claim to primacy (see below); is it sufficiently authoritative to be used as a fact? Seems too self-serving to me (no offense intended). The principle problem I have is that, obviously, patterns have been used in software at least since machine language was invented (since a language IS a group of patterns). (and of course hardware contains at its core a repeated pattern of circuits...) So, unless there is careful definition of the term which distinguishes it from language (and a number of other methods/procedures/venues/abstractions) I don't see how you can identify the start of it (surely some of the software development houses used formal design templates prior to 1987!) other than to claim that the GoF 1995 paper is the "recognized" start, in general. I recommend that sentence be deleted. It serves no real purpose, afaiks.<a href="/wiki/Special:Contributions/216.96.76.54" title="Special:Contributions/216.96.76.54">216.96.76.54</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=User_talk:216.96.76.54&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="User talk:216.96.76.54 (page does not exist)">talk</a>) 13:11, 20 July 2015 (UTC)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk:Software_design_pattern&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the definition given by the HillSide group, the definition given (at the top of the wiki page) of a design pattern is incorrect: <a class="external free" href="http://hillside.net/patterns/50-patterns-library/patterns/222-design-pattern-definition" rel="nofollow">http://hillside.net/patterns/50-patterns-library/patterns/222-design-pattern-definition</a></p>
<p>Note that the HillSide group stands as the governing body of patterns for the software world. Also note the Richard (Dick) Peter Gabriel (aka RPG) (director of the HillSide group, and also known for "worse is better") has analysed the work of Christopher Alexander (from whence patterns came) exhaustively, one example being RPG's book, "Patterns of Software": <a class="external free" href="http://dreamsongs.com/Books.html" rel="nofollow">http://dreamsongs.com/Books.html</a></p>
