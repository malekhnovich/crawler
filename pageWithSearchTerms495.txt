<p><b>Level design</b>, <b>Environment Design</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>game mapping</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is a discipline of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_development" title="Game development">game development</a> involving creation of <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Level_(computer_and_video_games)" title="Level (computer and video games)">levels</a>—locales, stages, or missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt1-4">[4]</a></sup> This is commonly done using a <a href="/wiki/Level_editor" title="Level editor">level editor</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Game_development_tool" title="Game development tool">game development software</a> designed for building levels; however, some games feature built-in level editing tools. Level design is both an artistic and technical process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early days of video games, a single <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" title="Game programmer">programmer</a> would create the maps and layouts for a game, and a discipline or profession dedicated solely to level design did not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early games often featured a level system of ascending difficulty as opposed to progression of story-line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first game genre that required significant amounts of time to design areas were text-based games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt3-7">[7]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/MUD" title="MUD">MUDs</a>. Often, promoted users were assigned to create new paths, new rooms, new equipment, and new actions, often using the game interface itself. <a href="/wiki/ZZT" title="ZZT">ZZT</a> is another early game notable for its user-accessible mapping and event triggering/scripting<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>1983's <i><a href="/wiki/Lode_Runner" title="Lode Runner">Lode Runner</a></i> was one of the first titles to ship with a level editor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGWcontest_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CGWcontest-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and its designer, Douglas Smith, reputedly paid neighborhood children to design levels for the game.</p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doom_(video_game)" title="Doom (video game)">Doom</a></i> (1993) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doom_II" title="Doom II">Doom II</a></i> (1994) were two of the first games to attract focused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mod_(computer_gaming)" title="Mod (computer gaming)">game modding</a> activity, and many <a href="/wiki/Doom_WAD" title="Doom WAD">WAD level files</a> were made for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt3_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt3-7">[7]</a></sup> One of the reasons was a clear separation between the level files and game engine itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt3_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt3-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)#Mods" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quake_III_Arena#Mods" title="Quake III Arena">Quake 3</a>, and many other games have notable mapping tools and communities<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="some community links/examples maybe (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> focusing on <a href="/wiki/User-generated_content" title="User-generated content">user-generated content</a>.</p>
<p>In certain games, such as <a href="/wiki/Roguelike" title="Roguelike">roguelike</a> games, levels may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procedurally_generated" title="Procedurally generated">procedurally generated</a>. In these cases, the original game programmer controls how the variations of rooms and tunnels are formed, by tweaking the <a href="/wiki/Random_seed" title="Random seed">randomly seeded</a> algorithms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg/256px-Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg/384px-Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg/512px-Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg 2x" width="256"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Warzone_2100_-_EditWorld_-_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The level editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Warzone_2100" title="Warzone 2100">Warzone 2100</a></i>.</div>

<p>Level design for each individual level in a modern game typically starts with concept art, sketches, renderings, and physical models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Once completed, these concepts transform into extensive documentation, environment modeling, and the placing of game specific entities (actors), usually with the aid of a level editor.</p>
<p>A level editor may be distributed as a complete stand-alone package, at times, rivaling commercial 3D modelling software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup> There are various steps involved in laying out a map and these steps may vary dramatically across the many different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_and_video_game_genres" title="Computer and video game genres">game genres</a> that exist today.</p>
<p>General steps include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Laying out the large-scale features of the map, such as hills, cities, rooms, tunnels, etc., for players and enemies to move around in;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Determining environmental conditions and "ground rules" such as day/night, weather, scoring systems, allowable weapons or gameplay types, time limits, and starting resources.</li>
<li>Specifying certain regions where certain gameplay activities or behaviors occur, such as resource harvesting, base building, water travelling, etc.;</li>
<li>Specifying non-static parts of a level, such as doors, keys and buttons with associated mechanisms, teleporters, hidden passageways, etc.;</li>
<li>Specifying locations of various entities, such as player units, enemies, monster spawn points, ladders, coins, resource nodes, weapons, save points,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> etc.;</li>
<li>Specifying the start and exit locations for one or more players;</li>
<li>Adding aesthetic details such as level-specific <a href="/wiki/Texture_mapping" title="Texture mapping">graphic textures</a>, sounds, animation, lighting and music;</li>
<li>Introducing <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">scripted</a> event locations, where certain actions by the player can trigger specified changes;</li>
<li>Placing <a href="/wiki/Pathfinding" title="Pathfinding">pathfinding</a> nodes that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-player_characters" title="Non-player characters">non-player characters</a> take as they walk around, the actions they will take in response to specific triggers, and any dialog they might have with the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The first level of the game usually designed to get players to explore the mechanics of the game, notably in <a href="/wiki/World_1-1" title="World 1-1">World 1-1</a> of <a href="/wiki/Super_Mario_Bros." title="Super Mario Bros.">Super Mario Bros.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut_scene" title="Cut scene">Cut scenes</a> may be triggered by events in a level, but require distinctly different skills, and may be created by a different person or team.</p>
<p>The Level Design Process may be iterated several times before achieving the desired outcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Level designers and/or concept artists may also be required to provide a pre-rendered map of the level (or entire game world) for the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Level_bugs">Level bugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Level bugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many map bugs that level designers try to avoid, but sometimes go unnoticed for some time.</p>
<p>A player might get stuck in map geometry with no way to escape or to die. A player might be able to find a specific spot where they do not have to move to gain <a href="/wiki/Experience_point" title="Experience point">experience</a>, because monsters are constantly spawned but can be easily and immediately killed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In multiplayer maps, a player may be able to reach areas of the map designed to be inaccessible, for example, reaching an advantageous rooftop position and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camping_(computer_gaming)" title="Camping (computer gaming)">camping</a> other players. In the worst case, a player might be able to fall out-of-bounds of a map where other players cannot reach them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Invisible_wall" title="Invisible wall">Invisible walls</a> are cited to be level design bugs, and might be "left-over geometry" from an earlier version of the level or an object's improperly aligned "collision box".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some cases, specific mapping tools can be designed to automatically detect problems such as falling "outside" a level, and reaching "stuck" areas. Careful level designers run these tools as the last step before releasing a new version of a level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In most cases, the best way to improve a map is by playtesting it with experienced players, and allowing them to try to exploit any problems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Level_designer">Level designer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Level designer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A level designer is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_designer" title="Game designer">game designer</a> who creates environments and scenarios using a level editor and other tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreNovak76_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-MooreNovak76-20">[20]</a></sup> Level designers will usually work on a level from pre-production to completion; working with both incomplete and complete versions of the game. <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" title="Game programmer">Game programmers</a> usually produce level editors and design tools for the designers to use. This eliminates the need for designers to access or modify <a href="/wiki/Codebase" title="Codebase">game code</a>. As opposed to the level editing tools sometimes available to the community, level designers often work with placeholders and <a href="/wiki/Software_prototyping" title="Software prototyping">prototypes</a> aiming for level consistency and clear layout before required <a href="/wiki/Video_game_art" title="Video game art">artwork</a> is produced by game artists. Many level designers have skills as both a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_art" title="Visual art">visual artist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_designer" title="Game designer">game designers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlesBlog_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-BlesBlog-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreNovak76_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-MooreNovak76-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> although in recent years the responsibility for visual, structural and gameplay related tasks has been increasingly divided among several specialists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_level_designers">Notable level designers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable level designers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of individuals have made significant contributions to the field of PC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_person_shooter" title="First person shooter">first person shooter</a> levels. These level designers include: <a href="/wiki/John_Romero" title="John Romero">John Romero</a>, responsible for a great deal of the level design for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doom_(video_game)" title="Doom (video game)">Doom</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gray_(game_designer)" title="Richard Gray (game designer)">Richard "Levelord" Gray</a>, creator of a number of levels for <i><a href="/wiki/Duke_Nukem_3D" title="Duke Nukem 3D">Duke Nukem 3D</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/SiN" title="SiN">SiN</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_goals">Design goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Level design is necessary for two primary purposes - providing players with a goal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates111112_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bates111112-22">[22]</a></sup> and providing players with enjoyable play experience. Good level design strives to produce quality gameplay, provide an <a href="/wiki/Immersion_(virtual_reality)" title="Immersion (virtual reality)">immersive</a> experience, and sometimes, especially in story-based games, to advance the storyline. Skilled use of textures and audio is necessary to produce immersive player experience.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay_alteration">Gameplay alteration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gameplay alteration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maps' design can significantly impact the gameplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates111112_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bates111112-22">[22]</a></sup> For example, the gameplay may be shifted towards a platformer (by careful placement of platforms) or a puzzle game<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> (by extensive use of buttons, keys, and doors). Some FPS maps may be designed to prevent <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">sniping</a> by not including any long hallways, while other maps may allow for a mix of sniping and closer combat.</p>
<p><i>Gimmick maps</i> are sometimes created to explore selected features of gameplay, such as sniping or fist fighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> While they are briefly useful to level designers and interesting to experienced players, they are usually not included in final list of levels of the game because of their limited replay value.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Player_directing">Player directing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Player directing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Levels are generally constructed with flow control in mind,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> that is directing the player towards the goal of the level and preventing confusion and idling. This can be accomplished by various means.</p>
<p>Often the level layouts features <a href="/wiki/Power-up" title="Power-up">power-ups</a> and items aligned in path and combinations that collecting them inevitably progresses the game and advances the story-line. This is one of the basic player direction technique and is most often seen in platformers.</p>
<p>Lighting and illumination, as well as distinctly coloured objects are often used to unambiguously steer the player towards the correct path. Similarly, clearly marked choke-points can be introduced.</p>
<p>Another method is strategic placement of obstacles and aesthetic environment props, that direct the player's attention to "clear" paths instead. This is often used in closed, "stuffed" environments.</p>
<p>Levels may be designed to force the players to explore the map and advance. Most RTS maps give each player a starting base, but will have resource distribution and terrain features designed to draw players out of their base and engage each other. Teamplay maps can provide noticeable advantages to one team over another, when designed poorly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hidden_features">Hidden features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hidden features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Level designers sometimes create hidden rooms and areas that usually require more effort for the player to reach or to notice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt2-27">[27]</a></sup> These usually give some additional rewards, such as ammo or powerups. Casual players usually do not discover these, but these areas are interesting enough to be discovered and documented by dedicated gamers. Sometimes, they serve as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(virtual)" title="Easter egg (virtual)">easter eggs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt2_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt2-27">[27]</a></sup> containing messages such as the level designers' names or pictures, or political or humorous messages. One of the first games with a 3D engine to feature hidden features was <a href="/wiki/Wolfenstein_3D" title="Wolfenstein 3D">Wolfenstein 3D</a>, where certain walls could be "pushed" to reveal hidden passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShahArt2_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-ShahArt2-27">[27]</a></sup> For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i> has many secret areas that reward the player with ammo, weapons, quad damage powerups, and in one hard-to-reach secret area, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Dopefish" title="Dopefish">Dopefish</a> makes an appearance. In fact, the hardest difficulty level, titled "Nightmare", is only reachable through a secret portal in the fourth dimension's entrance hall.</p>
<p>Sometimes, a whole level may be designed as a <a href="/wiki/Level_(video_gaming)#Secret_levels" title="Level (video gaming)">secret level</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Level_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A wide variety of tools may be used by someone designing a level. Although it is faster to design models and textures with general purpose multimedia creation tools, games usually require the data to be in a unique format suited for that <a href="/wiki/Game_engine" title="Game engine">game's engine</a>. For this, specific compilers and converters of models, textures, and audio data may be required to lay out a level.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve Corporation</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hammer_Editor" title="Hammer Editor">Hammer Editor</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Epic_Games" title="Epic Games">Epic</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UnrealEd" title="UnrealEd">UnrealEd</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unreal_Development_Kit" title="Unreal Development Kit">UDK</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BioWare" title="BioWare">BioWare</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_Toolset" title="Aurora Toolset">Aurora Toolset</a></li>
