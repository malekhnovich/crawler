<p><b>Ivar Hjalmar Jacobson</b> (born 1939) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_people" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, known as major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language" title="Unified Modeling Language">UML</a>, <a href="/wiki/Objectory" title="Objectory">Objectory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rational_Unified_Process" title="Rational Unified Process">Rational Unified Process</a> (RUP), <a href="/wiki/Aspect-oriented_software_development" title="Aspect-oriented software development">aspect-oriented software development</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Essence_(OMG_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Essence (OMG standard) (page does not exist)">Essence</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ivar Jacobson was born in <a href="/wiki/Ystad" title="Ystad">Ystad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> on 2 September 1939. He received his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masters_degree" title="Masters degree">Master</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_Engineering" title="Electrical Engineering">Electrical Engineering</a> degree at <a href="/wiki/Chalmers_University_of_Technology" title="Chalmers University of Technology">Chalmers Institute of Technology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a> in 1962. After his work at Ericsson, he formalized the language and method he had been working on in his a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Technology" title="Royal Institute of Technology">Royal Institute of Technology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> in 1985 on the thesis Language Constructs for Large Real Time Systems.</p>
<p>After his master's degree, Jacobson joined Ericsson and worked in R&amp;D on computerized switching systems AKE <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/AXE_telephone_exchange" title="AXE telephone exchange">AXE</a> including <a href="/wiki/PLEX_(programming_language)" title="PLEX (programming language)">PLEX</a>. After his PhD thesis in April 1987, he started Objective Systems with Ericsson as a major customer. A majority stake of the company was acquired by Ericsson in 1991, and the company was renamed <a href="/wiki/Objectory_AB" title="Objectory AB">Objectory AB</a>. Jacobson developed the software method Object-Oriented Software Engineering (OOSE) published 1992, which was a simplified version of the commercial software process Objectory (short for Object Factory).</p>
<p>In October 1995 Ericsson divested Objectory to <a href="/wiki/Rational_Software" title="Rational Software">Rational Software</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Jacobson started working with <a href="/wiki/Grady_Booch" title="Grady Booch">Grady Booch</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Rumbaugh" title="James Rumbaugh">James Rumbaugh</a>, known collectively as the Three Amigos.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> bought Rational in 2003, Jacobson decided to leave, after he stayed on until May 2004 as an executive technical consultant.</p>
<p>In mid-2003 Jacobson formed Ivar Jacobson International (IJI) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which operates across three continents with offices in the UK, the US, Sweden, Switzerland, China, and Singapore.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ericsson">Ericsson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ericsson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967 at Ericsson Jacobson proposed the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_component" title="Software component">software components</a> in the new generation of <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> controlled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telephone_switch" title="Telephone switch">telephone switches</a> <a href="/wiki/Ericsson" title="Ericsson">Ericsson</a> was developing. In doing this he invented <a href="/wiki/Sequence_diagram" title="Sequence diagram">sequence diagrams</a>, and developed collaboration diagrams. He also used state transition diagrams to describe the message flows between components.</p>
<p>Jacobson saw a need for <i>blueprints</i> for software development. He was one of the original developers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specification_and_Design_Language" title="Specification and Design Language">Specification and Design Language</a> (SDL). In 1975, SDL became a standard in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Telecoms_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Telecoms industry (page does not exist)">telecoms industry</a>.</p>
<p>At Objectory he also invented <a href="/wiki/Use_case" title="Use case">use cases</a> as a way to specify functional software requirements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rational_Software">Rational Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rational Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At Rational, Jacobson and his friends, <a href="/wiki/Grady_Booch" title="Grady Booch">Grady Booch</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Rumbaugh" title="James Rumbaugh">James Rumbaugh</a>, designed the <a href="/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language" title="Unified Modeling Language">UML</a> and his Objectory Process evolved to become the <a href="/wiki/Rational_Unified_Process" title="Rational Unified Process">Rational Unified Process</a> under the leadership of Philippe Kruchten.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Essential_Unified_Process">Essential Unified Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Essential Unified Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2005, Jacobson announced the <a href="/wiki/Essential_Unified_Process" title="Essential Unified Process">Essential Unified Process</a> or “EssUP” for short. EssUP was a new “Practice” centric software development process that stood on the shoulders of modern but established software development practices. It was a fresh new start, integrating successful practices sourced from the three leading process camps: the unified process camp, the <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">agile software development</a> camp and the process improvement camp. Each one of them contributed different capabilities: structure, agility and process improvement.</p>
<p>Ivar has described EssUP as a "super light and agile" RUP, and IJI have integrated EssUP into <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_Studio_Team_System" title="Visual Studio Team System">Visual Studio Team System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_(software)" title="Eclipse (software)">Eclipse</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EssWork">EssWork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: EssWork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Standing on the experience of EssUP Ivar and his team, in particular <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Spence_(software_engineer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ian Spence (software engineer) (page does not exist)">Ian Spence</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_Wei_Ng&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pan Wei Ng (page does not exist)">Pan Wei Ng</a>, developed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EssWork&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EssWork (page does not exist)">EssWork</a> starting in 2006. EssWork is a framework for working with methods. It is based on a kernel of universal elements always prevalent in software development endeavors. On top of the kernel some fifteen practices have been defined. A team can create their own method by composing practices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SEMAT">SEMAT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivar_Jacobson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: SEMAT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2009, Jacobson, <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Meyer" title="Bertrand Meyer">Bertrand Meyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Soley" title="Richard Soley">Richard Soley</a> ("the Troika") started an initiative called <a href="/wiki/SEMAT" title="SEMAT">SEMAT</a> (Software Engineering Method and Theory) to seek to develop a rigorous, theoretically sound basis for software engineering practice, and its wide adoption by industry and academia. SEMAT has been inspired by the work at IJI, but it is a fresh new start. It has resulted in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Essence_(OMG_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Essence (OMG standard) (page does not exist)">Essence</a>, which at the time of this writing has been recommended as an OMG standard.</p>
