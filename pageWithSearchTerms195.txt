<p>From its beginnings in the 1960s, writing software has evolved into a profession concerned with how best to maximize the quality of software and of how to create it. Quality can refer to how maintainable software is, to its stability, speed, usability, testability, readability, size, cost, security, and number of flaws or "bugs", as well as to less measurable qualities like elegance, conciseness, and customer satisfaction, among many other attributes. How best to create high quality software is a separate and controversial problem covering software design principles, so-called "best practices" for writing code, as well as broader management issues such as optimal team size, process, how best to deliver software on time and as quickly as possible, work-place "culture", hiring practices, and so forth. All this falls under the broad rubric of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The evolution of software engineering is notable in a number of areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Emergence as a profession: By the early 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Software_engineering_had_already_emerged_as_a_%27%27bona_fide%27%27_profession&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Software engineering had already emerged as a ''bona fide'' profession (page does not exist)">software engineering professionalism</a>, to stand beside computer science and traditional engineering.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women,_girls_and_information_technology" title="Women, girls and information technology">Role of women</a>: In the 1940s, 1950s, and 1960s, men often filled the more prestigious and better paying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardware_engineering" title="Hardware engineering">hardware engineering</a> roles, but often delegated the writing of software to women.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Legends such as <a href="/wiki/Grace_Hopper" title="Grace Hopper">Grace Hopper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Fenton" title="Jamie Fenton">Jamie Fenton</a>, as well as many other unsung women, filled many <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a> jobs during the first several decades of software engineering.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Today, fewer women work in software engineering than in other professions, a situation whose cause is not clearly identified. It is often attributed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_discrimination" title="Sexual discrimination">sexual discrimination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyberculture" title="Cyberculture">cyberculture</a>, or different educational choices made by women.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> Many academic and professional organizations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> consider this situation unbalanced and are trying hard to solve it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Processes: <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">Processes</a> have become a big part of software engineering and are hailed for their potential to improve software and sharply criticized for their potential to constrict programmers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Cost of hardware: The relative cost of software versus hardware has changed substantially over the last 50 years. When <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframes</a> were expensive and required large support staffs, the few organizations buying them also had the resources to fund large, expensive custom software engineering projects. Computers are now much more numerous and much more powerful, which has several effects on software. The larger market can support large projects to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_off_the_shelf" title="Commercial off the shelf">commercial off the shelf</a> software, as done by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>. The cheap machines allow each programmer to have a terminal capable of fairly rapid <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilation</a>. The programs in question can use techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Garbage_collection_(computer_science)" title="Garbage collection (computer science)">garbage collection</a>, which make them easier and faster for the programmer to write. On the other hand, many fewer organizations are interested in employing programmers for large custom software projects, instead using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_off_the_shelf" title="Commercial off the shelf">commercial off the shelf</a> software as much as possible.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_pioneering_era">The pioneering era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The pioneering era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most important development was that new computers were coming out almost every year or two, rendering existing ones obsolete. Software people had to rewrite all their programs to run on these new machines. Programmers did not have computers on their desks and had to go to the "machine room". Jobs were run by signing up for machine time or by operational staff. Jobs were run by putting punched cards for input into the machine's card reader and waiting for results to come back on the printer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The field was so new that the idea of management by schedule was non-existent. Making predictions of a project's completion date was almost impossible. Computer hardware was application-specific. Scientific and business tasks needed different machines. Due to the need to frequently translate old software to meet the needs of new machines, high-order languages like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FORTRAN" title="FORTRAN">FORTRAN</a>, <a href="/wiki/COBOL" title="COBOL">COBOL</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ALGOL" title="ALGOL">ALGOL</a> were developed. Hardware vendors gave away systems software for free as hardware could not be sold without software. A few companies sold the service of building custom software but no software companies were selling packaged software.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The notion of reuse flourished. As software was free, user organizations commonly gave it away. Groups like IBM's scientific user group SHARE offered catalogs of reusable components. Academia did not yet teach the principles of computer science. Modular programming and data abstraction were already being used in programming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1945_to_1965:_The_origins">1945 to 1965: The origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1945 to 1965: The origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Putative origins for the term <i>software engineering</i> include a 1965 letter from ACM president <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Oettinger" title="Anthony Oettinger">Anthony Oettinger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> use by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J.P._Eckert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J.P. Eckert (page does not exist)">J.P. Eckert</a> during a conference in 1965 or 1966, lectures by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_T._Ross" title="Douglas T. Ross">Douglas T. Ross</a> at MIT in the 1950s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or use by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Hamilton_(scientist)" title="Margaret Hamilton (scientist)">Margaret Hamilton</a> while working on the Apollo guidance software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NATO_Science_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NATO Science Committee (page does not exist)">NATO Science Committee</a> sponsored two conferences<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> on software engineering in 1968 (Garmisch, Germany — see <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_publications_in_computer_science#Software_engineering:_Report_of_a_conference_sponsored_by_the_NATO_Science_Committee" title="List of publications in computer science">conference report</a></i>) and 1969, which gave the field its initial boost. Many<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> believe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="(February 2016)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> these conferences marked the official start of the profession of <i>software engineering</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1965_to_1985:_The_software_crisis">1965 to 1985: The software crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1965 to 1985: The software crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Software engineering was spurred by the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Software_crisis" title="Software crisis">software crisis</a></i> of the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, which identified many of the problems of software development. Many projects ran over budget and schedule. Some projects caused property damage. A few projects caused loss of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The software crisis was originally defined in terms of <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>, but evolved to emphasize <a href="/wiki/Software_quality_assurance" title="Software quality assurance">quality</a>. Some used the term <i>software crisis</i> to refer to their inability to hire enough qualified programmers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Cost and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budget_overrun" title="Budget overrun">Budget Overruns</a>: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS/360" title="OS/360">OS/360</a> operating system was a classic example. This decade-long<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> project from the 1960s eventually produced one of the most complex software systems at the time. OS/360 was one of the first large (1000 programmers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) software projects. <a href="/wiki/Fred_Brooks" title="Fred Brooks">Fred Brooks</a> claims in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mythical_Man_Month" title="The Mythical Man Month">The Mythical Man Month</a></i> that he made a multimillion-dollar mistake of not developing a coherent <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">architecture</a> before starting development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Property Damage: Software defects can cause property damage. Poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_security" title="Software security">software security</a> allows hackers to steal identities, costing time, money, and reputations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Life and Death: Software defects can kill. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embedded_systems" title="Embedded systems">embedded systems</a> used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a> machines failed so catastrophically that they administered <a href="/wiki/Lethal_dose" title="Lethal dose">lethal doses</a> of <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> to patients. The most famous of these failures is the <i><a href="/wiki/Therac-25" title="Therac-25">Therac-25</a></i> incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_G._Neumann" title="Peter G. Neumann">Peter G. Neumann</a> has kept a contemporary list of software problems and disasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The software crisis has been fading from view, because it is psychologically extremely difficult to remain in crisis mode for a protracted period (more than 20 years). Nevertheless, software – especially real-time embedded software – remains risky and is pervasive, and it is crucial not to give in to complacency. Over the last 10–15 years <a href="/wiki/Michael_A._Jackson" title="Michael A. Jackson">Michael A. Jackson</a> has written extensively about the nature of software engineering, has identified the main source of its difficulties as lack of specialization, and has suggested that his problem frames provide the basis for a "normal practice" of software engineering, a prerequisite if software engineering is to become an engineering science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1985_to_1989:_.22No_Silver_Bullet.22">1985 to 1989: "<i>No Silver Bullet</i>"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: 1985 to 1989: "No Silver Bullet"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For decades, solving the software crisis was paramount to researchers and companies producing software tools. The cost of owning and maintaining software in the 1980s was twice as expensive as developing the software.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> • During the 1990s, the cost of ownership and maintenance increased by 30% over the 1980s. • In 1995, statistics showed that half of surveyed development projects were operational, but were not considered successful. • The average software project overshoots its schedule by half. • Three-quarters of all large software products delivered to the customer are failures that are either not used at all, or do not meet the customer’s requirements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_projects">Software projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Software projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Seemingly, every new technology and practice from the 1970s through the 1990s was trumpeted as a <i><a href="/wiki/Silver_bullet" title="Silver bullet">silver bullet</a></i> to solve the software crisis. Tools, discipline, <a href="/wiki/Formal_methods" title="Formal methods">formal methods</a>, process, and professionalism were touted as silver bullets:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Tools: Especially emphasized were tools: <a href="/wiki/Structured_programming" title="Structured programming">structured programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_software_engineering" title="Computer-aided software engineering">CASE</a> tools such as ICL's <a href="/wiki/CADES" title="CADES">CADES</a> CASE system, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ada_programming_language" title="Ada programming language">Ada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Documentation" title="Documentation">documentation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standards</a> were touted as silver bullets.</li>
<li>Discipline: Some pundits argued that the software crisis was due to the lack of discipline of programmers.</li>
<li>Formal methods: Some believed that if formal engineering methodologies would be applied to software development, then production of software would become as predictable an industry as other branches of engineering. They advocated proving all programs correct.</li>
<li>Process: Many advocated the use of defined processes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodology_(software_engineering)" title="Methodology (software engineering)">methodologies</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model" title="Capability Maturity Model">Capability Maturity Model</a>.</li>
<li>Professionalism: This led to work on a code of ethics, licenses, and professionalism.</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Brooks" title="Fred Brooks">Fred Brooks</a> published his <i><a href="/wiki/No_Silver_Bullet" title="No Silver Bullet">No Silver Bullet</a></i> article, arguing that no individual technology or practice would ever make a 10-fold improvement in productivity within 10 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Debate about silver bullets raged over the following decade. Advocates for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ada_programming_language" title="Ada programming language">Ada</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_componentry" title="Software componentry">components</a>, and processes continued arguing for years that their favorite technology would be a silver bullet. Skeptics disagreed. Eventually, almost everyone accepted that no silver bullet would ever be found. Yet, claims about <i>silver bullets</i> pop up now and again, even today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> interpret<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="(February 2016)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_silver_bullet" title="No silver bullet">no silver bullet</a></i> to mean that software engineering failed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, with further reading, Brooks goes on to say: "We will surely make substantial progress over the next 40 years; an order of magnitude over 40 years is hardly magical ..."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The search for a single key to success never worked. All known technologies and practices have only made incremental improvements to productivity and quality. Yet, there are no silver bullets for any other profession, either. Others interpret <i>no silver bullet</i> as proof that software engineering has finally matured and recognized that projects succeed due to hard work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, it could also be said that there are, in fact, a range of <i>silver bullets</i> today, including lightweight methodologies (see "<a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">Project management</a>"), spreadsheet calculators, customized <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">browsers</a>, in-site search engines, database report generators, integrated design-test coding-editors with memory/differences/undo, and specialty shops that generate niche software, such as information web sites, at a fraction of the cost of totally customized web site development. Nevertheless, the field of software engineering appears too complex and diverse for a single "silver bullet" to improve most issues, and each issue accounts for only a small portion of all software problems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_to_1999:_Prominence_of_the_Internet">1990 to 1999: Prominence of the Internet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990 to 1999: Prominence of the Internet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rise of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> led to very rapid growth in the demand for international information display/e-mail systems on the World Wide Web. Programmers were required to handle illustrations, maps, photographs, and other images, plus simple animation, at a rate never before seen, with few well-known methods to optimize image display/storage (such as the use of thumbnail images).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The growth of browser usage, running on the HTML language, changed the way in which information-display and retrieval was organized. The widespread network connections led to the growth and prevention of international <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">computer viruses</a> on MS Windows computers, and the vast proliferation of spam e-mail became a major design issue in e-mail systems, flooding communication channels and requiring semi-automated pre-screening. Keyword-search systems evolved into web-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engines</a>, and many software systems had to be re-designed, for international searching, depending on <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">search engine optimization</a> (SEO) techniques. Human natural-language translation systems were needed to attempt to translate the information flow in multiple foreign languages, with many software systems being designed for multi-language usage, based on design concepts from human translators. Typical computer-user bases went from hundreds, or thousands of users, to, often, many-millions of international users.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_present.5Bwhen.3F.5D:_Lightweight_methodologies">2000 to present<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>: Lightweight methodologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000 to present[when?]: Lightweight methodologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the expanding demand for software in many smaller organizations, the need for inexpensive software solutions led to the growth of simpler, faster methodologies that developed running software, from requirements to deployment, quicker &amp; easier. The use of rapid-prototyping evolved to entire <i>lightweight methodologies</i>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_Programming" title="Extreme Programming">Extreme Programming</a> (XP), which attempted to simplify many areas of software engineering, including requirements gathering and reliability testing for the growing, vast number of small software systems. Very large software systems still used heavily-documented methodologies, with many volumes in the documentation set; however, smaller systems had a simpler, faster alternative approach to managing the development and maintenance of software calculations and algorithms, information storage/retrieval and display.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_trends_in_software_engineering">Current trends in software engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current trends in software engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Software engineering is a young discipline, and is still developing. The directions in which software engineering is developing include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects">Aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aspect-oriented_programming" title="Aspect-oriented programming">Aspects</a> help software engineers deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quality_attributes" title="Quality attributes">quality attributes</a> by providing tools to add or remove <a href="/wiki/Boilerplate_code" title="Boilerplate code">boilerplate code</a> from many areas in the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a>. Aspects describe how all objects or functions should behave in particular circumstances. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspect_(computer_science)" title="Aspect (computer science)">aspects</a> can add <a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">debugging</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_logging" title="Data logging">logging</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lock_(software_engineering)" title="Lock (software engineering)">locking</a> control into all objects of particular types. Researchers are currently working to understand how to use aspects to design general-purpose code. Related concepts include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_programming" title="Generative programming">generative programming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Template_(programming)" title="Template (programming)">templates</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agile">Agile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Agile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">Agile software development</a> guides <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> projects that evolve rapidly with changing expectations and competitive markets. Proponents of this method believe that heavy, document-driven processes (like <a href="/wiki/TickIT" title="TickIT">TickIT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model" title="Capability Maturity Model">CMM</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISO_9000" title="ISO 9000">ISO 9000</a>) are fading in importance<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Some people believe that companies and agencies export many of the jobs that can be guided by heavy-weight processes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Related concepts include <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrum_(development)" title="Scrum (development)">scrum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lean_software_development" title="Lean software development">lean software development</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental">Experimental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Experimental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Experimental_software_engineering" title="Experimental software engineering">Experimental software engineering</a> is a branch of software engineering interested in devising <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> on software, in collecting data from the experiments, and in devising laws and theories from this data. Proponents of this method advocate that the nature of software is such that we can advance the knowledge on software through experiments only<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_product_lines">Software product lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Software product lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Product_family_engineering" title="Product family engineering">Software product lines, aka product family engineering,</a> is a systematic way to produce <i>families</i> of software systems, instead of creating a succession of completely individual products. This method emphasizes extensive, systematic, formal <a href="/wiki/Code_reuse" title="Code reuse">code reuse</a>, to try to industrialize the software development process.</p>
<p>The Future of Software Engineering conference (FOSE), held at ICSE 2000, documented the state of the art of SE in 2000 and listed many problems to be solved over the next decade. The FOSE tracks at the ICSE 2000 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and the ICSE 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> conferences also help identify the state of the art in software engineering.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_engineering_today">Software engineering today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Software engineering today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The profession is trying to define its boundary and content. The Software Engineering Body of Knowledge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SWEBOK" title="SWEBOK">SWEBOK</a> has been tabled as an ISO standard during 2006 (ISO/IEC TR 19759).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2006, <i>Money Magazine</i> and <i>Salary.com</i> rated software engineering as the best job in America in terms of growth, pay, stress levels, flexibility in hours and working environment, creativity, and how easy it is to enter and advance in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prominent_figures_in_the_history_of_software_engineering">Prominent figures in the history of software engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Prominent figures in the history of software engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Bachman" title="Charles Bachman">Charles Bachman</a> (born 1924) is particularly known for his work in the area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Databases" title="Databases">databases</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laszlo_Belady" title="Laszlo Belady">Laszlo Belady</a> (born 1928) the editor-in-chief of the IEEE Transactions on Software Engineering in the 1980s</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fred_Brooks" title="Fred Brooks">Fred Brooks</a> (born 1931) best known for managing the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OS/360" title="OS/360">OS/360</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Chen" title="Peter Chen">Peter Chen</a>, known for the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entity-relationship_model" title="Entity-relationship model">entity-relationship modeling</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edsger_Dijkstra" title="Edsger Dijkstra">Edsger Dijkstra</a> (1930–2002) developed the framework for proper programming.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Parnas" title="David Parnas">David Parnas</a> (born 1941) developed the concept of <a href="/wiki/Information_hiding" title="Information hiding">information hiding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Modular_programming" title="Modular programming">modular programming</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michael_A._Jackson" title="Michael A. Jackson">Michael A. Jackson</a> (born 1936) software engineering methodologist responsible for JSP method of program design; JSD method of system development (with John Cameron); and Problem Frames method for analysing and structuring software development problems.</li>
</ul>
