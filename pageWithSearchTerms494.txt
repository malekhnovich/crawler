<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Game design</h1>

<p><b>Game design</b> is the art of applying <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> and aesthetics to create a <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">game</a> to facilitate interaction between players for entertainment or for medical, educational, or experimental purposes. Game design can be applied both to games and, increasingly, to other interactions, particularly virtual ones (see <a href="/wiki/Gamification" title="Gamification">gamification</a>).</p>
<p>Game design creates goals, rules, and challenges to define a sport, <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_game" title="Tabletop game">tabletop game</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casino_game" title="Casino game">casino game</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a>, <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a> that produces desirable interactions among its participants and, possibly, spectators.</p>
<p>Academically, game design is part of <a href="/wiki/Game_studies" title="Game studies">game studies</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a> studies strategic decision making (primarily in non-game situations). Games have historically inspired seminal research in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>, economics, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimization_theory" title="Optimization theory">optimization theory</a>. Applying game design to itself is a current research topic in <a href="/wiki/Metadesign" title="Metadesign">metadesign</a>.</p>

<p>Sports (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_sports" title="History of sports">history of sports</a>), gambling, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_games" title="Board games">board games</a> are known, respectively, to have existed for at least ten thousand, six thousand, and five thousand years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_process">Folk process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Folk process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tabletop games played today whose descent can be traced from ancient times include <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">go</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pachisi" title="Pachisi">pachisi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backgammon" title="Backgammon">backgammon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahjong" title="Mahjong">mahjong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mancala" title="Mancala">mancala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pick-up_sticks" title="Pick-up sticks">pick-up sticks</a>. The rules of these games were not codified until early modern times and their features gradually evolved and changed over time, through the <a href="/wiki/Folk_process" title="Folk process">folk process</a>. Given this, these games are not considered to have had a designer or been the result of a <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design process</a> in the modern sense.</p>
<p>After the rise of commercial game publishing in the late 19th century, many games which had formerly evolved via folk processes became commercial properties, often with custom scoring pads or preprepared material. For example, the similar public domain games <a href="/wiki/Generala" title="Generala">Generala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yacht_(dice_game)" title="Yacht (dice game)">Yacht</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yatzy" title="Yatzy">Yatzy</a> led to the commercial game <a href="/wiki/Yahtzee" title="Yahtzee">Yahtzee</a> in the mid-1950s.</p>
<p>Today, many commercial games, such as <a href="/wiki/Taboo_(game)" title="Taboo (game)">Taboo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balderdash" title="Balderdash">Balderdash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pictionary" title="Pictionary">Pictionary</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Time%27s_Up!_(game)" title="Time's Up! (game)">Time's Up!</a>, are descended from traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parlour_games" title="Parlour games">parlour games</a>. Adapting traditional games to become commercial properties is an example of game design.</p>
<p>Similarly, many sports, such as soccer and baseball, are the result of folk processes, while others were designed, such as basketball, invented in 1891 by <a href="/wiki/James_Naismith" title="James Naismith">James Naismith</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_media">New media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: New media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Technological advances have provided new media for games throughout history. The printing press allowed packs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playing_cards" title="Playing cards">playing cards</a>, adapted from <a href="/wiki/Mahjong" title="Mahjong">Mahjong</a> tiles, to be mass-produced, leading to many new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Card_games" title="Card games">card games</a>. Accurate topographic maps produced as lithographs and provided free to Prussian officers helped popularize <a href="/wiki/Wargaming" title="Wargaming">wargaming</a>. Cheap bookbinding (printed labels wrapped around cardboard) led to mass-produced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_games" title="Board games">board games</a> with custom boards. Inexpensive (hollow) lead figurine casting contributed to the development of <a href="/wiki/Miniature_wargaming" title="Miniature wargaming">miniature wargaming</a>. Cheap custom dice led to <a href="/wiki/Poker_dice" title="Poker dice">poker dice</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_disc" title="Flying disc">Flying discs</a> led to <a href="/wiki/Disc_golf" title="Disc golf">disc golf</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultimate_(game)" title="Ultimate (game)">Ultimate</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_computers" title="Personal computers">Personal computers</a> contributed to the popularity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_games" title="Computer games">computer games</a>, leading to the wide availability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_consoles" title="Video game consoles">video game consoles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">video games</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_phones" title="Smart phones">Smart phones</a> have led to a proliferation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_games" title="Mobile games">mobile games</a>.</p>
<p>The first games in a new medium are frequently adaptations of older games. <a href="/wiki/Pong" title="Pong">Pong</a>, one of the first widely disseminated video games, adapted table tennis. Later games will often exploit distinctive properties of a new medium. Adapting older games and creating original games for new media are both examples of game design.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Game_studies" title="Game studies">Game studies</a></div>
<p>Game studies or gaming theory is a discipline that deals with the critical study of games, game design, players, and their role in society and culture. Prior to the late-twentieth century, the academic study of games was rare and limited to fields such as history and <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>. As the video game revolution took off in the early 1980s, so did academic interest in games, resulting in a field that draws on diverse methodologies and schools of thought. These influences may be characterized broadly in three ways: the social science approach, the humanities approach, and the industry and engineering approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Broadly speaking, the social scientific approach has concerned itself with the question of "What do games do to people?" Using tools and methods such as surveys, controlled laboratory experiments, and ethnography researchers have investigated both the positive and negative impacts that playing games could have on people. More sociologically informed research has sought to move away from simplistic ideas of gaming as either 'negative' or 'positive', but rather seeking to understand its role and location in the complexities of everyday life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_2012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford_2012-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general terms, the humanities approach has concerned itself with the question of "What meanings are made through games?" Using tools and methods such as interviews, ethnographies and participant observation, researchers have investigated the various roles that videogames play in people's lives and activities together with the meaning they assign to their experiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>From an industry perspective, a lot of game studies research can be seen as the academic response to the videogame industry's questions regarding the products it creates and sells. The main question this approach deals with can be summarized as "How can we create better games?" with the accompanying "What makes a game good?" "Good" can be taken to mean many different things, including providing an entertaining and an engaging experience, being easy to learn and play, and being innovative and having novel experiences. Different approaches to studying this problem have included looking at describing how to design games<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and extracting guidelines and rules of thumb for making better games<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_decision_making">Strategic decision making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strategic decision making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">Game theory</a></div>
<p>Game theory is a study of strategic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">decision making</a>. Specifically, it is "the study of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical models</a> of conflict and cooperation between intelligent rational decision-makers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An alternative term suggested "as a more descriptive name for the discipline" is <i>interactive <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision theory</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The subject first addressed <a href="/wiki/Zero-sum_game" title="Zero-sum game">zero-sum games</a>, such that one person's gains exactly equal net losses of the other participant or participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Today, however, game theory applies to a wide range of behavioral relations, and has developed into an <a href="/wiki/Umbrella_term" title="Umbrella term">umbrella term</a> for the logical side of decision science.</p>
<p>The games studied in game theory are well-defined mathematical objects. To be fully defined, a game must specify the following elements: the <a href="/wiki/Player_(game)" title="Player (game)"><i>players</i> of the game</a>, the <i>information</i> and <i>actions</i> available to each player at each decision point, and the <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility"><i>payoffs</i></a> for each outcome. (Rasmusen refers to these four "essential elements" by the acronym "PAPI".)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r7_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-r7-10">[10]</a></sup> A game theorist typically uses these elements, along with a <a href="/wiki/Solution_concept" title="Solution concept">solution concept</a> of their choosing, to deduce a set of equilibrium <a href="/wiki/Strategy_(game_theory)" title="Strategy (game theory)">strategies</a> for each player such that, when these strategies are employed, no player can profit by unilaterally deviating from their strategy. These equilibrium strategies determine an <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> to the game—a stable state in which either one outcome occurs or a set of outcomes occur with known probability.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_elements">Design elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Games can be characterized by "what the player does."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craw_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-craw-11">[11]</a></sup> This is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a>. Major key elements identified in this context are tools and rules that define the overall context of game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_of_play">Tools of play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tools of play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Games are often classified by the components required to play them (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miniatures_game" title="Miniatures game">miniatures</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Ball_game" title="Ball game">ball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Card_game" title="Card game">cards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">a board and pieces</a>, or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_game" title="Computer game">computer</a>). In places where the use of leather is well established, the ball has been a popular game piece throughout recorded history, resulting in a worldwide popularity of ball games such as <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a>, basketball, <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>, tennis, and volleyball. Other tools are more idiosyncratic to a certain region. Many countries in Europe, for instance, have unique standard decks of <a href="/wiki/Playing_card" title="Playing card">playing cards</a>. Other games such as <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> may be traced primarily through the development and evolution of its game pieces.</p>
<p>Many game tools are tokens, meant to represent other things. A token may be a pawn on a board, <a href="/wiki/Play_money" title="Play money">play money</a>, or an intangible item such as a point scored.</p>
<p>Games such as <a href="/wiki/Hide-and-seek" title="Hide-and-seek">hide-and-seek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tag_(game)" title="Tag (game)">tag</a> do not utilise any obvious tool; rather, their interactivity is defined by the environment. Games with the same or similar rules may have different gameplay if the environment is altered. For example, hide-and-seek in a school building differs from the same game in a park; an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_race" title="Auto race">auto race</a> can be radically different depending on the <a href="/wiki/Race_track" title="Race track">track</a> or <a href="/wiki/Street_racing" title="Street racing">street</a> course, even with the same cars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule_development">Rule development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rule development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Game_mechanics" title="Game mechanics">Game mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Balance_(game_design)" title="Balance (game design)">Balance (game design)</a></div>
<p>Whereas games are often characterized by their tools, they are often defined by their rules. While rules are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_rules" title="House rules">subject to variations and changes</a>, enough change in the rules usually results in a "new" game. There are exceptions to this in that some games deliberately involve the changing of their own rules, but even then there are often immutable <a href="/wiki/Meta" title="Meta">meta</a>-rules.</p>
<p>Rules generally determine turn order, the rights and responsibilities of the players, and each player's goals. Player rights may include when they may spend resources or move tokens.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Victory_conditions">Victory conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Victory conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Common win conditions are being first to amass a certain quota of points or tokens (as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Settlers_of_Catan" title="Settlers of Catan">Settlers of Catan</a>), having the greatest number of tokens at the end of the game (as in <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a>), or some relationship of one's game tokens to those of one's opponent (as in chess's <a href="/wiki/Checkmate" title="Checkmate">checkmate</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_or_multiplayer">Single or multiplayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Single or multiplayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most games require multiple players. However, single-player games are unique in respect to the type of challenges a player faces. Many games described as "single-player" may be termed actually puzzles or recreations. Unlike a game with multiple players competing with or against each other to reach the game's goal, a one-player game is a battle solely against an element of the environment (an artificial opponent), against one's own skills, against time, or against chance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storyline_and_plot">Storyline and plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Storyline and plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stories told in games may focus on narrative elements that can be communicated through the use of mechanics and player choice. Narrative plots in games generally have a clearly defined and simplistic structure. Mechanical choices on the part of the designer(s) often drastically effect narrative elements in the game. However, due to a lack of unified and standardized teaching and understanding of narrative elements in games, individual interpretations, methods, and terminology vary wildly. Because of this, most narrative elements in games are created unconsciously and intuitively. However, as a general rule, game narratives increase in complexity and scale as player choice or game mechanics increase in complexity and scale. One example of this is removing a players ability to directly affect the plot for a limited time. This lack of player choice necessitates an increase in mechanical complexity, and could be used as a metaphor to symbolize depression that is felt by a character in the narrative.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luck_and_strategy">Luck and strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Luck and strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A game's tools and rules will result in its requiring skill, strategy, <a href="/wiki/Luck" title="Luck">luck</a>, or a combination thereof, and are classified accordingly.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Games_of_skill" title="Games of skill">Games of skill</a> include games of physical skill, such as <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tug_of_war" title="Tug of war">tug of war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hopscotch" title="Hopscotch">hopscotch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shooting_sport" title="Shooting sport">target shooting</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stake_(lawn_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stake (lawn game) (page does not exist)">stake</a>, and games of mental skill such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Checkers" title="Checkers">checkers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Games_of_strategy" title="Games of strategy">Games of strategy</a> include checkers, chess, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go_(board_game)" title="Go (board game)">go</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arimaa" title="Arimaa">arimaa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tic-tac-toe" title="Tic-tac-toe">tic-tac-toe</a>, and often require special equipment to play them. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Games_of_chance" title="Games of chance">Games of chance</a> include gambling games (<a href="/wiki/Blackjack" title="Blackjack">blackjack</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mah-jongg" title="Mah-jongg">mah-jongg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a>, etc.), as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snakes_and_ladders" title="Snakes and ladders">snakes and ladders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock,_paper,_scissors" title="Rock, paper, scissors">rock, paper, scissors</a>; most require equipment such as cards or <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a>.</p>
<p>Most games contain two or all three of these elements. For example, <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> and baseball involve both physical skill and strategy while <a href="/wiki/Tiddlywinks" title="Tiddlywinks">tiddlywinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">poker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a> combine strategy and chance. Many card and board games combine all three; most <a href="/wiki/Trick-taking_game" title="Trick-taking game">trick-taking games</a> involve mental skill, strategy, and an element of chance, as do many strategic board games such as <a href="/wiki/Risk_(game)" title="Risk (game)">Risk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Settlers_of_Catan" title="Settlers of Catan">Settlers of Catan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carcassonne_(board_game)" title="Carcassonne (board game)">Carcassonne</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_educational_tool">Use as educational tool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Use as educational tool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Learning_through_play" title="Learning through play">Learning through play</a></div>
<p>By learning through play<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[a]</a></sup> children can develop social and <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> skills, mature emotionally, and gain the <a href="/wiki/Self-confidence" title="Self-confidence">self-confidence</a> required to engage in new experiences and environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Key ways that young children learn include playing, being with other people, being active, exploring and new experiences, talking to themselves, communication with others, meeting physical and mental challenges, being shown how to do new things, practicing and repeating skills and having fun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Play develops children's content knowledge and provides children the opportunity to develop social skills, competences and disposition to learn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Play-based learning is based on a Vygotskian model of <a href="/wiki/Scaffolding" title="Scaffolding">scaffolding</a> where the teacher pays attention on specific elements of the play activity and provides encouragement and feedback on children's learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-text2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-text2-16">[15]</a></sup> When children engage in real-life and imaginary activities, play can be challenging in children's thinking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> To extend the learning process, sensitive intervention can be provided with adult support when necessary during play-based learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-text2_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-text2-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_process">Development process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Development process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Game_designer"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_team">Development team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Development team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_artist">Game artist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Game artist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Game_artist" title="Game artist">Game artist</a></div>
<p>A game artist is an artist who creates art for one or more types of games. Game artists are responsible for all of the aspects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_development" title="Game development">game development</a> that call for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_art" title="Visual art">visual art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-18">[17]</a></sup> Game artists are often noted in <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collectible_card_game" title="Collectible card game">collectible card games</a> and video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artofvideogames_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-artofvideogames-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Game_testing" title="Game testing">Game testing</a></div>
<p>Game testing, a subset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_development" title="Game development">game development</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">software testing</a> process for <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality control</a> of video games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Novak95_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Novak95-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxland3012_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxland3012-22">[21]</a></sup> The primary function of game testing is the discovery and documentation of <a href="/wiki/Software_bug" title="Software bug">software defects</a> (aka bugs). Interactive entertainment software testing is a highly technical field requiring <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a> expertise, analytic competence, critical evaluation skills, and endurance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxland301_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxland301-24">[23]</a></sup> In recent years the field of game testing has come under fire for being excessively strenuous and unrewarding, both financially and emotionally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategies">Strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Board_games">Board games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Board games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:237px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DarrowPage1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="672" height="380" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DarrowPage1.png/235px-DarrowPage1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DarrowPage1.png/353px-DarrowPage1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/DarrowPage1.png/470px-DarrowPage1.png 2x" width="235"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Charles_Darrow" title="Charles Darrow">Charles Darrow</a>'s 1935 patent for <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i> includes specific design elements developed during the prototype phase. Prototypes are very common in the later stages of board game design, and "prototype circles" in many cities today provide an opportunity for designers to play and critique each other's games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neyfakh_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-neyfakh-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wadley_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-wadley-27">[26]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">Board game</a> design is the development of rules and presentational aspects of a board game. When a player takes part in a game, it is the player's self-subjection to the rules that creates a sense of purpose for the duration of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neyfakh_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-neyfakh-26">[25]</a></sup> Maintaining the players' interest throughout the gameplay experience is the goal of board game design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wadley_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-wadley-27">[26]</a></sup> To achieve this, board game designers emphasize different aspects such as social interaction, strategy, and competition, and target players of differing needs by providing for short versus long-play, and luck versus skill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wadley_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-wadley-27">[26]</a></sup> Beyond this, board game design reflects the culture in which the board game is produced.</p>
<p>The most ancient board games known today are over 5000 years old. They are frequently <a href="/wiki/Abstract_strategy_game" title="Abstract strategy game">abstract</a> in character and their design is primarily focused on a core set of simple rules. Of those that are still played today, games like <a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">go</a> (c.400BC), <a href="/wiki/Mancala" title="Mancala">mancala</a> (c.700AD), and <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> (c.600AD) have gone through many presentational and/or rule variations. In the case of chess, for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_variants" title="Chess variants">new variants</a> are developed constantly, to focus on certain aspects of the game, or just for variation's sake.</p>
<p>Traditional board games date from the nineteenth and early twentieth century. Whereas ancient board game design was primarily focused on rules alone, traditional board games were often influenced by <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> mores. Academic (e.g. history and geography) and moral didacticism were important design features for traditional games, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> associations between dice and the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">Devil</a> meant that early American game designers eschewed their use in board games entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-28">[27]</a></sup> Even traditional games that did use dice, like <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i> (based on the 1906 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Landlord%27s_Game" title="The Landlord's Game">The Landlord's Game</a></i>), were rooted in educational efforts to explain political concepts to the masses. By the 1930s and 1940s, board game design began to emphasize amusement over education, and characters from comic strips, radio programmes, and (in the 1950s) television shows began to be featured in board game adaptations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent developments in modern board game design can be traced to the 1980s in Germany, and have led to increased popularity of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-style_board_game" title="German-style board game">German-style board games</a>" (also known as "Eurogames" or "designer games"). The design emphasis of these board games is to give players meaningful choices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neyfakh_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-neyfakh-26">[25]</a></sup> This is manifested by eliminating elements like randomness and luck to be replaced by skill, strategy, and resource competition, by removing the potential for players to fall irreversibly behind in the early stages of a game, and by reducing the number of rules and possible player options to produce what <a href="/wiki/Alan_R._Moon" title="Alan R. Moon">Alan R. Moon</a> has described as "elegant game design".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neyfakh_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-neyfakh-26">[25]</a></sup> The concept of elegant game design has been identified by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a>'</i>s Leon Neyfakh as related to <a href="/wiki/Mihaly_Csikszentmihalyi" title="Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi">Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi</a>'s concept of "<a href="/wiki/Flow_(psychology)" title="Flow (psychology)">flow</a>" from his 1990 book, "Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neyfakh_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-neyfakh-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern technological advances have had a democratizing effect on board game design, with services like <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> providing designers with essential startup capital and tools like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3D_Printing" title="3D Printing">3D printers</a> facilitating the production of game pieces and board game prototypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> A modern adaptation of figure games are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miniature_wargame" title="Miniature wargame">miniature wargames</a> like <i><a href="/wiki/Warhammer_40,000" title="Warhammer 40,000">Warhammer 40,000</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Card_games">Card games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Card games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The design of <a href="/wiki/Card_game" title="Card game">card games</a> is constricted by the type of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deck_of_cards" title="Deck of cards">deck of cards</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">Tarot</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Suit_(cards)" title="Suit (cards)">four-suited</a> Latin decks. Card games can be played for fun, like <a href="/wiki/Go_Fish" title="Go Fish">Go Fish</a>, or for profit like <a href="/wiki/Poker" title="Poker">Poker</a>.</p>
<p>In Asian cultures, special sets of tiles can serve the same function as cards, as in <a href="/wiki/Mahjong" title="Mahjong">mahjong</a>, a game similar to (and thought to be the distant ancestor of) the Western card game <a href="/wiki/Rummy" title="Rummy">rummy</a>. Western <a href="/wiki/Dominoes" title="Dominoes">dominoes</a> games are believed to have developed from Asian tile games in the 18th century.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Magic:_The_Gathering" title="Magic: The Gathering">Magic: The Gathering</a></i> was the first <a href="/wiki/Collectible_card_game" title="Collectible card game">collectible card game</a> (or "trading card game") in 1993.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The line between card and board games is not clear-cut, as many card games, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solitaire" title="Solitaire">solitaire</a>, involve playing cards to form a "tableau", a spatial layout or board. Many board games, in turn, uses specialized decks of cards as randomization devices, such as a sub-type of wargames called <a href="/wiki/Board_wargame#Card-driven" title="Board wargame">card-driven wargames</a>.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dice_game" title="Dice game">Dice games</a> are among the oldest known games and have often been associated with gambling. The oldest known dice game is a backgammon set that was discovered by archaeologists excavating the site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnt_City" title="Burnt City">Burnt City</a>, which was abandoned in 2100 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Non-gambling dice games, such as <a href="/wiki/Yatzy" title="Yatzy">Yatzy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poker_dice" title="Poker dice">Poker dice</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Yahtzee" title="Yahtzee">Yahtzee</a> became popular in the mid-20th century.</p>
<p>The line between dice and board games is not clear-cut, as dice are often used as randomization devices in board games, such as Monopoly or <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">Risk</a>, while serving as the central drivers of play in games such as <a href="/wiki/Backgammon" title="Backgammon">Backgammon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pachisi" title="Pachisi">Pachisi</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casino_games">Casino games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Casino games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_edge" title="House edge">House edge</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Slot_machine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Slot_machine.jpg/220px-Slot_machine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Slot_machine.jpg/330px-Slot_machine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Slot_machine.jpg/440px-Slot_machine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Slot_machine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
All casino games are designed to mathematically favor the house. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_edge" title="House edge">house edge</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Slot_machine" title="Slot machine">slot machine</a> can range widely between 2 and 15 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Casino_game" title="Casino game">Casino game</a> design can entail the creation of an entirely new casino game, the creation of a variation on an existing casino game, or the creation of a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side_bet" title="Side bet">side bet</a> on an existing casino game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lubin_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-lubin-33">[32]</a></sup> Casino game mathematician, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Shackleford" title="Michael Shackleford">Michael Shackleford</a> has noted that it is much more common for casino game designers today to make successful variations than entirely new casino games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup> Gambling columnist <a href="/wiki/John_Grochowski" title="John Grochowski">John Grochowski</a> points to the emergence of community-style <a href="/wiki/Slot_machine" title="Slot machine">slot machines</a> in the mid-1990s, for example, as a successful variation on an existing casino game type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Unlike the majority of other games which are designed primarily in the interest of the player, one of the central aims of casino game design is to optimise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_advantage" title="House advantage">house advantage</a> and maximise revenue from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambler" title="Gambler">gamblers</a>. Successful casino game design works to provide entertainment for the player and revenue for the gambling house. To maximise player entertainment, casino games are designed with simple easy-to-learn rules that emphasize winning (i.e. whose rules enumerate many victory conditions and few loss conditions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup>), and that provide players with a variety of different gameplay postures (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand_(card_games)" title="Hand (card games)">card hands</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lubin_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-lubin-33">[32]</a></sup> Player entertainment value is also enhanced by providing gamblers with familiar gaming elements (e.g. dice and cards) in new casino games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lubin_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-lubin-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup> To maximise success for the gambling house, casino games are designed to be easy for <a href="/wiki/Croupier" title="Croupier">croupiers</a> to operate and for <a href="/wiki/Pit_manager" title="Pit manager">pit managers</a> to oversee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lubin_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-lubin-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup> The two most fundamental rules of casino game design is that the games must be non-fraudable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lubin_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-lubin-33">[32]</a></sup> (including being as nearly as possible immune from <a href="/wiki/Advantage_gambling" title="Advantage gambling">advantage gambling</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup>), and that they must mathematically favor the house winning. Shackleford suggests that the optimum casino game design should give the house an edge of smaller than 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shackleford_34-5"><a href="#cite_note-shackleford-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role-playing_games">Role-playing games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Role-playing games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The design of role-playing games requires the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Campaign_setting" title="Campaign setting">setting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Character_creation" title="Character creation">characters</a>, and basic <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game_system" title="Role-playing game system">gameplay rules or mechanics</a>. After a role-playing game is produced, additional design elements are often devised by the players themselves. In many instances, for example, <a href="/wiki/Character_creation" title="Character creation">character creation</a> is left to the players. Likewise, the progression of a role-playing game is determined in large part by the <a href="/wiki/Gamemaster" title="Gamemaster">gamemaster</a> whose individual campaign design may be directed by one of several <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game_theory" title="Role-playing game theory">role-playing game theories</a>.</p>
<p>There is no central core for <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_game" title="Tabletop role-playing game">tabletop role-playing game</a> theory because different people want such different things out of the games. Probably the most famous category of RPG theory, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GNS_Theory" title="GNS Theory">GNS Theory</a> assumes that people want one of three things out of the game – a better, more interestingly challenging game, to create a more interesting <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">story</a>, or a better simulation – in other words better rules to support <a href="/wiki/Worldbuilding" title="Worldbuilding">worldbuilding</a>. GNS Theory has been abandoned by its creator, partly because it neglects emotional investment, and partly because it just didn't work properly. There are techniques that people use (such as <a href="/wiki/Dice_pool" title="Dice pool">dice pools</a>) to better create the game they want – but with no consistent goal or agreement for what makes for a good game there's no overarching theory generally agreed on.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Game_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design" title="Video game design">Video game design</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png/220px-Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png/330px-Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png/440px-Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_for_Mandicor_0.0.5.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Video game <a href="/wiki/Software_prototyping" title="Software prototyping">prototypes</a> created during the pre-production design phase are often used as a <a href="/wiki/Proof_of_concept" title="Proof of concept">proof of concept</a> for the implementation of new rules or gameplay features.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Video game design is a process that takes place in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_development#Pre-production" title="Game development">pre-production</a> phase of video game development. In the video game industry, game design describes the creation of the content and rules of a video game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brathwaite_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brathwaite-39">[38]</a></sup> The goal of this process for the game designer is to provide players with the opportunity to make meaningful decisions in relation to playing the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brathwaite_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brathwaite-39">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design#Game_elements" title="Video game design">Elements of video game design</a> such as the establishment of fundamental <a href="/wiki/Gameplay" title="Gameplay">gameplay</a> rules provide a framework within which players will operate, while the addition of narrative structures provide players with a reason to care about playing the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> To establish the rules and narrative, an internally consistent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_world" title="Game world">game world</a> is created, requiring visual, audio, and programming development for world, <a href="/wiki/Characterization" title="Characterization">character</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Level_design" title="Level design">level design</a>. The amount of work that is required to accomplish this often demands the use of a design team which may be divided into smaller <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design#Disciplines" title="Video game design">game design disciplines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> In order to maintain internal consistency between the teams, a specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_design_document" title="Software design document">software design document</a> known as a "<a href="/wiki/Game_design_document" title="Game design document">game design document</a>" (and sometimes an even broader scope "game bible" document) provides overall contextual guidance on ambient mood, appropriate tone, and other less tangible aspects of the game world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>An important aspect of video game design is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" title="Human-computer interaction">human-computer interaction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PippinBarr.com_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-PippinBarr.com-43">[42]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Game_feel" title="Game feel">game feel</a>.</p>

<p>The first military <a href="/wiki/Wargaming" title="Wargaming">war games</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Kriegsspiel_(wargame)" title="Kriegsspiel (wargame)">Kriegsspiel</a>, were designed in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in the 19th century to train staff officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KRIEG_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-KRIEG-44">[43]</a></sup> They are also <a href="/wiki/Wargaming" title="Wargaming">played as a hobby for entertainment</a>.</p>
<p>Modern war games are designed to test <a href="/wiki/Military_doctrine" title="Military doctrine">doctrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_strategy" title="Military strategy">strategies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military_tactics" title="Military tactics">tactics</a> in full scale exercises with <a href="/wiki/Opposing_force" title="Opposing force">opposing forces</a> at venues like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Training_Center" title="National Training Center">NTC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Polk#JRTC_moves_to_Polk" title="Fort Polk">JRTC</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Multinational_Readiness_Center" title="Joint Multinational Readiness Center">JMRC</a>, involving <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> countries.</p>
