<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Integrated circuit</h1>

<p>An <b>integrated circuit</b> or <b>monolithic integrated circuit</b> (also referred to as an <b>IC</b>, a <b>chip</b>, or a <b>microchip</b>) is a set of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_circuit" title="Electronic circuit">electronic circuits</a> on one small plate ("chip") of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_material" title="Semiconductor material">semiconductor material</a>, normally <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. This can be made much smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Discrete_circuit" title="Discrete circuit">discrete circuit</a> made from independent <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">electronic components</a>. ICs can be made very compact, having up to several billion <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">electronic components</a> in an area the size of a human fingernail. The half-pitch between nodes in a circuit has been made smaller as the technology advances; in 2008 it dropped below 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and was reduced to around 14 nanometers in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>ICs were made possible by experimental discoveries showing that <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device" title="Semiconductor device">semiconductor devices</a> could perform the functions of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> and by mid-20th-century technology advancements in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">semiconductor device fabrication</a>. The integration of large numbers of tiny <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> into a small chip was an enormous improvement over the manual assembly of circuits using discrete <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">electronic components</a>. The integrated circuit's <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> capability, reliability and building-block approach to <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit_design" title="Integrated circuit design">circuit design</a> ensured the rapid adoption of standardized integrated circuits in place of designs using discrete transistors.</p>
<p>ICs have two main advantages over <a href="/wiki/Discrete_circuit" title="Discrete circuit">discrete circuits</a>: cost and performance. Cost is low because the chips, with all their components, are printed as a unit by <a href="/wiki/Photolithography" title="Photolithography">photolithography</a> rather than being constructed one transistor at a time. Furthermore, packaged ICs use much less material than discrete circuits. Performance is high because the IC's components switch quickly and consume little power (compared to their discrete counterparts) as a result of the small size and close proximity of the components. As of 2012, typical chip areas range from a few square millimeters to around 450 mm<sup>2</sup>, with up to 9 million <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> per mm<sup>2</sup>.</p>
<p>Integrated circuits are used in virtually all electronic equipment today and have revolutionized the world of <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">Computers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, and other digital <a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">home appliances</a> are now inextricable parts of the structure of modern societies, made possible by the low cost of ICs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <i>integrated circuit</i> is defined as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>A circuit in which all or some of the circuit elements are inseparably associated and electrically interconnected so that it is considered to be indivisible for the purposes of construction and commerce.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Circuits meeting this definition can be constructed using many different technologies, including <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_transistor" title="Thin-film transistor">thin-film transistor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thick_film_technology" title="Thick film technology">thick film technology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_integrated_circuit" title="Hybrid integrated circuit">hybrid integrated circuit</a>. However, in general usage <i>integrated circuit</i> has come to refer to the single-piece circuit construction originally known as a <i>monolithic integrated circuit</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention">Invention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Invention_of_the_integrated_circuit" title="Invention of the integrated circuit">Invention of the integrated circuit</a></div>
<p>Early developments of the integrated circuit go back to 1949, when German engineer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_Jacobi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Werner Jacobi (page does not exist)">Werner Jacobi</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" title="Siemens AG">Siemens AG</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> filed a patent for an integrated-circuit-like semiconductor amplifying device<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacobi1949_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-jacobi1949-8">[8]</a></sup> showing five transistors on a common substrate in a 3-stage <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifier</a> arrangement. Jacobi disclosed small and cheap <a href="/wiki/Hearing_aid" title="Hearing aid">hearing aids</a> as typical industrial applications of his patent. An immediate commercial use of his patent has not been reported.</p>
<p>The idea of the integrated circuit was conceived by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Dummer" title="Geoffrey Dummer">Geoffrey W.A. Dummer</a> (1909–2002), a radar scientist working for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Radar_Establishment" title="Royal Radar Establishment">Royal Radar Establishment</a> of the British <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a>. Dummer presented the idea to the public at the Symposium on Progress in Quality Electronic Components in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> on 7 May 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He gave many symposia publicly to propagate his ideas, and unsuccessfully attempted to build such a circuit in 1956.</p>
<p>A precursor idea to the IC was to create small ceramic squares (wafers), each containing a single miniaturized component. Components could then be integrated and wired into a bidimensional or tridimensional compact grid. This idea, which seemed very promising in 1957, was proposed to the US Army by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kilby" title="Jack Kilby">Jack Kilby</a> and led to the short-lived Micromodule Program (similar to 1951's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Project_Tinkertoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Project Tinkertoy (page does not exist)">Project Tinkertoy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, as the project was gaining momentum, Kilby came up with a new, revolutionary design: the IC.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kilby_solid_circuit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Jack_Kilby" title="Jack Kilby">Jack Kilby</a>'s original integrated circuit</div>

<p>Newly employed by <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>, Kilby recorded his initial ideas concerning the integrated circuit in July 1958, successfully demonstrating the first working integrated example on 12 September 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIJackBuilt_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIJackBuilt-11">[11]</a></sup> In his patent application of 6 February 1959,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Kilby described his new device as "a body of semiconductor material … wherein all the components of the electronic circuit are completely integrated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The first customer for the new invention was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kilby won the 2000 Nobel Prize in Physics for his part in the invention of the integrated circuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> His work was named an <a href="/wiki/List_of_IEEE_milestones" title="List of IEEE milestones">IEEE Milestone</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Half a year after Kilby, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Noyce" title="Robert Noyce">Robert Noyce</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor" title="Fairchild Semiconductor">Fairchild Semiconductor</a> developed his own idea of an integrated circuit that solved many practical problems Kilby's had not. Noyce's design was made of <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, whereas Kilby's chip was made of <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>. Noyce credited <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Lehovec" title="Kurt Lehovec">Kurt Lehovec</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_C._Sprague_(inventor)" title="Robert C. Sprague (inventor)">Sprague Electric</a> for the <i>principle of <a href="/wiki/P%E2%80%93n_junction_isolation" title="P–n junction isolation">p–n junction isolation</a></i> caused by the action of a biased p–n junction (the diode) as a key concept behind the IC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor" title="Fairchild Semiconductor">Fairchild Semiconductor</a> was also home of the first silicon-gate IC technology with <a href="/wiki/Self-aligned_gate" title="Self-aligned gate">self-aligned gates</a>, the basis of all modern <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> computer chips. The technology was developed by Italian physicist <a href="/wiki/Federico_Faggin" title="Federico Faggin">Federico Faggin</a> in 1968, who later joined Intel in order to develop the very first single-chip Central Processing Unit (CPU) (<a href="/wiki/Intel_4004" title="Intel 4004">Intel 4004</a>), for which he received the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Technology_and_Innovation" title="National Medal of Technology and Innovation">National Medal of Technology and Innovation</a> in 2010.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generations">Generations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Generations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early days of simple integrated circuits, the technology's large scale limited each chip to only a few transistors, and the low degree of integration meant the design process was relatively simple. Manufacturing yields were also quite low by today's standards. As the technology progressed, millions, then billions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> of transistors could be placed on one chip, and good designs required thorough planning, giving rise to new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y_diagram" title="Y diagram">design methods</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SSI.2C_MSI_and_LSI">SSI, MSI and LSI <span id="SSI.2C_MSI_and_LSI"></span><span id="SSI"></span><span id="MSI"></span><span id="LSI"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: SSI, MSI and LSI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first integrated circuits contained only a few transistors. Early digital circuits containing tens of transistors provided a few logic gates, and early linear ICs such as the <a href="/wiki/Plessey" title="Plessey">Plessey</a> SL201 or the <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a> TAA320 had as few as two transistors. The number of transistors in an integrated circuit has increased dramatically since then. The term "large scale integration" (LSI) was first used by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Landauer" title="Rolf Landauer">Rolf Landauer</a> when describing the theoretical concept;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that term gave rise to the terms "small-scale integration" (SSI), "medium-scale integration" (MSI), "very-large-scale integration" (VLSI), and "ultra-large-scale integration" (ULSI). The early integrated circuits were SSI.</p>
<p>SSI circuits were crucial to early <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> projects, and aerospace projects helped inspire development of the technology. Both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minuteman_missile" title="Minuteman missile">Minuteman missile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> needed lightweight digital computers for their inertial guidance systems. Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_guidance_computer" title="Apollo guidance computer">Apollo guidance computer</a> led and motivated integrated-circuit technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> it was the Minuteman missile that forced it into mass-production. The Minuteman missile program and various other Navy programs accounted for the total $4 million integrated circuit market in 1962, and by 1968, U.S. Government space and defense spending still accounted for 37% of the $312 million total production. The demand by the U.S. Government supported the nascent integrated circuit market until costs fell enough to allow firms to penetrate the industrial, and eventually, the consumer markets. The average price per integrated circuit dropped from $50.00 in 1962 to $2.33 in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Integrated circuits began to appear in consumer products by the turn of the decade, a typical application being <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">FM</a> inter-carrier sound processing in television receivers.</p>
<p>The first MOS chips were small-scale integration chips for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forging_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-forging-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next step in the development of integrated circuits, taken in the late 1960s, introduced devices which contained hundreds of transistors on each chip, called "medium-scale integration" (MSI).</p>
<p>In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Wanlass" title="Frank Wanlass">Frank Wanlass</a> demonstrated a single-chip 16-bit shift register he designed, with an incredible (at the time) 120 transistors on a single chip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forging_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-forging-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>MSI devices were attractive economically because while they cost little more to produce than SSI devices, they allowed more complex systems to be produced using smaller circuit boards, less assembly work (because of fewer separate components), and a number of other advantages.</p>
<p>Further development, driven by the same economic factors, led to "large-scale integration" (LSI) in the mid-1970s, with tens of thousands of transistors per chip.</p>
<p>SSI and MSI devices often were manufactured by masks created by hand-cutting <a href="/wiki/Rubylith" title="Rubylith">Rubylith</a>. An engineer would inspect and verify the completeness of each mask. LSI devices contain so many transistors, interconnecting wires, and other features that it is considered impossible for a human to check the masks or even do the original design entirely by hand. The engineer depends on computer programs and other hardware aids to do most of this work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Integrated circuits such as 1K-bit RAMs, calculator chips, and the first microprocessors, that began to be manufactured in moderate quantities in the early 1970s, had under 4000 transistors. True LSI circuits, approaching 10,000 transistors, began to be produced around 1974, for computer main memories and second-generation microprocessors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VLSI">VLSI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: VLSI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The final step in the development process, starting in the 1980s and continuing through the present, was "very-large-scale integration" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLSI" title="VLSI">VLSI</a>). The development started with hundreds of thousands of transistors in the early 1980s, and continues beyond several billion transistors as of 2009.</p>
<p>Multiple developments were required to achieve this increased density. Manufacturers moved to smaller design rules and cleaner fabrication facilities, so that they could make chips with more transistors and maintain adequate yield. The path of process improvements was summarized by the <a href="/wiki/International_Technology_Roadmap_for_Semiconductors" title="International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors">International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors</a> (ITRS). <a href="/wiki/Electronic_design_automation" title="Electronic design automation">Design tools</a> improved enough to make it practical to finish these designs in a reasonable time. The more energy-efficient <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> replaced <a href="/wiki/NMOS_logic" title="NMOS logic">NMOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/PMOS_logic" title="PMOS logic">PMOS</a>, avoiding a prohibitive increase in power consumption.</p>
<p>In 1986 the first one-megabit <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">RAM</a> chips were introduced, containing more than one million transistors. Microprocessor chips passed the million-transistor mark in 1989 and the billion-transistor mark in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The trend continues largely unabated, with chips introduced in 2007 containing tens of billions of memory transistors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ULSI.2C_WSI.2C_SOC_and_3D-IC">ULSI, WSI, SOC and 3D-IC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: ULSI, WSI, SOC and 3D-IC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To reflect further growth of the complexity, the term <i>ULSI</i> that stands for "ultra-large-scale integration" was proposed for chips of more than 1 million transistors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wafer-scale_integration" title="Wafer-scale integration">Wafer-scale integration</a> (WSI) is a means of building very large integrated circuits that uses an entire silicon wafer to produce a single "super-chip". Through a combination of large size and reduced packaging, WSI could lead to dramatically reduced costs for some systems, notably massively parallel supercomputers. The name is taken from the term Very-Large-Scale Integration, the current state of the art when WSI was being developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System-on-a-chip" title="System-on-a-chip">system-on-a-chip</a> (SoC or SOC) is an integrated circuit in which all the components needed for a computer or other system are included on a single chip. The design of such a device can be complex and costly, and building disparate components on a single piece of silicon may compromise the efficiency of some elements. However, these drawbacks are offset by lower manufacturing and assembly costs and by a greatly reduced power budget: because signals among the components are kept on-die, much less power is required (see <a href="#Packaging">Packaging</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_integrated_circuit" title="Three-dimensional integrated circuit">three-dimensional integrated circuit</a> (3D-IC) has two or more layers of active electronic components that are integrated both vertically and horizontally into a single circuit. Communication between layers uses on-die signaling, so power consumption is much lower than in equivalent separate circuits. Judicious use of short vertical wires can substantially reduce overall wire length for faster operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>ICs have consistently migrated to smaller feature sizes over the years, allowing more circuitry to be packed on each chip. This increased capacity per unit area can be used to decrease cost or increase functionality—see <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a> which, in its modern interpretation, states that the number of transistors in an integrated circuit doubles every two years. In general, as the feature size shrinks, almost everything improves—the cost per transistor and the <a href="/wiki/Low-power_electronics" title="Low-power electronics">switching power consumption</a> per transistor go down, and the <a href="/wiki/Clock_rate" title="Clock rate">speed</a> goes up—see <a href="/wiki/Dennard_scaling" title="Dennard scaling">Dennard scaling</a>. However, ICs with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometer</a>-scale devices are not without their problems, principal among which is leakage current (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subthreshold_leakage" title="Subthreshold leakage">subthreshold leakage</a> for a discussion of this), although innovations in <a href="/wiki/High-%CE%BA_dielectric" title="High-κ dielectric">high-κ dielectrics</a> aim to solve these problems. Since these speed and power consumption gains are apparent to the end user, there is fierce competition among the manufacturers to use finer geometries. This process, and the expected progress over the next few years, is described by the <a href="/wiki/International_Technology_Roadmap_for_Semiconductors" title="International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors">International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors</a> (ITRS).</p>

<p>Among the most advanced integrated circuits are the <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a> or "<b>cores</b>", which control everything from computers and cellular phones to digital <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave ovens</a>. Digital <a href="/wiki/Computer_memory" title="Computer memory">memory chips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Application-specific_integrated_circuit" title="Application-specific integrated circuit">application-specific integrated circuits</a> (ASICs) are examples of other families of integrated circuits that are important to the modern <a href="/wiki/Information_society" title="Information society">information society</a>. While the cost of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit_design" title="Integrated circuit design">designing</a> and developing a complex integrated circuit is quite high, when spread across typically millions of production units the individual IC cost is minimized. The performance of ICs is high because the small size allows short traces which in turn allows low <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">power</a> logic (such as <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a>) to be used at fast switching speeds.</p>
<p>In current research projects, integrated circuits are also developed for <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensoric</a> applications in <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">medical implants</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Bioelectronics" title="Bioelectronics">bioelectronic</a> devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birkholz2015_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birkholz2015-32">[32]</a></sup> Special sealing techniques have to be applied in such biogenic environments to avoid <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradation</a> of the exposed semiconductor materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham2011_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham2011-33">[33]</a></sup> As one of the few materials well established in <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> technology, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_nitride" title="Titanium nitride">titanium nitride</a> (TiN) turned out as exceptionally stable and well suited for electrode applications in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_implants" title="Medical implants">medical implants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haemmerle2002_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haemmerle2002-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCT2010_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCT2010-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<p>Integrated circuits can be classified into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_circuit" title="Analog circuit">analog</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_circuit" title="Digital circuit">digital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mixed-signal_integrated_circuit" title="Mixed-signal integrated circuit">mixed signal</a> (both analog and digital on the same chip).</p>
<p>Digital integrated circuits can contain anywhere from one to millions of <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">logic gates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flip-flop_(electronics)" title="Flip-flop (electronics)">flip-flops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiplexer" title="Multiplexer">multiplexers</a>, and other circuits in a few square millimeters. The small size of these circuits allows high speed, low power dissipation, and reduced <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_cost" title="Manufacturing cost">manufacturing cost</a> compared with board-level integration. These digital ICs, typically <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processors" title="Digital signal processors">DSPs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a>, work using binary mathematics to process "one" and "zero" signals.</p>
<p>Analog ICs, such as sensors, <a href="/wiki/Power_network_design_(IC)" title="Power network design (IC)">power management circuits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Operational_amplifier" title="Operational amplifier">operational amplifiers</a>, work by processing continuous signals. They perform functions like <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Active_filter" title="Active filter">active filtering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demodulation" title="Demodulation">demodulation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frequency_mixer" title="Frequency mixer">mixing</a>. Analog ICs ease the burden on circuit designers by having expertly designed analog circuits available instead of designing a difficult analog circuit from scratch.</p>
<p>ICs can also combine analog and digital circuits on a single chip to create functions such as <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">A/D converters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">D/A converters</a>. Such mixed-signal circuits offer smaller size and lower cost, but must carefully account for signal interference.</p>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Category:Electronic_component_distributors" title="Category:Electronic component distributors">electronic component distributors</a> often further sub-categorize the huge variety of integrated circuits now available:</p>
<ul>
<li>Digital ICs are further sub-categorized as logic ICs, <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_memory" title="Semiconductor memory">memory</a> chips, interface ICs (<a href="/wiki/Logic_level" title="Logic level">level shifters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serializer/deserializer" title="Serializer/deserializer">serializer/deserializer</a>, etc.), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Management_IC" title="Power Management IC">Power Management ICs</a>, and programmable devices.</li>
<li>Analog ICs are further sub-categorized as linear ICs and RF ICs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mixed-signal_integrated_circuit" title="Mixed-signal integrated circuit">mixed-signal integrated circuits</a> are further sub-categorized as data acquisition ICs (including A/D converters, D/A converter, <a href="/wiki/Digital_potentiometer" title="Digital potentiometer">digital potentiometers</a>) and clock/timing ICs.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabrication">Fabrication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fabrication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_fabrication" title="Semiconductor fabrication">Semiconductor fabrication</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> were identified as the most likely materials for a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-state_(electronics)" title="Solid-state (electronics)">solid-state</a> <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tube</a></i>. Starting with <a href="/wiki/Copper(I)_oxide" title="Copper(I) oxide">copper oxide</a>, proceeding to <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, the materials were systematically studied in the 1940s and 1950s. Today, <a href="/wiki/Monocrystalline_silicon" title="Monocrystalline silicon">monocrystalline silicon</a> is the main <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(printing)" title="Substrate (printing)">substrate</a> used for ICs although some III-V compounds of the periodic table such as <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> are used for specialized applications like <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LEDs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> and the highest-speed integrated circuits. It took decades to perfect methods of creating <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> without defects in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline_structure" title="Crystalline structure">crystalline structure</a> of the semiconducting material.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">Semiconductor</a> ICs are fabricated in a <a href="/wiki/Planar_process" title="Planar process">planar process</a> which includes three key process steps –  imaging, deposition and etching. The main process steps are supplemented by doping and cleaning.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Monocrystalline_silicon" title="Monocrystalline silicon">Mono-crystal silicon</a> <a href="/wiki/Wafer_(electronics)" title="Wafer (electronics)">wafers</a> (or for special applications, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_on_sapphire" title="Silicon on sapphire">silicon on sapphire</a> or gallium arsenide wafers) are used as the <i>substrate</i>. <a href="/wiki/Photolithography" title="Photolithography">Photolithography</a> is used to mark different areas of the substrate to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doping_(Semiconductors)" title="Doping (Semiconductors)">doped</a> or to have polysilicon, insulators or metal (typically aluminium) tracks deposited on them.</p>
<ul>
<li>Integrated circuits are composed of many overlapping layers, each defined by photolithography, and normally shown in different colors. Some layers mark where various dopants are diffused into the substrate (called diffusion layers), some define where additional ions are implanted (implant layers), some define the conductors (polysilicon or metal layers), and some define the connections between the conducting layers (via or contact layers). All components are constructed from a specific combination of these layers.</li>
<li>In a self-aligned <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> process, a <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a> is formed wherever the gate layer (polysilicon or metal) crosses a diffusion layer.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">Capacitive structures</a>, in form very much like the parallel conducting plates of a traditional electrical capacitor, are formed according to the area of the "plates", with insulating material between the plates. Capacitors of a wide range of sizes are common on ICs.</li>
<li>Meandering stripes of varying lengths are sometimes used to form on-chip <a href="/wiki/Resistor" title="Resistor">resistors</a>, though most logic circuits do not need any resistors. The ratio of the length of the resistive structure to its width, combined with its sheet resistivity, determines the resistance.</li>
<li>More rarely, <a href="/wiki/Inductor" title="Inductor">inductive structures</a> can be built as tiny on-chip coils, or simulated by <a href="/wiki/Gyrator" title="Gyrator">gyrators</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since a CMOS device only draws current on the <i>transition</i> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(logic)" title="Boolean algebra (logic)">logic</a> <a href="/wiki/State_(computer_science)" title="State (computer science)">states</a>, CMOS devices consume much less current than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipolar_transistor" title="Bipolar transistor">bipolar</a> devices.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">random-access memory</a> is the most regular type of integrated circuit; the highest density devices are thus memories; but even a <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> will have memory on the chip. (See the regular array structure at the bottom of the first image.) Although the structures are intricate – with widths which have been shrinking for decades – the layers remain much thinner than the device widths. The layers of material are fabricated much like a photographic process, although light <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> cannot be used to "expose" a layer of material, as they would be too large for the features. Thus <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> of higher frequencies (typically <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>) are used to create the patterns for each layer. Because each feature is so small, <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopes</a> are essential tools for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_process" title="Industrial process">process</a> engineer who might be <a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">debugging</a> a fabrication process.</p>
<p>Each device is tested before packaging using automated test equipment (ATE), in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Wafer_testing" title="Wafer testing">wafer testing</a>, or wafer probing. The wafer is then cut into rectangular blocks, each of which is called a <i>die</i>. Each good <a href="/wiki/Die_(integrated_circuit)" title="Die (integrated circuit)">die</a> (plural <i>dice</i>, <i>dies</i>, or <i>die</i>) is then connected into a package using aluminium (or gold) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_wire" title="Bond wire">bond wires</a> which are <a href="/wiki/Thermosonic_bonding" title="Thermosonic bonding">thermosonically bonded</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> to <i>pads</i>, usually found around the edge of the die. . <a href="/wiki/Thermosonic_bonding" title="Thermosonic bonding">Thermosonic bonding</a> was first introduced by A. Coucoulas which provided a reliable means of forming these vital electrical connections to the outside world. After packaging, the devices go through final testing on the same or similar ATE used during wafer probing. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_CT_scanning" title="Industrial CT scanning">Industrial CT scanning</a> can also be used. Test cost can account for over 25% of the cost of fabrication on lower-cost products, but can be negligible on low-yielding, larger, or higher-cost devices.</p>
<p>As of 2005, a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_fabrication_plant" title="Semiconductor fabrication plant">fabrication facility</a> (commonly known as a <i>semiconductor fab</i>) costs over US$1 billion to construct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The cost of a fabrication facility rises over time (<a href="/wiki/Rock%27s_law" title="Rock's law">Rock's law</a>) because much of the operation is automated. Today, the most advanced processes employ the following techniques:</p>
<ul>
<li>The wafers are up to 300 mm in diameter (wider than a common dinner plate).</li>
<li>Use of 32 nanometer or smaller chip manufacturing process. <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/NEC" title="NEC">NEC</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMD" title="AMD">AMD</a> are using ~32 nanometers for their <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> chips. IBM and AMD introduced <a href="/wiki/Immersion_lithography" title="Immersion lithography">immersion lithography</a> for their 45 nm processes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Copper_interconnect" title="Copper interconnect">Copper interconnects</a> where copper wiring replaces aluminium for interconnects.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-K" title="Low-K">Low-K</a> dielectric insulators.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silicon_on_insulator" title="Silicon on insulator">Silicon on insulator</a> (SOI).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strained_silicon" title="Strained silicon">Strained silicon</a> in a process used by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Strained_silicon_directly_on_insulator" title="Strained silicon directly on insulator">strained silicon directly on insulator</a> (SSDOI).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multigate_device" title="Multigate device">Multigate devices</a> such as tri-gate transistors being manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> from 2011 in their 22 nm process.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit_packaging" title="Integrated circuit packaging">Integrated circuit packaging</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RUS-IC.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="403" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/RUS-IC.JPG/220px-RUS-IC.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/RUS-IC.JPG/330px-RUS-IC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7e/RUS-IC.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RUS-IC.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Soviet MSI nMOS chip made in 1977, part of a four-chip calculator set designed in 1970<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div>

<p>The earliest integrated circuits were packaged in ceramic flat packs, which continued to be used by the military for their reliability and small size for many years. Commercial circuit packaging quickly moved to the <a href="/wiki/Dual_in-line_package" title="Dual in-line package">dual in-line package</a> (DIP), first in ceramic and later in plastic. In the 1980s pin counts of VLSI circuits exceeded the practical limit for DIP packaging, leading to <a href="/wiki/Pin_grid_array" title="Pin grid array">pin grid array</a> (PGA) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leadless_chip_carrier" title="Leadless chip carrier">leadless chip carrier</a> (LCC) packages. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_mount" title="Surface mount">Surface mount</a> packaging appeared in the early 1980s and became popular in the late 1980s, using finer lead pitch with leads formed as either gull-wing or J-lead, as exemplified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small-outline_integrated_circuit" title="Small-outline integrated circuit">small-outline integrated circuit</a> – a carrier which occupies an area about 30–50% less than an equivalent <a href="/wiki/Dual_in-line_package" title="Dual in-line package">DIP</a>, with a typical thickness that is 70% less. This package has "gull wing" leads protruding from the two long sides and a lead spacing of 0.050 inches.</p>
<p>In the late 1990s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PQFP" title="PQFP">plastic quad flat pack</a> (PQFP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_small-outline_package" title="Thin small-outline package">thin small-outline package</a> (TSOP) packages became the most common for high pin count devices, though PGA packages are still often used for high-end <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a>. Intel and AMD are currently transitioning from PGA packages on high-end microprocessors to <a href="/wiki/Land_grid_array" title="Land grid array">land grid array</a> (LGA) packages.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ball_grid_array" title="Ball grid array">Ball grid array</a> (BGA) packages have existed since the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Flip_chip" title="Flip chip">Flip-chip Ball Grid Array</a> packages, which allow for much higher pin count than other package types, were developed in the 1990s. In an FCBGA package the die is mounted upside-down (flipped) and connects to the package balls via a package substrate that is similar to a printed-circuit board rather than by wires. FCBGA packages allow an array of input-output signals (called Area-I/O) to be distributed over the entire die rather than being confined to the die periphery.</p>
<p>Traces out of the die, through the package, and into the <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit board</a> have very different electrical properties, compared to on-chip signals. They require special design techniques and need much more electric power than signals confined to the chip itself.</p>
<p>When multiple dies are put in one package, it is called SiP, for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_In_Package" title="System In Package">System In Package</a></i>. When multiple dies are combined on a small substrate, often ceramic, it's called an MCM, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-Chip_Module" title="Multi-Chip Module">Multi-Chip Module</a>. The distinction between a big MCM and a small printed circuit board is sometimes fuzzy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chip_labeling_and_manufacture_date">Chip labeling and manufacture date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chip labeling and manufacture date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most integrated circuits large enough to include identifying information include four common sections: the manufacturer's name or logo, the part number, a part production batch number and serial number, and a four-digit code that identifies when the chip was manufactured. Extremely small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_mount_technology" title="Surface mount technology">surface mount technology</a> parts often bear only a number used in a manufacturer's lookup table to find the chip characteristics.</p>
<p>The manufacturing date is commonly represented as a two-digit year followed by a two-digit week code, such that a part bearing the code 8341 was manufactured in week 41 of 1983, or approximately in October 1983.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit_layout_design_protection" title="Integrated circuit layout design protection">Integrated circuit layout design protection</a></div>
<p>The possibility of copying by photographing each layer of an integrated circuit and preparing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photomasks" title="Photomasks">photomasks</a> for its production on the basis of the photographs obtained is the main reason for the introduction of legislation for the protection of layout-designs.The Semiconductor Chip Protection Act (SCPA) of 1984 established a new type of intellectual property protection for mask works that are fixed in semiconductor chips. It did so by amending title 17 of the United States chapter 9 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>A diplomatic conference was held at Washington, D.C., in 1989, which adopted a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_on_Intellectual_Property_in_Respect_of_Integrated_Circuits&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Treaty on Intellectual Property in Respect of Integrated Circuits (page does not exist)">Treaty on Intellectual Property in Respect of Integrated Circuits</a> (IPIC Treaty).</p>
<p>The Treaty on Intellectual Property in respect of Integrated Circuits, also called Washington Treaty or IPIC Treaty (signed at Washington on 26 May 1989) is currently not in force, but was partially integrated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRIPS" title="TRIPS">TRIPS</a> agreement.</p>
<p>National laws protecting IC layout designs have been adopted in a number of countries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_developments">Other developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_device" title="Programmable logic device">programmable logic devices</a> were developed. These devices contain circuits whose logical function and connectivity can be programmed by the user, rather than being fixed by the integrated circuit manufacturer. This allows a single chip to be programmed to implement different LSI-type functions such as <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">logic gates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adder_(electronics)" title="Adder (electronics)">adders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Processor_register" title="Processor register">registers</a>. Current devices called <a href="/wiki/Field-programmable_gate_array" title="Field-programmable gate array">field-programmable gate arrays</a> can now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> implement tens of thousands of LSI circuits in parallel and operate up to 1.5 GHz.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The techniques perfected by the integrated circuits industry over the last three decades have been used to create very small mechanical devices driven by electricity using a technology known as <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">microelectromechanical systems</a>. These devices are used in a variety of commercial and military applications. Example commercial applications include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DLP_projector" title="DLP projector">DLP projectors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inkjet_printer" title="Inkjet printer">inkjet printers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">accelerometers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MEMS_gyroscope" title="MEMS gyroscope">MEMS gyroscopes</a> used to deploy automobile <a href="/wiki/Airbag" title="Airbag">airbags</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2014, the vast majority of all transistors are fabricated in a single layer on one side of a chip of silicon in a flat 2-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Planar_process" title="Planar process">planar process</a>. Researchers have produced prototypes of several promising alternatives, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>fabricating transistors over the entire surface of a small sphere of silicon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>various approaches to stacking several layers of transistors to make a <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_integrated_circuit" title="Three-dimensional integrated circuit">three-dimensional integrated circuit</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Through-silicon_via" title="Through-silicon via">through-silicon via</a>, "monolithic 3D",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> stacked wire bonding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> etc.</li>
<li>transistors built from other materials: <a href="/wiki/Graphene#Transistors" title="Graphene">graphene transistors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite#Semiconductor" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite transistors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube_field-effect_transistor" title="Carbon nanotube field-effect transistor">carbon nanotube field-effect transistor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> transistor, transistor-like <a href="/wiki/Nanowire#Electronic_devices" title="Nanowire">nanowire electronic devices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_field-effect_transistor" title="Organic field-effect transistor">organic field-effect transistor</a>, etc.</li>
<li>modifications to the substrate, typically to make "<a href="/wiki/Transistor#Flexible_transistors" title="Transistor">flexible transistors</a>" for a <a href="/wiki/Flexible_display" title="Flexible display">flexible display</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Flexible_electronics" title="Flexible electronics">flexible electronics</a>, possibly leading to a <a href="/wiki/Roll-away_computer" title="Roll-away computer">roll-away computer</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the past, radios could not be fabricated in the same low-cost processes as microprocessors. But since 1998, a large number of radio chips have been developed using CMOS processes. Examples include Intel's DECT cordless phone, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheros" title="Atheros">Atheros</a>'s 802.11 card.</p>
<p>Future developments seem to follow the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-core" title="Multi-core">multi-core</a> multi-microprocessor paradigm, already used by the Intel and AMD dual-core processors. Rapport Inc. and IBM started shipping the <a href="/wiki/Kilocore" title="Kilocore">KC256</a> in 2006, a 256-core microprocessor. Intel, as recently as February–August 2011, unveiled a prototype, "not for commercial sale" chip that bears 80 cores. Each core is capable of handling its own task independently of the others. This is in response to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heat-versus-speed_limit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heat-versus-speed limit (page does not exist)">heat-versus-speed limit</a> that is about to be reached using <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">existing transistor technology</a> (see: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_design_power" title="Thermal design power">thermal design power</a>). This design provides a new challenge to chip programming. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_programming_language" title="Parallel programming language">Parallel programming languages</a> such as the open-source <a href="/wiki/X10_(programming_language)" title="X10 (programming language)">X10</a> programming language are designed to assist with this task.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the early 2000s, the integration of optical functionality (<a href="/wiki/Optical_computing" title="Optical computing">optical computing</a>) into silicon chips has been actively pursued in both academic research and in industry resulting in the successful commercialization of silicon based integrated optical transceivers combining optical devices (modulators, detectors, routing) with CMOS based electronics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicon_labelling_and_graffiti">Silicon labelling and graffiti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Silicon labelling and graffiti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To allow identification during production most silicon chips will have a serial number in one corner. It is also common to add the manufacturer's logo. Ever since ICs were created, some chip designers have used the silicon surface area for surreptitious, non-functional images or words. These are sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Chip_art" title="Chip art">chip art</a>, silicon art, silicon graffiti or silicon doodling.</p>
