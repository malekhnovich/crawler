<p>The <b>cleanroom software engineering</b> process is a <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development process</a> intended to produce software with a certifiable level of <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">reliability</a>. The cleanroom process was originally developed by <a href="/wiki/Harlan_Mills" title="Harlan Mills">Harlan Mills</a> and several of his colleagues including Alan Hevner at <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The focus of the cleanroom process is on defect prevention, rather than defect removal. The name "cleanroom" was chosen to invoke the <a href="/wiki/Cleanroom" title="Cleanroom">cleanrooms</a> used in the electronics industry to prevent the introduction of defects during the fabrication of <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>. The cleanroom process first saw use in the mid to late 1980s. Demonstration projects within the military began in the early 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Recent work on the cleanroom process has examined fusing cleanroom with the automated verification capabilities provided by specifications expressed in <a href="/wiki/Communicating_sequential_processes" title="Communicating sequential processes">CSP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_principles">Central principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanroom_software_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Central principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic principles of the cleanroom process are</p>
<dl>
<dt>Software development based on <a href="/wiki/Formal_methods" title="Formal methods">formal methods</a></dt>
<dd>Software tool support based on some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_formalism" title="Mathematical formalism">mathematical formalism</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Model_checking" title="Model checking">model checking</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_algebras" title="Process algebras">process algebras</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petri_nets" title="Petri nets">Petri nets</a>. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Box_Structure_Method&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Box Structure Method (page does not exist)">Box Structure Method</a> might be one such means of specifying and designing a software product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Verification that the design correctly implements the specification is performed through team review, often with software tool support.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Incremental implementation under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_quality_control" title="Statistical quality control">statistical quality control</a></dt>
<dd>Cleanroom development uses an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iterative" title="Iterative">iterative</a> approach, in which the product is developed in increments that gradually increase the implemented functionality. The quality of each increment is measured against pre-established standards to verify that the development process is proceeding acceptably. A failure to meet quality standards results in the cessation of testing for the current increment, and a return to the design phase.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Statistically sound <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">testing</a></dt>
<dd>Software testing in the cleanroom process is carried out as a statistical experiment. Based on the formal specification, a representative subset of software input/output trajectories is selected and tested. This sample is then statistically analyzed to produce an estimate of the reliability of the software, and a level of confidence in that estimate.</dd>
</dl>
