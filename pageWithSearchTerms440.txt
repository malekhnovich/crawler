<p><b>User experience design</b> (<b>UX</b>, <b>UXD</b>, <b>UED</b> or <b>XD</b>) is the process of enhancing user satisfaction by improving the <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">accessibility</a>, and pleasure provided in the interaction between the user and the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> User experience design encompasses traditional <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Human–computer interaction">human–computer interaction</a> (HCI) design, and extends it by addressing all aspects of a product or service as perceived by users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The field of user experience design is a <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_design" title="Conceptual design">conceptual design</a> discipline and has its roots in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_factors" title="Human factors">human factors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>, a field that, since the late 1940s, has focused on the interaction between human users, machines, and the contextual environments to design systems that address the user's experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> With the proliferation of workplace computers in the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/User_experience" title="User experience">user experience</a> became an important concern for designers. It was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_Norman" title="Donald Norman">Donald Norman</a>, a user experience architect, who coined the term "user experience," and brought it to a wider audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I invented the term because I thought human interface and usability were too narrow. I wanted to cover all aspects of the person's experience with the system including industrial design graphics, the interface, the physical interaction and the manual. Since then the term has spread widely, so much so that it is starting to lose its meaning.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Donald Norman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adaptivepath2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-adaptivepath2007-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The term also has a more recent connection to <a href="/wiki/User-centered_design" title="User-centered design">user-centered design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Human–computer interaction">human–computer interaction</a>, and also incorporates elements from similar user-centered design fields.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>User experience design includes elements of <a href="/wiki/Interaction_design" title="Interaction design">interaction design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_architecture" title="Information architecture">information architecture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/User_research" title="User research">user research</a>, and other disciplines, and is concerned with all facets of the overall experience delivered to users. Following is a short analysis of its constituent parts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_design">Visual design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Visual design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Visual design, also commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, <a href="/wiki/User_interface_design" title="User interface design">user interface design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication_design" title="Communication design">communication design</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Visual_communication" title="Visual communication">visual communication</a>, represents the aesthetics or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Look-and-feel" title="Look-and-feel">look-and-feel</a> of the front end of any <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a>. Graphic treatment of interface elements is often perceived as the visual design. The purpose of visual design is to use visual elements like colors, images, and symbols to convey a message to its audience. Fundamentals of <a href="/wiki/Gestalt_psychology" title="Gestalt psychology">Gestalt psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visual perception</a> give a cognitive perspective on how to create effective visual communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_architecture">Information architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Information architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Information architecture is the art and science of structuring and organizing the information in products and services to support <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Findability" title="Findability">findability</a>.</p>
<p>In the context of information architecture, information is separate from both knowledge and data, and lies nebulously between them. It is information about objects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The objects can range from websites, to software applications, to images et al. It is also concerned with <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>: terms used to describe and represent content objects such as documents, people, process, and organizations.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Structuring.2C_organization.2C_and_labeling">Structuring, organization, and labeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structuring, organization, and labeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structuring" title="Structuring">Structuring</a> is reducing information to its basic building units and then relating them to each other. Organization involves grouping these units in a distinctive and meaningful manner. Labeling means using appropriate wording to support easy navigation and findability.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finding_and_managing">Finding and managing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finding and managing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Findability is the most critical success factor for information architecture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If users are not able to find required information without browsing, searching or asking, then the findability of the information architecture fails. Navigation needs to be clearly conveyed to ease finding of the contents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_design">Interaction design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interaction design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interaction_design" title="Interaction design">Interaction design</a></div>
<p>There are many key factors to understanding interaction design and how it can enable a pleasurable end user experience. It is well recognized <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that building great user experience requires interaction design to play a pivotal role in helping define what works best for the users. High demand for improved user experiences and strong focus on the end-users have made interaction designers critical in conceptualizing design that matches <a href="/wiki/User_expectations" title="User expectations">user expectations</a> and standards of the latest UI patterns and components. While working, interaction designers take several things in consideration. A few of them are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UXMatters_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-UXMatters-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Defining interaction patterns best suited in the context</li>
<li>Incorporating user needs collected during user research into the designs</li>
<li>Features and information that are important to the user</li>
<li>Interface behavior like drag-drop, selections, and mouse-over actions</li>
<li>Effectively communicating strengths of the system</li>
<li>Making the interface intuitive by building affordances</li>
<li>Maintaining consistency throughout the system.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the last few years, the role of interaction designer has shifted from being just focused on specifying UI components and communicating them to the engineers to a situation now where designers have more freedom to design contextual interfaces which are based on helping meet the user needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Therefore, User Experience Design evolved into a multidisciplinary design branch that involves multiple technical aspects from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_graphics_design" title="Motion graphics design">motion graphics design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animation</a> to <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programming</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usability">Usability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Usability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">Usability</a></div>
<p>Usability is the extent to which a product can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals with effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction in a specified context of use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Usability is attached with all tools used by humans and is extended to both digital and non-digital devices. Thus, it is a subset of user experience but not wholly contained. The section of usability that intersects with user experience design is related to humans' ability to use a system or application. Good usability is essential to a positive user experience but does not alone guarantee it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessibility">Accessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">Accessibility</a></div>
<p>Accessibility of a system describes its ease of reach, use and understanding. In terms of user experience design it can also be related to the overall comprehensibility of the information and features. It contributes to shorten the learning curve attached with the system. Accessibility in many contexts can be related to the ease of use for people with disabilities and comes under usability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human.E2.80.93computer_interaction">Human–computer interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Human–computer interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" title="Human-computer interaction">Human-computer interaction</a></div>
<p>Human–computer interaction is concerned with the design, evaluation and implementation of interactive computing systems for human use and with the study of major phenomena surrounding them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Human–computer interaction is the main contributor to user experience design because of its emphasis on human performance rather than mere usability. It provides key research findings which inform the improvement of systems for the people. Human-computer interaction extends its study towards more integrated interactions, such as tangible interactions, which is generally not covered in the practice of user experience. User experience cannot be manufactured or designed; it has to be incorporated in the design. Understanding the user's emotional quotient plays a key role while designing a user experience. The first step while designing the user experience is determining the reason a visitor will be visiting the website or use the application in question. Then the user experience can be designed accordingly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">Design</a></div>
<p>User experience design incorporates most or all of the above disciplines to positively impact the overall experience a person has with a particular interactive system and its provider. User experience design most frequently defines a sequence of interactions between a user (individual person) and a system, virtual or physical, designed to meet or support user needs and goals, primarily, while also satisfying systems requirements and organizational objectives.</p>
<p>Typical outputs include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Persona_(user_experience)" title="Persona (user experience)">Persona</a> (an archetypal user for whom the product or service is being designed)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Website_wireframe" title="Website wireframe">Wireframes</a> (screen blueprints or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storyboards" title="Storyboards">storyboards</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">Prototypes</a> (for interactive or in-the-mind simulation)</li>
<li>Written specifications (describing the behavior or design)</li>
<li>Site audit (usability study of existing assets)</li>
<li>Flows and navigation maps</li>
<li>User stories or scenarios</li>
<li>Sitemaps and content inventory</li>
<li>High-fidelity visual mockups (precise visual layout and design of the expected product or interface)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_design_process">General design process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: General design process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While designing a product or service for a client, it is of utmost importance that the designers are on the same page as the client. All the information collected, plans made, design executed will reflect on the final product. Rigorous analysis must be done before proceeding to the design stage and then numerous testings done to optimize the site as per best standards so that the competitive edge is maintained. Leading Digital marketing companies combine three elements to provide the best responsive product to the customer. These are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Researching about the <a href="/wiki/Target_audience" title="Target audience">target audience</a></li>
<li>Understanding the company's business goals</li>
<li>And most importantly apply out of the box thinking.</li>
</ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brainstorming" title="Brainstorming">Brainstorming</a> and testing ultimately leads them to finalize the design for their customers. Let's have a detailed look at the step by step process of product design:</p>
<ul>
<li>Collecting information about the problem</li>
</ul>
<p>The UX designer needs to find out as much as they can about people, processes, and products before the design phase. Designers can do this by meeting with the clients or business stakeholders frequently to know what their requirements are, or by conducting interviews with users in their home or work spaces. This kind of qualitative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/User_research" title="User research">research</a> helps designers create products and services that better serve user needs.</p>
<ul>
<li>Getting ready to design</li>
</ul>
<p>After research, the designer must make sense of the data they've collected. Typically this is done through modeling of the users and their environments. User modeling or <a href="/wiki/Persona_(user_experience)" title="Persona (user experience)">personas</a> are composite archetypes based on behavior patterns uncovered during research. Personas provide designers a precise way of thinking and communicating about how groups of users behave, how they think, what they want to accomplish and why.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Once created, personas help the designer to understand the users' goals in specific contexts, which is particularly useful during ideation and for validating design concepts. Other types of models include work flow models, artifact models, and physical models.</p>
<ul>
<li>Design</li>
</ul>
<p>When the designer has a firm grasp on the user's needs and goals, they begin to sketch out the interaction framework (also known as <a href="/wiki/Website_wireframe" title="Website wireframe">wireframes</a>). This stage defines the high-level structure of screen layouts, as well as the product's flow, behavior, and organization. There are many kinds of materials that can be involved in during this iterative phase, from whiteboards to paper prototypes. As the interaction framework establishes an overall structure for product behavior, a parallel process focused on the visual and industrial designs. The visual design framework defines the experience attributes, visual language, and the visual style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once a solid and stable framework is established, wireframes are translated from sketched storyboards to full-resolution screens that depict the user interface at the pixel level. At this point, it's critical for the programming team to collaborate closely with the designer. Their input is necessary to creating a finished design that can and will be built while remaining true to the concept.</p>
<ul>
<li>Test and iterate</li>
</ul>
<p>Usability testing is carried out through prototypes. The target users are given various tasks to perform on the prototypes. Any issues or problems faced by the users are collected as field notes and these notes are used to make changes in the design and reiterate the testing phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Usability testing is, at its core, a means to "evaluate, not create".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="UX_Deliverables">UX Deliverables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: UX Deliverables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UX designers' main goal is to solve the end-users' problems, and thus the ability to communicate the design to stakeholders and developers is critical to the ultimate success of the design. Regarding UX specification documents, these requirements depend on the client or the organization involved in designing a product. The four major deliverables are: a title page, an introduction to the feature, wireframes and a version history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Depending on the type of project, the specification documents can also include flow models, cultural models, personas, user stories, scenarios and any prior user research. Documenting design decisions, in the form of annotated wireframes, gives the developer the necessary information they may need to successfully code the project.</p>
<p>Depending on the company, a user experience designer may need to be a jack of all trades. It is not uncommon to see a user experience designer jump in at the beginning of the project lifecycle, where the problem set and project definition is vague, or after the project requirements document has been finalized and wireframes and functional annotations need to be created.</p>
<p>The following details the responsibilities a user experience designer may have at each phase of a project:</p>
<p><b>At the beginning, when the project is more conceptual:</b></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">Ethnographic</a> research</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">Surveying</a></li>
<li>Customer feedback and testing</li>
<li>Focus group administration</li>
<li>Non-directed interview</li>
<li>Contextual Interview</li>
<li>Mental modeling</li>
<li>Mood boards</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Card_sorting" title="Card sorting">Card sorting</a></li>
<li>Competitive analysis</li>
<li>Contextual Inquiry</li>
</ul>
<p><b>While the project is underway:</b></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireframing" title="Wireframing">Wireframing</a></li>
<li>Heuristic analysis</li>
<li>Expert evaluation</li>
<li>Pluralistic walkthrough</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personas" title="Personas">Personas</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/User_scenario" title="User scenario">Scenario</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">Prototypes</a></li>
<li>System mapping</li>
<li>Experience mapping</li>
<li>User testing/<a href="/wiki/Usability_testing" title="Usability testing">usability testing</a></li>
</ul>
<p><b>After the project has launched:</b></p>
<ul>
<li>User testing/usability testing</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/A/B_testing" title="A/B testing">A/B testing</a></li>
<li>Additional wireframing as a result of test results and fine-tuning<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>As with the fields mentioned above, user experience design is a highly multi-disciplinary field, incorporating aspects of <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>, and business. Depending on the purpose of the product, UX may also involve content design disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Communication_design" title="Communication design">communication design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instructional_design" title="Instructional design">instructional design</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Game_design" title="Game design">game design</a>. The subject matter of the content may also warrant collaboration with a <a href="/wiki/Subject-matter_expert" title="Subject-matter expert">subject-matter expert</a> on planning the UX from various backgrounds in business, government, or private groups. More recently, <a href="/wiki/Content_strategy" title="Content strategy">content strategy</a> has come to represent a sub-field of UX.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphic_designers">Graphic designers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Graphic designers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graphic designers focus on the aesthetic appeal of the design. Information is communicated to the users through text and images. Much importance is given to how the text and images look and attract the users. Graphic designers have to make stylistic choices about things like font color, font type, and image locations. Graphic designers focus on grabbing the user's attention with the way the design looks. Graphic designers create visual concepts, using computer software or by hand, to communicate ideas that inspire, inform, and captivate consumers. They develop the overall layout and production design for various applications such as advertisements, brochures, magazines, and corporate reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_designers">Visual designers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Visual designers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The visual designer (VisD) ensures that the visual representation of the design effectively communicates the data and hints at the expected behavior of the product. At the same time, the visual designer is responsible for conveying the brand ideals in the product and for creating a positive first impression; this responsibility is shared with the industrial designer if the product involves hardware. In essence, a visual designer must aim for maximum <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a> combined with maximum desirability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_designers">Interaction designers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Interaction designers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interaction designers (IxD) are responsible for understanding and specifying how the product should behave. This work overlaps with the work of both visual and industrial designers in a couple of important ways. When designing physical products, interaction designers must work with industrial designers early on to specify the requirements for physical inputs and to understand the behavioral impacts of the mechanisms behind them. Interaction designers cross paths with visual designers throughout the project. Visual designers guide the discussions of the brand and emotive aspects of the experience, Interaction designers communicate the priority of information, flow, and functionality in the interface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_the_design">Testing the design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Testing the design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Usability_testing" title="Usability testing">Usability testing</a></div>
<p>Usability testing is the most common method used by designers to test their designs. The basic idea behind conducting a usability test is to check whether the design of a product or brand works well with the target users. While carrying out usability testing, two things are being tested for: Whether the design of the product is successful and if it is not successful, how can it be improved. While designers are testing, they are testing the design and not the user. Also, every design is evolving. The designers carry out usability testing at every stage of the design process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=User_experience_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>User experience design is integrated into <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> and other forms of application development to inform feature requirements and interaction plans based upon the users' goals. Every new software introduced must keep pace with the rapid technological advancements. The benefits associated with integration of these design principles include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Avoiding unnecessary product features</li>
<li>Simplifying design documentation and customer-centric technical publications</li>
<li>Improving the usability of the system and therefore its acceptance by customers</li>
<li>Expediting design and development through detailed and properly conceived guidelines</li>
<li>Incorporating business and marketing goals while protecting the user's freedom of choice</li>
</ul>
