<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Outline_(list)" title="Outline (list)">outline</a> is provided as an overview of and topical guide to prehistoric technology.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_technology" title="Prehistoric technology">Prehistoric technology</a></b> – <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> that predates <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>. History is the study of the past using written records; it is also the record itself. Anything prior to the first written accounts of history is <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> (meaning "before history"), including earlier technologies. About 2.5 million years before writing was developed, technology began with the earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominids</a> who used <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a>, which they may have used to start fires, hunt, cut food, and bury their dead.</p>

<p>Prehistoric technology can be described as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">Prehistoric</a> – "before we had written records," from the Latin word for "before," præ. Prehistory is the span of time before recorded history, that is, before the invention of writing systems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">Technology</a> – making, modification, usage, and knowledge of tools, machines, techniques, crafts, systems, and methods of organization, in order to solve a problem, improve a preexisting solution to a problem, achieve a goal, handle an applied input/output relation or perform a specific function.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_World_prehistoric_technology">Old World prehistoric technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Old World prehistoric technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Three-age_system" title="Three-age system">Three-age system</a> – in archaeology and physical anthropology, the periodization of human prehistory into three consecutive time periods, each named after the main material used in its respective tool-making technologies: the Stone Age, the Bronze Age, and the Iron Age.</li>
<li>Beginning of prehistoric technology – the earliest technology began (2.5 million years) before recorded history, that is, at the beginning of the Stone Age.</li>
<li>Latest prehistoric technology – the level of technology reached before true writing was introduced differed by region (and usually included proto-writing)...
<ul>
<li>Latest prehistoric technology in the Near East – cultures in the Near East achieved the development of writing first, during their Bronze Age.</li>
<li>Latest prehistoric technology in the rest of the Old World: Europe, India, and China reached Iron Age technological development before the introduction of writing there.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stone_Age_technology_in_the_Old_World">Stone Age technology in the Old World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stone Age technology in the Old World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> – broad prehistoric period, lasting roughly 2.5 million years, during which stone was widely used in the manufacture of implements with a sharp edge, a point, or a percussion surface. The period began with hominids and ended between 6000 and 2000 BCE with the advent of metalworking.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleolithic_technology">Paleolithic technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleolithic technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> – prehistoric period of human history distinguished by the development of the most primitive stone tools discovered (Grahame Clark's Modes I and II), and covers roughly 99% of human technological prehistory.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lower_Paleolithic_technology">Lower Paleolithic technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lower Paleolithic technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lower_Paleolithic" title="Lower Paleolithic">Lower Paleolithic</a> – earliest subdivision of the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> or Old <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>. It spans the time from around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E13_s" title="1 E13 s">2.5 million years</a> ago when the first evidence of craft and use of <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">hominids</a> appears in the current <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_record" title="Archaeological record">archaeological record</a>, until around 300,000 years ago, spanning the <a href="/wiki/Oldowan" title="Oldowan">Oldowan</a> ("mode 1") and <a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> ("mode 2") <a href="/wiki/Lithic_technology" title="Lithic technology">lithic technology</a>.
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_tools" title="Stone tools">Stone tool use</a> – early human (hominid) use of stone tool technology, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hand_axe" title="Hand axe">hand axe</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Tool_use_by_animals" title="Tool use by animals">similar to that of primates</a>, which is found to be limited to the intelligence levels of modern children aged 3 to 5 years. Ancestors of homo sapiens (modern man) used stone tools as follows:
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_habilis" title="Homo habilis">Homo habilis</a></i> ("handy man") – first "homo" species. It lived from approximately <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2.3–1.4">2.3 to 1.4</a> million years ago</span> in Africa and created stone tools called <a href="/wiki/Oldowan" title="Oldowan">Oldowan</a> tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_ergaster" title="Homo ergaster">Homo ergaster</a></i> – in eastern and southern Africa about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2.5–1.7">2.5 to 1.7</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>, it refined <a href="/wiki/Oldowan" title="Oldowan">Oldowan</a> tools and developed the first <a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biface" title="Biface">bifacial</a> axes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> ("upright man") – lived about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=1.8–1.3">1.8 to 1.3</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Asia" title="West Asia">West Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and is thought to be the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominid</a> to hunt in coordinated groups, use complex tools, and care for infirm or weaker companions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_antecessor" title="Homo antecessor">Homo antecessor</a></i> – earliest hominid in Northern Europe. It lived from 1.2 million to 800,000 years ago and used stone tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a></i> – lived between 600,000 and 400,000 years ago and used stone tool technology similar to the <a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> tools used by <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Control_of_fire_by_early_humans" title="Control of fire by early humans">Control of fire by early humans</a> – European and Asian sites dating back 1.5 million years ago seem to indicate controlled use of <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a> by <i>H. erectus</i>. A northern <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> site from about 690,000 to 790,000 years ago suggests that man could light fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">Burial</a> – the act of placing a deceased person into the ground.
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a></i> – may have been the first species to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleolithic_burial" title="Paleolithic burial">bury their dead</a> about 500,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Paleolithic_technology">Middle Paleolithic technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Middle Paleolithic technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Middle_Paleolithic" title="Middle Paleolithic">Middle Paleolithic</a> period – in Europe and the Near East during which the <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> lived (c. 300,000–28,000 years ago). Their technology is mainly the <a href="/wiki/Mousterian" title="Mousterian">Mousterian</a>. The earliest evidence (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mungo_Man" title="Mungo Man">Mungo Man</a>) of settlement in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> dates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Australia_before_1901" title="History of Australia before 1901">to around 40,000 years ago</a> when modern humans likely crossed from Asia by island-hopping. The <a href="/wiki/Bhimbetka_rock_shelters" title="Bhimbetka rock shelters">Bhimbetka rock shelters</a> exhibit the earliest traces of human life in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, some of which are approximately 30,000 years old.
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis" title="Homo neanderthalensis">Homo neanderthalensis</a></i>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">Stone tools</a> – <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis" title="Homo neanderthalensis">homo neanderthalensis</a></i> used <a href="/wiki/Mousterian" title="Mousterian">Mousterian</a> stone tools that date back to around 300,000 years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and include smaller, knife-like and scraper tools.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">Burials</a> – <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis" title="Homo neanderthalensis">homo neanderthalensis</a></i> buried their dead, doing so in shallow graves along with stone tools and animal bones, although the reasons and significance of the burials are disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evolving_graves_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-evolving_graves-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">Homo sapiens</a></i> – the only living <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Homo" title="Homo">Homo</a></i> genus originated in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> about 200,000 years ago. Greater mental capability and ability to walk erect provided freed hands for manipulating objects, which allowed for far greater use of <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sternberg_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sternberg-15">[15]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_Middle_Paleolithic" title="Art of the Middle Paleolithic">Art of the Middle Paleolithic</a> –</li>
<li>Burial – intentional burial, particularly with <a href="/wiki/Grave_goods" title="Grave goods">grave goods</a>, may be one of the earliest detectable forms of religious practice since it may signify a "concern for the dead that transcends daily life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lieberman_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-lieberman-16">[16]</a></sup> The earliest undisputed human burial so far dates back 130,000 years. Human skeletal remains stained with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_ochre" title="Red ochre">red ochre</a> were discovered in the Skhul cave at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qafzeh" title="Qafzeh">Qafzeh</a>, Israel with a variety of grave goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lieberman2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lieberman2-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_Paleolithic_Revolution">Upper Paleolithic Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Upper Paleolithic Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic_Revolution" title="Upper Paleolithic Revolution">Upper Paleolithic Revolution</a> – theoretical occurrence between 60,000 and 30,000 years ago, possibly the origin of language, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_modernity" title="Behavioral modernity">modern human behavior</a>, accompanied radical advancements in technology made possible by it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabora328-342_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gabora328-342-18">[18]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Behavioral_modernity" title="Behavioral modernity">Behavioral modernity</a> – a set of traits that distinguish <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> from extinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominid</a> lineages. <i>Homo sapiens</i> reached full behavior modernity around 50,000 years ago due to a <a href="/wiki/Encephalization" title="Encephalization">highly developed</a> <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a> capable of abstract <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reasoning" title="Reasoning">reasoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Introspection" title="Introspection">introspection</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sternberg_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sternberg-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mellars_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-mellars-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Tools – included <a href="/wiki/Aurignacian" title="Aurignacian">Aurignacian</a> tools, such as stone bladed tools, tools made of antlers, and tools made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_tools" title="Bone tools">bones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MellarsArcheology_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-MellarsArcheology-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_clothing_and_textiles#Prehistoric_development" title="History of clothing and textiles">Clothing</a> – evidence, such as possible sewing needles from around 40,000 years ago and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-21">[21]</a></sup> dyed <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> fibers dated 36,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> found in a prehistoric cave in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Georgia" title="Republic of Georgia">Republic of Georgia</a> suggest that people were wearing clothes at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Human beings may have begun wearing clothing as far back as 190,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travis2-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_Upper_Paleolithic" title="Art of the Upper Paleolithic">Art of the Upper Paleolithic</a> – included cave painting, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Venus_figurines" title="Venus figurines">Venus figurines</a>, carvings and engravings of bone and ivory, and musical instruments such as flutes. The most common subject matter was large animals that were hunted by the people of the time.

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Altamira_and_Paleolithic_Cave_Art_of_Northern_Spain" title="Cave of Altamira and Paleolithic Cave Art of Northern Spain">Cave of Altamira and Paleolithic Cave Art of Northern Spain</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4a_Valley_Paleolithic_Art" title="Côa Valley Paleolithic Art">Côa Valley Paleolithic Art</a></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolithic_technology">Mesolithic technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mesolithic technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Mesolithic – the transitional period between the Paleolithic <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> warm period around 11,660 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> and ending with the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic</a> introduction of farming, the date of which varied in each geographical region. Adaptation was required during this period due to climate changes that affected environment and the types of available food.
<ul>
<li>Stone tool changes – small stone tools called <a href="/wiki/Microlith" title="Microlith">Microliths</a>, including small <a href="/wiki/Bladelets" title="Bladelets">bladelets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microburin" title="Microburin">microburins</a>, emerged during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Weapons – <a href="/wiki/Spear#Prehistory" title="Spear">spears</a> or arrows were found at the earliest known Mesolithic battle site at <a href="/wiki/Cemetery_117" title="Cemetery 117">Cemetery 117</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Holmegaard_bow" title="Holmegaard bow">Holmegaard</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_(weapon)" title="Bow (weapon)">bows</a> were found in the <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> of Northern Europe dating from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolithic_period" title="Mesolithic period">Mesolithic period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comstock_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-comstock-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic_Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Neolithic Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> – first agricultural revolution, representing a transition from <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunting and gathering</a> nomadic life to an <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> existence. It evolved independently in six separate locations worldwide circa 10,000–7000 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> (8,000–5,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">BC</a>). The earliest known evidence exists in the tropical and subtropical areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwest_Asia" title="Southwest Asia">southwestern</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Asia" title="Southern Asia">southern Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">northern</a>/<a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">central Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-28">[28]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>Defining characteristics
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origins_of_agriculture" title="Origins of agriculture">Introduction of agriculture</a> – a defining characteristic of Neolithic societies, which resulted in a swing from a nomadic lifestyle to one that was more sedentary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw422-423_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw422-423-29">[29]</a></sup> and the use of agricultural tools such as the <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">plough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digging_stick" title="Digging stick">digging stick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hoe_(tool)" title="Hoe (tool)">hoe (tool)</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">Domestication</a> – of animals, including <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_domestic_dog" title="Origin of the domestic dog">dogs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw422-423_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw422-423-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">Pottery</a> – emerged as a defining characteristic of the Neolithic period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw422-423_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw422-423-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Other
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neolithic_architecture" title="Neolithic architecture">Architecture</a> – included houses and villages built of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mud-brick" title="Mud-brick">mud-brick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">wattle and daub</a> and the construction of storage facilities, tombs and monuments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">Metalworking</a> – <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> use began as early as 9000 BC in the Middle East;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discovery_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-discovery-31">[31]</a></sup> and a copper pendant found in northern Iraq dated to 8700 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_writing_ancient_numbers" title="History of writing ancient numbers">Numeric counting</a> – record keeping evolved from a system of counting using small clay tokens that began in <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> about 8000 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proto-writing" title="Proto-writing">Proto-writing</a> – ideographic and/or early mnemonic symbols used to convey information, probably devoid of direct linguistic content. These systems emerged in the early Neolithic period, as early as the 7th millennium BCE.
<ul>
<li>Neolithic signs in Europe
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vin%C4%8Da_signs" title="Vinča signs">Vinča signs</a> (<a href="/wiki/T%C4%83rt%C4%83ria_tablets" title="Tărtăria tablets">Tărtăria tablets</a>), ca. 5300 BCE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haarmann:_T.C4.83rt.C4.83ria_tablets-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neolithic_signs_in_China" title="Neolithic signs in China">Neolithic signs in China</a> – at a range of Neolithic sites in China, small numbers of symbols of either pictorial or simple geometric nature have been unearthed which were incised into or drawn or painted on artifacts, mostly on pottery but in some instances on turtle shells, animal bones or artifacts made from bone or jade.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jiahu_symbols" title="Jiahu symbols">Jiahu symbols</a>, carved on <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_shell" title="Animal shell">shells</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a>, ca. 6600 BC</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_tools" title="Stone tools">Stone tools</a> – ground and polished tools were created during the Neolithic period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw422-423_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw422-423-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Religious structures – such as the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe" title="Göbekli Tepe">Göbekli Tepe</a> built about 12,000 years ago.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">Wheel</a> – in the late Neolithic period, the wheel was introduced for making pottery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_Iron_Age_technology_in_the_Old_World">Prehistoric Iron Age technology in the Old World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prehistoric Iron Age technology in the Old World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> – age characterized by the widespread use of iron or steel, which coincided with other changes in society, including differing agricultural practices, religious beliefs and artistic styles.
<ul>
<li>Tools – best tools and weapons were made from steel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_prehistory_and_the_beginning_of_history">End of prehistory and the beginning of history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: End of prehistory and the beginning of history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Development of true writing systems – in the Old World, true writing systems developed from <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> writing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a> (<a href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BC" title="4th millennium BC">4th millennium BC</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> archaic (pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_(script)" title="Cuneiform (script)">cuneiform</a>) writing and the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a> are generally considered the earliest true writing systems, both emerging out of their ancestral proto-literate symbol systems from 3400–3200 BC with earliest coherent texts from about <a href="/wiki/26th_century_BC" title="26th century BC">2600 BC</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_from_proto-writing_to_true_writing">Transition from proto-writing to true writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transition from proto-writing to true writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>General developmental stages leading from proto-writing to true writing:
<ul>
<li><i>Picture writing system</i>: glyphs directly represent objects and ideas or objective and ideational situations. In connection with this the following substages may be distinguished:
<ol>
<li>The mnemonic: glyphs primarily a reminder;</li>
<li>The pictographic (pictography): glyphs represent directly an object or an objective situation such as (A) chronological, (B) notices, (C) communications, (D) totems, titles, and names, (E) religious, (F) customs, (G) historical, and (H) biographical;</li>
<li>The ideographic (ideography): glyphs represent directly an idea or an ideational situation.</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li><i>Transitional system</i>: glyphs refer not only to the object or idea which it represents but to its name as well.</li>
<li><i>Phonetic system</i>: glyphs refer to sounds or spoken symbols irrespective of their meanings. This resolves itself into the following substages:
<ol>
<li>The verbal: glyph (<a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logogram</a>) represents a whole word;</li>
<li>The syllabic: glyph represent a syllable;</li>
<li>The alphabetic: glyph represent an elementary sound.</li>
</ol>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistoric_technology_of_the_Americas">Prehistoric technology of the Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Prehistoric technology of the Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The New World, or American, periods began with the crossing of the <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabaskan_language" title="Athabaskan language">Athabaskan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aleut" title="Aleut">Aleuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yupik peoples</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Bering Land Bridge</a> onto the North American continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In their book, <i>Method and Theory in American Archaeology,</i> Gordon Willey and Philip Phillips defined five cultural stages for the Americas, including the three prehistoric <a href="/wiki/Lithic_stage" title="Lithic stage">Lithic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaic_period_in_the_Americas" title="Archaic period in the Americas">Archaic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formative_stage" title="Formative stage">Formative stages</a>. The historic stages are the <a href="/wiki/Classic_stage" title="Classic stage">Classic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-Classic_stage" title="Post-Classic stage">Post-Classic stages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassells9_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cassells9-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleo-Indian" title="Paleo-Indian">Paleo-Indian period</a> – the first people who entered, and subsequently inhabited, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_continent" title="American continent">American continent</a> during the final <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">glacial episodes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">late Pleistocene period</a>. Evidence suggests big-game hunters crossed the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a> from Asia into North America over a land and ice bridge (<a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a>), that existed between 45,000 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a> – 12,000 BCE,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> following herds of large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a> far into <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabaskan" title="Athabaskan">Athabaskan</a>-speakers</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleuts" title="Aleuts">Aleuts</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yup'ik</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithic_technology">Lithic technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lithic technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lithic_technology" title="Lithic technology">Lithic technology</a> – occurred from 12,000 to 6,000 years before present and included the <a href="/wiki/Clovis_culture" title="Clovis culture">Clovis culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Folsom_tradition" title="Folsom tradition">Folsom tradition</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plano_culture" title="Plano culture">Plano culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassells9_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cassells9-38">[38]</a></sup> Clovis culture was once considered the first culture to use projectile points to hunt on the North American continent. Since then, a <a href="/wiki/Manis_Mastodon_Site" title="Manis Mastodon Site">pre-Clovis site was found in Manis, Washington</a> that found use of projectile points to hunt mastodons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaic_period_technology">Archaic period technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Archaic period technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaic_period_in_the_Americas" title="Archaic period in the Americas">Archaic</a> – was dated from 8,000 to 2,000 years before present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassells9_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cassells9-38">[38]</a></sup> People were hunters of small game, such as deer, antelope and rabbits, and gatherers of wild plants, moving seasonally to hunting and gathering sites. Late in the Archaic period, about 200-500 CE, corn was introduced into the diet and pottery-making became an occupation for storing and caring food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formative_stage_technology">Formative stage technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_prehistoric_technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Formative stage technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Formative_stage" title="Formative stage">Formative stage</a> – followed the Archaic period and continued until the point of contact by European people. Cultures from that period include that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Pueblo_People" title="Ancient Pueblo People">Ancient Pueblo People</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippian_culture" title="Mississippian culture">Mississippian culture</a> and Olmec cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassells9_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cassells9-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
