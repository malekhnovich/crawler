<p><b>Industrial design</b> is a process of <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> applied to <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> that are to be manufactured through techniques of <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirkham1999_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirkham1999-3">[3]</a></sup> Its key characteristic is that design is separated from manufacture: the creative act of determining and defining a product's form and features takes place in advance of the physical act of making a product, which consists purely of repeated, often automated, replication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENoblet199321-22_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENoblet199321-22-5">[5]</a></sup> This distinguishes industrial design from <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a>-based design, where the form of the product is determined by the product's creator at the time of its creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENoblet199321_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENoblet199321-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>All manufactured products are the result of a design process, but the nature of this process can take many forms: it can be conducted by an individual or a large team; it can emphasize intuitive <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a> or calculated <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_design" title="Evidence-based design">scientific decision-making</a>, and often emphasizes both at the same time; and it can be influenced by factors as varied as <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">materials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_process_management" title="Manufacturing process management">production processes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_strategy" title="Business strategy">business strategy</a> and prevailing social, commercial or aesthetic attitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-4">[4]</a></sup> The role of an industrial designer is to create and execute design solutions for problems of form, function, usability, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_ergonomics" title="Physical ergonomics">physical ergonomics</a>, marketing, brand development, and sales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">Precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For several millennia before the onset of <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> design, technical expertise and manufacture lay together in the hands of individual <a href="/wiki/Craft_production" title="Craft production">craftsmen</a>, who determined the form of a product at the point of its creation according to their own manual skill, the parameters set by their clients, the experience accumulated through their own experimentation and traditional knowledge passed on to them through training or apprenticeship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENoblet199321_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENoblet199321-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Division_of_labour" title="Division of labour">division of labour</a> that underlies the practice of industrial design did have precedents in the pre-industrial era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11-2">[2]</a></sup> The growth of trade in the medieval period led to the emergence of large workshops in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>, where groups of more specialist craftsmen made objects with common forms through the repetitive duplication of models defined by their shared training and technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011-8">[8]</a></sup> Competitive pressures in the early 16th century led to the emergence in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_books&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pattern books (page does not exist)">pattern books</a>: collections of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engravings" title="Engravings">engravings</a> illustrating decorative forms and motifs that could be applied to a wide range of products, and whose creation therefore took place in advance of their application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011-8">[8]</a></sup> The use of <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">drawing</a> to specify how something is later to be constructed was first developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architects" title="Architects">architects</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipwrights" title="Shipwrights">shipwrights</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaynes1991108_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaynes1991108-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 17th century the scale of the artistic patronage of centralised monarchical states such as <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> led to the growth of large government-operated manufacturing operations epitomised by the <a href="/wiki/Gobelins_Manufactory" title="Gobelins Manufactory">Gobelins Manufactory</a>, opened in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1667 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">Louis XIV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011-8">[8]</a></sup> Here teams of hundreds of craftsmen, including specialist artists, decorators and engravers, produced sumptuously decorated products ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapestries" title="Tapestries">tapestries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">furniture</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalwork" title="Metalwork">metalwork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coach_(carriage)" title="Coach (carriage)">coaches</a>, all under the creative supervision of the King's leading artist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Le_Brun" title="Charles Le Brun">Charles Le Brun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011-12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198011-12-10">[10]</a></sup> This pattern of large-scale royal patronage was repeated in the court porcelain factories of the early 18th century, such as the <a href="/wiki/Meissen_porcelain" title="Meissen porcelain">Meissen porcelain</a> workshops established in 1709 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach" title="Grand Duchy of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach">Grand Duke of Saxony</a>, where patterns from a range of sources, including court goldsmiths, sculptors and engravers, were used as models for the vessels and figurines for which it became famous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198012_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198012-11">[11]</a></sup> As long as reproduction remained craft-based, however, the form and artistic quality of the product remained in the hands of the individual craftsman, and tended to decline as the scale of production increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198012-13_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198012-13-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_industrial_design">Birth of industrial design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Birth of industrial design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The emergence of industrial design is specifically linked to the growth of industrialisation and mechanisation that began with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> in the mid 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeskett198010-11-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirkham1999_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirkham1999-3">[3]</a></sup> The rise of industrial manufacture changed the way objects were made, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanisation" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a> changed patterns of <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumption</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">growth of empires</a> broadened tastes and diversified markets, and the emergence of a wider <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> created demand for fashionable styles from a much larger and more heterogeneous population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBenton2000380_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBenton2000380-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first use of the term "industrial design" is often attributed to the industrial designer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Claude_Sinel" title="Joseph Claude Sinel">Joseph Claude Sinel</a> in 1919 (although he himself denied this in interviews), but the discipline predates 1919 by at least a decade. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Dresser" title="Christopher Dresser">Christopher Dresser</a> is considered among the first independent industrial designers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dresser_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dresser-14">[14]</a></sup> Industrial design's origins lie in the industrialization of consumer products. For instance the <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Werkbund" title="Deutscher Werkbund">Deutscher Werkbund</a>, founded in 1907 and a precursor to the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a>, was a state-sponsored effort to integrate traditional crafts and industrial mass-production techniques, to put <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> on a competitive footing with <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>The earliest use of the term may have been in <a href="/wiki/The_Art_Journal" title="The Art Journal"><i>The Art Union</i>, A monthly Journal of the Fine Arts</a>, 1839.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Dyce’s report to the Board of Trade on foreign schools of Design for Manufactures. Mr Dyces official visit to France, Prussia and Bavaria for the purpose of examining the state of schools of design in those countries will be fresh in the recollection of our readers. His report on this subject was ordered to be printed some few months since, on the motion of Mr Hume.</p>
<p>The school of St Peter, at Lyons was founded about 1750 for the instruction of draftsmen employed in preparing patterns for the silk manufacture. It has been much more successful than the Paris school and having been disorganized by the revolution, was restored by Napoleon and differently constituted, being then erected into an Academy of Fine Art: to which the study of design for silk manufacture was merely attached as a subordinate branch. It appears that all the students who entered the school commence as if they were intended for artists in the higher sense of the word and are not expected to decide as to whether they will devote themselves to the Fine Arts or to Industrial Design, until they have completed their exercises in drawing and painting of the figure from the antique and from the living model. It is for this reason, and from the fact that artists for industrial purposes are both well paid and highly considered (as being well instructed men) that so many individuals in France engage themselves in <i>both</i> pursuits.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><i>The Practical Draughtsman's Book of Industrial Design</i> by <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Eug%C3%A8ne_Armengaud" title="Jacques-Eugène Armengaud">Jacques-Eugène Armengaud</a> was printed in 1853.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-practical_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-practical-15">[15]</a></sup> The subtitle of the (translated) work explains, that it wants to offer a "complete course of mechanical, engineering, and architectural drawing." The study of those types of technical drawing, according to Armengaud, belong to the field of industrial design. This work paved the way for a big expansion in the field drawing education in France, the United Kingdom and the United States.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Lepper" title="Robert Lepper">Robert Lepper</a> helped to establish one of the country's first industrial design degree programs at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institute_of_Technology" title="Carnegie Institute of Technology">Carnegie Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Mellon_magazine_-_Winter_2002_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Mellon_magazine_-_Winter_2002-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Product_design" title="Product design">Product design</a> and industrial design overlap in the fields of <a href="/wiki/User_interface_design" title="User interface design">user interface design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_design" title="Information design">information design</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interaction_design" title="Interaction design">interaction design</a>. Various schools of industrial design specialize in one of these aspects, ranging from pure art colleges (product styling), to mixed programs of engineering and design, to related disciplines such as exhibit design and interior design, to schools that almost completely subordinated aesthetic design to concerns of usage and ergonomics, the so-called <i>functionalist</i> school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Except for certain functional areas of overlap between industrial design and engineering design, educational programs in the U.S. for engineering design require accreditation by the Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology (ABET) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in contrast to programs for industrial design which are accredited by the National Association of Schools of Art and Design (NASAD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions">Institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most industrial designers complete a design or related program at a vocational school or university. Relevant programs include graphic design, interior design, industrial design, architectural technology, and drafting Diplomas and degrees in industrial design are offered at vocational schools and universities worldwide. Diplomas and degrees take two to four years of study. The study results in a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Industrial_Design" title="Bachelor of Industrial Design">Bachelor of Industrial Design</a> (B.I.D.), <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (B.Sc) or <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">Bachelor of Fine Arts</a> (B.F.A.). Afterwards, the bachelor programme can be extended to postgraduate degrees such as <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Design" title="Master of Design">Master of Design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Fine_Arts" title="Master of Fine Arts">Master of Fine Arts</a> and others to a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_industrial_design">Definition of industrial design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Definition of industrial design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Industrial design studies function and form—and the connection between product, user, and environment. Generally, industrial design professionals work in small scale design, rather than overall design of complex systems such as buildings or ships. Industrial designers don't usually design motors, electrical circuits, or gearing that make machines move, but they may affect technical aspects through usability design and form relationships. Usually, they work with other professionals such as engineers who design the mechanical aspects of the product assuring functionality and manufacturability, and with marketers to identify and fulfill customer needs and expectations.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Industrial design (ID) is the professional service of creating and developing concepts and specifications that optimize the function, value and appearance of products and systems for the mutual benefit of both user and manufacturer.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Industrial_Designers_Society_of_America" title="Industrial Designers Society of America">Industrial Designers Society of America</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Design, itself, is often difficult to describe to non-designers and engineers, because the meaning accepted by the design community is not made of words. Instead, the definition is created as a result of acquiring a critical framework for the analysis and creation of artifacts. One of the many accepted (but intentionally unspecific) definitions of design originates from <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_School_of_Design" title="Carnegie Mellon School of Design">Carnegie Mellon's School of Design</a>, "Design is the process of taking something from its existing state and moving it to a preferred state."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This applies to new artifacts, whose existing state is undefined, and previously created artifacts, whose state stands to be improved.</p>
<p>Industrial design can overlap significantly with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_design" title="Engineering design">engineering design</a>, and in different countries the boundaries of the two concepts can vary, but in general <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> focuses principally on functionality or Utility of Products whereas industrial design focuses principally on <i>aesthetic and user-interface</i> aspects of products. In many jurisdictions this distinction is effectively defined by <a href="/wiki/Regulation_and_licensure_in_engineering" title="Regulation and licensure in engineering">credentials and/or licensure</a> required to engage in the practice of engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcadc_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcadc-21">[21]</a></sup> "Industrial design" as such does not overlap much with the engineering sub-discipline of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">industrial engineering</a>, except for the latter's sub-specialty of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>.</p>
<p>At the 29th General Assembly in Gwangju, South Korea,2015, the Professional Practise Committee unveiled a renewed definition of industrial design as follows: "Industrial Design is a strategic problem-solving process that drives innovation, builds business success and leads to a better quality of life through innovative products, systems, services and experiences." An extended version of this definition is as follows: "Industrial Design is a strategic problem-solving process that drives innovation, builds business success and leads to a better quality of life through innovative products, systems, services and experiences. Industrial Design bridges the gap between what is and what’s possible. It is a trans-disciplinary profession that harnesses creativity to resolve problems and co-create solutions with the intent of making a product, system, service, experience or a business, better. At its heart, Industrial Design provides a more optimistic way of looking at the future by reframing problems as opportunities. It links innovation, technology, research, business and customers to provide new value and competitive advantage across economic, social and environmental spheres. Industrial Designers place the human in the centre of the process. They acquire a deep understanding of user needs through empathy and apply a pragmatic, user centric problem solving process to design products, systems, services and experiences. They are strategic stakeholders in the innovation process and are uniquely positioned to bridge varied professional disciplines and business interests. They value the economic, social and environmental impact of their work and their contribution towards co-creating a better quality of life. "<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_process">Design process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although the process of design may be considered 'creative,' many analytical processes also take place. In fact, many industrial designers often use various design methodologies in their creative process. Some of the processes that are commonly used are user research, sketching, comparative product research, model making, prototyping and testing. These processes are best defined by the industrial designers and/or other team members. Industrial designers often utilize 3D software, <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_industrial_design" title="Computer-aided industrial design">computer-aided industrial design</a> and CAD programs to move from concept to production. They may also build a prototype first and then use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_CT_scanning" title="Industrial CT scanning">industrial CT scanning</a> to test for interior defects and generate a CAD model. From this the manufacturing process may be modified to improve the product.</p>
<p>Product characteristics specified by industrial designers may include the overall form of the object, the location of details with respect to one another, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colors" title="Colors">colors</a>, texture, form, and aspects concerning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomic" title="Ergonomic">use of the product</a>. Additionally they may specify aspects concerning the production process, <a href="/wiki/Material_selection" title="Material selection">choice of materials</a> and the way the product is presented to the consumer at the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale" title="Point of sale">point of sale</a>. The inclusion of industrial designers in a product development process may lead to added value by improving <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a>, lowering production costs and developing more appealing products.</p>
<p>Industrial design may also focus on technical concepts, products, and processes. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>, usability, and ergonomics, it can also encompass engineering, usefulness, market placement, and other concerns—such as psychology, desire, and the emotional attachment of the user. These <a href="/wiki/Architectural_design_values" title="Architectural design values">values</a> and accompanying aspects that form the basis of industrial design can vary—between different schools of thought, and among practicing designers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_design_rights">Industrial design rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Industrial design rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_design_rights" title="Industrial design rights">Industrial design rights</a></div>
<p>Industrial design rights are <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> rights that make exclusive the visual design of objects that are not purely utilitarian. A design patent would also be considered under this category. An industrial design consists of the creation of a shape, configuration or composition of pattern or color, or combination of pattern and color in three-dimensional form containing aesthetic value. An industrial design can be a two- or three-dimensional pattern used to produce a product, industrial commodity or handicraft. Under the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Agreement_Concerning_the_International_Deposit_of_Industrial_Designs" title="Hague Agreement Concerning the International Deposit of Industrial Designs">Hague Agreement Concerning the International Deposit of Industrial Designs</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WIPO" title="WIPO">WIPO</a>-administered treaty, a procedure for an international registration exists. An applicant can file for a single international deposit with WIPO or with the national office in a country party to the treaty. The design will then be protected in as many member countries of the treaty as desired.</p>

<p>A number of industrial designers have made such a significant impact on culture and daily life that their work is documented by historians of social science. <a href="/wiki/Alvar_Aalto" title="Alvar Aalto">Alvar Aalto</a>, renowned as an <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a>, also designed a significant number of household items, such as chairs, stools, lamps, a tea-cart, and vases. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Loewy" title="Raymond Loewy">Raymond Loewy</a> was a prolific American designer who is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a> corporate logo, the original <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> logo (in use until 2000), the <a href="/wiki/PRR_S1" title="PRR S1">PRR S1</a> steam locomotive, the <a href="/wiki/Studebaker_Starlight" title="Studebaker Starlight">Studebaker Starlight</a> (including the later iconic bulletnose), as well as <a href="/wiki/Schick_(razors)" title="Schick (razors)">Schick</a> electric razors, <a href="/wiki/Electrolux" title="Electrolux">Electrolux</a> refrigerators, short-wave radios, <a href="/wiki/Le_Creuset" title="Le Creuset">Le Creuset</a> French ovens, and a complete line of modern furniture, among many other items.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Teague" title="Richard A. Teague">Richard A. Teague</a>, who spent most of his career with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Motor_Company" title="American Motor Company">American Motor Company</a>, originated the concept of using interchangeable body panels so as to create a wide array of different vehicles using the same stampings. He was responsible for such unique automotive designs as the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Pacer" title="AMC Pacer">Pacer</a>, <a href="/wiki/AMC_Gremlin" title="AMC Gremlin">Gremlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/AMC_Matador" title="AMC Matador">Matador coupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Cherokee_(XJ)" title="Jeep Cherokee (XJ)">Jeep Cherokee</a>, and the complete interior of the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Premier" title="Eagle Premier">Eagle Premier</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Viktor_Schreckengost" title="Viktor Schreckengost">Viktor Schreckengost</a> designed bicycles manufactured by Murray bicycles for Murray and Sears, Roebuck and Company. With engineer Ray Spiller, he designed the first truck with a cab-over-engine configuration, a design in use to this day. Schreckengost also founded The Cleveland Institute of Art's school of industrial design.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oskar_Barnack" title="Oskar Barnack">Oskar Barnack</a> was a German optical engineer, precision mechanic, industrial designer, and the father of 35mm photography. He developed the <a href="/wiki/Leica_Camera" title="Leica Camera">Leica</a>, which became the hallmark for photography for 50 years, and remains a high-water mark for mechanical and optical design.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_and_Ray_Eames" title="Charles and Ray Eames">Charles and Ray Eames</a> were most famous for their pioneering furniture designs, such as the <a href="/wiki/Eames_Lounge_Chair_Wood" title="Eames Lounge Chair Wood">Eames Lounge Chair Wood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eames_Lounge_Chair" title="Eames Lounge Chair">Eames Lounge Chair</a>. Other influential designers included <a href="/wiki/Henry_Dreyfuss" title="Henry Dreyfuss">Henry Dreyfuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Noyes" title="Eliot Noyes">Eliot Noyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Vassos" title="John Vassos">John Vassos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russel_Wright" title="Russel Wright">Russel Wright</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dieter_Rams" title="Dieter Rams">Dieter Rams</a> is a German industrial designer closely associated with the consumer products company <a href="/wiki/Braun" title="Braun">Braun</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Functionalism_(architecture)" title="Functionalism (architecture)">Functionalist</a> school of industrial design.</p>
<p>Many of <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>'s recent iconic products were designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ive" title="Jonathan Ive">Jonathan Ive</a>.</p>
