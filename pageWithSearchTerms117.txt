<p><b>Bertrand Meyer</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/aɪər/ 'ire' in 'fire'">aɪər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[mɛjɛʁ]</a></span>; born 21 November 1950) is a French academic, author, and consultant in the field of computer languages. He created the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_(programming_language)" title="Eiffel (programming language)">Eiffel programming language</a> and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Design_by_contract" title="Design by contract">design by contract</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_academic_career">Education and academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertrand_Meyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bertrand Meyer received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_degree" title="Master degree">master degree</a> in engineering from the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique" title="École Polytechnique">École Polytechnique</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, a second <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's degree</a> from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Nancy" title="Université de Nancy">Université de Nancy</a>. He had a technical and managerial career for nine years at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lectricit%C3%A9_de_France" title="Électricité de France">Électricité de France</a>, and for three years was on the faculty at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>.</p>
<p>Since October 2001, he has been Professor of Software Engineering at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETH_Z%C3%BCrich" title="ETH Zürich">ETH Zürich</a>, the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology, where he pursues research on building trusted components (reusable software elements) with a guaranteed level of quality.</p>
<p>His other activities include being adjunct professor at <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">Monash University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> (1998–2003) and membership of the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Technologies" title="French Academy of Technologies">French Academy of Technologies</a>. He is also active as a consultant (object-oriented system design, architectural reviews, technology assessment), trainer in object technology and other software topics, and conference speaker. As former head of the ETH Computer Science department, he is one of the founders and the current president of <a href="/wiki/Informatics_Europe" title="Informatics Europe">Informatics Europe</a>, the association of European computer science departments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_languages">Computer languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertrand_Meyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Computer languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Meyer pursues the ideal of simple, elegant and user-friendly computer languages and is one of the earliest and most vocal proponents of <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-oriented programming</a> (OOP). His book <i><a href="/wiki/Object-Oriented_Software_Construction" title="Object-Oriented Software Construction">Object-Oriented Software Construction</a></i> is widely considered to be the best work on presenting the case for OOP.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (November 2009)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Bertrand_Meyer#Dubious" title="Talk:Bertrand Meyer">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Other books he has written include <i>Eiffel: The Language</i> (a description of the Eiffel language), <i>Object Success</i> (a discussion of object technology for managers), <i>Reusable Software</i> (a discussion of reuse issues and solutions), <i>Introduction to the Theory of Programming Languages</i> and <i>Touch of Class</i>. He has authored numerous articles and edited conference proceedings.</p>
<p>He is the initial designer of the Eiffel method and language and has continued to participate in its evolution, and is the originator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_by_Contract" title="Design by Contract">Design by Contract</a> development method<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>His experiences with object technology through the <a href="/wiki/Simula" title="Simula">Simula</a> language, as well as early work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_data_types" title="Abstract data types">abstract data types</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formal_specification" title="Formal specification">formal specification</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Z_notation" title="Z notation">Z notation</a>), provided some of the background for the development of Eiffel. Eiffel has been influential in the development of other languages including <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language)" title="C Sharp (programming language)">C#</a> and <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertrand_Meyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, Meyer was the "senior award" winner of the first AITO <a href="/wiki/Dahl-Nygaard_Prize" title="Dahl-Nygaard Prize">Dahl-Nygaard award</a>. This prize, named after the two creators of object technology, is awarded annually to a senior and a junior researchers who have made significant technical contributions to the field of Object Orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Meyer was recognized as honorary doctor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_State_University_of_Information_Technologies,_Mechanics_and_Optics" title="Saint Petersburg State University of Information Technologies, Mechanics and Optics">Saint Petersburg State University of Information Technologies, Mechanics and Optics</a>.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://is.ifmo.ru/english/" rel="nofollow">[3]</a></p>
<p>On 9 June 2007, Meyer received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_System_Award" title="Software System Award">Software System Award</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> for "impact on software quality" in recognition of the design of Eiffel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He is a 2008 Fellow of the ACM.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wikipedia_hoax">Wikipedia hoax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bertrand_Meyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wikipedia hoax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 28 December 2005, an anonymous user falsely announced Meyer's death on <a href="/wiki/German_Wikipedia" title="German Wikipedia">German Wikipedia</a>'s biography of Meyer. The hoax was reported five days later by the <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Heise" title="Heinz Heise">Heise News Ticker</a> and the article was immediately corrected. Many major news media outlets in Germany and Switzerland picked up the story, creating the German Wikipedia's version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seigenthaler_incident" title="Seigenthaler incident">Seigenthaler incident</a>. Meyer went on to publish a positive evaluation of Wikipedia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> concluding "The system succumbed to one of its potential flaws, and quickly healed itself. This doesn't affect the big picture. Just like those about me, rumors about Wikipedia's downfall have been grossly exaggerated."</p>
