<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ubiquitous computing</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Ubiquitous computing</b> (or "<b>ubicomp</b>") is a concept in <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> where computing is made to appear anytime and everywhere. In contrast to desktop computing, ubiquitous computing can occur using any device, in any location, and in any format. A user interacts with the computer, which can exist in many different forms, including laptop computers, tablets and terminals in everyday objects such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fridge" title="Fridge">fridge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Smartglasses" title="Smartglasses">a pair of glasses</a>. The underlying technologies to support ubiquitous computing include <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, advanced <a href="/wiki/Middleware" title="Middleware">middleware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_code" title="Mobile code">mobile code</a>, sensors, microprocessors, new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I/O" title="I/O">I/O</a> and user interfaces, networks, mobile protocols, location and positioning and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=New_materials&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="New materials (page does not exist)">new materials</a>.</p>
<p>This paradigm is also described as <b>pervasive computing</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nieuwdorp2007-Discourse_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nieuwdorp2007-Discourse-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ambient_intelligence" title="Ambient intelligence">ambient intelligence</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or "everyware".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Each term emphasizes slightly different aspects. When primarily concerning the objects involved, it is also known as <a href="/wiki/Physical_computing" title="Physical computing">physical computing</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internet_of_Things" title="Internet of Things">Internet of Things</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haptic_technology" title="Haptic technology">haptic computing</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and "things that think". Rather than propose a single definition for ubiquitous computing and for these related terms, a taxonomy of properties for ubiquitous computing has been proposed, from which different kinds or flavors of ubiquitous systems and applications can be described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poslad_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Poslad-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ubiquitous computing touches on a wide range of research topics, including <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_computing" title="Mobile computing">mobile computing</a>, location computing, mobile networking, context-aware computing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensor_network" title="Sensor network">sensor networks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interaction" title="Human-computer interaction">human-computer interaction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_concepts">Core concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ubiquitous_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Core concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At their core, all models of ubiquitous computing share a vision of small, inexpensive, robust networked processing devices, distributed at all scales throughout everyday life and generally turned to distinctly common-place ends. For example, a domestic ubiquitous computing environment might interconnect lighting and environmental controls with personal biometric monitors woven into clothing so that illumination and heating conditions in a room might be modulated, continuously and imperceptibly. Another common scenario posits refrigerators "aware" of their suitably tagged contents, able to both plan a variety of menus from the food actually on hand, and warn users of stale or spoiled food.</p>
<p>Ubiquitous computing presents challenges across computer science: in systems design and engineering, in systems modelling, and in user interface design. Contemporary human-computer interaction models, whether <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command-line" title="Command-line">command-line</a>, menu-driven, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GUI" title="GUI">GUI</a>-based, are inappropriate and inadequate to the ubiquitous case. This suggests that the "natural" interaction paradigm appropriate to a fully robust ubiquitous computing has yet to emerge – although there is also recognition in the field that in many ways we are already living in a ubicomp world (see also the main article on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_User_Interface" title="Natural User Interface">Natural User Interface</a>). Contemporary devices that lend some support to this latter idea include <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" title="Digital audio player">digital audio players</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">radio-frequency identification</a> tags, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interactive_whiteboard" title="Interactive whiteboard">interactive whiteboards</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Weiser" title="Mark Weiser">Mark Weiser</a> proposed three basic forms for ubiquitous system devices (see also <a href="/wiki/Smart_device" title="Smart device">smart device</a>): tabs, pads and boards.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Tabs</i>: wearable centimetre sized devices</li>
<li><i>Pads</i>: hand-held decimetre-sized devices</li>
<li><i>Boards</i>: metre sized interactive display devices.</li>
</ul>
<p>These three forms proposed by Weiser are characterized by being macro-sized, having a planar form and on incorporating visual output displays. If we relax each of these three characteristics we can expand this range into a much more diverse and potentially more useful range of ubiquitous computing devices. Hence, three additional forms for ubiquitous systems have been proposed:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poslad_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Poslad-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Dust</i>: miniaturized devices can be without visual output displays, e.g. Micro Electro-Mechanical Systems (<a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">MEMS</a>), ranging from nanometres through micrometers to millimetres. See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_dust" title="Smart dust">Smart dust</a>.</li>
<li><i>Skin</i>: fabrics based upon light emitting and conductive polymers, organic computer devices, can be formed into more flexible non-planar display surfaces and products such as clothes and curtains, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OLED_display" title="OLED display">OLED display</a>. MEMS device can also be painted onto various surfaces so that a variety of physical world structures can act as networked surfaces of MEMS.</li>
<li><i>Clay</i>: ensembles of MEMS can be formed into arbitrary three dimensional shapes as artefacts resembling many different kinds of physical object (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangible_interface" title="Tangible interface">Tangible interface</a>).</li>
</ul>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Information_Age:_Economy,_Society_and_Culture#The_Rise_of_the_Network_Society" title="The Information Age: Economy, Society and Culture">The Rise of the Network Society</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Castells" title="Manuel Castells">Manuel Castells</a> suggests that there is an ongoing shift from already-decentralised, stand-alone microcomputers and mainframes towards entirely pervasive computing. In his model of a pervasive computing system, Castells uses the example of the Internet as the start of a pervasive computing system. The logical progression from that paradigm is a system where that networking logic becomes applicable in every realm of daily activity, in every location and every context. Castells envisages a system where billions of miniature, ubiquitous inter-communication devices will be spread worldwide, "like pigment in the wall paint".</p>
<p>Ubiquitous computing may be seen to consist of many layers, each with their own roles, which together form a single system:</p>
<p><b>Layer 1:</b> <i>task management layer</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Monitors user task, context and index</li>
<li>Map user's task to need for the services in the environment</li>
<li>To manage complex dependencies</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Layer 2:</b> <i>environment management layer</i></p>
<ul>
<li>To monitor a resource and its capabilities</li>
<li>To map service need, user level states of specific capabilities</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Layer 3:</b> <i>environment layer</i></p>
<ul>
<li>To monitor a relevant resource</li>
<li>To manage reliability of the resources</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ubiquitous_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Weiser" title="Mark Weiser">Mark Weiser</a> coined the phrase "ubiquitous computing" around 1988, during his tenure as Chief Technologist of the <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" title="Xerox PARC">Palo Alto Research Center (PARC)</a>. Both alone and with PARC Director and Chief Scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Seely_Brown" title="John Seely Brown">John Seely Brown</a>, Weiser wrote some of the earliest papers on the subject, largely defining it and sketching out its major concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiser91_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiser91-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiser96_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiser96-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognizing_the_effects_of_extending_processing_power">Recognizing the effects of extending processing power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ubiquitous_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recognizing the effects of extending processing power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recognizing that the extension of processing power into everyday scenarios would necessitate understandings of social, cultural and psychological phenomena beyond its proper ambit, Weiser was influenced by many fields outside computer science, including "<a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Modernism" title="Post-Modernism">post-Modernism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociology_of_science" title="Sociology of science">sociology of science</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist_criticism" title="Feminist criticism">feminist criticism</a>". He was explicit about "the humanistic origins of the 'invisible ideal in post-modernist thought'",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiser96_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weiser96-8">[8]</a></sup> referencing as well the ironically <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopian</a> <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ubik" title="Ubik">Ubik</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andy_Hopper" title="Andy Hopper">Andy Hopper</a> from Cambridge University UK proposed and demonstrated the concept of "Teleporting" – where applications follow the user wherever he/she moves.</p>
<p>Roy Want, while a researcher and student working under Andy Hopper at Cambridge University, worked on the "Active Badge System", which is an advanced location computing system where personal mobility that is merged with computing.</p>
<p>Bill Schilit (now at Google) also did some earlier work in this topic, and participated in the early Mobile Computing workshop held in Santa Cruz in 1996.</p>
<p>Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ken_Sakamura" title="Ken Sakamura">Ken Sakamura</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> leads the Ubiquitous Networking Laboratory (UNL), <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/T-Engine_Forum" title="T-Engine Forum">T-Engine Forum</a>. The joint goal of Sakamura's Ubiquitous Networking specification and the T-Engine forum, is to enable any everyday device to broadcast and receive information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> has also contributed significant research in this field, notably <i>Things That Think</i> consortium (directed by <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Ishii_(computer_scientist)" title="Hiroshi Ishii (computer scientist)">Hiroshi Ishii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Paradiso" title="Joseph A. Paradiso">Joseph A. Paradiso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Picard" title="Rosalind Picard">Rosalind Picard</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">Media Lab</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the CSAIL effort known as <a href="/wiki/Project_Oxygen" title="Project Oxygen">Project Oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Other major contributors include <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>'s <a class="external text" href="http://ubicomplab.cs.washington.edu/" rel="nofollow">Ubicomp Lab</a> (directed by <a href="/wiki/Shwetak_Patel" title="Shwetak Patel">Shwetak Patel</a>), <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Tech</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology_College_of_Computing" title="Georgia Institute of Technology College of Computing">College of Computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>'s <a class="external text" href="http://pac.cs.cornell.edu/" rel="nofollow">People Aware Computing Lab</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">NYU</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NYU_Interactive_Telecommunications_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NYU Interactive Telecommunications Program (page does not exist)">Interactive Telecommunications Program</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Irvine" title="UC Irvine">UC Irvine</a>'s Department of Informatics, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_Research" title="Intel Research">Intel Research</a> and Equator,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Ajou University UCRi &amp; CUS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ubiquitous_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the earliest ubiquitous systems was artist <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Jeremijenko" title="Natalie Jeremijenko">Natalie Jeremijenko</a>'s "Live Wire", also known as "Dangling String", installed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> during <a href="/wiki/Mark_Weiser" title="Mark Weiser">Mark Weiser</a>'s time there. This was a piece of string attached to a <a href="/wiki/Stepper_motor" title="Stepper motor">stepper motor</a> and controlled by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LAN" title="LAN">LAN</a> connection; network activity caused the string to twitch, yielding a <i>peripherally noticeable</i> indication of traffic. Weiser called this an example of <i><a href="/wiki/Calm_technology" title="Calm technology">calm technology</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A present manifestation of this trend is the widespread diffusion of mobile phones. Many of mobile phones supporting high speed data transmission, video services, and mobile devices with powerful computational ability. Although these mobile devices are not necessarily manifestations of ubiquitous computing, there are examples, such as Japan's Yaoyorozu ("Eight Million Gods") Project in which mobile devices, coupled with radio frequency identification tags demonstrate that ubiquitous computing is already present in some form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ambient_Devices" title="Ambient Devices">Ambient Devices</a> has produced an "orb", a "dashboard", and a "<a href="/wiki/Weather_beacon" title="Weather beacon">weather beacon</a>": these decorative devices receive data from a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_network" title="Wireless network">wireless network</a> and report current events, such as stock prices and the weather, like the <a href="/wiki/Nabaztag" title="Nabaztag">Nabaztag</a> produced by Violet Snowden.</p>
<p>The Australian futurist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Pesce" title="Mark Pesce">Mark Pesce</a> has produced a highly configurable 52-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> <a href="/wiki/LAMP_(software_bundle)" title="LAMP (software bundle)">LAMP</a> enabled lamp which uses <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> named <i>MooresCloud</i> after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moore%27s_Law" title="Moore's Law">Moore's Law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Computer_Intelligence_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Unified Computer Intelligence Corporation (page does not exist)">Unified Computer Intelligence Corporation</a> has launched a device called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ubi_%E2%80%93_The_Ubiquitous_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ubi – The Ubiquitous Computer (page does not exist)">Ubi – The Ubiquitous Computer</a></i> that is designed to allow voice interaction with the home and provide constant access to information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ubiquitous computing research has focused on building an environment in which computers allow humans to focus attention on select aspects of the environment and operate in supervisory and policy-making roles. Ubiquitous computing emphasizes the creation of a human computer interface that can interpret and support a user's intentions. For example, MIT's Project Oxygen seeks to create a system in which computation is as pervasive as air:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In the future, computation will be human centered. It will be freely available everywhere, like batteries and power sockets, or oxygen in the air we breathe...We will not need to carry our own devices around with us. Instead, configurable generic devices, either handheld or embedded in the environment, will bring computation to us, whenever we need it and wherever we might be. As we interact with these "anonymous" devices, they will adopt our information personalities. They will respect our desires for privacy and security. We won't have to type, click, or learn new computer jargon. Instead, we'll communicate naturally, using speech and gestures that describe our intent...</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is a fundamental transition that does not seek to escape the physical world and "enter some metallic, gigabyte-infested cyberspace" but rather brings computers and communications to us, making them "synonymous with the useful tasks they perform".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Network_robot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Network robot (page does not exist)">Network robots</a> link ubiquitous networks with <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a>, contributing to the creation of new lifestyles and solutions to address a variety of social problems including the aging of population and nursing care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ubiquitous_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Privacy is easily the most often-cited criticism of ubiquitous computing (ubicomp), and may be the greatest barrier to its long-term success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>An article by Linda Little and Pam Briggs on this privacy issue, states that: "These are the kinds of privacy principles that have been established by the industry – but over the past two years, we have been trying to understand whether such principles reflect the concerns of the ordinary citizen. Some of the key research questions we have been addressing are: What are users' key concerns regarding privacy management in a ubiquitous context and do they reflect 'expert' privacy principles? Do these concerns vary as a function of context? Will users have enough confidence in privacy management procedures to hand-over management and administration of their privacy preferences? Motahari, et al., (2007) argue people do not have a complete understanding of the threats to their privacy. While users of ubicomp systems are aware of inappropriate use of their personal information, legal obligations and inadequate security they are less aware of setting preferences for who has access and any social inferences that can be made by observations by other people. They further argue a holistic approach is needed as traditional approaches and current investigations are not enough to address privacy threats in ubiquitous computing. Recognising – in line with a number of other researchers (Harper &amp; Singleton, 2001; Paine, et al., 2007) – that privacy concerns are likely to be highly situation-dependent, we have developed a method of enquiry which displays a rich context to the user in order to elicit more detailed information about those privacy factors that underpin our acceptance of ubiquitous computing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Public policy problems are often "preceded by long shadows, long trains of activity", emerging slowly, over decades or even the course of a century. There is a need for a long-term view to guide policy decision making, as this will assist in identifying long-term problems or opportunities related to the ubiquitous computing environment. This information can reduce uncertainty and guide the decisions of both policy makers and those directly involved in system development (Wedemeyer et al. 2001). One important consideration is the degree to which different opinions form around a single problem. Some issues may have strong consensus about their importance, even if there are great differences in opinion regarding the cause or solution. For example, few people will differ in their assessment of a highly tangible problem with physical impact such as terrorists using new weapons of mass destruction to destroy human life. The problem statements outlined above that address the future evolution of the human species or challenges to identity have clear cultural or religious implications and are likely to have greater variance in opinion about them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
