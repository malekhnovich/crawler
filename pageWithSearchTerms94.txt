<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Unified Modeling Language</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Interaction_diagram&amp;redirect=no" title="Interaction diagram">Interaction diagram</a>)</span></div>

<p>The <b>Unified Modeling Language</b> (<b>UML</b>) is a general-purpose, developmental, <a href="/wiki/Modeling_language" title="Modeling language">modeling language</a> in the field of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a>, that is intended to provide a standard way to visualize the design of a system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>UML was originally motivated by the desire to standardize the disparate notational systems and approaches to software design developed by <a href="/wiki/Grady_Booch" title="Grady Booch">Grady Booch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivar_Jacobson" title="Ivar Jacobson">Ivar Jacobson</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Rumbaugh" title="James Rumbaugh">James Rumbaugh</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rational_Software" title="Rational Software">Rational Software</a> in 1994–95, with further development led by them through 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997 UML was adopted as a standard by the <a href="/wiki/Object_Management_Group" title="Object Management Group">Object Management Group</a> (OMG), and has been managed by this organization ever since. In 2005 UML was also published by the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) as an approved ISO standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since then it has been periodically revised to cover the latest revision of UML.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1418" data-file-width="2000" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg/320px-OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg/480px-OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg/640px-OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OO_Modeling_languages_history.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
History of object-oriented methods and notation</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_UML_1.x">Before UML 1.x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before UML 1.x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UML has been evolving since the second half of the 1990s and has its roots in the object-oriented methods developed in the late 1980s and early 1990s. The timeline (see image) shows the highlights of the history of object-oriented modeling methods and notation.</p>
<p>It is originally based on the notations of the <a href="/wiki/Booch_method" title="Booch method">Booch method</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Object-modeling_technique" title="Object-modeling technique">object-modeling technique</a> (OMT) and <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_software_engineering" title="Object-oriented software engineering">object-oriented software engineering</a> (OOSE), which it has integrated into a single language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rational_Software_Corporation" title="Rational Software Corporation">Rational Software Corporation</a> hired <a href="/wiki/James_Rumbaugh" title="James Rumbaugh">James Rumbaugh</a> from <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> in 1994 and after that the company became the source for two of the most popular object-oriented modeling approaches of the day:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Rumbaugh's <a href="/wiki/Object-modeling_technique" title="Object-modeling technique">object-modeling technique</a> (OMT) and <a href="/wiki/Grady_Booch" title="Grady Booch">Grady Booch</a>'s method. They were soon assisted in their efforts by <a href="/wiki/Ivar_Jacobson" title="Ivar Jacobson">Ivar Jacobson</a>, the creator of the <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_software_engineering" title="Object-oriented software engineering">object-oriented software engineering</a> (OOSE) method, who joined them at Rational in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UML_1.x">UML 1.x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UML 1.x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under the technical leadership of those three (Rumbaugh, Jacobson and Booch), a consortium called the <a href="/wiki/UML_Partners" title="UML Partners">UML Partners</a> was organized in 1996 to complete the <i>Unified Modeling Language (UML)</i> specification, and propose it to the Object Management Group (OMG) for standardisation. The partnership also contained additional interested parties (for example <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>). The UML Partners' UML 1.0 draft was proposed to the OMG in January 1997 by the consortium. During the same month the UML Partners formed a group, designed to define the exact meaning of language constructs, chaired by <a href="/wiki/Cris_Kobryn" title="Cris Kobryn">Cris Kobryn</a> and administered by Ed Eykholt, to finalize the specification and integrate it with other standardization efforts. The result of this work, UML 1.1, was submitted to the OMG in August 1997 and adopted by the OMG in November 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the first release a task force was formed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> to improve the language, which released several minor revisions, 1.3, 1.4, and 1.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The standards it produced (as well as the original standard) have been noted as being ambiguous and inconsistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UML_2.x">UML 2.x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: UML 2.x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UML 2.0 major revision replaced version 1.5 in 2005, which was developed with an enlarged consortium to improve the language further to reflect new experience on usage of its features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although UML 2.1 was never released as a formal specification, versions 2.1.1 and 2.1.2 appeared in 2007, followed by UML 2.2 in February 2009. UML 2.3 was formally released in May 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spec_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-spec-11">[11]</a></sup> UML 2.4.1 was formally released in August 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spec_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-spec-11">[11]</a></sup> UML 2.5 was released in October 2012 as an "In process" version and was officially released in June 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spec_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-spec-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four parts to the UML 2.x specification:</p>
<ol>
<li>The Superstructure that defines the notation and semantics for diagrams and their model elements</li>
<li>The Infrastructure that defines the core metamodel on which the Superstructure is based</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Object_Constraint_Language" title="Object Constraint Language">Object Constraint Language</a> (OCL) for defining rules for model elements</li>
<li>The UML Diagram Interchange that defines how UML 2 diagram layouts are exchanged</li>
</ol>
<p>The current versions of these standards follow: UML Superstructure version 2.4.1, UML Infrastructure version 2.4.1, OCL version 2.3.1, and UML Diagram Interchange version 1.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Versions_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Versions-12">[12]</a></sup> It continues to be updated and improved by the revision task force, who resolve any issues with the language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UML offers a way to visualize a system's architectural blueprints in a diagram (see image), including elements such as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>any <a href="/wiki/Activity_(UML)" title="Activity (UML)">activities</a> (jobs);</li>
<li>individual <a href="/wiki/Component_(UML)" title="Component (UML)">components</a> of the system;
<ul>
<li>and how they can interact with other <a href="/wiki/Component-based_software_engineering" title="Component-based software engineering">software components</a>;</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>how the system will run;</li>
<li>how entities interact with others (components and interfaces);</li>
<li>external <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Although originally intended for object-oriented design documentation, UML has been extended to a larger set of design documentation (as listed above),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and been found useful in many contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UML.2C_Success_Stories_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UML.2C_Success_Stories-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_development_methods">Software development methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Software development methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UML is not a development method by itself;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> however, it was designed to be compatible with the leading object-oriented software development methods of its time, for example <a href="/wiki/Object-modeling_technique" title="Object-modeling technique">OMT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Booch_method" title="Booch method">Booch method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Objectory" title="Objectory">Objectory</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Rational_Unified_Process" title="Rational Unified Process">RUP</a> that it was originally intended to be used with when work began at Rational Software.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling">Modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is important to distinguish between the UML model and the set of diagrams of a system. A diagram is a partial graphic representation of a system's model. The set of diagrams need not completely cover the model and deleting a diagram does not change the model. The model may also contain documentation that drives the model elements and diagrams (such as written use cases).</p>
<p>UML diagrams represent two different views of a system model:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Static (or <i>structural</i>) view: emphasizes the static structure of the system using objects, attributes, operations and relationships. It includes <a href="/wiki/Class_diagram" title="Class diagram">class diagrams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Composite_structure_diagram" title="Composite structure diagram">composite structure diagrams</a>.</li>
<li>Dynamic (or <i>behavioral</i>) view: emphasizes the dynamic behavior of the system by showing collaborations among objects and changes to the internal states of objects. This view includes <a href="/wiki/Sequence_diagram" title="Sequence diagram">sequence diagrams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activity_diagram" title="Activity diagram">activity diagrams</a> and <a href="/wiki/UML_state_machine" title="UML state machine">state machine diagrams</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>UML models can be exchanged among UML tools by using the <a href="/wiki/XML_Metadata_Interchange" title="XML Metadata Interchange">XML Metadata Interchange</a> (XMI) format.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_diagrams">Structure diagrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Structure diagrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Structure diagrams emphasize the things that must be present in the system being modeled. Since structure diagrams represent the structure, they are used extensively in documenting the <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">software architecture</a> of software systems. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Component_diagram" title="Component diagram">component diagram</a> describes how a software system is split up into components and shows the dependencies among these components.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_diagrams">Behavior diagrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Behavior diagrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Behavior diagrams emphasize what must happen in the system being modeled. Since behavior diagrams illustrate the behavior of a system, they are used extensively to describe the functionality of software systems. As an example, the <a href="/wiki/Activity_diagram" title="Activity diagram">activity diagram</a> describes the business and operational step-by-step activities of the components in a system.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_diagrams">Interaction diagrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interaction diagrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Interaction diagrams, a subset of behavior diagrams, emphasize the flow of control and data among the things in the system being modeled. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meta_modeling">Meta modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Meta modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meta-Object_Facility" title="Meta-Object Facility">Meta-Object Facility</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M0-m3.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="602" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/M0-m3.png/320px-M0-m3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/M0-m3.png/480px-M0-m3.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/M0-m3.png 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M0-m3.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration of the Meta-Object Facility</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Object Management Group (OMG) has developed a <a href="/wiki/Metamodeling" title="Metamodeling">metamodeling</a> architecture to define the UML, called the <a href="/wiki/Meta-Object_Facility" title="Meta-Object Facility">Meta-Object Facility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> MOF is designed as a four-layered architecture, as shown in the image at right. It provides a meta-meta model at the top, called the M3 layer. This M3-model is the language used by Meta-Object Facility to build metamodels, called M2-models.</p>
<p>The most prominent example of a Layer 2 Meta-Object Facility model is the UML metamodel, which describes the UML itself. These M2-models describe elements of the M1-layer, and thus M1-models. These would be, for example, models written in UML. The last layer is the M0-layer or data layer. It is used to describe runtime instances of the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The meta-model can be extended using a mechanism called <a href="/wiki/Stereotype_(UML)" title="Stereotype (UML)">stereotyping</a>. This has been criticised as being insufficient/untenable by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Henderson-Sellers" title="Brian Henderson-Sellers">Brian Henderson-Sellers</a> and Cesar Gonzalez-Perez in "Uses and Abuses of the Stereotype Mechanism in UML 1.x and 2.0".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UsesAbusesStereotype_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-UsesAbusesStereotype-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption">Adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UML has been found useful in many design contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UML.2C_Success_Stories_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-UML.2C_Success_Stories-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>It has been treated, at times, as a design <a href="/wiki/Silver_bullet" title="Silver bullet">silver bullet</a>, which has led to problems in its usage. Misuse of it includes excessive usage of it (design every little part of the system's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Programming_code" title="Programming code">code</a> with it, which is unnecessary) and assuming that anyone can design anything with it (even those who haven't <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmed</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is seen to be a large language, with many <a href="/wiki/Syntax_(programming_languages)" title="Syntax (programming languages)">constructs</a> in it. Some (including <a href="/wiki/Ivar_Jacobson" title="Ivar Jacobson">Jacobson</a>) feel that there are too many and that this hinders the learning (and therefore usage) of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Common criticisms of UML from industry include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-petre_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-petre-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>not useful: "[does] not offer them advantages over their current, evolved practices and representations"</li>
<li>too complex, particularly for communication with clients: "unnecessarily complex" and "The best reason not to use UML is that it is not ‘readable’ for all stakeholders. How much is UML worth if a business user (the customer) can not understand the result of your modelling effort?"</li>
<li>need to keep UML and code in sync, as with documentation generally</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critique_of_UML_1.x">Critique of UML 1.x</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unified_Modeling_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Critique of UML 1.x">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Cardinality notation</dt>
<dd>As with database Chen, Bachman, and ISO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ER_diagram" title="ER diagram">ER diagrams</a>, class models are specified to use "look-across" <a href="/wiki/Cardinality_(data_modeling)" title="Cardinality (data modeling)">cardinalities</a>, even though several authors (<a href="/wiki/Merise" title="Merise">Merise</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Elmasri &amp; Navathe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> amongst others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>) prefer same-side or "look-here" for roles and both minimum and maximum cardinalities. Recent researchers (Feinerer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Dullea et. alia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>) have shown that the "look-across" technique used by UML and ER diagrams is less effective and less coherent when applied to <i>n</i>-ary relationships of order strictly greater than 2.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Feinerer says: "Problems arise if we operate under the look-across semantics as used for UML associations. Hartmann<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> investigates this situation and shows how and why different transformations fail.", and: "As we will see on the next few pages, the look-across interpretation introduces several difficulties which prevent the extension of simple mechanisms from binary to <i>n</i>-ary associations."</dd>
</dl>
