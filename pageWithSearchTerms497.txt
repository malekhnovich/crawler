<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Glass art</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>

<a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> era <a href="/wiki/Millefiori" title="Millefiori">millefiori</a> style glass cup from <a href="/wiki/Emona" title="Emona">Emona</a> (present <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>) grave.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Glass art</b> refers to individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Works_of_art" title="Works of art">works of art</a> that are substantially or wholly made of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. It ranges in size from monumental works and installation pieces, to wall hangings and windows, to works of art made in studios and factories, including glass jewelry and tableware.</p>
<p>As a decorative and functional medium, glass was extensively developed in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_blowing#Origins" title="Glass blowing">Invented by the Phoenicians</a>, was brought to the fore by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>. In the Middle Ages, the builders of the great <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> cathedrals of Europe took the art of glass to new heights with the use of <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> windows as a major architectural and decorative element. Glass from <a href="/wiki/Murano" title="Murano">Murano</a>, in the Venetian Lagoon, (also known as Venetian glass) is the result of hundreds of years of refinement and invention. Murano is still held as the birthplace of modern glass art.</p>
<p>The turn of the 19th Century was the height of the old art glass movement while the factory glass blowers were being replaced by mechanical bottle blowing and continuous window glass. Great <a href="/wiki/Studio" title="Studio">ateliers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_glass" title="Tiffany glass">Tiffany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lalique" title="Lalique">Lalique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daum_(studio)" title="Daum (studio)">Daum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall%C3%A9" title="Gallé">Gallé</a>, the Corning schools in upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> state, and <a href="/wiki/Steuben_Glass_Works" title="Steuben Glass Works">Steuben Glass Works</a> took glass art to new levels.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_fashion">Glass fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Glass fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewelry">Jewelry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Jewelry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imperfect_For_You.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="285" data-file-width="400" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Imperfect_For_You.jpg/220px-Imperfect_For_You.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Imperfect_For_You.jpg/330px-Imperfect_For_You.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Imperfect_For_You.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imperfect_For_You.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Imperfect for You</i>, knitted glass by <a href="/wiki/Carol_Milne" title="Carol Milne">Carol Milne</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first uses of glass were in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_beads" title="Glass beads">beads</a> and other small pieces of jewelry and decoration. Beads and jewelry are still among the most common uses of glass in art, and can be <a href="/wiki/Lampworking" title="Lampworking">worked without a furnace</a>.</p>
<p>It later became fashionable to wear functional jewelry with glass elements, such as pocketwatches and monocles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wearables_and_couture">Wearables and couture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wearables and couture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in the late 20th century, glass couture refers to the creation of exclusive custom-fitted clothing made from sculpted glass. These are made to order for the body of the wearer. They are partly or entirely made of glass with extreme attention to fit and flexibility. The result is usually delicate, and not intended for regular use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_vessels">Glass vessels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Glass vessels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the earliest and most practical works of glass art were glass vessels. Goblets and pitchers were popular as <a href="/wiki/Glassblowing" title="Glassblowing">glassblowing</a> developed as an art form. Many early methods of etching, painting, and forming glass were honed on these vessels. For instance the <a href="/wiki/Millefiori" title="Millefiori">millefiori</a> technique dates back at least to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>. More recently, <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">lead glass</a> or crystal glass were used to make vessels that rang like a bell when struck.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, mass produced glass work including artistic glass vessels were sometimes known as <a href="/wiki/Factory_glass" title="Factory glass">factory glass</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_architecture">Glass architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Glass architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stained_glass_windows">Stained glass windows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stained glass windows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, glass became more widely produced, and used for windows in buildings. <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">Stained glass</a> became common for windows in cathedrals and grand civic buildings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_facades_and_structural_glass">Glass facades and structural glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Glass facades and structural glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The invention of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_glass" title="Plate glass">plate glass</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bessemer_process" title="Bessemer process">Bessemer process</a> allowed for glass to be used in larger segments, to support more structural loads, and to be produced at larger scales. A striking example of this was <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace" title="The Crystal Palace">the Crystal Palace</a> in 1851, one of the first buildings to use glass as a primary structural material.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, glass became used for tables and shelves, for internal walls, and even for floors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_sculptures">Glass sculptures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Glass sculptures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1432" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg/180px-Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg/270px-Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg/360px-Glass.sculpture.kewgardens.london.arp.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>

<p>Some of the best known glass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sculptures" title="Sculptures">sculptures</a> are statuesque or monumental structures such as the statues by Livio Seguso, or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanislav_Libensk%C3%BD" title="Stanislav Libenský">Stanislav Libenský</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaroslava_Brychtov%C3%A1" title="Jaroslava Brychtová">Jaroslava Brychtová</a>. Another example is René Roubícek's <i>"Object" 1960</i>, a blown and hot-worked piece of 52.2 cm (20.6 in)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> shown at the "Design in an Age of Adversity" exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Corning_Museum_of_Glass" title="Corning Museum of Glass">Corning Museum of Glass</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_glass_and_the_studio_glass_movement">Art glass and the studio glass movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Art glass and the studio glass movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 20th century, most glass production happened in factories. Even individual glassblowers making their own personalized designs would do their work in those large shared buildings. The idea of "art glass", small decorative works made of art, often with designs or objects inside, flourished. Pieces produced in small production runs, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lampwork" title="Lampwork">Lampwork</a> figures of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stanislav_Brychta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stanislav Brychta (page does not exist)">Stanislav Brychta</a>, are generally called <a href="/wiki/Art_glass" title="Art glass">art glass</a>.</p>
<p>By the 1970s, there were good designs for smaller furnaces, and in the United States this gave rise to the "<a href="/wiki/Studio_glass" title="Studio glass">studio glass</a>" movement of glassblowers who blew their glass outside of factories, often in their own studios. This coincided with a move towards smaller production runs of particular styles. This movement spread to other parts of the world as well.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_panels">Glass panels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Glass panels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg" title="Water Walk by Paul Housberg"><img alt="Water Walk by Paul Housberg" data-file-height="772" data-file-width="423" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg/100px-Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg/150px-Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg/200px-Water_Walk_by_Paul_Housberg-thin.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a></div>
<p>Combining many of the above techniques, but focusing on art represented in the glass rather than its shape, glass panels or walls can reach tremendous sizes. These may be installed as walls or on top of walls, or hung from a ceiling. Large panels can be found as part of outdoor installation pieces or for interior use. Dedicated lighting is often part of the artwork.</p>
<p>Techniques used include <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a>, carving (wheel carving, engraving, or acid etching), frosting, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enameling" title="Enameling">enameling</a>, and gilding (including <a href="/wiki/Angel_gilding" title="Angel gilding">Angel gilding</a>). An artist may combine techniques through <a href="/wiki/Masking_(in_art)" title="Masking (in art)">masking</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screen-printing" title="Screen-printing">silkscreening</a>. Glass panels or walls may also be complemented by running water or dynamic lights.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques_and_processes">Techniques and processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Techniques and processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several of the most common techniques for producing glass art include: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_blowing" title="Glass blowing">blowing</a>, kiln-casting, fusing, slumping, pate-de-verre, flame-working, hot-sculpting and cold-working. Cold work includes traditional stained glass work as well as other methods of shaping glass at room temperature. Glass can also be cut with a diamond saw, or copper wheels embedded with abrasives, and polished to give gleaming facets; the technique used in creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterford_crystal" title="Waterford crystal">Waterford crystal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Art is sometimes etched into glass via the use of acid, caustic, or abrasive substances. Traditionally this was done after the glass was blown or cast. In the 1920s a new mould-etch process was invented, in which art was etched directly into the mould, so that each cast piece emerged from the mould with the image already on the surface of the glass. This reduced manufacturing costs and, combined with a wider use of colored glass, led to cheap glassware in the 1930s, which later became known as Depression glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As the types of acids used in this process are extremely hazardous, abrasive methods gained popularity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Knitted_and_felted_glass">Knitted and felted glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Knitted and felted glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Knitted glass</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Mixed_media" title="Mixed media">technique</a> developed in 2006 by artist <a href="/wiki/Carol_Milne" title="Carol Milne">Carol Milne</a>, incorporating <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lost-wax_casting" title="Lost-wax casting">lost-wax casting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">mold-making</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glass_casting#Kiln_casting" title="Glass casting">kiln-casting</a>. It produces works that look knitted, though they are made entirely of glass.</p>
<p>Chinese artists Zhengcui Guo and Peng Yi premiered a technique of <i>felted glass</i> or "glelting", with positive critical reviews, at the 2015 Ha You Arts Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_printing">Glass printing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Glass printing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Neri_Oxman#Mediated_Matter" title="Neri Oxman">Mediated Matter</a> group and Glass Lab at MIT produced a prototype 3D printer that could print with glass, through their <i>G3DP</i> project. This printer allowed creators to vary optical properties and thickness of their pieces. The first works that they printed were a series of artistic vessels, which were included in the <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Hewitt,_Smithsonian_Design_Museum" title="Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum">Cooper Hewitt</a>'s <i>Beauty</i> exhibit in 2016.</p>
<p>Glass printing is theoretically possible at large and small physical scales, and has the capacity for mass production. However as of 2016 production still requires hand-tuning, and has mainly been used for one-off sculptures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern_making">Pattern making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pattern making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methods to make patterns on glass include <a href="/wiki/Caneworking" title="Caneworking">caneworking</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Murrine" title="Murrine">murrine</a>, engraving, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enameling" title="Enameling">enameling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millefiori" title="Millefiori">millefiori</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flamework&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flamework (page does not exist)">flamework</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilding</a>.</p>
<p>Methods used to combine glass elements and work glass into final forms include <a href="/wiki/Lampworking" title="Lampworking">lampworking</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Historical collections of glass art can be found in general museums. Modern works of glass art can be seen in a dedicated glass museums and museums of contemporary art. These include the Chrysler Museum in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Glass" title="Museum of Glass">Museum of Glass</a> in Tacoma, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Museum_of_Art" title="Toledo Museum of Art">Toledo Museum of Art</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corning_Museum_of_Glass" title="Corning Museum of Glass">Corning Museum of Glass</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Corning_(city),_New_York" title="Corning (city), New York">Corning, NY</a>, which houses the world's largest collection of glass art and history, with more than 45,000 objects in its collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corning_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corning-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts</a> in Boston features a 42.5 feet (13.0 m) tall glass sculpture, <i><a href="/wiki/Lime_Green_Icicle_Tower" title="Lime Green Icicle Tower">Lime Green Icicle Tower</a></i>, by Dale Chihuly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfa_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfa-7">[7]</a></sup> In February 2000 the <a href="/wiki/Smith_Museum_of_Stained_Glass_Windows" title="Smith Museum of Stained Glass Windows">Smith Museum of Stained Glass Windows</a>, located in Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Navy_Pier" title="Navy Pier">Navy Pier</a>, opened as the first museum in America dedicated solely to stained glass windows. The museum features works by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Comfort_Tiffany" title="Louis Comfort Tiffany">Louis Comfort Tiffany</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Lafarge" title="John Lafarge">John Lafarge</a>, and is open daily free to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a> has a collection of extremely detailed models of flowers made of painted glass. These were <a href="/wiki/Lampworking" title="Lampworking">lampworked</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Blaschka" title="Leopold Blaschka">Leopold Blaschka</a> and his son Rudolph, who never revealed the method he used to make them. The <a href="/wiki/Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka" title="Leopold and Rudolf Blaschka">Blaschka</a> <a href="/wiki/Glass_Flowers" title="Glass Flowers">Glass Flowers</a> are still an inspiration to glassblowers today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The UK's <a href="/wiki/National_Glass_Centre" title="National Glass Centre">National Glass Centre</a> is located in the city of <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland, Tyne and Wear</a>.</p>
