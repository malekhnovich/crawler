<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Information theory</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>.</div>
<p><b>Information theory</b> studies the <a href="/wiki/Quantification_(science)" title="Quantification (science)">quantification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">storage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">communication</a> of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>. It was originally proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claude_E._Shannon" title="Claude E. Shannon">Claude E. Shannon</a> in 1948 to find fundamental limits on <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">signal processing</a> and communication operations such as <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">data compression</a>, in a landmark paper entitled "<a href="/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication" title="A Mathematical Theory of Communication">A Mathematical Theory of Communication</a>". Now this theory has found applications in many other areas, including <a href="/wiki/Statistical_inference" title="Statistical inference">statistical inference</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurobiology" title="Neurobiology">neurobiology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> the evolution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and function<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of molecular codes, model selection in <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thermal_physics" title="Thermal physics">thermal physics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computing" title="Quantum computing">quantum computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism_detection" title="Plagiarism detection">plagiarism detection</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">pattern recognition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anomaly_detection" title="Anomaly detection">anomaly detection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A key measure in information theory is "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_entropy" title="Information entropy">entropy</a>". Entropy quantifies the amount of uncertainty involved in the value of a <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variable</a> or the outcome of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_process" title="Random process">random process</a>. For example, identifying the outcome of a fair <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coin_flip" title="Coin flip">coin flip</a> (with two equally likely outcomes) provides less information (lower entropy) than specifying the outcome from a roll of a <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">die</a> (with six equally likely outcomes). Some other important measures in information theory are <a href="/wiki/Mutual_information" title="Mutual information">mutual information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Error_exponent" title="Error exponent">error exponents</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_entropy" title="Relative entropy">relative entropy</a>.</p>
<p>Applications of fundamental topics of information theory include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">lossless data compression</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZIP_(file_format)" title="ZIP (file format)">ZIP files</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossy_data_compression" title="Lossy data compression">lossy data compression</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3s</a> and <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEGs</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel coding</a> (e.g. for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSL" title="DSL">Digital Subscriber Line (DSL)</a>).</p>
<p>The field is at the intersection of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurobiology" title="Neurobiology">neurobiology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>. Its impact has been crucial to the success of the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager</a> missions to deep space, the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a>, the feasibility of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, the development of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, the study of <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> and of human perception, the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>, and numerous other fields. Important sub-fields of information theory include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Source_coding" title="Source coding">source coding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Channel_coding" title="Channel coding">channel coding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithmic_complexity_theory" title="Algorithmic complexity theory">algorithmic complexity theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_information_theory" title="Algorithmic information theory">algorithmic information theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information-theoretic_security" title="Information-theoretic security">information-theoretic security</a>, and measures of information.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Information theory studies the transmission, processing, utilization, and extraction of information. Abstractly, information can be thought of as the resolution of uncertainty. In the case of communication of information over a noisy channel, this abstract concept was made concrete in 1948 by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">Claude Shannon</a> in his paper "<a href="/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication" title="A Mathematical Theory of Communication">A Mathematical Theory of Communication</a>", in which "information" is thought of as a set of possible messages, where the goal is to send these messages over a noisy channel, and then to have the receiver reconstruct the message with low probability of error, in spite of the channel noise. Shannon's main result, the <a href="/wiki/Noisy-channel_coding_theorem" title="Noisy-channel coding theorem">noisy-channel coding theorem</a> showed that, in the limit of many channel uses, the rate of information that is asymptotically achievable is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a>, a quantity dependent merely on the statistics of the channel over which the messages are sent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Information theory is closely associated with a collection of pure and applied disciplines that have been investigated and reduced to engineering practice under a variety of <a href="/wiki/Rubric_(academic)" title="Rubric (academic)">rubrics</a> throughout the world over the past half century or more: <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_system" title="Adaptive system">adaptive systems</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticipatory_system" title="Anticipatory system">anticipatory systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex systems</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complexity_science" title="Complexity science">complexity science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informatics_(academic_field)" title="Informatics (academic field)">informatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Systems_science" title="Systems science">systems sciences</a> of many descriptions. Information theory is a broad and deep mathematical theory, with equally broad and deep applications, amongst which is the vital field of <a href="/wiki/Coding_theory" title="Coding theory">coding theory</a>.</p>
<p>Coding theory is concerned with finding explicit methods, called <i>codes</i>, for increasing the efficiency and reducing the error rate of data communication over noisy channels to near the <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">Channel capacity</a>. These codes can be roughly subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">data compression</a> (source coding) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error-correction" title="Error-correction">error-correction</a> (channel coding) techniques. In the latter case, it took many years to find the methods Shannon's work proved were possible. A third class of information theory codes are cryptographic algorithms (both <a href="/wiki/Code_(cryptography)" title="Code (cryptography)">codes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">ciphers</a>). Concepts, methods and results from coding theory and information theory are widely used in <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis" title="Cryptanalysis">cryptanalysis</a>. <i>See the article <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ban_(unit)" title="Ban (unit)">ban (unit)</a> for a historical application.</i></p>
<p>Information theory is also used in <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_(information_gathering)" title="Intelligence (information gathering)">intelligence gathering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, and even in <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">musical composition</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_information_theory" title="History of information theory">History of information theory</a></div>
<p>The landmark event that <b>established</b> the discipline of information theory, and brought it to immediate worldwide attention, was the publication of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claude_E._Shannon" title="Claude E. Shannon">Claude E. Shannon</a>'s classic paper "<a href="/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication" title="A Mathematical Theory of Communication">A Mathematical Theory of Communication</a>" in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bell_System_Technical_Journal" title="Bell System Technical Journal">Bell System Technical Journal</a></i> in July and October 1948.</p>
<p>Prior to this paper, limited information-theoretic ideas had been developed at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a>, all implicitly assuming events of equal probability. <a href="/wiki/Harry_Nyquist" title="Harry Nyquist">Harry Nyquist</a>'s 1924 paper, <i>Certain Factors Affecting Telegraph Speed</i>, contains a theoretical section quantifying "intelligence" and the "line speed" at which it can be transmitted by a communication system, giving the relation <span class="texhtml"><i>W</i> = <i>K</i> log <i>m</i></span> (recalling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_constant" title="Boltzmann's constant">Boltzmann's constant</a>), where <i>W</i> is the speed of transmission of intelligence, <i>m</i> is the number of different voltage levels to choose from at each time step, and <i>K</i> is a constant. <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Hartley" title="Ralph Hartley">Ralph Hartley</a>'s 1928 paper, <i>Transmission of Information</i>, uses the word <i>information</i> as a measurable quantity, reflecting the receiver's ability to distinguish one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_of_symbols" title="Sequence of symbols">sequence of symbols</a> from any other, thus quantifying information as <span class="texhtml"><i>H</i> = log <i>S</i><sup><i>n</i></sup> = <i>n</i> log <i>S</i></span>, where <i>S</i> was the number of possible symbols, and <i>n</i> the number of symbols in a transmission. The unit of information was therefore the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_digit" title="Decimal digit">decimal digit</a>, much later renamed the <a href="/wiki/Hartley_(unit)" title="Hartley (unit)">hartley</a> in his honour as a unit or scale or measure of information. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a> in 1940 used similar ideas as part of the statistical analysis of the breaking of the German second world war <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis_of_the_Enigma" title="Cryptanalysis of the Enigma">Enigma</a> ciphers.</p>
<p>Much of the mathematics behind information theory with events of different probabilities were developed for the field of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann" title="Ludwig Boltzmann">Ludwig Boltzmann</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Willard_Gibbs" title="J. Willard Gibbs">J. Willard Gibbs</a>. Connections between information-theoretic entropy and thermodynamic entropy, including the important contributions by <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Landauer" title="Rolf Landauer">Rolf Landauer</a> in the 1960s, are explored in <i><a href="/wiki/Entropy_in_thermodynamics_and_information_theory" title="Entropy in thermodynamics and information theory">Entropy in thermodynamics and information theory</a></i>.</p>
<p>In Shannon's revolutionary and groundbreaking paper, the work for which had been substantially completed at Bell Labs by the end of 1944, Shannon for the first time introduced the qualitative and quantitative model of communication as a statistical process underlying information theory, opening with the assertion that</p>
<dl>
<dd>"The fundamental problem of communication is that of reproducing at one point, either exactly or approximately, a message selected at another point."</dd>
</dl>
<p>With it came the ideas of</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_entropy" title="Information entropy">information entropy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(information_theory)" title="Redundancy (information theory)">redundancy</a> of a source, and its relevance through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Source_coding_theorem" title="Source coding theorem">source coding theorem</a>;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Mutual_information" title="Mutual information">mutual information</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_capacity" title="Channel capacity">channel capacity</a> of a noisy channel, including the promise of perfect loss-free communication given by the <a href="/wiki/Noisy-channel_coding_theorem" title="Noisy-channel coding theorem">noisy-channel coding theorem</a>;</li>
<li>the practical result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shannon%E2%80%93Hartley_law" title="Shannon‚ÄìHartley law">Shannon‚ÄìHartley law</a> for the channel capacity of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaussian_channel" title="Gaussian channel">Gaussian channel</a>; as well as</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a>‚Äîa new way of seeing the most fundamental unit of information.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantities_of_information">Quantities of information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quantities of information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quantities_of_information" title="Quantities of information">Quantities of information</a></div>
<p>Information theory is based on <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>. Information theory often concerns itself with measures of information of the distributions associated with random variables. Important quantities of information are <a href="/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory)" title="Entropy (information theory)">entropy</a>, a measure of information in a single <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variable</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mutual_information" title="Mutual information">mutual information</a>, a measure of information in common between two random variables. The former quantity is a property of the probability distribution of a random variable and gives a limit on the rate at which data generated by independent samples with the given distribution can be reliably <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">compressed</a>. The latter is a property of the joint distribution of two random variables, and is the maximum rate of reliable communication across a noisy <a href="/wiki/Channel_(communications)" title="Channel (communications)">channel</a> in the limit of long block lengths, when the channel statistics are determined by the joint distribution.</p>
<p>The choice of logarithmic base in the following formulae determines the <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_entropy" title="Information entropy">information entropy</a> that is used. A common unit of information is the <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Binary_logarithm" title="Binary logarithm">binary logarithm</a>. Other units include the <a href="/wiki/Nat_(unit)" title="Nat (unit)">nat</a>, which is based on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_logarithm" title="Natural logarithm">natural logarithm</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciban" title="Deciban">hartley</a>, which is based on the <a href="/wiki/Common_logarithm" title="Common logarithm">common logarithm</a>.</p>

<p>Intuitively, the entropy <span class="texhtml"><i>H<sub>X</sub></i></span> of a discrete random variable <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i></span> is a measure of the amount of <i>uncertainty</i> associated with the value of <span class="texhtml"><i>X</i></span> when only its distribution is known.</p>
<p>The entropy of a source that emits a sequence of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> symbols that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically_distributed" title="Independent and identically distributed">independent and identically distributed</a> (iid) is <span class="texhtml"><i>N¬∑H</i></span> bits (per message of <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i></span> symbols). <p>Although it is sometimes used as a 'distance metric', KL divergence is not a true <a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a> since it is not symmetric and does not satisfy the <a href="/wiki/Triangle_inequality" title="Triangle inequality">triangle inequality</a> (making it a semi-quasimetric).</p>
<p>Another interpretation of the KL divergence is the "unnecessary surprise" introduced by a prior from the truth: suppose a number <i>X</i> is about to be drawn randomly from a discrete set with probability distribution <i>p(x)</i>. If Alice knows the true distribution <i>p(x)</i>, while Bob believes (has a <a href="/wiki/Prior_probability" title="Prior probability">prior</a>) that the distribution is <i>q(x)</i>, then Bob will be more <a href="/wiki/Self-information" title="Self-information">surprised</a> than Alice, on average, upon seeing the value of <i>X</i>. The KL divergence is the (objective) expected value of Bob's (subjective) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surprisal" title="Surprisal">surprisal</a> minus Alice's surprisal, measured in bits if the <i>log</i> is in base 2. In this way, the extent to which Bob's prior is "wrong" can be quantified in terms of how "unnecessarily surprised" it's expected to make him.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_quantities">Other quantities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other quantities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other important information theoretic quantities include <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9nyi_entropy" title="R√©nyi entropy">R√©nyi entropy</a> (a generalization of entropy), <a href="/wiki/Differential_entropy" title="Differential entropy">differential entropy</a> (a generalization of quantities of information to continuous distributions), and the <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mutual_information" title="Conditional mutual information">conditional mutual information</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coding_theory">Coding theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Coding theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coding_theory" title="Coding theory">Coding theory</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CDSCRATCHES.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="130" data-file-width="283" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/CDSCRATCHES.jpg/220px-CDSCRATCHES.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/CDSCRATCHES.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

A picture showing scratches on the readable surface of a CD-R. Music and data CDs are coded using error correcting codes and thus can still be read even if they have minor scratches using <a href="/wiki/Error_detection_and_correction" title="Error detection and correction">error detection and correction</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coding_theory" title="Coding theory">Coding theory</a> is one of the most important and direct applications of information theory. It can be subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">source coding</a> theory and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error_correction" title="Error correction">channel coding</a> theory. Using a statistical description for data, information theory quantifies the number of bits needed to describe the data, which is the information entropy of the source.</p>
<ul>
<li>Data compression (source coding): There are two formulations for the compression problem:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossless_data_compression" title="Lossless data compression">lossless data compression</a>: the data must be reconstructed exactly;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lossy_data_compression" title="Lossy data compression">lossy data compression</a>: allocates bits needed to reconstruct the data, within a specified fidelity level measured by a distortion function. This subset of Information theory is called <a href="/wiki/Rate%E2%80%93distortion_theory" title="Rate‚Äìdistortion theory">rate‚Äìdistortion theory</a>.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Error-correcting codes (channel coding): While data compression removes as much <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(information_theory)" title="Redundancy (information theory)">redundancy</a> as possible, an error correcting code adds just the right kind of redundancy (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error_correction" title="Error correction">error correction</a>) needed to transmit the data efficiently and faithfully across a noisy channel.</li>
</ul>
<p>This division of coding theory into compression and transmission is justified by the information transmission theorems, or source‚Äìchannel separation theorems that justify the use of bits as the universal currency for information in many contexts. However, these theorems only hold in the situation where one transmitting user wishes to communicate to one receiving user. In scenarios with more than one transmitter (the multiple-access channel), more than one receiver (the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Broadcast_channel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Broadcast channel (page does not exist)">broadcast channel</a>) or intermediary "helpers" (the <a href="/wiki/Relay_channel" title="Relay channel">relay channel</a>), or more general <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">networks</a>, compression followed by transmission may no longer be optimal. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Network_information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Network information theory (page does not exist)">Network information theory</a> refers to these multi-agent communication models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_theory">Source theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Source theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any process that generates successive messages can be considered a <b><a href="/wiki/Communication_source" title="Communication source">source</a></b> of information. A memoryless source is one in which each message is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_identically_distributed_random_variables" title="Independent identically distributed random variables">independent identically distributed random variable</a>, whereas the properties of <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodicity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stationary_process" title="Stationary process">stationarity</a> impose less restrictive constraints. All such sources are <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">stochastic</a>. These terms are well studied in their own right outside information theory.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Channel_capacity">Channel capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Channel capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_to_other_fields">Applications to other fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Applications to other fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence_uses_and_secrecy_applications">Intelligence uses and secrecy applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Intelligence uses and secrecy applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Information theoretic concepts apply to <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryptanalysis" title="Cryptanalysis">cryptanalysis</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turing" title="Turing">Turing</a>'s information unit, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ban_(unit)" title="Ban (unit)">ban</a>, was used in the <a href="/wiki/Ultra" title="Ultra">Ultra</a> project, breaking the German <a href="/wiki/Enigma_machine" title="Enigma machine">Enigma machine</a> code and hastening the <a href="/wiki/Victory_in_Europe_Day" title="Victory in Europe Day">end of World War II in Europe</a>. Shannon himself defined an important concept now called the <a href="/wiki/Unicity_distance" title="Unicity distance">unicity distance</a>. Based on the <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(information_theory)" title="Redundancy (information theory)">redundancy</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Plaintext" title="Plaintext">plaintext</a>, it attempts to give a minimum amount of <a href="/wiki/Ciphertext" title="Ciphertext">ciphertext</a> necessary to ensure unique decipherability.</p>
<p>Information theory leads us to believe it is much more difficult to keep secrets than it might first appear. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brute_force_attack" title="Brute force attack">brute force attack</a> can break systems based on <a href="/wiki/Public-key_cryptography" title="Public-key cryptography">asymmetric key algorithms</a> or on most commonly used methods of <a href="/wiki/Symmetric-key_algorithm" title="Symmetric-key algorithm">symmetric key algorithms</a> (sometimes called secret key algorithms), such as <a href="/wiki/Block_cipher" title="Block cipher">block ciphers</a>. The security of all such methods currently comes from the assumption that no known attack can break them in a practical amount of time.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_theoretic_security" title="Information theoretic security">Information theoretic security</a> refers to methods such as the <a href="/wiki/One-time_pad" title="One-time pad">one-time pad</a> that are not vulnerable to such brute force attacks. In such cases, the positive conditional <a href="/wiki/Mutual_information" title="Mutual information">mutual information</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Plaintext" title="Plaintext">plaintext</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ciphertext" title="Ciphertext">ciphertext</a> (conditioned on the <a href="/wiki/Key_(cryptography)" title="Key (cryptography)">key</a>) can ensure proper transmission, while the unconditional mutual information between the plaintext and ciphertext remains zero, resulting in absolutely secure communications. In other words, an eavesdropper would not be able to improve his or her guess of the plaintext by gaining knowledge of the ciphertext but not of the key. However, as in any other cryptographic system, care must be used to correctly apply even information-theoretically secure methods; the <a href="/wiki/Venona_project" title="Venona project">Venona project</a> was able to crack the one-time pads of the Soviet Union due to their improper reuse of key material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudorandom_number_generation">Pseudorandom number generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pseudorandom number generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pseudorandom_number_generator" title="Pseudorandom number generator">Pseudorandom number generators</a> are widely available in computer language libraries and application programs. They are, almost universally, unsuited to cryptographic use as they do not evade the deterministic nature of modern computer equipment and software. A class of improved random number generators is termed <a href="/wiki/Cryptographically_secure_pseudorandom_number_generator" title="Cryptographically secure pseudorandom number generator">cryptographically secure pseudorandom number generators</a>, but even they require <a href="/wiki/Random_seed" title="Random seed">random seeds</a> external to the software to work as intended. These can be obtained via <a href="/wiki/Extractor_(mathematics)" title="Extractor (mathematics)">extractors</a>, if done carefully. The measure of sufficient randomness in extractors is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Min-entropy" title="Min-entropy">min-entropy</a>, a value related to Shannon entropy through <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9nyi_entropy" title="R√©nyi entropy">R√©nyi entropy</a>; R√©nyi entropy is also used in evaluating randomness in cryptographic systems. Although related, the distinctions among these measures mean that a <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variable</a> with high Shannon entropy is not necessarily satisfactory for use in an extractor and so for cryptography uses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seismic_exploration">Seismic exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Seismic exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One early commercial application of information theory was in the field of seismic oil exploration. Work in this field made it possible to strip off and separate the unwanted noise from the desired seismic signal. Information theory and <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">digital signal processing</a> offer a major improvement of resolution and image clarity over previous analog methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiotics">Semiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Semiotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Concepts from information theory such as redundancy and code control have been used by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Semioticians&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Semioticians (page does not exist)">semioticians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ferruccio_Rossi-Landi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ferruccio Rossi-Landi (page does not exist)">Rossi-Landi</a> to explain ideology as a form of message transmission whereby a dominant social class emits its message by using signs that exhibit a high degree of redundancy such that only one message is decoded among a selection of competing ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous_applications">Miscellaneous applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Information theory also has applications in <a href="/wiki/Gambling_and_information_theory" title="Gambling and information theory">gambling and investing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_hole_information_paradox" title="Black hole information paradox">black holes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">bioinformatics</a>.</p>
